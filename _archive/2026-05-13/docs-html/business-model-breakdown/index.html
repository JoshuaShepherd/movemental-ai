01  ·  Overview

What Movemental actually is, in plain prose

Movemental is a company whose mission is to build the digital infrastructure a movement leader needs in the age of AI — specifically, a fully-owned publishing platform that integrates books, courses, articles, video, community, commerce, and AI-assisted authorship into a single ecosystem the leader controls. We are not a hosting service, a content management system, a Substack competitor, or a WordPress theme shop. We are movement infrastructure, which means the product we ship is inseparable from the relational network we curate and the economic model we refuse to break. Our conviction is that technology, properly ordered, should amplify human calling rather than extract from it; our business model is the tangible expression of that conviction.

We do two things with the same core capability. The primary business is digital publishing platforms built for individual movemental thought leaders — people with decades of earned credibility who have been forced to rent space on Substack, Patreon, WordPress, and Teachable while surrendering 15–85% of their revenue and all of their audience data. The future-tense, second business is operational AI tooling for movement organizations — nonprofits, church networks, denominational bodies, and para-church institutions that need fundraising dashboards, board reporting, prospect research, and staff-safe AI adoption without hiring a six-figure technology team. Both arms are expressions of the same thesis: the tools movement leaders and movement organizations actually need are not being built by incumbents, and the gap is now wide enough that a small, lean team can build them faster than any agency can propose them.

The simple one-sentence version, the one we use in elevator pitches and the top of every founding document, is this: Movemental helps movement leaders own their digital publishing platform and amplify their impact through AI-powered content creation and network effects — and helps the organizations around them adopt AI in ways that protect trust instead of eroding it. Everything else in this document is an expansion of that sentence.

The four crises we address

To understand our product, you have to understand the market state we are responding to. We see four intersecting crises. First, a credibility crisis: when 40–60% of online content is AI-generated, the traditional signals of real expertise collapse, and a movement leader with thirty years of lived practice shows up in a feed indistinguishable from a content farm. Second, a formation crisis: consumer platforms are engineered for growth and performance, not formation and substance, so the tools our people have to use actively pull them away from the work they are called to do. Third, an AI crisis: AI is simultaneously the tidal wave drowning legitimate voices and the only realistic amplification tool capable of lifting them back up, and navigating that tension with integrity is now a professional requirement leaders were never trained for. Fourth, a publishing crisis: traditional publishing is too slow, too gatekept, and too extractive, while the modern SaaS alternatives capture the majority of revenue in exchange for minimal amplification. Movemental exists in the collision point of these four crises.

What we are explicitly not

Because the word "platform" is overloaded, it is worth being explicit. Movemental is not another social-media network, not a content mill, not an influencer agency, not a celebrity amplification tool, not a generic SaaS product, not an open platform anyone can join, and not a traditional publisher. It is a curated infrastructure layer for a specific, qualified group of people whose life and work catalyze visible, verifiable, multiplying movement. The curation is the product as much as the code is.

02  ·  Founding team & equity

Why Josh, Alan, Brad, and Rich — and what it means that Alan and Brad are each 10% co-founders who are also movement leaders

Movemental has four founders, with equity distributed 70 / 10 / 10 / 10. This split is not an accident, and it is the single most important thing to understand about how this company makes decisions and who it serves. The three 10% co-founders — Alan Hirsch, Brad Brisco, and Rich Robinson — are not traditional advisors. They are founding equity partners whose names, networks, and lived practice are structurally part of the company's credibility thesis.

Alan Hirsch (10%) is the most widely read voice in missional ecclesiology on the planet — twenty books, a global audience, four decades of field practice, and the signature frameworks (mDNA, APEST / 5Q, apostolic genius, missional-incarnational impulse) that most of the other hundred leaders we serve are actively teaching in some form. His role inside Movemental is twofold. He is our prototype thought leader: his fully-built platform at alan-hirsch is the reference implementation that proves every promise we make to every future leader. And he is our movemental conscience: his forty-year commitment to scenius, mutual elevation, and recovery-over- invention is why our business model looks the way it does. Crucially, Alan is himself a movement leader, which means the platform we built with him is not a vendor experiment — it is the working demonstration of what we intend to build with every other leader in the scenius.

Brad Brisco (10%) is a nationally recognized church-planting leader with deep institutional footing through NAMB, the Send Network, Forge America, and Forge Kansas City, and a substantial body of work on covocational and neighborhood-based mission (Covocational Church Planting, Next Door as It Is in Heaven, The Missional Quest, ReThink). Brad matters for two reasons. He is the bridge between movemental theology and institutional infrastructure — he can walk into rooms that Alan does not walk into and move the conversation without translation loss. And he is a movement leader in his own right with his own corpus, his own audience, and his own need for exactly the platform we build; his site is the second proof of concept that demonstrates the model works for a leader with a smaller, denser, more practitioner-oriented corpus than Alan's.

Rich Robinson (10%) brings coaching, market-activation, and leader-development expertise. Where Alan is the theological centre and Brad is the institutional bridge, Rich is the one who translates the value proposition into individual leader relationships and keeps the scenius warm as it grows.

The reason the 10% co-founder stakes for Alan and Brad matter economically and philosophically is that they encode a structural commitment: the leaders who exemplify what we serve are themselves owners of the company that serves them. We are not a vendor pointing at testimonials. We are a scenius whose architecture includes our own leaders as first-class members of it. Every leader we onboard sees Alan's and Brad's platforms as a demonstration not of aspirational marketing but of what founding members are themselves running on. That is a very different conversation than "here's a case study."

03  ·  The scenius of 100

Why we serve a curated network of ~100 movement leaders, not the open market

Scenius is Brian Eno's word for the intelligence of a scene — the phenomenon in which a small cluster of practitioners collectively becomes more capable than any one of them working alone. It is the exact opposite of the "lone genius" myth that drives most creator-economy platforms. Alan has taught for years that collaborative genius is both morally better and strategically smarter than isolated individual achievement, and Movemental is built on that conviction as a first-principle design choice, not as flavouring.

Concretely, that means we are not trying to serve the three million creators on Substack. We are building for a curated founding cohort of roughly 100 movement leaders — the globally recognized practitioners whose life, work, and communication catalyze visible, verifiable movement that multiplies beyond their direct oversight. Our author maturity model (detailed in docs/business-docs/core-docs/24- maturity-model-qualification.md) uses a three-layer qualification: an mDNA foundation gate (mature discipleship, missional presence, organic systems), a movemental leadership gate (visible followers, reproducible patterns, second-generation fruit), and a content credibility and embodied integration gate (verifiable track record, domain authority, life-matches- message integrity). We say no to roughly nine candidates for every one we admit, and we say so loudly, because the quality of the scenius is the economic engine of the whole model.

Why 100 rather than 10 or 10,000? At 10, the platform is a boutique and network effects do not compound. At 10,000, the quality floor collapses and we become what we set out to replace. At 100, three things happen simultaneously that do not happen at any other scale. First, cross- linking density becomes high enough that individual creator reach multiplies 28–500×: when one hundred credible sites link to each other around the same subject territory, each individual site rises in both classical SEO and the new LLM- driven generative search answers. Second, the scenius becomes capable of peer verification — members vouch for new candidates, catch drift, and preserve standards in a way no platform policy team ever could. Third, the economics become self-reinforcing: once a leader is embedded in a hundred-node network of mutually amplifying credibility, the switching cost to leave for a neutral platform is no longer about software features — it is about losing a scenius.

Alan's platform exists inside this logic as the prototype the network orbits; Brad's platform demonstrates that the logic extends beyond Alan's specific cultural location. The other ninety-eight are the economic and theological point of the company.

04  ·  The leader platforms

What the platform is, what it replaces, and why Alan's build is the reference

A Movemental platform is a complete React/Next.js application, owned by the leader, running on shared Supabase and Vercel infrastructure, that integrates five capabilities into one dashboard: a digital bookstore and e-reader (Shopify Storefront catalog sync, Stripe checkout, Kindle- quality reading experience, reading analytics); a learning management system (course builder, modules, lessons, progress tracking, certificates, cohort management); a professional blogging and article platform (TipTap rich text, auto-save, version history, Liveblocks real-time collaborative editing, scheduled publishing, pgvector semantic search, full-text search); a video streaming and hosting layer (upload or YouTube/Vimeo embed, custom player, resume position, watch analytics, series organization); and an AI-assisted amplification engine (twenty-plus specialized OpenAI Agents trained on movemental theology, voice-baseline fingerprinting for drift protection, cross-platform backlink intelligence, and semantic discovery). It replaces the fractured ecosystem of WordPress + Substack + Patreon + Teachable + Mailchimp + YouTube + a custom bookstore with one unified, owned surface.

The platform ships in two to four weeks per leader, not six to twelve months, because it is not a custom build. It is a shared codebase templatized around a tenant.config.ts file and CSS variable theming; every new leader is a new deployment with their own domain, branding, content, and audience data, scoped by a TENANT_ORG_ID at the database layer through PostgreSQL Row-Level Security. One deployment per organization preserves the feel of a bespoke site — its own URL, its own typography, its own voice — while the shared infrastructure beneath it captures the economics and the network effects. This is the architectural reason our pricing is defensible: a single-tenant deployment from an agency is a $50,000–$150,000 commitment because every piece is built once. Our architecture amortizes every piece across the scenius.

Alan Hirsch — the prototype platform (primary demo)

Alan's site is the reference implementation and the flagship demo for every future conversation. It lives in the alan-hirsch repo and runs the complete architecture: Next.js 15 App Router with Turbopack, Drizzle ORM against Supabase PostgreSQL (148 tables in the public schema), the six-layer type-safety chain (Drizzle → Zod → services → API routes → hooks → UI) that makes downstream changes traceable and safe, twenty-two specialized AI agents built on the OpenAI Agents SDK, Vercel AI SDK streaming, a Shopify-synced bookstore, Stripe checkout, pgvector semantic search at 1536 dimensions, and a full Row-Level Security model scoped by organization_id. The content side includes Alan's full twenty-book corpus, the articles library, the video archive, the mDNA Formation Course (31 lessons across 9 modules), and the voice-baseline system that keeps AI- assisted writing recognizably Alan's. When we show a prospective leader what Movemental is, we walk them through Alan's platform. It is the product spec rendered in public.

Brad Brisco — the second proof of concept (secondary demo)

Brad's platform is intentionally the second one we built, and it serves as our demonstration that the architecture is not a bespoke Alan-shaped artefact. Brad's corpus is denser and more practitioner-facing — covocational and neighborhood-based church planting, Send Network and Forge training design, Missional Essentials curriculum, shorter competency modules rather than long theological works — and his platform shows how the same primitives rearrange around a different voice and a different audience without the code diverging. Brad sits in institutional rooms Alan does not, so his platform also shows how the Movemental model interacts with established denominational networks, Send Institute publishing workflows, and multi-stakeholder content governance. He is the bridge case: same architecture, different centre of gravity, full ownership, 90% revenue retention.

The hundred-leader network around them

Every leader after Alan and Brad is deployed on the same shared core, with their own tenant config, their own domain, their own content, and their own 90% revenue share. As each platform goes live, it joins the cross-linking and semantic-discovery layer — internal backlinks across platforms, network-wide pgvector search, and the shared AI agent infrastructure that allows readers on one leader's site to surface substantively related work on another's. That cross-referencing is the mechanical source of the 28–500× amplification we quote. It is the scenius expressed as software.

05  ·  The organization arm

How we serve the organizations around our leaders — and what the non-profit dashboard is

Movemental has a second arm aimed at the organizations that employ, support, and surround our leaders: nonprofits, denominations, church networks, camps, mission agencies, seminaries, and para-church institutions. These organizations face the same crises our leaders face — credibility collapse, AI anxiety, stretched staff, donor fatigue, formation drift — but their needs are operational rather than publishing. They need prospect research and donor pipeline tools, board-ready reporting, staff-safe AI adoption frameworks, cross-reference dashboards for their existing relational networks, and governance scaffolding that lets them use new technology without eroding trust. The non-profit dashboard at Dev/repos/non-profit-dashboard is the working prototype of this arm.

The non-profit dashboard is a React + Vite single-page application organized around donor and prospect workflows for a mission-driven nonprofit (first built for Youthfront, a Kansas City ministry). Its pages — Dashboard, Pipeline, Connections, Prospect Profile, Insider Directory, Search Guides, Upload / Cross-Reference, Getting Started Guide, Documentation Resources, Video Tutorial — give a small development team the same operational intelligence an enterprise fundraising shop would pay six figures a year for. It is purposefully built around an AI agent (AIAgent.tsx) that is context-aware of which page the user is on, so prospect research, follow-up drafting, and relational cross-referencing happen in situ rather than in a separate chat tab. Like our leader platforms, it is owned by the organization, not rented.

The dashboard also encodes a governance framework we call Safety → Sandbox → Skills → Solutions. Before any staff member uses AI on ministry work, the organization names what it is protecting, who owns what, and where the lines are (Safety). Then a bounded, synthetic-data experimentation window gives staff first-hand evidence of what AI does well and badly in their actual context (Sandbox). Then human skills are formed through practice in abstraction, de-identification, and output evaluation (Skills). Only then do specific tools move into live operational workflows (Solutions). That framework is not incidental. It is the product. The dashboard is the software expression of a mission-aligned AI adoption path that a nonprofit board, a donor, and a parent could each defend.

Commercially, the organization arm is priced on a tiered subscription basis (expected in the low hundreds per month for the operational dashboards), plus cohort training programs at roughly $3,000–$10,000 per three-week sprint, plus white-glove executive consulting at $10,000–$50,000 for bespoke engagements, plus (in its mature form) a nonprofit arm able to receive grants and donations for organizations that need the work but cannot afford market pricing. The cohort and consulting tiers are immediate revenue; the subscription dashboards are the long-term recurring layer; the grants arm is the equity lever that lets the mission scale into contexts that market pricing excludes.

The two arms are structurally complementary, not parallel. Lessons from serving individual leaders (voice preservation, RAG over corpus, content governance, shared AI infrastructure) translate directly into operational tooling for organizations. Lessons from serving organizations (governance frameworks, staff training, board reporting, donor intelligence) translate back into tools our leaders need as their own work becomes institutional. One company, one set of foundations, two market expressions.

06  ·  Economic model

$1,000 + 10% — why the numbers are the way they are, and why they will not change

The headline economic model for a leader platform is $1,000 upfront plus a 10% share of platform-generated revenue, with the leader retaining the other 90%. Every piece of that structure is load-bearing. The $1,000 entry price is deliberately an order of magnitude below what an agency would quote for work of comparable scope ($50,000–$150,000) because the pricing is missional: we want leaders whose work is multiplying to adopt the platform before their economics are solved, not after. The 10% revenue share exists because our success must be tied to theirs; we make money when they make money, and we never extract from a leader whose platform is not yet producing (our no-revenue-share floor sits at $100 / month in platform earnings). The 90% creator retention is the inverse of Substack (which captures ~15% after payment processing), Patreon (12–18% effective), Teachable (15–20% effective), and traditional publishers (85–90% extraction). Ninety-ten is our non-negotiable charter, written into the foundational documents as something that cannot be walked back without breaking what Movemental is.

As the network matures, the $1,000 entry tier sits alongside tiered development pricing: $2,500 for enhanced platforms with moderate customization, $5,000 for premium platforms with extensive custom features, $10,000-plus for enterprise custom development. The 10% revenue share stays flat across all tiers. We do not believe in penalizing success with higher take rates; the leaders generating more revenue are, by construction, the ones producing the strongest network effects, and their continued retention at 90% is the thing that attracts the next cohort.

The cost structure beneath this is lean by design. Direct costs are Vercel and Supabase hosting plus modest software licensing; overhead is near zero; labor is founder-led, AI-augmented development, with contractors engaged only for maintenance, customer service, and cohort facilitation as we grow. Because every leader platform is a redeployment of a shared codebase rather than a bespoke build, the marginal cost of onboarding a new leader falls with every deployment while the revenue share accumulates. Gross margins on the revenue-share side run at roughly 95%; blended margins at steady state sit around 85%. That is what makes the numbers below feasible.

Projected growth trajectory

In the foundation phase (roughly 2026, the first 25 leaders), we expect approximately $40,000 in total revenue against $75,000 in operating expense — an investment year by design, in which the economics are secondary to proving the scenius works. In the expansion phase (2027, 100 leaders in network), revenue grows to roughly $795,000 against $400,000 in operating expense, with margins flipping decisively positive as network effects lift per-platform revenue from $6,000 to roughly $72,000 annually. In the maturation phase (2028–2030, 500–1,000 leaders), we project revenue of $5M–$24.5M at 50% net margin as the network reaches full amplification density. These numbers are best understood not as forecasts but as the shape of the curve the scenius model produces if the quality floor holds — which is precisely why our qualification process is so strict.

Why we do not take venture capital

Our cost structure, founder alignment, and long-horizon thesis allow us to grow to cash-flow positive inside year two without outside capital. The reason we structure around that rather than fundraise aggressively is that a venture-backed cap table introduces growth-rate pressure that is incompatible with the quality-curated scenius model. An investor whose return profile requires a ten-year exit will, correctly from their point of view, push for open onboarding, higher take rates, and a larger addressable market — which is exactly the set of decisions that would break the product. Our 90/10 revenue share, our invite-only gate, our $1,000 entry price, and our Alan/Brad co-founder structure are non-negotiable for the same reason.

07  ·  Strategic moats

Why this is defensible, in four layers

Network effects. Each new qualified leader makes every existing leader more valuable. Cross-platform backlink density improves SEO and LLM-surface visibility for the entire network; semantic search across platforms lets a reader on Alan's site discover substantively relevant work on any other member's site; network-wide analytics and benchmarking compound as the dataset grows; and qualified peer vouching creates the kind of trust that AI-generated content fundamentally cannot fake. None of this is replicable by a competitor without already having the network — which is the definition of a moat.

AI data advantage. The accumulated corpus of movemental content across the network — books, articles, video transcripts, courses, assessments — is a proprietary training substrate that gives our AI agents a semantic gravity no general-purpose model can match. Voice-baseline fingerprinting, theological-drift detection, and cross-corpus citation all become better as the corpus grows. A new entrant could build the agents; they could not build the corpus.

Speed and cost. Because the shared codebase, the tenant-config architecture, and the AI-assisted development workflow together let us deploy a full-stack publishing platform in two to four weeks at the $1,000 price point, we operate in an economic regime that agencies and incumbents structurally cannot enter. An agency that tried to match our pricing would lose money on every engagement; an incumbent that tried to match our speed would have to rebuild their delivery model. This is a classic disruptive-innovation position.

Trust and brand ethos. Alan and Brad are not testimonials, they are co-founders. The company's theological convictions are not marketing language, they are the charter. Our 90/10 revenue share is not a promotional offer, it is a commitment the founders have publicly locked. The result is a brand credibility layer that acts as a filter on both sides: it attracts the leaders we want to serve and repels the leaders we do not. That is hard to replicate with a press release.

08  ·  Long horizon

The ten-to-twenty-year arc, and the commitments that cannot move

The long-term vision is a Movemental Intelligence Network: a global ecosystem in which movemental leaders own their digital presence, collaborate authentically, and amplify collective impact through shared AI cognition. The near-term expression is the hundred-leader scenius and the organization arm; the mid-term expression is geographic expansion (English-speaking markets first, then Spanish, Portuguese, Mandarin, Arabic; then Global South networks in Africa, Asia, and indigenous communities); the long-term expression is a genuine ecosystem in which content creation, learning and development, community building, movement multiplication, and impact measurement all flow through infrastructure designed for service rather than extraction.

The charter-level commitments that must not move as we scale are short and concrete. Platform ownership stays with the leader — their content, their audience, their revenue, their domain, their export rights — always, without exception. The 90/10 revenue split stays as it is; nine-one goes back in favour of the creator, not the other way around. Quality curation stays — we remain a curated network, not an open platform. Scenius values stay — mutual elevation over competition, collaboration over platform protection, peer verification over individual metrics. AI ethics stay — voice preservation, theological integrity, human agency, transparent disclosure. Mission alignment stays — the technology serves the mission, and when it stops doing that we cut it, no matter how impressive it is. And founder stewardship stays — the four of us hold the responsibility of keeping these commitments intact, and we cannot delegate that responsibility away.

This is, finally, what the whole document is about. Movemental is an attempt to demonstrate in software, in business, and in relationship what it looks like when technology is properly ordered — when it amplifies human calling instead of extracting from it, strengthens authentic community instead of substituting for it, and advances movements instead of drowning them. Alan's platform shows the model works for a globally-read theologian. Brad's platform shows it works for an institutionally-networked practitioner. The non-profit dashboard shows it extends to the organizations that surround them. The ninety-eight leaders we onboard behind them are the point. The hundred-plus organizations that adopt the dashboard arm are the multiplier. And the 90% revenue retention, the curated gate, and the co-founder structure are the guarantees that none of it reverts to the mean.
