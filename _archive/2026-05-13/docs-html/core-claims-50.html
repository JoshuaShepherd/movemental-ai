I. The moment

What is actually happening to leaders, organizations, and institutions on the AI frontier, and how to stand inside it without flinching.

01 Trello · verbatim

The wiser way.

Gut check: What posture am I taking right now — avoidance, rush, or something more demanding?

Source: Trello "Credibility Claims" card 1 — The Wiser Way. Word-for-word from the card description.

There is a wiser way to navigate AI.

Most leaders today are pulled toward one of two instincts. Some avoid it, hoping it will pass or fearing what it might do to their people and their work. Others rush in, adopting tools quickly, speaking with a level of confidence they have not yet earned, and reorganizing their organizations around capabilities they do not fully understand.

Neither path holds.

Avoidance quietly concedes the future. Uncritical adoption reshapes your organization faster than your wisdom can keep up. Both lead to loss of identity, just at different speeds.

There is another way.

It is slower, more honest, and more demanding. It refuses the pressure to appear like an expert. It accepts that this is a real frontier, not a solved problem. It requires leaders to stay present, to make decisions in public, to learn in real time, and to take responsibility for what their organization becomes as these tools are introduced.

This way is not about mastering AI.

It is about remaining recognizable as yourself while you engage it.

A year from now, most organizations will be more efficient. Fewer will still be coherent. Fewer still will be faithful to what they were called to be.

The work is not to move faster than the technology.

The work is to lead in such a way that your people, your voice, and your mission are not quietly replaced in the process.

That is the wiser way.

Most leaders ask the wrong question first. They ask whether they are behind. The honest answer is: probably not. ChatGPT was released in late 2022. The most experienced AI consultant a church or nonprofit could hire today has, at most, three and a half years of practice with the technology now reshaping the sector. There is real urgency in April 2026. It is not the kind that demands you rush. It is the kind that demands you climb the four levels of the AI Stewardship Sequence in order, starting now.

Safety is urgent first. Most organizations are already using AI without governance, and insurers are starting to notice. A staff member pasting donor records into a free chat tool is a real exposure. Sandbox becomes urgent the moment Safety is real. Bounded exploration is where missed value lives. Each month an organization stays outside structured experimentation is a month of time savings, donor responsiveness, content reach, and operational stewardship that quietly does not happen. The cost is invisible until peers who learned earlier start to pull ahead. Sandbox is the environment where stewardship can actually take place, and it only becomes possible once Safety has cleared the room.

Skills becomes urgent the moment Sandbox has produced something worth teaching. Solutions becomes urgent the moment Skills have formed staff who can be trusted to deploy. The order is not bureaucratic. It is what makes the next step trustworthy.

The honest sentence is not, "You are behind." It is this: you are exactly where the field is, and the field is young. The question is whether you will climb the four steps in order, starting now, or wait until the urgency on the first step arrives in the form of an incident.

AI is not, first, a technology problem.

Gut check: Could our senior leader describe this organization's relationship to AI in one paragraph, from memory, in a hallway, without a deck?

Source: articles/this-is-not-a-tools-problem.md.

When a leadership team sits down to address AI, the first move is almost always the same. They treat it as a technology problem. We need an AI strategy. Let's get IT involved. Should we use Claude or ChatGPT? Should we pay for Copilot, Gemini, or build something internal? What is the right vendor stack? What policy will counsel accept? That sequence sounds responsible. It is also the sequence that produces the weakest outcomes.

AI is not, first, a technology problem. It is a human one.

Before it is technological, it is psychological — leaders processing fear, pressure, and the responsibility of governing a capability they did not choose. It is sociological — teams learning what it means to work alongside something that drafts faster than they can think. It is anthropological — staff renegotiating what counts as authorship, judgment, and presence. For churches and many nonprofits, it is also theological — a question about truth, voice, formation, and what kind of mediation is consistent with the mission.

The technology is real. The technology choices matter. But every one of those choices sits downstream of how the people in the organization have been formed to use them. An organization that has not done the human work adaptively — built shared language, shared judgment, and shared boundaries — will simply ship its unresolved questions through whatever model it picks.

This is precisely what the AI Stewardship Sequence is for. Safety, Sandbox, and Skills exist to do the human work the technology cannot do for itself. Solutions only deploys after that work is real. If a senior leader cannot describe, in plain language, the organization's human relationship to AI — what is at stake, who decides, what is off-limits, and why — the organization does not yet have an AI posture. It has a vendor list.

06 Documented

The real tension: the cost of standing still versus the cost of rushing in.

Gut check: Are we paralyzed, hurried, or actually doing the integration work that lets either decision land?

Source: articles/ai-collapses-the-cost-of-integration.md; articles/the-movemental-thesis.md.

The choice is not between integrity and impact. It is between two costs that look opposite and quietly have the same root.

Standing still has a cost. It is rarely dramatic. It does not show up as a single missed quarter or a single lost donor. It shows up as drift. Staff start using consumer AI tools without governance. Peers learn faster than you. Operational decisions get made by leaders who have never seen what a careful experiment looks like inside their own walls. Eighteen months in, the organization has not preserved itself. It has absorbed the consequences of choices it never named.

Rushing in has a cost too, and it is louder. In 2025, MIT's NANDA initiative published a widely-cited study of enterprise AI adoption. The headline made the rounds: roughly ninety-five percent of generative AI pilots produced no measurable return. The reason did not. The pilots did not fail because the models were weak or because the leaders were careless. They failed because AI was bolted onto organizations that had not yet integrated their own knowledge, workflows, and judgment. The technology amplified incoherence faster than the incoherence could be fixed. Plugging a powerful capability into a fragmented foundation does not produce leverage. It produces speed in the wrong direction.

So the real tension is between two failures. The first is paralysis, doing nothing while the field shifts and the organization quietly loses the right to its own future decisions. The second is hasty deployment, spending a year and a budget on a pilot that was always going to fail because the foundation underneath it was never integrated.

The way through is neither. It is the AI Stewardship Sequence: do the integration work first, do it patiently, and let the technology arrive where it can compound rather than amplify the gaps. Standing still is a threat. Rushing is a threat. The patient sequence between them is the only path that holds.

Every organization carries two intelligences: informational and relational.

Gut check: Could anyone outside the founder's head describe both what we know and whom we are connected to, accurately, today?

Source: articles/two-intelligences-integration.md; articles/relational-intelligence.md; articles/intelligence-fragmentation.md.

When something feels broken inside an organization, leaders reach for one of two diagnoses. A content problem, or a systems problem. Both miss what is actually happening. Every organization carries two kinds of intelligence, and almost no one names them out loud.

Informational intelligence is what the organization knows and produces. The books and articles. The talks, sermons, podcasts, and recorded teaching. The PDFs, decks, training materials, and donor reports. The CMS, the Squarespace site, the WordPress blog, the Notion workspace, the four shared drives no one fully owns. This part is intuitive. Most leaders nod at the inventory. Most also recognize, when they look at it honestly, that it is scattered.

Relational intelligence is the harder one to see, because most organizations do not store it anywhere. It is the living memory of who the organization is actually connected to, and what condition the connection is in. Which donors gave for which reasons. Which volunteers stayed and why. Which pastors recommended you to which networks. Which board member knows which philanthropist personally. Which alumna lit up at last year's keynote. Which partner organization has been quietly cooling for six months. This intelligence is real and valuable, and almost always lives in the heads of three or four senior people. When one of them leaves, a chunk of the organization's actual capacity walks out the door with them.

The standard attempt to fix this is a CRM, and that is a reasonable attempt. But ask the next question. In an age where AI is interacting daily with the informational side of the organization, is the relational side actually integrated, or is it just a contact list with notes attached? In most organizations, the CRM ends up being one more fragment in a fragmented relational landscape. Names captured, but not the quality of connection. Activity logged, but not the context of trust or the history of shared work that makes the relationship matter.

So the structural reality is this. Both intelligences are present in every organization. Both are fragmented. And the two have rarely been connected to each other. What is taught is not connected to who carries it. What is known is not connected to whom it is known with.

When informational intelligence becomes integrated and activated — the body of work holds together, can be navigated, and can be searched without producing nonsense — and when relational intelligence becomes integrated and activated alongside it — the people who carry the work are visible, connected, and able to move ideas through real trust — something different becomes possible. Ideas travel with their context. Trust travels with what is being said. The work compounds rather than resets. That intersection is where transformation actually happens. Not from content alone. Not from community alone. From both, integrated and active.

12 Documented

The fragmentation problem: integrated work compounds. Fragmented work produces slop.

Gut check: Does our response to AI begin with integration, or with more production?

Source: articles/the-fragmentation-tax.md; articles/ai-collapses-the-cost-of-integration.md.

Every piece of work an organization produces is either connected to the rest of its work or it is not. That distinction used to be a productivity issue. With AI in the room, it has become a credibility issue.

Integrated work compounds. A new article extends an existing argument. A new training session builds on a recorded one. A new donor letter draws from a body of language the organization has actually used. A new staff member can find their way into the work without a senior leader translating it for them. Each new piece adds weight to what is already there.

Fragmented work was already unworkable with humans alone. People could not find the right document. Teams duplicated each other's analysis. New hires rebuilt, in parallel, the position the organization had already taken three years earlier. Donors carried fuzzier and fuzzier mental pictures of what the organization actually did. Strategic conversations began by re-establishing what the organization is, every time.

AI does not relieve any of that. It worsens it. When fragmented work is fed into a model, the output is fluent, confident, and slightly off. Articles read smoothly and contradict each other. Voice drifts on every surface. Ideas appear without the context that gave them weight. What used to be quiet incoherence becomes loud incoherence — the kind a serious newcomer can spot in ninety minutes.

The plain version of the rule is this. Integrated work compounds, credibly. Fragmented work produces slop, faster.

Which is why any organization whose response to AI begins with production rather than integration — more content, more campaigns, more pilots, more tools — is volunteering for a future in which both their AI efforts and their other technological efforts simply do not work well. Not because AI is the wrong investment. Because integration is the precondition that makes any subsequent technological investment land.

Integration is the unglamorous step. It is also the one the next decade rewards.

If you want a single diagnostic question for whether your organization is fragmented in public, this is the one to keep returning to. Ninety minutes is generous. Serious newcomers are increasingly rare. A simple map is a low bar. The test is unforgiving on purpose.

Polish no longer signals depth. AI just made surface craft free.

Polish used to cost money. The cost was the filter. A well-edited essay, a clean structure, a careful voice, these were expensive to produce, so encountering them was a reasonable proxy for someone having had something to say.

AI has not removed signal. It has repriced it. The cost of surface craft has dropped to nearly zero, while the cost of real expertise, the kind earned over decades, has not moved at all. Authorship, formation, judgment, and trust, the anthropology underneath AI use.

The AI Stewardship Sequence: Safety → Sandbox → Skills → Solutions.

Gut check: Where are we actually standing, and what is the next honest step?

Source: articles/the-ssss-framework.md; articles/ssss-field-guide-for-organizational-leaders.md; Trello card AI Stewardship Sequence (SSSS).

Picture a staircase. Four treads. A landing at the top. No elevator beside it. You can complain about the architecture, or you can climb. What you cannot do is arrive on the fourth tread without touching the first three and still claim you are standing on something solid.

The path is plain on purpose. Safety: governance, conviction, and boundaries before tools, vendors, or Tuesday deadlines decide for you. Sandbox: bounded exploration that turns curiosity into shared evidence. Skills: formation toward judgment, not training theater on buttons. The plainness is not modesty. It is discipline. The claim the sequence makes is smaller than a methodology and larger than a slogan: the order is the framework. Treat the four words as a menu and you will reorder them within a quarter. Treat them as a fixed order you climb and you give the organization something it can stand on while the ground keeps moving.

A homepage is a sequence of decisions, not a layout.

Most B2B and SaaS homepages ship as a Frankenstein of sections borrowed from whoever the team last admired. The result is "fine", and indistinguishable from every competitor that did the same. The cure is to pick a playbook on purpose: decide what each section is doing, why it sits where it sits, and which question it is supposed to answer in the visitor's head.

This view collects the strongest published frameworks, StoryBrand, Demand Curve, April Dunford's positioning, the conversion-copy school (Wiebe, Dry, Laja), and the classic copywriting structures (AIDA, PAS, BAB, 4Ps), and pairs them with a section-by-section checklist. Treat it as a menu. The point is to choose, not to use them all.

A. Whole-page essentials

Before any individual section, the page as a whole has to pass a few hard tests. These cut across every framework below.

The five-second test

Peep Laja (Wynter, CXL) · Joanna Wiebe (Copyhackers) · Demand Curve.

Show the homepage to a stranger from the target audience. Take it away after five seconds. They should be able to answer:

  1. What is this product?
  2. Who is it for?
  3. What outcome does it promise?
  4. What makes it different from the obvious alternative?
  5. What am I supposed to do next?

Any answer the visitor has to invent is the section to fix first.

Above-the-fold message hierarchy

Joanna Wiebe. Copyhackers.

The above-the-fold area walks the visitor down a five-step ladder:

  1. Pre-headline / category cue. Tells the reader what kind of thing this is so they know whether to keep reading.
  2. Headline / main value claim. The single biggest outcome promised, not the feature, the result.
  3. Subhead / clarifier. Adds the specifics, who it is for, how it works, what it replaces.
  4. Proof element. One credible signal close to the main claim, recognizable customer, precise stat, real quote.
  5. Primary call to action. Exactly one. Secondary actions are visibly subordinate.

Clarity over cleverness ("Mom test")

Harry Dry, Marketing Examples; Basecamp; Paul Graham.

Read the headline out loud. Could a sharp 14-year-old who has never heard of the category understand what you do? Cleverness, metaphors, and category jargon push everything that follows into a fog. Plain almost always wins. Clever beats plain only when it is also clear.

Whole-page checklist

Synthesized from Wiebe, Dunford, Laja, Demand Curve, and Marketing Examples.

  1. Does the page name a specific reader, not "everyone"?
  2. Does the page name the alternative the reader is currently using or considering, and why ours is better for them, not in the abstract?
  3. Is there one primary CTA repeated through the page, with secondary CTAs visibly subordinate?
  4. Is there proof within one screen of every major claim, logos, numbers, named customers, screenshots of real outputs?
  5. Could you delete each section and notice it missing? If a section can vanish without consequence, it is filler.
  6. Does the page sequence emotion before logic, felt problem first, mechanism second, evidence third, or the reverse, and is that on purpose?
  7. Does the visual hierarchy match the content hierarchy? Largest type carries the largest claim; nav and footer are the quietest things on screen.
  8. Was the page written without a competitor or template open, and only audited against them at the end?

B. Framework library

Seven canonical structures the rest of this view references. Each is shorthand for the same underlying job, get a real reader from "what is this?" to "I want to act."

StoryBrand SB7

Donald Miller, Building a StoryBrand.

  1. A character who wants something (the customer, not the brand).
  2. has a problem, external, internal, philosophical.
  3. and meets a guide (you) with empathy and authority.
  4. who gives them a plan, clear, simple, three steps.
  5. and calls them to action.
  6. that ends in success…
  7. or helps them avoid failure.

Strongest when the audience is non-technical and value is outcome-driven. Risk: every page sounds the same ("we know you're frustrated…"). Use the structure, not the voice.

Demand Curve LP hierarchy

Julian Shapiro / Demand Curve.

  1. Hero, what, for whom, why it matters, primary CTA.
  2. Logo bar, instant credibility.
  3. Problem agitation, name the pain in their words.
  4. Mechanism, how it works, in one screen.
  5. Features, three to five concrete capabilities.
  6. Outcomes / proof, case studies, numbers, named users.
  7. Objections, pricing, security, "is this for me?".
  8. Final CTA, same as the hero, restated with momentum.

The default modern SaaS structure. Boring on purpose. Reliable when there is no creative anchor; predictable when there is.

April Dunford positioning

April Dunford, Obviously Awesome & Sales Pitch.

  1. Competitive alternatives. What would they do without you?
  2. Unique attributes. What can you do that those alternatives can't?
  3. Value those attributes enable. Translate to outcomes.
  4. Best-fit customers. Who pays the most for that value?
  5. Market category. The frame in which the value is obvious.

Not a copy template, a strategy upstream of copy. Use this to decide what the page should claim before writing a word of it.

AIDA

Elias St. Elmo Lewis (1898). Still useful.

  1. Attention, break the scroll.
  2. Interest, make them lean in.
  3. Desire, make them want the outcome.
  4. Action, make the next step trivial.

Best as a sanity check across the page, not as a section template.

PAS. Problem · Agitate · Solve

Direct-response staple; widely used by Copyhackers.

  1. Problem. Name what they are stuck on.
  2. Agitate. Make the cost of staying stuck felt.
  3. Solve. Position the product as the resolution.

High-tension; risks feeling manipulative if the agitation is ginned up. Works only when the problem is real and the words come from real customers.

BAB. Before · After · Bridge

Direct-response copywriting standard.

  1. Before. Their current world.
  2. After. The world they want.
  3. Bridge. The product as the route between.

Lower-pressure cousin of PAS. Useful for outcome-driven products where the gap between current and possible state is the story.

4Ps. Promise · Picture · Proof · Push

Henry Hoke, popularized by direct-response copywriters.

  1. Promise. The big outcome.
  2. Picture. Make the outcome vivid.
  3. Proof. Show evidence.
  4. Push. Ask for the action.

Cleanest framework when you already know what to say. Lacks the diagnostic of Dunford or the empathy arc of StoryBrand.

C1

Hero

The first screen. The job is to answer "what, for whom, why now, and what next" within five seconds.

Purpose

  • Tell the right reader they are in the right place.
  • State the single biggest promise, in their language.
  • Give one obvious next action.
  • Earn the next thirty seconds of attention.

Key questions

  1. Could a target reader paraphrase the headline 30 seconds after closing the tab?
  2. Does the headline name an outcome, not a feature or category abstraction?
  3. Is a specific reader implied, visible in the subhead or a single supporting line?
  4. Is the primary CTA visually dominant over secondary actions?
  5. Is there a proof element within the first screen (logo bar, named customer, real number)?
  6. Does the visual support the claim, or is it decorative?

Playbook approaches

A. Outcome headline + clarifier

StoryBrand / Demand Curve default. Stripe, Linear, Notion, Loom, Webflow.

Big, plain promise. Subhead names the audience and the mechanism. One CTA, with one quiet secondary.

e.g. "Payments infrastructure for the internet.. Millions of companies of all sizes use Stripe online and in person…"

B. Contrarian / category-redefining

April Dunford.

Headline names the alternative being rejected; subhead claims a new category. Higher-risk, higher-reward; works when the product really does not fit the obvious shelf.

e.g. Basecamp's "Project management's gone sideways. Basecamp puts it back."

C. Problem-first

PAS / direct-response.

Headline is the felt pain, not the product. Subhead pivots to the fix. Strong when the audience knows the pain by name; weak when the pain is unrecognized.

e.g. "Tired of spreadsheets pretending to be a CRM?"

D. Manifesto / posture

Category leaders / movements. Patagonia, Basecamp, 37signals.

Headline is a stance, not a feature or a problem. Used by category leaders who can afford to teach the audience how to read them. Risks vagueness; demands proof immediately below.

e.g. "There is a wiser way to navigate AI." (cf. claim 01)

Failure modes

  • Two competing headlines. A "main" headline and a "dramatic" tagline that pull in different directions.
  • Two equal CTAs. "Get a demo" and "Start free" presented identically, neither dominates, both lose.
  • Hero image as decoration. Stock illustration, abstract gradient, blurry product screenshot. The visual should make the claim more credible, not just fill space.
  • Category jargon. "AI-native operating system for…", fine in pitch decks, poison on a homepage.
  • Eyebrow as the only specifier. A small "for nonprofit operators" eyebrow does not rescue a headline that names no audience.
C2

Trust bar / social proof

Sits within the first scroll, often immediately under the hero. The job is to short-circuit doubt before the reader has to ask.

Purpose

  • Make the implicit "can I trust you?" question disappear.
  • Help the reader self-identify ("oh, organizations like mine use this").
  • Reduce perceived risk before the next claim has to do work.

Key questions

  1. Will the target reader recognize at least three of the names?
  2. Are the names relevant to the reader's category, not just impressive in the abstract?
  3. Is the relationship honest? "Used by" is different from "as featured in" is different from "trusted by 10,000 teams."
  4. Is the proof a specific number instead of a vague superlative?
  5. Is it placed where the reader actually sees it, within the first scroll, not buried at the bottom?

Playbook approaches

A. Logo bar (5–8 logos)

Five to eight customer logos in muted greyscale, on a neutral surface. Above the bar: "Used by teams at" or "Trusted by". Default for almost every modern SaaS.

B. Numbers-first

Two or three large stats with short labels, "10,000 teams", "$2.4B processed", "98% renewal". Use when the logos are not yet recognizable but the numbers are real.

C. Named-quote anchor

A single named pull-quote with photo and role, in place of (or alongside) a logo bar. Stronger than logos when the audience trusts people more than brands, common for movements, advisory businesses, B2B sold to founders.

D. Press / institutional badge

"As featured in" with editorial logos. Useful for consumer or general-audience products; weak for B2B where category logos do more work.

Failure modes

  • Logos no one in the audience recognizes. Worse than no logo bar, signals "we tried."
  • "Trusted by 10,000 teams" with no logos and no quotes. A claim that contradicts the lack of evidence around it.
  • Inflated relationship. Counting trial accounts as "customers" or single-event speakers as "partners". Reader can usually tell.
  • Too small to read. Logos shrunk to fit the band; nobody can identify them; the proof never lands.
C3

Problem framing

The section that names the pain in the reader's own words. Done well, it earns the right to introduce the product.

Purpose

  • Make the reader say "yes, that's me" in their head.
  • Surface a pain they already feel but may not have named precisely.
  • Establish that you understand the situation better than the reader expected.
  • Create the gap the rest of the page fills.

Key questions

  1. Are the pain words from voice-of-customer research, interviews, support tickets, sales-call transcripts, or invented in the marketing room?
  2. Is the problem specific, not "things are inefficient"?
  3. Is there one dominant pain, or a buffet that dilutes everything?
  4. Is the framing honest, or does it manufacture urgency that does not exist?
  5. Does the section resolve into the value prop, or just leave the reader anxious?

Playbook approaches

A. PAS (Problem · Agitate · Solve)

Three blocks. Block one names the problem. Block two walks through the cost of staying stuck. Block three pivots to the product. Best when the pain is acute and widely felt.

B. "The old way vs. the new way"

Two columns. Left column lists the painful current behaviors, in plain language. Right column lists the product-enabled behaviors, in symmetrical structure. Used by Linear, Superhuman, Notion early pages.

C. Three-bullet pain summary

Just three sentences naming three pains, no agitation. Lower-pressure; works when the reader is sophisticated enough that any further pushing reads as theatrical.

D. BAB before-state ("if you're like most…")

A single rich paragraph painting the reader's current world: what their week looks like, what their team is stuck on, what costs are quietly accruing. Followed by "there is another way." Best when prose voice is a strength.

Failure modes

  • Strawman pain. A problem stated so generically nobody actually has it ("collaboration is hard").
  • Agitation theater. Manufactured urgency, "you're falling behind!", that the reader can smell from the first scroll.
  • Five problems. A buffet of pains, none of them owned. Pick the dominant one.
  • Pain that the product does not actually solve. Naming a pain to look empathic, then pivoting to features that address something else.
C4

Value proposition

The bridge from "yes, that's me" to "and here is what changes." This is where the product enters the room.

Purpose

  • State, in one paragraph, what the product does and what it changes.
  • Translate features into outcomes the reader cares about.
  • Make the unique attribute legible, what only this product does.
  • Earn the right to the next section ("how").

Key questions

  1. Is the value stated as outcome, not feature?
  2. Could a competitor's homepage make the same claim verbatim? If yes, it is not a value proposition, it is a category description.
  3. Is the unique-attribute (Dunford) claim explicit, or only implied?
  4. Is there one promise, or three competing ones?
  5. Is there immediate proof of the promise, number, screenshot, named customer, or does the page ask for trust before earning it?

Playbook approaches

A. 4Ps. Promise & Picture

One sentence promise. One paragraph picturing what changes. One stat or screenshot as proof. Used by Stripe, Vercel, Linear at the section under the hero.

B. Dunford unique-attribute claim

State the attribute (what only you do), the value it enables, and the alternative it replaces, in two sentences. Boring and devastatingly effective.

C. StoryBrand "the plan"

Three named steps the customer takes with you (e.g. "1. Sign up. 2. Connect your data. 3. Ship faster."). Reduces perceived risk by making the path concrete.

D. Posture statement (movement / authority)

Stake a position about the category itself before describing the product. Works when the brand is a point of view; collapses when the product cannot back the position with capability.

Failure modes

  • "Transform your workflow." Every vague verb that could be on any homepage in any category.
  • Feature list disguised as value. Bullets that name the feature ("AI-powered search") instead of what changes for the reader.
  • Three competing promises. "Faster, cheaper, better", pick one and earn it.
  • No proof in the same screen. A bold promise floating on white space, with the screenshot two scrolls away.
C5

How it works

Mechanism. The section that lets the reader picture themselves using the product without having to imagine it.

Purpose

  • Make the mechanism legible, what they do, what it does, what they get.
  • Reduce buyer risk by showing the path is concrete.
  • Show real product, not a marketing diagram.
  • Set up features as specifics of a coherent flow, not a list.

Key questions

  1. Are there three steps, not seven?
  2. Is each step a real verb the user actually performs?
  3. Are the screenshots / videos real product with realistic data, not marketing illustrations?
  4. Is complexity revealed honestly, or hidden under a too-simple metaphor?
  5. Could a reader explain the flow to a colleague after one read?

Playbook approaches

A. 3-step linear

Three numbered cards in a row. Each: verb + one-line description + small visual. The classic SaaS default. Used by Stripe Atlas, Linear onboarding, Notion templates.

B. Narrated screenshot tour

Long scroll of alternating screenshots and short captions. Each screenshot shows real product state. Used by Linear, Loom, Pitch.

C. Single embedded demo / video

One 60–90 second product walkthrough. Highest conversion when the product is visual; weakest when the value is conceptual or relational.

D. Before-and-after comparison

A single image or animation of the same task done with and without the product. Strong when the productivity delta is obvious; gimmicky when it isn't.

Failure modes

  • Seven-step flow. The product is easier than the diagram makes it look.
  • Lorem-ipsum screenshots. Empty states, placeholder names, blue boxes. The reader stops believing the product is real.
  • Vague verbs. "Streamline." "Integrate." "Optimize." Replace each with a real user action.
  • Marketing illustration in place of UI. A pretty isometric graphic instead of an actual screen. Useful as decoration; useless as proof.
C6

Features / capabilities

The section that substantiates the value proposition with concrete capability. Has to resist becoming a feature dump.

Purpose

  • Prove the value prop is real, not aspirational.
  • Give the reader specifics they can hold in their hands.
  • Anchor the deeper product / feature pages.
  • Make each feature earn its slot by tying to a real outcome.

Key questions

  1. Does each feature carry an outcome, not just a name?
  2. Is each feature shown with a real screenshot / GIF, or only described?
  3. Are features prioritized, or listed in alphabetical / accidental order?
  4. Are there too many, could the page lose half of them and feel stronger?
  5. Are features grouped by job-to-be-done, not by internal product taxonomy?

Playbook approaches

A. Bento grid (3–6 cards)

Asymmetric grid; each cell pairs a feature name, a one-line outcome, and a screenshot or GIF. Apple set the visual standard; Linear and Vercel adapted it for SaaS.

B. Alternating image + text rows

Three to five rows. Each: heading (outcome), paragraph, product screenshot. Image alternates left/right. The long-standing SaaS workhorse.

C. Tabbed feature explorer

Horizontal tab strip; one feature visible at a time. Used when there are many features and the page would otherwise scroll forever. Risk: tabs hide content from scanners.

D. Outcome-grouped feature list

Two or three outcome headings ("Ship faster", "Cut support load"); each owns three short feature bullets. Best when audiences are mixed and you want each segment to find their version.

Failure modes

  • Feature dump. Twelve cards, no hierarchy, no outcomes. Reader skims, learns nothing.
  • "Powerful, intuitive, fast." Adjective soup. Replace with one specific behavior per card.
  • Screenshots that don't show real data. Test data with names like "John Doe" and "Acme Inc" undermines every feature it touches.
  • Internal taxonomy. Grouping by team / module instead of by user goal. Makes the reader do the translation work.
C7

Use cases / audiences

The section that lets segments self-identify and find their version of the value. Optional, but if the product serves more than one audience, almost always necessary.

Purpose

  • Let each target reader see themselves named.
  • Route serious leads to deeper segment pages.
  • Show the product's reach without diluting the main promise.
  • Replace generic "for everyone" with specific "for these people, here is what changes."

Key questions

  1. Are the segments real groups the company actually sells to, or marketing fiction?
  2. Does each segment have its own proof, at least one named customer, screenshot, or quote?
  3. Do segments link to deeper pages, or are they end-states?
  4. Are there too many? Three segments is usually plenty; six dilutes everything.
  5. Does each segment's promise differ meaningfully, or is it the same value prop renamed?

Playbook approaches

A. Three-card segment row

Three cards: persona / role / industry. Each card has its own headline, one paragraph, and a deeper-page link. Used by Linear, Vercel ("for engineering", "design", "product"), Notion ("teams", "personal", "enterprise").

B. Tabbed audience switcher

Tab strip, "Churches · Nonprofits · Institutions", with the same hero-style block underneath each tab, rewritten for that audience. Compact when audiences are close cousins; thin when they really diverge.

C. Industry / role index

A grid of 6–10 named segments linking to dedicated landing pages. Used by big platforms (Stripe, AWS) where the product is broadly applicable and SEO is part of the acquisition strategy.

D. Named-customer segment proof

For each segment, a single representative named customer with a one-paragraph case story. Highest-trust option; demands real customer relationships.

Failure modes

  • Segments without proof. Three personas, zero named customers. Marketing fiction the buyer can sense.
  • Identical content per segment. Tabs that say the same thing with the noun swapped.
  • Six segments on the home page. Pick three; let the rest live on a sub-nav.
  • Persona caricatures. "Sarah, 34, marketing director, hates spreadsheets." Internal artifact; unwelcome on a public homepage.
C8

Differentiation

The section that addresses "why not the obvious alternative?" before the reader leaves the page to find out.

Purpose

  • Name the alternative the buyer is already considering.
  • State the real, defensible reason to pick this instead.
  • Be honest about the tradeoff, every choice has one.
  • Pre-empt the question that costs the most deals.

Key questions

  1. Is the alternative named, by category or by competitor, or hidden behind "the old way"?
  2. Is the differentiation specific ("we run on your own infra") rather than generic ("simpler, faster")?
  3. Does the section acknowledge a tradeoff, or pretend the product is better at everything?
  4. Is the comparison fair? Buyers can smell a stacked deck instantly.
  5. Does the section have its own proof, or just claims?

Playbook approaches

A. "Old way / new way" two-column

Two columns. Left: behaviors of the old way (named or implied). Right: behaviors of the new way. Best when the alternative is a category, not a single competitor.

B. Named comparison table

Compare directly against named competitors on five to eight criteria. High-trust when honest; instantly fails when every cell is checked for you and unchecked for them.

C. "We are not …"

A short paragraph or sub-page stating what the product is not trying to be. Used by Basecamp, 37signals, Linear ("not a Jira replacement"). Gives buyers permission to reject the wrong-fit version.

D. Dunford competitive-alternative paragraph

Direct paragraph: "Most teams comparing us are looking at X. Pick X if Y. Pick us if Z." High-clarity; demands confidence in the positioning.

Failure modes

  • Stacked-deck comparison table. Every box checked for us, every box unchecked for them. Buyer disengages.
  • Generic differentiation. "Simpler. Faster. Smarter." Empty calories.
  • No tradeoff named. A product that is better at everything is a product that is better at nothing in particular.
  • Bashing the competitor. Reads as insecure; the buyer notices.
C9

Testimonials / case studies

The section that turns the product's claims into other people's words and outcomes. The deepest form of proof a homepage carries.

Purpose

  • Move "we say so" to "they say so."
  • Make the outcome measurable and concrete.
  • Help the reader see a customer who looks like them.
  • Carry the trust into the final CTA.

Key questions

  1. Are the testimonials named with role and company, not "Marketing Director, anonymous"?
  2. Do the quotes carry specifics, a number, a workflow, a before-and-after, instead of generic praise?
  3. Do the customers look like the target reader?
  4. Is there a case study behind at least one quote, with a real metric?
  5. Does every quote sound the same? If yes, they were ghost-written or aggressively edited; the reader can usually tell.

Playbook approaches

A. Single anchor pull-quote

One large quote, with photo, name, role, company, and metric. Used by Vercel, Linear at the top of the proof band. High-trust when the customer is recognizable.

B. Three-card testimonial row

Three quotes side by side, each with face and named attribution. The default. Loses force when all three quotes sound interchangeable.

C. Case study row with metric

Three cards. Each card: customer logo, headline metric ("3.2× faster onboarding"), one-line context, link to full case. Demands real cases, fakes here are a brand tax for years.

D. Embedded video testimonial

30–90 second customer video. Highest-trust signal a homepage can carry; demands a customer willing to be on camera. Usually paired with a written quote for scanners.

Failure modes

  • Anonymous "Marketing Manager" quotes. Less than no proof, the reader assumes you couldn't get permission.
  • Generic praise. "Game-changer. Couldn't live without it." Replace with one specific thing the customer can now do.
  • Every quote sounds the same. Marketing edited too hard. Let the customer's voice stay slightly rough.
  • Stock photos. "Smiling young professional in glasses" telegraphs that the testimonials are fabricated.
C10

Pricing teaser

Optional on the homepage; mandatory somewhere accessible. Pre-qualifies, reduces sticker shock, and surfaces the tier most readers should pick.

Purpose

  • Make the price obvious so the reader can rule themselves in or out.
  • Surface the obvious starting tier without forcing a click.
  • Reduce "is this for me?" anxiety before the final CTA.
  • Distinguish self-serve from sales-led without hiding either.

Key questions

  1. Is the price visible within two clicks of the homepage?
  2. Is there a recommended tier, or are all tiers presented as equals?
  3. Is "Contact us" the only path? If so, the homepage is filtering aggressively, make sure that is on purpose.
  4. Are the differences between tiers legible without a magnifying glass?
  5. Does the free / trial offer match the rest of the page's promise, or feel like a different product?

Playbook approaches

A. Three-tier card preview

Free / Pro / Enterprise. Each card with price, headline feature set, and CTA. The middle tier is highlighted ("most popular"). The default for product-led SaaS.

B. "Starting at $X" pointer

A single line on the homepage, "Starts at $49/mo", linked to a full pricing page. Lighter footprint; works when the page has bigger jobs to do.

C. Calculator / interactive estimator

Sliders or inputs that let the reader compute their own price. Used when pricing scales with usage and a single number would mislead.

D. Sales-led / "Contact us"

No public price. Standard for enterprise / strategic sales. Should still surface enough qualifying signals ("for organizations with X+ seats") to keep the wrong fits from booking calls.

Failure modes

  • Hidden price for a self-serve product. Forces every prospect through a gate; kills product-led growth.
  • Five-tier matrix on the home page. The full pricing comparison belongs on /pricing, not the front door.
  • "Custom pricing" with no qualifier. Either tell them roughly what to expect, or tell them who you do not sell to.
  • Free tier indistinguishable from paid. Reader sees no reason to upgrade; conversion stalls.
C11

FAQ

Sometimes the strongest section on the page. Done well, it answers the objections that cost real deals.

Purpose

  • Address the real objections in the reader's own language.
  • Pre-empt support, sales, and security questions.
  • Reclaim search-traffic intent ("does X integrate with Y?").
  • Give honest, specific answers, not marketing-friendly evasions.

Key questions

  1. Are the questions ones real prospects ask, or sanitized versions thereof?
  2. Are the answers specific, or do they evade?
  3. Are pricing, security, data, and "is this for me?" all covered?
  4. Are there too many? Five strong questions beat fifteen weak ones.
  5. Does the FAQ link to deeper resources where appropriate?

Playbook approaches

A. Five-question accordion

Five tightly scoped questions, each with a 1–2 paragraph answer. The default. Anything more belongs on a dedicated FAQ / docs page.

B. Buyer-stage grouping

Questions grouped by stage, "Evaluating", "Implementing", "Pricing & security". Used when buyers come with very different concerns at different points.

C. "Is this for me?" qualifier

A single question, "Is this for my organization?", with an honest paragraph naming who the product is and isn't for. High-trust; demands self-knowledge.

D. Inline objection-handlers (no FAQ section)

Skip the FAQ section. Instead, address objections inline at the moment they arise, pricing concern next to pricing, security concern next to features. Used by Stripe and Vercel.

Failure modes

  • Marketing-written questions. "Why is your product so great?", the reader stops trusting the section instantly.
  • Evasive answers. "We take security very seriously" instead of "SOC 2 Type II, audit reports available on request."
  • Kitchen-sink list. Twenty-five questions; nobody reads any of them.
  • Stale questions. Pricing answers that no longer match the pricing page; integrations that no longer exist.
C12

Final CTA

The closing band. The job is to capture the reader who has just made up their mind, with no ambiguity about the next step.

Purpose

  • End the page with momentum, not exhaustion.
  • Restate the primary CTA, not introduce a new one.
  • Make the next step trivial, one click, no hunting.
  • Match the voice and confidence of the hero, not a quiet contact form at the bottom.

Key questions

  1. Is this the same primary CTA as the hero, or a weaker substitute?
  2. Is the verb specific ("Start a 14-day trial") rather than vague ("Get started")?
  3. Does the section restate the promise in one sentence above the button?
  4. Is there a secondary low-commit option (book a call, watch a demo) for readers who are not yet ready?
  5. Is the CTA visually dominant, most contrast on the page in this band?

Playbook approaches

A. Restated promise + primary CTA

One-sentence promise (echoing the hero), one button, small secondary link. The default, clean, unambiguous, impossible to misuse.

B. Two-step (low-commit then high)

"Watch a 90-second demo" as the primary CTA, "Talk to us" secondary. Use when the buying decision is too heavy for "Start trial" to be honest.

C. Calendar-embedded

Inline calendar (Calendly / Cal.com) in the final section. Removes a click; signals confidence. Best for advisory / sales-led offers.

D. Quiet, deliberate close

A short statement of posture instead of a CTA flourish. Used by movements / authority brands where the reader self-selects ("if this resonates, here is the next step").

Failure modes

  • Footer-only CTA. The reader who just decided has nothing to click on without scrolling further.
  • Weak verb. "Learn more." End the page; do not punt the decision again.
  • Ambiguous next step. Three buttons of equal weight ("Trial / Demo / Pricing"). Pick the one you want them to take.
  • New promise at the bottom. A different value claim at the end confuses the page's argument right at the moment of action.

Using this view

How to take this from a reference document to a homepage decision.

  1. Pick one whole-page framework (B). Most Movemental surfaces will use a hybrid of StoryBrand (empathy arc) and Dunford (positioning), but commit. Borrowing one move from each of seven frameworks is how pages end up incoherent.
  2. Walk the section list (C1–C12) in order. For each section, choose one playbook variant. Note the choice in the page brief.
  3. Audit against the whole-page checklist (A) only after the draft exists. Audits before drafts kill voice.
  4. Cross-reference Core claims for the language. Almost every promise this homepage will make already exists, in stronger form, in the claims view, use it, don't reinvent it.
  5. Print and walk away. Read the page aloud the next morning. Anything that survives that test ships.

A. What these pages are called in the industry

A few overlapping conventions, used inconsistently. Pick a noun and stick with it; readers will not, but the team needs to.

Solutions pages

Most common B2B SaaS term. Salesforce, HubSpot, Adobe, Microsoft.

Top-level "Solutions" nav with sub-routes by audience, industry, role, or use case. Strong SEO play. Fits enterprise buyers searching "X solution for [my industry]."

Generic "solutions" copy has the worst reputation in B2B, shorthand for "we have not picked an audience." Use the structure, not the word.

Industry / vertical pages

Salesforce Industry Clouds, Stripe Industry, Plaid, Twilio.

Pages organized by the customer's industry, healthcare, financial services, retail. The default pattern when sales motions diverge by industry. "Stripe for SaaS", "Stripe for marketplaces."

Persona / role pages

Linear, Notion, Webflow, Loom, per-team pages.

Pages organized by the buyer's role: "for engineers", "for product teams", "for marketing." Default for product-led horizontal SaaS where the same product serves multiple functions.

Audience / customer-type pages

The Movemental pattern.

Pages organized by the kind of organization the buyer leads, church, nonprofit, school, startup, agency. The right framing when "industry" is too coarse and "role" is too narrow.

/churches, /nonprofits, and /institutions are customer-type pages in this taxonomy.

Use case pages

Stripe /use-cases, Notion /templates, Vercel /uses.

Pages organized by the job the customer is hiring you to do, "Run payroll", "Onboard new staff", "Sunset legacy systems." Strong SEO play; intent-aligned.

Often complements rather than replaces audience pages, same product, same buyer, different job.

Customer / community pages

Patagonia, 37signals, partner showcases.

Not selling pages, pages that describe a community or ecosystem the brand is part of. Where the Movemental movement-leaders / trusted-voices surface lives. Not on the audience-page shelf.

Movemental's working vocabulary

Per CLAUDE.md and docs/build/strategy/movement-leaders-as-ecosystem-layer.md.

  • Audience pages, /churches, /nonprofits, /institutions. The buying surfaces. Customer-type pages in industry terms.
  • Trusted voices, /movement-leaders, /voices. Ecosystem / community surface, not an audience page. Frames as definition + practitioner fit, never as a fourth audience funnel.
  • Use case pages are not part of Movemental's current IA. Plausible future surface (e.g. "Drafting a public AI posture"), but not implemented today.

B. Pages, tabs, or a hub?

The first decision is structural, not editorial. Ship the wrong structure and the right copy will not save it.

When distinct pages

  1. The value proposition genuinely differs per audience, not "the same product with different examples."
  2. The buying motion / decision-makers differ. A senior pastor and a development director do not buy the same way.
  3. You need same-audience proof, testimonials, logos, case studies that only land for that audience.
  4. SEO matters. Separate URLs rank for separate intent.
  5. Sales runs distinct motions per audience and needs a stable URL to send.
  6. Each audience can carry 5+ sections of genuinely unique content.

When tabs / accordion

  1. The value prop is identical; only words and examples shift.
  2. Audiences are illustrative, not commercially distinct.
  3. The audience is already mid-funnel and has self-identified.
  4. You have two or three audiences, no more.
  5. SEO for these terms is not a goal.
  6. Total content per audience would barely fill one screen.

The hub-plus-sub-pages pattern

Stripe /customers, HubSpot /industries, Notion /teams.

The dominant SaaS pattern. A short, opinionated hub page describes who the product is for and why; the hub names each segment in one paragraph and links out to a deep sub-page per segment. Example URL shape:

  • /who-we-serve, hub with a 3-card overview, shared trust band, and audience switcher.
  • /who-we-serve/churches, full audience page (D1–D10).
  • /who-we-serve/nonprofits, full audience page.
  • /who-we-serve/institutions, full audience page.

Why it wins: distinct URLs for SEO and sales handoff; shared proof at the hub for buyers who haven't yet picked a frame; shallow nav cost (one item, not three); a natural place to put cross-segment proof and "compare segments" content.

Failure modes (structural)

  • Tabs that hide everything. Three audiences as tabs on /audiences. Search engines see one URL with one paragraph; sales has nothing to send.
  • Three near-duplicate pages. Distinct URLs, identical copy with the noun swapped. No SEO benefit; dilutes brand voice.
  • Audience pages with no hub. /churches, /nonprofits, /institutions appear in nav as siblings of /pricing, buyers who haven't picked yet have no intermediate landing.
  • Movement leaders as a fourth audience. Per Movemental doctrine: collapses the trusted-voices ecosystem layer into a buying funnel and confuses everything downstream.

C. Whole-page essentials for an audience page

The homepage's job is "what is this and why should I keep reading." An audience page's job is different: "this is for me, and here is what changes."

The two questions every audience page must answer

April Dunford, Sales Pitch.

  1. Why is this for me specifically? Not "for organizations." For this kind of organization, with these constraints, this language, these stakes.
  2. What changes for me, concretely, that would not change with the obvious alternative? Outcome, not feature. Differentiated, not generic.

If a single sentence in the hero answers both, the page already works. Everything else makes it deeper, not more legible.

Audience-page checklist

Synthesized: Dunford, Wiebe, Drift persona-led marketing, Stripe customer-page patterns.

  1. Does the URL contain the audience term (/churches, not /audience-1)?
  2. Is the audience named in the H1, in the meta title, in the OG image?
  3. Does the page use language from this audience's interviews, not the homepage's voice rewritten?
  4. Does every proof element on the page come from this audience? No mixing testimonials.
  5. Does the page show one full case study from this audience, not just quotes?
  6. Does the page route to this audience's CTA (the right form, the right calendar, the right next step)?
  7. Could a member of this audience read the page and recognize themselves on every section, not just the hero?
  8. Does the page name the alternative this audience is currently using, and address it?
D1

Hero (audience-named)

The audience-page hero has a harder job than the homepage hero: it has to make the reader feel recognized before it does anything else.

Purpose

  • Confirm to the audience the page is for them, in their words.
  • State the unique promise for this audience, not the homepage promise translated.
  • Show one piece of audience-specific proof (logo, quote, number) within the first screen.
  • Send them to the right next step for their buying motion.

Key questions

  1. Is the audience named in the H1?
  2. Does the subhead carry an outcome specific to this audience, something the homepage hero would not say?
  3. Is there a same-audience proof element within the first screen?
  4. Does the CTA match this audience's buying motion (book a call vs. start trial vs. RFP)?
  5. Could a member of this audience read the hero alone and feel "yes, this is for me" within five seconds?

Playbook approaches

A. "For [audience], [outcome]"

Headline names the audience and the outcome in one line. Subhead names a constraint specific to the audience.

e.g. "For nonprofit leaders trying to lead staff into AI without losing the mission."

B. Audience-pain hero

Headline is the audience's specific pain in their words. Subhead pivots to "there is another way." Risks feeling theatrical if the pain is not real.

e.g. "You're being asked to have an AI policy you do not yet have."

C. Quote-as-hero

A 1–2 sentence quote from a customer in this audience, used as the H1 itself, with the customer's name and role under it. High-trust; demands a strong customer relationship and consent.

D. Posture for this audience

A stance specific to this audience's situation, not a product claim. Use when the audience has a particular cultural or institutional anxiety the product addresses (e.g. for institutions: governance + reputational stakes).

Failure modes

  • Homepage hero with the noun swapped. Worst-case audience page; the reader can tell instantly.
  • Audience word in the eyebrow only. A small "for nonprofit leaders" tag does not save a generic H1.
  • Stock photo of "audience". Smiling pastors / generic boardroom. Worse than no image.
  • CTA mismatched to motion. "Start free trial" on an institutional page where the real motion is "book a call."
D2

Problem framing in their words

The section that proves you understand this audience's specific situation, not "leaders" generally.

Purpose

  • Make the audience say "yes, that's me", at a finer grain than the homepage allowed.
  • Surface category-specific constraints (governance, donor trust, theological care, board cycles).
  • Use language this audience uses, interview transcripts, sales-call notes, not marketing voice.
  • Earn the right to introduce the product in their stakes.

Key questions

  1. Are the pain words from voice-of-customer interviews with this audience?
  2. Does the section name category-specific constraints the homepage would not?
  3. Is there one dominant pain or a buffet?
  4. Is the framing honest, not manufactured?
  5. Does it resolve into the value prop, or just leave the reader anxious?

Playbook approaches

A. PAS in the audience's voice

Three blocks. Problem, agitate, solve. The agitation comes from concrete category facts (a denominational review, a 990 cycle, a board's risk appetite), not abstract anxiety.

B. "You know this if…" inventory

A bulleted list of recognizable scenes from this audience's life. Each bullet is concrete enough that only this audience would write it. Loses force the moment a bullet could appear on the homepage.

e.g. "You know this if you've had to draft an AI line for the staff handbook before you'd worked out what you actually believe about it."

C. Before-state paragraph

A single rich paragraph painting this audience's current week, what their team is stuck on, what costs are quietly accruing, what board questions are coming. Followed by "there is another way."

Failure modes

  • Homepage problem reused. Generic SaaS pain ("things are inefficient") that does not belong on an audience page.
  • Strawman constraints. Inventing institutional pressures the audience does not actually feel.
  • Five problems. Pick the dominant one for this audience and own it.
  • Pain that the product does not solve. Naming a real audience pain just to look empathic, then pivoting to capabilities that address something else.
D3

Value proposition for this audience

The bridge from "yes, that's me" to "and here is what changes." Has to be measurably different from the homepage value prop, or the page is filler.

Purpose

  • State, in one paragraph, what changes for this audience.
  • Translate the product's unique attribute into outcomes this audience cares about.
  • Address the alternative this audience is most likely considering.
  • Earn the right to "how it works in your context."

Key questions

  1. Could the homepage make this same claim verbatim? If yes, the audience page is not yet doing its job.
  2. Is the outcome stated in audience-relevant terms (donor trust, board defensibility, staff formation), not generic ones?
  3. Is the unique attribute (Dunford) explicit, not implied?
  4. Is the obvious alternative for this audience named, even briefly?
  5. Is there same-audience proof in the next scroll?

Playbook approaches

A. Outcome paragraph in audience stakes

One paragraph naming the outcome in language only this audience uses. Anchored by one concrete number or screenshot.

B. "What changes / what stays the same"

Two-column. Left: what stays the same (your governance, your voice, your accountability). Right: what changes (clarity, speed, defensibility). Used by Stripe Climate, Patagonia for institutional buyers.

C. Three audience-stakes promises

Three short promises, each in this audience's stakes. Each backed by one specific feature or proof point. Avoid "faster, simpler, smarter."

Failure modes

  • Homepage value prop with audience word. The reader feels the swap and disengages.
  • Generic outcomes. "Save time, work smarter." Audience pages are where these are exposed as filler.
  • Three competing promises. Faster, cheaper, better, pick one for this audience.
  • No alternative named. The audience is comparing you to something specific; pretending otherwise is a tell.
D4

How it works in your context

Mechanism, but adapted. Same product, this audience's data, this audience's workflow, this audience's stakes.

Purpose

  • Show the product working with this audience's shape of data and decisions.
  • Reduce buyer risk by making the path concrete in their world.
  • Replace homepage screenshots with audience-realistic ones.
  • Set up audience-relevant features.

Key questions

  1. Are the screenshots / GIFs populated with realistic audience-shaped data?
  2. Are the user roles shown the ones this audience would have (deacon, dev director, dean), not "John Doe"?
  3. Is the flow described with verbs this audience uses?
  4. Could a colleague of the reader recognize the screen as "ours"?
  5. Are there three steps, not seven?

Playbook approaches

A. Three-step flow with audience verbs

Three numbered cards. Each: audience-language verb + one-line description + audience-realistic visual.

B. Narrated screenshot tour with audience scenarios

Long scroll of alternating screenshots and short captions, each anchored in a recognizable scene from this audience's week.

C. Embedded short demo per audience

A 60–90 second walkthrough showing the product running against this audience's shape of work. Same product, different demo data.

Failure modes

  • Same screenshots as homepage. The reader notices that the demo data is from a different category.
  • Generic placeholder names. "John Doe", "Acme Inc." Telegraphs that the audience-specific demo doesn't exist.
  • Marketing illustration in place of UI. Pretty isometric graphic instead of an actual screen, the reader stops believing the product fits.
  • Verbs this audience would not use. "Deploy", "ship", "sync" on a page for institutional leaders.
D5

Audience-relevant features

Audience pages get a curated subset of features, not the full grid. Cutting features here is a feature.

Purpose

  • Surface the features that matter most for this audience.
  • Reorder by audience priority, not internal product taxonomy.
  • Hide features that don't apply.
  • Anchor each feature to an outcome this audience cares about.

Key questions

  1. Are features prioritized for this audience rather than alphabetized?
  2. Does each carry an outcome named in this audience's stakes?
  3. Are features that don't apply removed (not just demoted)?
  4. Is there a "see all features" link out for the depth seekers?
  5. Could the page lose half the features and feel stronger?

Playbook approaches

A. Top-3 features for this audience

Three rows or cards. Each: feature name + audience-outcome line + screenshot. Link to the full-feature page for the curious.

B. Outcome-grouped feature list

Two or three outcome headings in this audience's language, each owning two or three feature bullets. Strong when audiences are mixed and you want each segment to find their version.

C. Feature subset + "see all" link

Show a deliberate subset (3–5) above the fold; link to the full feature catalog below. The Stripe and Vercel pattern.

Failure modes

  • Homepage feature grid copy-pasted. Defeats the entire purpose of the page.
  • Features with no outcome attached. The audience reads them as "and?"
  • Internal taxonomy. Grouping by team / module instead of by audience goal.
  • Features the audience cannot use. Showing API features to non-technical institutional buyers.
D6

Same-audience proof

The single most important difference between an audience page and a homepage. Every proof element here comes from this audience. No mixing.

Purpose

  • Show that organizations like the reader's already chose this.
  • Use logos, quotes, and numbers only from this audience.
  • Carry the trust into the case study and CTA.
  • Make peer-recognition do the heavy lifting.

Key questions

  1. Are all logos / names / quotes from this audience?
  2. Will the target reader recognize at least three of the names?
  3. Do quotes carry specifics, a number, a workflow, a before-and-after, not generic praise?
  4. Is there at least one named customer the reader could plausibly call as a reference?
  5. Does every quote sound the same? (If yes, edited too hard.)

Playbook approaches

A. Audience logo bar (5–8)

Logo bar of recognizable organizations from this audience only. The single highest-trust signal a short audience page can carry.

B. Three audience quotes

Three quotes side by side. Each: photo, name, role, organization, specific outcome. Loses force when one quote is generic.

C. Audience metric card

Two or three large numbers with audience-specific labels, "84% of partner congregations renewed", "3.4× faster board-policy turnaround." Use only when the numbers are real and audience-anchored.

Failure modes

  • Mixed-audience proof. A nonprofit page with two church quotes and one institutional one. The reader notices instantly.
  • Anonymous quotes. "Marketing director, anonymous", less than no proof.
  • Numbers from the wrong frame. "Processed $40M" on a page for organizations whose value is not transactional.
  • Unrecognizable names. A logo bar of organizations no one in this audience has heard of.
D7

Audience case study

One rich case from this audience showing the full journey. The proof a quote cannot carry alone.

Purpose

  • Make the value real with a named customer's story.
  • Show before-and-after legibly, not just praise.
  • Anchor at least one specific metric.
  • Give the reader something to forward to a colleague.

Key questions

  1. Is the customer named, with consent?
  2. Is there a specific metric or outcome, not generic praise?
  3. Is the before-and-after legible?
  4. Does the case look like the reader's organization, same scale, same constraints?
  5. Does the case link to a deeper story the reader can spend ten minutes with?

Playbook approaches

A. Long case story embedded

A 400–600 word case story embedded directly. Customer quote, before-state, what they did, outcome. The deepest single proof element on the page.

B. Card linking to full case

A summary card, customer logo, headline metric, one-line context, link to the full case study. Used when the page is short.

C. Video case study

30–90 second customer video. Highest-trust signal a page can carry. Demands a customer relationship with consent and time.

Failure modes

  • Anonymous case. "A leading nonprofit", the reader stops believing.
  • No metric. Praise without measurement; reads as testimonial inflation.
  • Wrong-scale case. A case from a 200-person organization on a page for 5-person organizations.
  • One case, repeated everywhere. The same customer's story on every audience page reads as "this is the only customer we have."
D8

Pricing or plan for this audience

Audience pages are where pricing-sensitivity differences show up. Different audiences have different procurement realities; pretending otherwise costs deals.

Purpose

  • Surface the relevant tier or plan for this audience.
  • Acknowledge audience-specific procurement realities (annual cycles, board approvals, nonprofit pricing).
  • Reduce "is this for me?" anxiety before the CTA.
  • Match the right buying motion (self-serve / book a call / RFP).

Key questions

  1. Is the relevant tier for this audience the visible one?
  2. Is there a nonprofit / institutional discount if applicable, named explicitly?
  3. Does the section acknowledge annual cycles, board approval, RFPs if they exist?
  4. Is the CTA tier-matched (self-serve trial vs. book a call vs. RFP intake)?
  5. Is "Contact us" the only option? If so, is that on purpose for this audience?

Playbook approaches

A. Audience-tier card

Show the relevant tier prominently, "Nonprofit plan", "Institutional plan", with audience-tuned features. Link to full pricing for context.

B. "Starting at" with audience note

A single line, "Starts at $X/mo. 30% nonprofit discount available." Lighter footprint; works when the page has bigger jobs.

C. Sales-led for institutional buyers

No public price. Surface qualifying signals ("designed for organizations with X+ staff") to keep wrong fits from booking calls. Standard for enterprise / institutional.

Failure modes

  • Hidden price for self-serve audiences. Forces every prospect through a gate; kills product-led growth.
  • No nonprofit discount mentioned on a nonprofit page when one exists. The audience finds out later and feels they were tested.
  • Self-serve CTA on an institutional page. Mismatched motion; the buyer's procurement office is confused.
  • "Custom pricing" with no qualifier. Either tell them roughly what to expect, or tell them who you do not sell to.

Audience-specific FAQ

The FAQ on an audience page does not echo the homepage. It addresses the questions this audience asks in sales calls, in support tickets, in their head while scrolling.

Purpose

  • Answer the objections this audience actually voices.
  • Address audience-specific governance, data, procurement, and theology questions.
  • Reclaim search-traffic intent ("does X work for nonprofits?").
  • Reduce the time sales spends repeating the same answers.

Key questions

  1. Are these real questions from this audience's sales calls, or marketing-friendly versions?
  2. Are audience-specific concerns addressed (e.g., 990 reporting, board defensibility, polity)?
  3. Are answers specific, not evasive?
  4. Is there a "is this for me?" question that names who the page is and isn't for?
  5. Five strong questions or fifteen weak ones?

Playbook approaches

A. Five audience-specific questions

Five questions in accordion form, each tightly scoped to this audience. The default.

B. Sub-FAQ for procurement / IT

A small follow-on block addressing security, data, governance, integration. Used when this audience runs a technical review (institutions, larger nonprofits).

C. Inline objection-handlers

Skip the FAQ section; address objections inline at the moment they arise, pricing concern next to pricing, data concern next to mechanism. The Stripe pattern.

Failure modes

  • Homepage FAQ reused. Reader sees the question they were going to ask is missing.
  • Marketing-friendly questions. "Why is your nonprofit plan so great?" Reader stops trusting the page.
  • Evasive answers. "We take governance seriously" instead of "Here is the policy template, vetted by counsel."
  • Audience-specific concern unaddressed. A nonprofit page that ignores the 990, an institutional page that ignores RFPs.
D10

Audience-appropriate CTA

The closing band. End with the next step that matches this audience's buying motion, not the homepage's default.

Purpose

  • Match the CTA to the audience's real buying motion.
  • Restate the audience promise, not the homepage promise.
  • Make the next step trivial; one click, no hunting.
  • Carry trust from the proof and case directly into action.

Key questions

  1. Does the CTA match this audience's buying motion (self-serve / sales call / RFP)?
  2. Is the verb specific ("Book a 30-minute conversation with our team", not "Get started")?
  3. Does the section restate the audience promise in one sentence above the button?
  4. Is there a secondary low-commit option (read the case, watch the demo)?
  5. Is the CTA the same primary action used elsewhere on this page, or has a new one snuck in?

Playbook approaches

A. Book a call (sales-led)

Inline calendar or single primary "Book a 30-minute conversation" CTA. The default for institutional and larger-nonprofit audiences with a real sales motion.

B. Free tier / trial (self-serve)

"Start free" or "Try it free" CTA. Used when the audience can buy without sales involvement and the product is low-risk to try.

C. Talk to a peer (community-led)

"Talk to another [audience] using this", inline introduction to a customer in the same audience. Highest trust; demands customer willingness.

D. Quiet posture close

A short statement of stance, with one quiet CTA. Used when the audience is mid-funnel and the page is doing qualification work, not conversion.

Failure modes

  • Same CTA as homepage when motion differs. "Start free" on a page where the real motion is a 6-month procurement.
  • Footer-only CTA. The reader who just decided has nothing to click.
  • Weak verb. "Learn more." End the page; do not punt the decision again.
  • Three CTAs of equal weight. Pick the one you actually want.
