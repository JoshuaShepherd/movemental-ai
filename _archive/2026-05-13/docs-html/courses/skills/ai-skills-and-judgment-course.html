Stage 03 · Course

Skills: judgment under load

Five modules that form the judgment staff need to use AI responsibly in real work. Not vendor training. Not prompt libraries. Formation — the kind of learning that leaves a person able to make good calls the rubric did not anticipate.

Format Cohort-based course, five modules, facilitated exercises
Duration Multi-week cohort (~3 hrs/week in compact form); Movemental’s deeper Skills engagement is often sixteen weeks after Sandbox Season
Audience Staff across the organization whose work will be meaningfully changed by assistance
Prerequisite A Safety frame that has landed, and a Sandbox season that produced real evidence

Why this is the real course

Skills is formation toward judgment, not training on buttons.

Of the four stages, Skills is the only one that earns the word "course" in its traditional sense — modules, exercises, measurable outcomes. The other three produce artifacts, decisions, and deployed workflows. Skills produces people.

The distinction matters because the temptation, throughout any AI rollout, is to collapse judgment into tooling. Vendors prefer this collapse. It sells certifications. It justifies the workshop line item. It offers the comforting illusion that the hard work of AI adoption is technical. It is not. The hard work is forming staff who can tell the difference between a plausible draft and a good one — and who will say so out loud in a meeting where velocity is the loudest voice in the room.

This course forms five specific capacities, in order, over five modules. None of them is a trick. All of them become habit with deliberate practice on real work. For the distinction between formation and vendor training, read Skills as Formation, Not Training on movemental.com.

A prompt without judgment writes faster nonsense. A policy without formed staff enforces nothing. Skills is the stage that turns both a prompt and a policy into practice that holds under pressure.

The five modules

A formation arc, in order.

The modules build on each other. Discernment is the foundation — staff who cannot tell plausible from good cannot benefit from any later module. Authorship, Verification, Prompting, and Workflow Thinking each rest on it.

Module 01 · Foundation

Discernment

How to tell plausible from good — and how to recognize drift before it becomes habit.

Explanation

Assistance is good at producing plausible output. Plausible output reads well. It sounds competent. It uses complete sentences, coherent structure, and appropriate vocabulary. It is almost never, in the first draft, good in the sense that matters for mission-driven work — which is faithful to the specific, accurate in substance, aware of context, and capable of bearing the weight of the audience it is aimed at.

Discernment is the trained ability to notice the difference quickly enough to intervene before the plausible version becomes the shipped version. It is built in two movements. First, staff learn to recognize the signatures of plausible-but-not-good output — generic warmth where specificity is required, smooth structure where tension belonged, confident numbers that are not in the source document, summaries that flatten disagreement into consensus. Second, staff learn to recognize drift — the slow pattern by which their own writing, over months of heavy assistance, begins to take on the shape of the model's voice rather than the organization's.

Drift is the harder failure to catch. Plausible output is identifiable in a given draft. Drift is identifiable only across many drafts, when staff look back at six months of work and notice that their cadence, their openings, and their transitions now all sound similar — like the output of a system rather than the voice of a person. This module teaches staff to name drift early and to build the practices that keep their voice attached to their body.

Example

A development officer runs a draft donor update through an assistant. The first paragraph opens, "We are thrilled to share our recent progress." It is plausible. It is not how the officer has opened a donor update in a decade — she opens with a specific moment, a name, a scene. The plausible opener is friendly, correct, and empty. A staff member with trained discernment notices the opener as smoothed, not bad, and replaces it with a specific scene in three minutes. A staff member without trained discernment ships the plausible version and, over two years, teaches the organization's donors that its communications now sound like every other nonprofit's.

What good looks like

Staff can read an assisted draft and, within minutes, name — out loud, in a team review — the places where it is plausible instead of good. They know the difference between smoothing a rough sentence (helpful) and smoothing away the specific thing that made the sentence useful (drift). They trust their own ear enough to correct in the direction of specificity, and they trust their colleagues enough to accept correction when their own ear missed something.

What goes wrong

Staff begin to accept plausible output as good because accepting it is faster than fixing it. The organization's voice compresses toward the model's voice within a quarter. Nobody notices, because no single draft feels that different from the last one — the difference is cumulative. Six months later, donors, volunteers, or board members remark that the organization's communications feel "less like you." By then, drift is policy.

Module 02 · Authorship

Authorship

Staying in control of the work — how to use assistance without outsourcing the voice the mission depends on.

Explanation

Authorship is the refusal to let the model become the final voice on anything that must sound like this organization's soul. It is not anti-assistance. Assistance is welcomed. Authorship is the discipline that distinguishes the parts of the work the human must still carry from the parts the human may delegate, and refuses the temptation to collapse the first kind into the second because the second is cheaper.

Authorship has two failure modes, and this module is built around refusing both. The first is over-reliance — the staff member who increasingly lets the model write the opening, the close, the connective tissue, and the hard paragraph, until the staff member is a curator of output rather than an author. The second is ghost authorship — the staff member who uses the model heavily but represents the work as solo, to themselves and to their colleagues, because admitting the assistance feels like admitting inadequacy. Over-reliance erodes the organization's voice. Ghost authorship erodes the staff member's own sense of themselves as a worker.

Authorship is the practice of using assistance visibly, deliberately, and with a clear hand on the steering. It is not a policy question. It is a character question that shows up in daily work.

Example

A communications director drafts a Sunday email to the organization's volunteer network. She uses the model for three tasks: to stress-test the logical flow of her argument, to generate three possible openings she can react to, and to suggest cuts in a paragraph she knows is too long. She writes the actual opening herself, after reading the model's suggestions. She rewrites the paragraph cuts in her own cadence, using the model's flag as input, not output. She ships the email in her voice, with assistance visible in her drafting process but not in her published sentences. That is authorship — assistance in service of her writing, not assistance as a substitute for it.

What good looks like

Staff can describe, in plain sentences, which parts of a given piece of work they delegated to assistance, which parts they kept, and why. They are comfortable saying "the model drafted this paragraph — I kept it because it was better than mine on this particular sentence-level task" and equally comfortable saying "I wrote this paragraph myself because I did not trust the model to hold the tension." They are not embarrassed by either statement.

What goes wrong

Staff stop being able to describe which parts of the work are theirs. The answer to "did you write this?" becomes a shrug. The organization's communications start to feel like someone else's voice wearing the organization's signature. Morale quietly erodes, because staff who cannot feel authorship in their own work stop feeling like their work matters. This is a formation problem, not a policy one — no AUP will fix it.

Module 03 · Verification

Verification

Checking facts, tone, and integrity — the specific habits that prevent plausible output from becoming a trust incident.

Explanation

Verification is the practice of treating every assisted output as unverified until it is verified. This is not paranoia. It is calibration. Assistance produces output in three categories: things the model knew, things the model inferred, and things the model fabricated. These three categories look identical on the page. They are not identical in consequence.

Verification has three specific movements — facts, tone, and integrity — and this module teaches each as a distinct habit.

  • Facts. Every number, name, date, attribution, and quoted phrase is checked against the source before it leaves the draft. There are no exceptions for small claims. Small claims are the ones that reach donors, partners, and regulators without anyone remembering to check them.
  • Tone. Every draft is read by the author aloud — or by a colleague in a voice review — to confirm that the cadence, the specificity, and the warmth sound like the organization, not like a generic approximation of it. This is the fastest way to catch drift at the sentence level.
  • Integrity. Every claim the organization will be responsible for is checked against what the organization actually did. A summary that slightly overstates a program outcome does not become true because the model produced it. Integrity verification is the habit of catching that overstatement before it becomes a reported fact.

Example

A grants officer uses assistance to reformat a prior-year grant report into a new funder's required structure. The model reproduces most of the content correctly. In one paragraph, it subtly shifts "served approximately 140 families" to "served more than 150 families." The officer catches the shift in a line-by-line verification pass against the source document. The shift was not malicious — the model smoothed the number toward a rounder figure. In a world without verification, that smoothed number becomes a reported outcome, which becomes the baseline for next year's report, which becomes a claim the organization cannot substantiate. Verification is the practice that prevents a single plausible sentence from becoming a compounding misrepresentation.

What good looks like

Staff run verification as a default pass, not as an exception. Fact checks are traceable to a source. Tone checks are logged as reads-aloud or colleague reviews. Integrity checks are explicit, especially for anything that leaves the organization with its name attached. Staff who catch errors in each other's assisted drafts are thanked, not resented — because a caught error is a functioning safety system.

What goes wrong

Staff stop verifying because verification is slow and the output looks right. A fabricated citation reaches a funder. A smoothed outcome reaches a board report. A confident but wrong quote reaches a social post. The incident is handled, quietly or loudly, but the underlying habit — trust without verification — persists, and the next incident is already being drafted somewhere in the organization. Verification is the discipline that keeps trust from being burned one small plausible sentence at a time.

Module 04 · Structured thinking

Prompting — as Structured Thinking, Not Tricks

Prompting is not a library of clever phrases. It is the discipline of saying clearly what you want, why you want it, and what good would look like.

Explanation

This module refuses the frame that prompting is a set of tricks. It is not. Prompting is structured thinking on paper. The "prompt engineer" mystique is a marketing artifact; the underlying skill is old — it is the ability to specify a task clearly enough that a competent executor could act on it without guessing. Staff who write good emails, good briefs, and good research questions already have the foundation. This module names that foundation and sharpens it for a new interlocutor.

A good prompt is built from five moves, and the module walks each one.

  1. Name the task. Not "help me with this email" — "draft a three-paragraph donor update that opens with a specific scene from our summer program, acknowledges a funding gap in the middle, and closes with a concrete invitation to an upcoming event."
  2. Name the audience. Who will read this, what do they already know, what do they need to believe or do after reading it.
  3. Name the voice. Whose voice is this in, and what does that voice sound like. (Often the best way to do this is to paste two prior paragraphs written in that voice.)
  4. Name the boundaries. What must not be claimed without verification, what must not be said, what tone is off-limits.
  5. Name what good looks like. What would make you say "this is ready to ship" — and what would make you say "this is close, but the third paragraph is smooth where it should be specific."

Those five moves replace the entire prompt-library industry. A staff member who can write them becomes a better thinker about their own work, regardless of whether assistance is involved — which is the most durable benefit of this module.

Example

Weak prompt: "Write a thank-you email to a major donor."

Structured prompt: "Draft a three-paragraph thank-you email to a major donor who gave $25,000 toward our housing initiative in Q1. Audience: a long-time supporter who has asked to hear program specifics, not just gratitude. Voice: warm, specific, not performative — here are two paragraphs of my prior correspondence with this donor so you can match the register [paste]. Boundaries: do not claim program outcomes that are not in the attached Q1 report; do not use the phrase 'transforming lives.' What good looks like: I want to be able to send this after one pass of editing, not three — the donor should recognize the specificity of the reference to her earlier questions about outcome measurement."

The second prompt produces a draft an order of magnitude closer to ready. It also, incidentally, forces the staff member to articulate what they want before they ask for it — which is the real skill.

What good looks like

Staff write prompts that are indistinguishable from well-written internal briefs — because that is what they are. Prompting becomes a thinking aid, not a magic incantation. Colleagues can read each other's prompts and give feedback on the thinking without touching the tool. The organization's best prompts are shared and refined as a form of writing practice.

What goes wrong

Prompting gets treated as a specialist skill. A few staff members become the "prompt people," and everyone else throws vague requests at the model and is disappointed with the results. The organization pursues prompt libraries instead of prompt literacy, which means staff can paste a recipe but cannot cook. The fix is not more recipes. The fix is teaching the five moves until they become how everyone already thinks.

Module 05 · Workflow

Workflow Thinking

Putting assistance inside the actual shape of real work — rather than as an isolated act of using a chatbot.

Explanation

The final module of Skills is the one that makes the previous four operational. It teaches staff to locate assistance inside the actual shape of their work — the inputs they receive, the outputs they produce, the people they hand work off to, the quality bars those people rely on. Without workflow thinking, assistance remains a private act of "using a chatbot." With workflow thinking, assistance becomes a predictable move inside a workflow other people can also operate.

The distinction looks small and is not. A staff member who uses assistance privately produces output whose quality the rest of the team cannot calibrate against. A staff member who uses assistance inside a workflow — with defined inputs, defined outputs, a defined human-in-the-loop, a defined quality gate — produces output the team can evaluate, reproduce, and, later in Stage Four, promote into a governed solution. Workflow thinking is the bridge between "this one staff member got a good result" and "this is now how our organization does this kind of work."

The module teaches four workflow moves: identifying the real inputs (what information the model needs, in what form), identifying the real outputs (what shape the final artifact takes, who it goes to), identifying the human-in-the-loop points (where verification, authorship, or discernment must intervene), and identifying the quality gate (what would cause this output to be sent back, caught, or paused). These four moves are the exact four moves Solutions, in Stage Four, will codify. The course prepares staff to think in those moves well before deployment is on the table.

Example

A volunteer coordinator who has run an assisted follow-up sequence three weeks in a row stops treating it as "me using the tool" and starts describing it as a workflow: Input — roster plus per-attendee notes. Output — first-draft messages in a shared folder. Human-in-the-loop — coordinator reviews every message before send, flags any attendee who needs a hand-written reply. Quality gate — no message ships if a verification check against the attendee's interest tags fails. Written down this way, the workflow becomes transferable — another staff member could operate it, the organization could track its outcomes, Solutions could eventually promote it. Before workflow thinking, the same practice was invisible to everyone except the coordinator.

What good looks like

Staff describe their use of assistance in workflow language by default. A team can read a one-paragraph workflow description and understand what is happening, where humans stay in the loop, and what would cause the workflow to fail. Assistance stops being a private act and becomes organizational knowledge, which is exactly what Stage Four needs to build on.

What goes wrong

Assistance stays as private practice. A few "hero operators" become indispensable because only they know how a given task gets done with assistance, and their departure becomes a single point of failure for a whole workflow. The organization learns that it has outsourced durable knowledge to the memory of individual staff — a failure mode that Solutions inherits directly if workflow thinking did not happen first.

Sample exercises

Practice in the real work, not in a sandbox of the sandbox.

Every module in the Skills course is built around exercises that use the staff member's own current work as the training material. There is no simulation. There is no stock example. The work is the work.

Exercise 01 · Discernment

Voice drift diagnostic

Bring three pieces of your own writing from the past twelve months — one from before you began using assistance, one from early adoption, one from last week. Read them aloud in sequence. Name, in writing, two specific places where you hear your voice more cleanly in the earlier pieces than in the later one. Share the diagnostic with a colleague who knows your writing, and ask whether they agree. This is the fastest way to make drift visible to yourself.

Exercise 02 · Authorship

Rewrite a donor email using AI, then evaluate where it loses voice

Take a donor email you have already drafted. Ask an assistant to rewrite it. Place the two versions side by side. Mark, in the assisted version, every sentence where the voice has smoothed, generalized, or drifted. Rewrite the assisted version in your own voice, preserving any structural improvement but restoring the specificity you lost. Report to your cohort: which parts of the assisted version were worth keeping, which were worth rejecting, and which were worth transforming. The exercise builds the muscle that Module 1 and Module 2 name together.

Exercise 03 · Verification

Line-by-line verification pass

Take a recent assisted draft that has already been shipped. Do a full verification pass on it — every number, every name, every attribution, every quoted phrase. Log what you find. If you find nothing, log that too. The point is not to catch yourself. The point is to make the verification pass a repeatable, calibrated habit rather than an occasional check.

Exercise 04 · Prompting

Rewrite a vague prompt using the five moves

Bring a prompt you recently used that produced a disappointing result. Rewrite it using the five moves — task, audience, voice, boundaries, what good looks like. Run both prompts against the same tool and compare outputs. Bring the comparison to your cohort. You will find, nearly every time, that the weak prompt reflects a weak brief, and that strengthening the prompt strengthens your own thinking about the work. This is the core lesson of Module 4.

Exercise 05 · Workflow

Document your most-used assisted task as a workflow

Pick a task you have used assistance for at least three times. Write it as a workflow: inputs, outputs, human-in-the-loop points, quality gates. Share it with a colleague and have them try to operate the workflow using only your written description. Revise based on what they could not reproduce. This is the exercise that most directly prepares your work to be promoted into a Stage Four deployment.

Outcome

Staff who can use AI responsibly — and refuse it responsibly.

At the end of the Skills course, the organization has staff who can do three things a policy cannot teach and a tool cannot substitute.

  • Recognize bad output. They can spot plausible-but-not-good drafts in minutes, not hours, and they can name specifically what is wrong. Drift, flattening, confident inaccuracy, smoothed conflict — all are in their vocabulary.
  • Correct it. They can restore specificity without rejecting assistance. They can keep what was structurally useful, reject what was tonally off, and verify what was asserted confidently. They are neither anti-AI purists nor credulous users.
  • Use AI responsibly. They can operate assistance inside a workflow other people can inherit, with verification as a default, authorship as a visible choice, and boundaries as a living practice rather than an archived document.

The whole point of Skills is to prepare humans for situations the policy cannot foresee.

This is the input Solutions is built on. Deployed workflows in Stage Four are only as safe as the judgment of the people operating them. Skills is what makes that judgment real — not in a certificate, but in how staff talk about their own work the week after the course ends.
