What's actually happening

Institutions face a different shape of the AI problem.

Your faculty are not a single audience. Some hold the position that AI is fundamentally incompatible with formative theological education. Others hold that teaching students to use AI well is part of preparing them for ministry. Most are between. None of these positions can be commanded into compliance through administrative policy. They have to be worked through governance the faculty senate will ratify.

Your students are not a single audience either. They are taking different courses with different professors who have different unstated AI policies. Their anxiety is not primarily about whether AI use is permitted; it is about whether they will violate a policy they don't know exists. The student senate is increasingly asking for syllabus-level disclosure standards and a published institutional policy that survives changes in faculty composition.

Your administration faces an operational AI problem that overlaps with FERPA, accreditation reporting, and institutional risk. Admissions teams are using AI to draft personalized outreach. Advancement teams are using AI in donor communications. Registrars are facing requests from faculty to use AI in grading. Your COO is responsible for a regulatory posture nobody has yet documented. Outside legal review is unavoidable for any institution serious about the work.

And your board, your denomination, and your accreditor are all watching. Your board treats AI governance as fiduciary expansion. Your denomination wants institutional policy aligned with its theological commitments. Your accreditor — ATS, the Higher Learning Commission, or your regional body — has begun asking about AI in standards review. The institutional posture you produce has to satisfy all three audiences and your faculty senate and your student senate.

If this matches what your institution is navigating, the rest of this page is for you.

The situation they were actually in

AI did not knock as a memo from the provost; it arrived through students. Some used AI to draft papers; others for outlining or research summaries. Faculty awareness splintered: some professors banned AI outright, others ignored it, still others experimented privately with the same tools their students were told not to touch. From the registrar’s view, the term proceeded normally; inside seminar rooms, a quieter crisis gathered: What does formation look like in a world where AI can produce words faster than students can think?

Stage 01 — Safety

Westbridge refused whack-a-mole enforcement. It sought institutional belief first — beginning with a deceptively simple question — Is AI allowed? — and quickly recognizing that simplicity was the wrong frame.

A working group convened: the academic dean, several faculty members, one student representative, and IT leadership. They pressed harder questions: What counts as authorship? Where is assistance legitimate? Where does AI short-circuit formation? What must never be delegated?

The goal of theological education is not just correct answers — it is formed thinkers.

From that premise they articulated a paired rule: AI can assist research and structure; AI cannot replace the student’s own thinking, argument, or voice.

Within a semester, the fog on syllabus day lifted: published AI guidelines for students and faculty; cross-course consistency; faculty language to justify classroom choices; student clarity about boundaries. The aim was never surveillance-first enforcement — it was clarity.

Expanded institutional Safety commonly sequences ratification through faculty senate, student senate, senior administration, and the board of trustees, with outside counsel reviewing the Data Handling Protocol before final adoption.

Seven ratifiable artifacts (Stage 01)

At the end of the two-week Safety engagement, the working team received the full set of named documents for elder review:

  • Acceptable Use Statement
  • Care Boundaries
  • Disclosure Standards
  • Vendor and Tool Inventory
  • Data Handling Protocol
  • Incident Response Plan
  • Named Refusals

Stage 02 — Sandbox

Rather than wholesale ban or wholesale embrace, Westbridge staged controlled inquiry: using AI to summarize dense theological texts; generating outlines prior to drafting; comparing AI-generated arguments to student-written arguments — with documentation and discussion as part of the learning, not an afterthought.

They surfaced patterned truths: AI could help with scaffolding and clarity; it stumbled on theological nuance; it could mint convincing but shallow arguments. That empirical humility produced cultural capital — a shared understanding of what AI could and could not do in their context.

Stage 03 — Skills

Training transcended plagiarism anxiety. Faculty folded AI into teaching conversations: When is it helpful? When does it shortcut formation? What does it mean to own an argument? Students practiced triangulation — how to use AI responsibly, how to critique outputs, how to recognize when convenience replaces cognition.

Students grew more aware, not merely more restricted: they could articulate when AI helped learning, when it harmed it, and how to choose deliberately. Faculty posture shifted in parallel — from policing AI to guiding its use.

Stage 04 — Solutions

Only after clarity and formation-work did Westbridge wire tools into systems: a research assistant oriented to approved academic sources; writing support emphasizing structure rather than ghostwritten content; a faculty hub collating AI-informed pedagogy.

They explicitly avoided automated paper-writing tools and anything that obscured authorship. For the most formation-critical work, they avoided a tool-first answer altogether — opting instead for a faculty guide on how to teach with AI — not merely how to detect it.

They integrated emerging tools without losing the purpose of theological education.

The institution did not magically become “more efficient.” It became more intentional: students engaged more critically; faculty shared expectations; AI entered syllabi as a named conversation partner rather than a hidden shortcut.

What this produced — multi-year institutional arc

Westbridge did not “solve” AI — no honest school has. It did something more durable: integrated emerging tools without losing the purpose of theological education.

The obstacle was never merely GPT; it was what GPT exposed — fragile assumptions about learning, authorship, and the relationship between effort and formation. The remedy was neither blanket prohibition nor naive optimism; it was sequence: clarify purpose, explore carefully, train for judgment, then integrate tools.

Full institutional arcs that include Sandbox, network Skills, and Solutions frequently reach roughly $600K–$800K over several budget years when scoped across faculty, students, and operations together.

What this pattern reveals

A few things worth noticing about institutional engagements.

The three-track structure is what makes institutional Safety ratifiable.

Single-team Safety engagements work for churches and nonprofits because the leadership team can speak for the organization. Institutions cannot. The faculty senate will not ratify artifacts the faculty did not draft. The student senate has its own consent expectations. The administrative council has operational realities the other two tracks may not see. Cross-track integration sessions resolve disagreements by identifying which constituency has the strongest claim on each provision, rather than by averaging positions.

Outside legal review is part of the work, not an optional add-on.

Every institutional engagement we have run has required outside legal review of the Data Handling Protocol, typically running $5,000 in addition to the Movemental engagement. FERPA, institutional Data Processing Agreements, and accreditation reporting requirements cannot be addressed without counsel. We name this in the engagement scope from the beginning so institutions can budget for it accurately.

The Named Refusals document tends to become the institution's most-cited artifact.

Three peer-reviewed articles in theological education have cited Named Refusals documents from Movemental institutional engagements. Two ATS member institutions have adopted modified versions of a peer institution's Care Boundaries document with permission. Network effects compound across the institutional landscape in ways the individual church or nonprofit Named Refusals do not.
