State of the union · Mission sector & AI

Whether they welcome it or not, AI is already inside nonprofit, church, and seminary life.

This paper synthesizes public research through early 2026: where adoption actually sits, what organizations tried first, where pilots stall, and what leaders can know without pretending the landscape is settled.

Abstract

Generative AI reached population-scale adoption faster than the personal computer or the internet in several national samples. Inside organizations, the picture is messier: broad use of chat-style tools, thin governance, and—across many enterprises—a steep drop from pilot to measurable outcomes. Mission-driven organizations mirror that tension: staff and students already use consumer tools; boards ask for strategy; policies often arrive late; and “successful” institutional AI is still rare in sector surveys. Theological education has responded with consortial programming and experiments in curriculum, while public opinion remains wary—especially on AI in spiritual or pastoral roles. The honest summary for April 2026 is pervasive informal use, uneven institutional maturity, and high stakes for trust.

How to read the numbers Corporate and nonprofit surveys use different populations and wordings. Treat percentages as directional unless you pull the primary PDF. Conflating distinct studies (for example, a “95%” pilot finding vs. a controlled productivity experiment) creates false precision.

1. The intersection — like it or not

AI intersects mission organizations in three layers, regardless of official policy.

Individual layer. Staff, volunteers, and students use ChatGPT-class tools for drafting, research, coding, and administration—often on personal accounts or unsanctioned logins. Industry reporting describes a “shadow” economy of consumer tools that outruns formal procurement. That pattern appears in mission contexts even when leadership has not approved a platform.

Program layer. Grant writing, fundraising copy, sermon preparation, liturgical language, communications, and curriculum development are all text-heavy. These are exactly the workflows large language models augment—sometimes brilliantly, sometimes dangerously—when tasks sit “inside” current model strengths.

Governance layer. Data handling, child safety, donor privacy, academic integrity, denominational polity, and accreditation expectations all collide with fast-moving tools. Sector surveys show AI-specific policies among the least implemented data practices in nonprofits—even while “adopting AI tools” has jumped into top operational priorities (NTEN, 2025).

2. Macro landscape — speed vs. depth

Stanford HAI AI Index (2026). The annual AI Index reports very wide organizational uptake of AI (often cited on the order of a large majority of surveyed firms using AI in at least one function), rapid spread of generative AI in business, and—at the same time—early-stage deployment of more autonomous “agentic” systems across functions. The headline: adoption breadth has run ahead of workflow depth.

The GenAI Divide (MIT-associated analysis, 2025). Secondary reporting on “The GenAI Divide: State of AI in Business 2025” describes roughly 95% of corporate generative AI pilots failing to deliver meaningful financial returns, with a small minority capturing outsized value—hence a “divide” between experimentation and P&L impact. Authors emphasize integration and learning fit over raw model quality. Mission orgs should not copy enterprise ROI math, but the pilot-to-nothing pattern is recognizable: budget burned, no durable workflow change.

McKinsey workplace surveys (e.g., 2025). Leaders report heavy planned AI spending alongside limited self-described maturity: very few organizations classify themselves as having AI fully embedded in workflows with strong outcomes; many report modest revenue or cost effects to date while remaining optimistic about future value. That mismatch explains board impatience and staff fatigue.

Jagged frontier (HBS / MIT / BCG field experiment, 2023). On realistic consulting tasks judged inside GPT-4’s capability frontier, AI users delivered substantial gains in throughput and rated quality; on a deliberately “outside frontier” task, AI users were less likely to be correct than those without AI. The lesson for churches and schools is not “ban AI”—it is that similar-looking tasks can have opposite safety profiles. Drafting a newsletter and auditing a spreadsheet are not equivalent risks.

3. Nonprofits — exploration without scaffolding

The TechSoup / Tapp State of AI in Nonprofits: 2025 (1,300+ respondents) sketches a sector in motion: strong interest in AI for grant writing, marketing, and operations; adoption skewing toward larger budgets; many organizations still without a formal AI strategy; heavy reliance on one or two people for technology decisions. Benchmark language in public summaries places “successful” integrated adoption in the low single digits—parallel to the enterprise GenAI divide.

NTEN’s 2025 Data Empowerment Report (~220 organizations) reinforces governance debt: policies for personal data and sharing are more common than policies governing AI use of organizational data. Yet AI adoption has risen to one of the top planned priorities alongside training and impact evaluation—i.e., organizations want capacity before they have always built safe rails.

Early responses and ends. The dominant early moves are efficiency plays (content, proposals, admin) and cautious pilots in donor analytics—ends that match stretched staffing. Rarer but growing: explicit ethics frameworks (e.g., NTEN’s equitable-AI resources), staff training, and partnership with vendors who accept nonprofit constraints.

4. Churches — cultural formation meets technical opacity

Few nationwide denominational datasets publish comparable adoption metrics to TechSoup’s benchmark; evidence is more indirect. Clergy and lay leaders use the same consumer assistants as other knowledge workers; youth and young-adult ministries contend with congregants’ daily AI use; counseling-adjacent applications raise ethics questions beyond efficiency.

Pew Research Center (2025) finds that 73% of U.S. adults say AI should play no role in advising people about faith in God—a striking boundary for religious leaders weighing AI-in-worship, AI-in-discipleship, or chatbot “pastoral” experiments. The same body of surveys notes widespread concern that AI could weaken human skills and relationships, even as many people remain open to AI for analytical or logistical tasks. Translation: parishioners may accept AI behind the scenes for operations while resisting AI in roles they regard as properly human and spiritual.

Early responses and ends. Denominations and networks have mixed experimental policies—everything from blanket caution to curated toolkits. Common ends: protecting theological authenticity, avoiding plagiarism in preaching and education, and preserving pastoral confidentiality when digital tools touch counseling notes or prayer requests. Few churches have solved the harder problem: formation—how leaders and members learn judgment alongside tool use.

5. Seminaries & institutions — accreditation, pedagogy, partnerships

Accredited theological education moves slower than the tech cycle—but not slowly enough to avoid disruption. The Association of Theological Schools (ATS), Atla, and the In Trust Center have convened an ongoing series, F[AI]thfully Co-Creating: Navigating Artificial Intelligence and Theological Education, including webinars, strategic-planning conversations, and stackable microcredentials focused on AI policy, pedagogy, and design. Schools publish case-style reflections on responsible experimentation—e.g., embedding AI literacy with ethical and theological framing (see ATS gallery pieces such as “Leading experiments in AI for theological education and the church”).

Early responses and ends. Ends here differ from the nonprofit sector’s first-wave efficiency story: academic integrity, formation of ministers who will serve AI-shaped congregations, preservation of human mentorship, and alignment with accreditation and library standards. Practical responses include syllabus language, citation norms for AI-assisted work, faculty development, and selective tool adoption in research and administration.

6. Early responses — a typology

Across sectors, most early moves cluster into five patterns:

  • Ignore / defer. Policy silence while tools spread informally—high risk for misconduct and inequality (power users vs. everyone else).
  • Efficiency push. Grant drafts, social posts, donor emails—fast wins, often without verification workflows.
  • Pilot theater. High-visibility experiments with unclear ownership; aligns with enterprise patterns where most pilots never scale (GenAI Divide reporting).
  • Governance-first. Data policies, acceptable-use statements, human-in-the-loop rules—slower, closer to NTEN’s warnings about missing AI-specific guardrails.
  • Formation-first. Training leaders to judge when AI helps, when it lies confidently, and when human presence is non-negotiable—aligned with the “jagged frontier” evidence and with pastoral boundaries flagged by Pew.

Reliability research. OpenAI’s 2025 paper Why Language Models Hallucinate argues that standard benchmarks reward guessing over calibrated uncertainty—so overconfidence is partly systemic. That matters whenever a mission organization treats fluent prose as authoritative truth.

7. State of the union — what we can say in April 2026

First: Informal adoption is already universal at the people layer; institutional maturity is not.

Second: Nonprofit benchmarks and enterprise studies agree on a strategic gap—lots of exploration, thin written strategy, uneven ROI—while Stanford-scale data show AI becoming omnipresent in the wider economy. Mission organizations feel squeezed between “we’re behind” narratives and “we see no benefit” local reality.

Third: Public attitude data (Pew) warn against placing AI in spiritually authoritative roles; theological schools and denominations are still negotiating pedagogy and policy; NTEN-style data show governance lagging usage.

Fourth: The constructive path is not merely more licenses—it is sequence: clarity on data and risk, bounded experiments, skill-building tied to real workflows, then scaled solutions—what Movemental calls Safety → Sandbox → Skills → Solutions. That ordering matches the failure modes the research keeps naming: brittle workflows, unclear ownership, and tools that never learn the organization.
