Executive summary

Youthfront AI Stewardship Proposal

Prepared for Youthfront, in response to Natasha Nikkel (Chief Operating Officer) · Movemental

Sandbox-first. Skills are layered in to keep experiments disciplined. Solutions (workflow integration) stay downstream and optional—not part of the default scope.

Scope. This proposal is for a guided Sandbox Program for Youthfront staff, supported by targeted Skills development, over a modular 3–12 month arc.

Donor communication, grant and report writing, camp and neighborhood messaging, family-program administration, and internal planning are all places where AI use is already tempting—and where uneven review creates real risk. The gap to close is not “more software,” but the distance between informal experimentation and an approved, repeatable practice your leaders can stand behind.

Context

  • Youthfront already holds a serious posture on safety, mission, and relational trust.
  • The priority now is structured discovery and staff capability in those real workflows—not a bundled enterprise rollout.

What you are buying

  • Sandbox Program (center): a bounded season of facilitated experiments, explicit out-of-scope rules, and a documented portfolio of what you tried, approved, and retired.
  • Skills (supporting layer): workshops and applied critique so work stays de-identified where needed, on-voice, verified, and disclosable—paired to Sandbox milestones, not a standalone “AI theory” track.
  • Solutions (only if warranted later): templates, approvals, or light automation after a short list of use cases is stable—scoped separately.

What you walk away with

  • Promoted use-case cards: owner, inputs, review chain, data touched, and hard “never here” lines.
  • A small internal library of anonymized examples your teams can reuse when coaching each other.
  • Plain-language artifacts for governance and external trust questions—so stated posture matches practice.
  • If you want it: light measurement on a short list of agreed workflows (time-on-task or rework).

Core proposal

Youthfront — Sandbox Program with Skills support

Organization: Youthfront (Kansas City) Audience: Executive team, program leadership, operations Horizon: Modular (often 3–12 months) Center of gravity: Sandbox · Skills layered in

This document describes a Sandbox Program supported by Skills development, using Movemental’s AI Stewardship Sequence as background logic—not as a four-layer product bundle.

Section 1 — The real problem

AI initiatives fail when organizations treat them as a purchase instead of a practice: either rushing tools (shadow workflows, uneven review) or avoiding alignment (staff still experiment; leadership still gets surprised).

For a youth-serving ministry, those failures show up in lived work: draft donor copy that never got a consistent review, internal plans built on half-known tool habits, or ministry-adjacent tasks where speed and formation pull in opposite directions. None of that requires bad intent—only a missing shared method.

Youthfront needs a steady method: learn in public, document what works, keep formation- and donor-adjacent work inside explicit review lanes, and build skill where credibility is earned or lost.

Section 2 — Movemental approach

What you are buying. A Sandbox Program (primary) with Skills layered in so experiments stay safe and on-mission. That work sits inside the broader logic of the AI Stewardship Sequence (Safety → Sandbox → Skills → Solutions). You are not purchasing a packaged “all four stages” implementation.

The sequence (compressed):

  • Safety — boundaries, disclosure, high-risk data lanes. Present as a guardrail thread during Sandbox; Youthfront keeps final policy decisions.
  • Sandbox — the core: disciplined discovery, promotion rules, portfolio documentation.
  • Skills — judgment, authorship, verification; supports Sandbox and continues as use cases mature.
  • Solutions — integration and automation only after a small set of patterns is proven; optional and later.

Movemental facilitates sessions, templates, and learning design. Youthfront retains final authority on what is in or out of scope—especially anything youth-identified, formation-facing, or sensitive in program contexts.

Where regulated or clinical data is in play, we align facilitation with your IT and compliance direction. Enterprise platform licensing, seat purchases, SSO/SCIM, HIPAA-scoped environments, and vendor agreements (BAAs, DPAs) sit outside this fee: they are procurement and systems work, not part of Movemental’s Sandbox and Skills engagement as priced here.

Section 3 — Proposed engagement

Core offer: Sandbox Program, with Skills workshops and critique cycles scheduled so they reinforce what the Sandbox is learning—not as a parallel curriculum.

Primary

Phase 1 — Sandbox Program

A season with a charter, cadence, and explicit out-of-scope list. Participants run a tight loop: intent → attempt → human review → log learning → promote, pause, or retire.

Cadence. Weekly or biweekly facilitated sessions (90–120 minutes), plus light async notes between sessions. During peak camp delivery (about June–August), we default to shorter live blocks, async updates, or wider spacing for field-heavy teams.

Cross-team discovery. Discovery should pull in the major functional areas where AI pressure is already showing up: Camp, Neighborhood, and Family ministries; Development and Marketing for fundraising and communications drafts; and Operations for what is already in use organizationally. Early work uses de-identified or synthetic inputs until a use case earns explicit live-data rules.

Example lanes (illustrative): donor-adjacent first drafts; internal summaries and briefs; public research memos; camp and neighborhood operations scaffolding where youth-facing rules are explicit.

Documentation. Each promoted use case becomes a one-page card: purpose, owner, inputs, review steps, limitations. If Youthfront uses an internal evaluation frame (e.g., proportionality / oversight rubrics), we can mirror it in promotion criteria.

Governance during the Sandbox (illustrative — names and titles set by Youthfront)

  • Executive leadership: final decision rights and board-facing posture.
  • Operations: tool access, cadence, and “what is allowed by default.”
  • Development / marketing: donor-facing and external-comms review lanes.
  • Ministry leadership: formation- and youth-adjacent boundaries and vetoes on experiments.

Layered in

Phase 2 — Skills development

Workshops on discernment, authorship, verification, de-identification, and disclosure—tied to anonymized examples from your categories of work. Not feature training; operator formation.

Downstream / optional

Phase 3 — Solutions (only if warranted)

Templates, approvals wiring, or light automation after a short list of use cases is stable. Scoped separately; not assumed in year one.

Section 4 — What Youthfront receives

Exact counts and calendar dates are fixed in the statement of work.

Included deliverables

  • Facilitated Sandbox sessions — consistent facilitator; agenda templates; decision logs.
  • Sandbox charter pack — scope, out-of-scope list, promotion rules, cadence options.
  • Use-case portfolio — promoted cards + retired experiments with reasons.
  • Experiment log templates — lightweight fields so teams do not invent their own paperwork.
  • Skills workshops — scheduled blocks aligned to Sandbox milestones (timing per option below).
  • Leadership guidance sessions — short executive blocks on posture, disclosure, and governance-style questions—including room for theological reflection as a stewardship topic, at Youthfront’s discretion.
  • Boundary / review guidance — checklists for promoted use cases (who must review, what must never be pasted, when to stop).
  • Async guidance window — scoped in writing (e.g., artifact review + office hours).

Optional / confirm at scope

  • Messaging alignment checkpoints — periodic review of draft outputs against your internal messaging and identity standards (for example, guidance some teams maintain in an internal “Base” hub). Movemental does not own your systems of record; we can facilitate rubric-based review if you want this explicitly in the SOW.

Section 5 — Outcomes and value

  • Time — less blank-page friction on agreed workflows; simple before/after signals if you want them.
  • Quality — fewer off-voice or under-reviewed external drafts.
  • Clarity — fewer side-channel AI conversations; a shared portfolio vocabulary.
  • Risk — fewer surprises; clearer disclosure and review habits.
  • Credibility — artifacts you can share with partners, funders, and reviewers when trust questions arise.

We do not promise miracle productivity. We promise a serious method and evidence you can point to.

Section 6 — Timeline options

Pick a shape; we tune dates to camp, development, and board calendars.

We work at the intersection of formation and technology practice: fewer promises, explicit boundaries, plain language for non-technical leaders, and artifacts that survive board-level scrutiny.

The AI Stewardship Sequence keeps the work from drifting into random tips. The goal is repeatable organizational judgment—not heroics from one enthusiastic power user.
