## 1. The Three-Section Structure (Author’s Vision)

The book is **three sections**, not seven parts.

### Section 1: Where We Are Now (Re: AI)

**Function:** Name the **kairos**—what’s actually going on that matters and isn’t banal or trite.

- **Reference image:** Something like the 2023 “AI as threat” slide (e.g. Center for Humane Technology or similar): the early-LLM threat list—massive scams, OnlyFans-style synthetic content, deepfakes, etc. Those were the threats as conceived early in the LLM release cycle. They’re still the **core** threats.
- **What this section does:** Here are the **kairos factors**—the distinctive factors, what’s in the air that matters. Things that need to be **deciphered**: we don’t know what’s real online; credibility is under pressure; trust collapse; the mechanisms that used to signal expertise are breaking down. This isn’t generic “AI is changing things.” It’s a clear, concrete framing of *this moment*: what’s actually happening that demands a response.

**Existing material that fits here:** Credibility crisis (Ch 1), “what’s real” / trust collapse, AI as both problem and solution (Ch 2), “where we are and why now” (Ch 3)—including why movement leaders were right to ignore SEO until now, and the shift to discoverability + network verification. The transcript’s “we are beginners working with a technology no one understands” and “we are about to have an AI credibility crisis” belong here. So does the **audience shift** (see below): from last March to now, the same audience has a completely different relationship to the topic—overwhelmed then, “I get it, it’s dumb” now—which is itself a kairos factor (speed of adoption, effective exposure without wisdom).

### Section 2: What Are We Going to Do About It?

**Function:** If there’s a kairos we need to respond to, **what’s the response?**

- This is the book’s **framework and practice**: how we navigate, what we refuse, what we’re free to do, how we lead. Amplification not replacement; voice preservation; scenius; transparency; pace and discernment; what to refuse (including theological integrity); what AI displaces and what we protect; practice parameters (agents, 70/30, spectrum and domains); content creation and formation; prompting as humane communication. The reframe—AI as anthropological/adaptive leadership task, not technological—sets up *who* responds (leaders and communities, not just tech people) and *how* (discernment, boundaries, networks).

**Existing material that fits here:** Everything in the current outline that is “the framework” and “the response”: reframe (not a tech challenge), finding a guide, credibility amplifier not faker, amplification not replacement, maturity model and experimentation, voice, scenius, transparency, pace and discernment, what to refuse and what we’re free to do, what AI displaces and what we protect, practice parameters, AI and content creation, AI and formation and leadership, prompting. Plus the long arc (Gutenberg to networks of trust, content that moves) as the direction of the response.

### Section 3: My 2025 as Case Study (Experimental / Demonstrative)

**Function:** Turn the author’s life into a **concrete starting point**—not exhibitionist, but demonstrative. “Here’s what we did in 2025.”

- **The problem this section solves:** Nobody actually understands AI, but everyone thinks they do. Effective exposure without actual wisdom—without real experience—is **insidious**: the same people who don’t understand the concept have been so inundated with conceptual references that they think they know. The book doesn’t say that harshly; it invites **fresh eyes** or **beginner’s mind**. What would we be looking at if we looked with fresh eyes?
- **What’s hard and under-explored:** How **actual people** use **actual** AIs. People don’t want to see how you use AI; it’s a tricky thing. So Section 3 is the place where the author says: here’s my 2025. It’s a **we** (we did this)—weird but deliberate—so we can make clear a concrete starting point at a time when almost no one has one.
- **Tone:** Don’t underestimate AI. Just because you see a million articles a day about AI doesn’t mean you know anything about it. The starting point we’re offering is “pretty astonishing”—not as a compliment, but as a warning: take the power and the strangeness of what’s possible seriously.

**Existing material that fits here:** The placeholder “My 2025 with AI” (Ch 11 in the 21-chapter outline)—year in review, Movemental + Alan Hirsch as centerpiece use case, what it illustrates (amplification, voice, transparency, human-in-the-loop, building in public). The transcript’s story (tool → machine → system → platform, October crisis, type-safety pivot, multiplication) and the evolution “showcase → demonstrate → prophetically model.” “Where I am now” (still learning, still wrong, invitation to slow down and discern) closes the loop. The key is to **position** this section as the “case study / starting point” so it doesn’t read as “all about me” but as “here’s one concrete picture of what it looks like when the framework is applied.”

## 2. The Thread Through the Notes (What Led to This)

### From the author transcript (origin, scope, crisis)

- **Beginner’s mind:** “We are beginners working with a technology no one understands.” “I remember thinking I’d reassure them with ‘beginner’s mind.’” That reassurance is now **Section 3’s** pitch: look with fresh eyes; here’s what we’d be looking at (my 2025).
- **Credibility crisis and timing:** “I began conceiving Movemental in January 2025 for a January 2026 audience… But now, just a year later, AI expertise is growing disproportionately. We are about to have an AI credibility crisis.” “No one understands the technology; it is immensely powerful; and people think they’ve figured it out. Dunning–Kruger on steroids. Including me.” That’s the **kairos** (Section 1) and the reason Section 3 has to be a *concrete* starting point—because the default is overconfidence without experience.
- **Being wrong and staying:** “I built this thinking I knew something. I was wrong.” “What I did next is changing my life again. I refused to stop experimenting… And I was willing to reconsider everything in light of new information shared in human relationship.” That’s the posture for the whole book and the bridge into “what we do about it” (relationship, accountability, scenius).

### From transcript analysis

- **Speed of change:** “The author conceived the project for a 2026 audience, but by 2026, the landscape had already changed. This is meta-commentary on the speed of change.” So Section 1 should name **speed** explicitly: fastest-adopted technology ever; part of the fastest wave of global tech adoption (unlike the car—seeing a car didn’t mean everyone adopted cars in a year). Daily reality of change and VUCA. Not trite—just true. You have an article on “speed and the velocity of language”; that thread belongs in Section 1.
- **Audience shift:** From last March till now, the author’s **conception of the audience** has entirely changed. Same audience (movement leaders who create/steward content). Completely different **reality or relationship to the topic**: then, more “overwhelmed”; now, more “I get it, it’s dumb.” So the book isn’t addressing a static persona; it’s addressing people in a rapidly shifting relationship to AI. Section 1 is where that shift is named—so the reader recognizes themselves and doesn’t feel talked down to.

### From existing chapters

- **Ch 1 (credibility crisis)** and **Ch 3 (where we are and why now)** already deliver “what’s going on”: flood of content, broken signals, trust collapse, “how do you know what’s real,” audience in different places, why this moment is different (discoverability + network verification). They need to be **framed as kairos**—what’s in the air that matters—and, where useful, linked to the “threat list” sensibility (early LLM threats: scams, synthetic content, credibility fraud) so it doesn’t feel abstract.
- **Ch 2 (AI as both problem and solution)** holds the tension that makes Section 1 land: we’re being asked to use the tool that created the problem; ignoring AI isn’t an option; uncritical adoption is dangerous.
- **Ch 11 (My 2025 with AI)** is the only chapter that currently belongs *entirely* in Section 3; it’s a placeholder that needs to become the case study / starting point, with the “we” and the “don’t underestimate AI” and “how actual people use actual AIs” framing made explicit.

## 4. Audience Shift and Speed (Explicit Beats to Add)

These are threads you named; they should be written into Section 1 (and referenced where relevant later).

- **Audience shift:** From March [2024/2025] till now, the same audience’s relationship to AI has entirely changed. Then: overwhelmed, anxious, “do I have to care?” Now: “I get it, it’s dumb”—but that’s often **effective exposure without wisdom**. So the book speaks to people who think they know *and* people who know they don’t; the common ground is that we all need a **concrete starting point** (Section 3).
- **Speed of adoption:** AI is the fastest-adopted major technology in history. It sits inside the fastest wave of global technological adoption we’ve seen—unlike, say, the car: revolutionary, but seeing a car didn’t mean everyone adopted cars in a year. The daily reality of change and VUCA isn’t something to be trite about; it just needs to be said. **Speed is an issue.** You have the “velocity of language” piece; that idea can anchor or complement this beat.
- **Don’t underestimate AI:** Section 3’s job is partly to show that the “starting point” is **astonishing**—not in a self-congratulatory way, but so the reader doesn’t dismiss what’s possible. A million articles about AI don’t equal understanding. Experience and concrete use (like “here’s what we did in 2025”) are the antidote to that illusion.
