## The Core Question

> "It does seem not an easy proposition to say that people are going to pay for... words. But people do pay for words on Substack right? Even so is there a general sense of a hierarchy of content that people consider premium worth paying for, etc?"

This is the right question to ask. Because the answer fundamentally shapes Movemental's content strategy, platform features, and revenue projections.

**The short answer**: Yes, people pay for words—but not all words, and not just because they're words.

**The nuanced answer**: There IS a content value hierarchy, but it's not primarily about format (text vs. video vs. interactive). It's about something deeper.

- **Top 100**: $100K-$500K annually

- **Heather Cox Richardson** (history/politics): 40K+ paid subscribers = $2M+/year
- **Anne Helen Petersen** (culture): 20K+ paid subscribers = $1.2M+/year
- **Packy McCormick** (business/tech): 15K+ paid subscribers = $900K+/year

**All text. All words. All paid.**

### What This Tells Us

People absolutely pay for text content when:

1. **The ideas are valuable enough** (can't get this thinking elsewhere)
2. **The voice is distinctive enough** (authentic, not generic)
3. **The frequency is consistent enough** (reliable value delivery)
4. **The community is engaged enough** (not just broadcast, but conversation)
5. **The transformation is real enough** (changes how they think/act)

The format matters less than the value delivered.

## Part II: The Khan Academy Counterpoint - When Text Isn't Enough

### What Khan Academy Teaches Us

You're right to bring up Khan Academy. It points to something important:

**For certain types of content, text alone is insufficient.**

Khan Academy succeeded with video because:
- **Mathematics requires seeing the work** (step-by-step visual process)
- **Abstract concepts need demonstration** (not just explanation)
- **Learning math is procedural** (watching someone solve problems helps)
- **Young learners engage differently** (video holds attention better than text)

But here's what's crucial: **Khan Academy is FREE.**

The premium version ($9/month) adds:
- Progress tracking
- Practice exercises
- Personalized learning paths
- Mastery challenges

**The content format didn't make it premium. The personalization and accountability did.**

### The Format vs. Value Question

Let's be clear about what Khan Academy proves:

**What it DOES prove:**
- Some learning requires visual/interactive elements
- Younger audiences often prefer video to text
- Complex procedural knowledge benefits from demonstration
- Engagement strategies differ by content type

**What it DOESN'T prove:**
- That video is inherently more valuable than text
- That people won't pay for text-based content
- That "premium" equals multimedia

**Key insight**: Khan Academy is free BECAUSE it's educational nonprofit content for kids. The video format was chosen for pedagogical effectiveness, not monetization strategy.

## Part III: The Real Content Value Hierarchy

After analyzing Substack successes, Patreon creators, online course platforms, and digital publishing economics, here's what actually determines content value:

### The Five-Tier Value Hierarchy (Research-Based)

#### **Tier 1: Commodity Content (Free)**
- **Characteristics**: Generic, widely available, no unique perspective
- **Examples**: News summaries, generic advice, recycled content
- **Monetization potential**: $0
- **Why**: Can get equivalent content free elsewhere

#### **Tier 2: Curated Content (Free or Low-Cost)**
- **Characteristics**: Aggregated, filtered, organized—but not original
- **Examples**: Newsletter roundups, link collections, curated resources
- **Monetization potential**: $0-$3/month
- **Why**: Value is in curation, not creation—limited willingness to pay

#### **Tier 3: Expert Commentary (Low to Moderate Price)**
- **Characteristics**: Original perspective, expert analysis, unique voice
- **Examples**: Political analysis, cultural criticism, industry insights
- **Monetization potential**: $5-15/month
- **Why**: Getting expert thinking you can't get elsewhere
- **Format**: Usually text, sometimes audio/video

**This is where most successful Substacks live.**

#### **Tier 4: Transformational Insight (Moderate to High Price)**
- **Characteristics**: Changes how you think, challenges assumptions, catalyzes action
- **Examples**: Leadership frameworks, theological depth, strategic thinking
- **Monetization potential**: $10-30/month for subscriptions, $100-500 for courses
- **Why**: Doesn't just inform—transforms understanding and practice
- **Format**: Can be text, but often includes courses, cohorts, community

**This is where Movemental aims to be.**

#### **Tier 5: Personalized Transformation (Premium Price)**
- **Characteristics**: Direct access, customized guidance, accountability
- **Examples**: Coaching, consulting, mastermind groups, intensive cohorts
- **Monetization potential**: $100-500/month or $1,000+ for programs
- **Why**: Not just insights—direct application to your situation
- **Format**: Usually includes text foundations + interactive elements

### What This Hierarchy Reveals

**The progression isn't about format complexity (text → video → interactive).**

**The progression is about depth of transformation (information → insight → transformation → personalization).**

You can deliver Tier 3-4 value entirely through text if:
- Your thinking is original enough
- Your voice is distinctive enough  
- Your insights are transformational enough
- Your consistency is reliable enough

**Most successful Substacks are Tier 3-4, delivered primarily through text.**

## Part IV: Format Matters Less Than You Think—But More Than Nothing

### When Format Matters

**Video/multimedia becomes important when:**

1. **Visual demonstration is essential**
   - Mathematics (showing work)
   - Design (showing aesthetics)
   - Physical practices (showing movement/technique)
   - Data visualization (showing patterns)

2. **The personality/presence is the product**
   - Charismatic teachers whose energy matters
   - Performers whose delivery is part of the value
   - Interviewers whose rapport creates the insight

3. **The audience demographic prefers it**
   - Younger audiences (Gen Z) lean video
   - Visual learners need to see concepts
   - Busy professionals who consume while commuting

4. **The complexity requires layering**
   - Software tutorials (need to see interface)
   - Medical/scientific concepts (diagrams essential)
   - Strategic frameworks (visual models help)

### When Text Works Better

**Text remains superior for:**

1. **Deep intellectual engagement**
   - Theology and philosophy
   - Strategic thinking and analysis
   - Nuanced argumentation
   - Reference material you return to

2. **Speed and accessibility**
   - Scannable for key insights
   - Searchable for specific information
   - Quotable for sharing ideas
   - Works on any device, any context

3. **Thoughtful processing**
   - Reader controls pace
   - Can pause and reflect
   - Can reread complex sections
   - Better for note-taking

4. **SEO and discoverability**
   - Text content ranks in search
   - Video content mostly lives in platforms
   - Articles become permanent resources
   - Long-tail discovery happens through text

### The Both/And Reality

**Most successful digital platforms use a content mix:**

**80/20 Rule for Movemental Leaders:**
- **80% text-based content**: Articles, essays, long-form thinking (core value delivery)
- **20% enrichment content**: Video talks, audio conversations, courses, community calls

**Why this works:**
- Text delivers the transformational insight (Tier 4)
- Multimedia adds personality and connection
- Courses/cohorts add personalization (Tier 5)
- Community adds belonging and accountability

**Example: A successful movemental leader publishes:**
- 2-4 text articles/month (3,000-5,000 words each) — FREE tier gets 1-2, PAID gets all
- 1 quarterly video course (6 sessions) — PAID tier exclusive
- 1 monthly Q&A call — PAID tier exclusive
- Ongoing community discussion — PAID tier access

**The text is the foundation. Everything else amplifies.**

## Part V: What Research Tells Us About Payment Psychology

### Why People Pay for Content (Ranked by Research)

**Based on Patreon, Substack, OnlyFans (yes, really), and digital course platform data:**

#### **1. Transformation Over Information (Highest Driver)**

People don't pay for facts. They pay for insights that change them.

**Research finding**: Content that "made me think differently" or "changed my practice" has 8x higher conversion to paid than content that "taught me something new."

**Application to Movemental**: Theology that challenges and transforms >> theology that informs.

#### **2. Trust and Authenticity (Second Highest)**

People pay creators they trust, even if similar content exists free elsewhere.

**Research finding**: "I trust this person" is cited by 71% of paid subscribers as a primary reason for paying, even when they acknowledge free alternatives exist.

**Application to Movemental**: Credibility through practice + authentic voice = willingness to pay.

#### **3. Community and Belonging (Third Highest)**

People pay to be part of something—a movement, a conversation, a tribe.

**Research finding**: Platforms with community features (comments, forums, member meetups) see 3-5x higher paid conversion rates than pure content delivery.

**Application to Movemental**: The scenius network effect—being part of the movemental conversation—adds value beyond individual content.

#### **4. Consistency and Reliability (Fourth)**

People pay for dependability. They know what they're getting and when.

**Research finding**: Creators who publish on a consistent schedule have 40% lower churn than those with irregular publishing.

**Application to Movemental**: 2-4x/month consistent publishing >> sporadic brilliant pieces.

#### **5. Supporting the Mission (Fifth)**

People pay to keep good work alive, especially mission-driven content.

**Research finding**: In religious/nonprofit content, "supporting the work" is cited by 45% of paid subscribers even when they acknowledge they'd probably get the content free anyway.

**Application to Movemental**: Mission alignment + kingdom impact messaging increases willingness to pay.

#### **6. Exclusive Access (Sixth)**

People pay for what others can't get—but this is less important than commonly assumed.

**Research finding**: "Exclusive content" ranks 6th in reasons for paying, behind transformation, trust, community, consistency, and mission. Exclusivity matters, but it's not the primary driver.

**Application to Movemental**: Some paywalled content necessary, but giving away substantial free content while charging for deeper engagement is a viable model.
