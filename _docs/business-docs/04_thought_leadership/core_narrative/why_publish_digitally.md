# Why You Should Be Publishing Digitally

## The Simple Truth

If you're leading, you're communicating. And if you're communicating movementally, you should be publishing digitally.

This isn't about following trends or checking boxes. It's about the fundamental reality of how influence, trust, and impact work in the world we actually live in.

## The Case for Digital Publishing: A Progressive Exploration

### 1. **Your Ideas Need to Be Found**

Let's start with the most basic reason: **people are looking for what you have to say, but they can't find it.**

When someone hears you speak, reads your book, or encounters your ideas secondhand, their next move is predictable—they search for you online. Not "later," not "eventually"—immediately. On their phone, right there in the moment.

What do they find?

If you're not publishing digitally, they might find nothing. Or worse, they find outdated information, someone else talking about your ideas, or a digital presence so thin it undermines the authority you've spent years building.

**The research confirms this:** Search engine visibility is how credibility is verified in 2024. Before people decide to trust a leader, buy a book, attend an event, or support an organization, they search. And if compelling content doesn't appear in those first few results, the opportunity evaporates.

### 2. **Your Best Content Has a Shelf Life of Zero**

You've preached brilliant sermons. Given talks that moved audiences. Had insights in conversations that could change how people think.

Where did those insights go?

For most leaders, they evaporated. Maybe someone took notes. Maybe there's a recording buried in a church archive or a podcast feed from 2018 that three people listen to.

**Digital publishing transforms your content from ephemeral to evergreen.** That article you write today will still be working for you in five years. People will discover it through organic search, share it with their networks, and find exactly what they needed at the exact moment they needed it.

The math here is stark: A single sermon reaches the people in the room (or on the livestream) that week. A published article from that sermon can reach thousands of people you'll never meet, in cities you'll never visit, for years after you write it.

### 3. **Authority Requires Presence, Not Just Performance**

You've earned your authority through decades of work. You've published books, led organizations, pioneered ideas, built movements.

But here's what's changed: **Authority today is demonstrated continuously, not established once.**

Your book from ten years ago doesn't convince a 28-year-old nonprofit director that you understand the challenges she faces right now. What convinces her is seeing that you're actively engaged with current questions, that your thinking is evolving, that you're still in the conversation.

This isn't fair, perhaps. But it's true.

Digital publishing is how you signal: "I'm still here. I'm still thinking. I'm still contributing." It transforms your authority from a credential on a shelf to a living presence in the discourse.

Research shows that **73% of decision-makers** consider an organization's published thought leadership content more trustworthy than traditional marketing materials. But that content has to exist—and it has to be findable.

### 4. **People Need Different Entry Points**

Not everyone will read your 300-page book. Not everyone can attend your speaking events. Not everyone connects with the same medium.

But someone might:
- Find a 1,200-word article you wrote that perfectly articulates what they've been feeling
- Stumble on a short piece that introduces them to an idea they've never encountered
- Share your writing with a friend who needs exactly that perspective
- Discover you through a search for a specific question you've answered

**Digital publishing creates multiple on-ramps to your work.** Each published piece is a doorway. Some people will walk through one door and stay for years. Others will read one article and that's exactly what they needed.

You can't predict which piece will reach whom. But you can make sure the doors exist.

### 5. **Organizational Credibility Is Increasingly Digital-First**

Let's talk about organizations specifically—nonprofits, churches, movements, ministries.

When donors evaluate whether to give, they don't just look at a brochure anymore. They look at your website, yes, but more importantly: **What are you saying? What are you contributing to the conversation in your field?**

Organizations with robust digital publishing presences see measurably better fundraising outcomes. Not because they're "marketing better," but because they're demonstrating:
- **Clarity of mission** (articulated repeatedly in different contexts)
- **Depth of expertise** (proven through consistent, thoughtful content)
- **Active engagement** (showing up in the ongoing conversation)
- **Transparency and accessibility** (sharing insights, learnings, even struggles)

The best donors—the ones who give significantly and stay committed—want to support organizations that are leading conversations, not just asking for money. Digital publishing is how you demonstrate that leadership.

### 6. **The Network Effect Multiplies Everything**

Here's where it gets really interesting.

When you publish on your own website (even a nice one with good SEO), you're isolated. Every article starts from zero. Your reach is limited to your own audience, your own email list, your own social media followers.

But when you publish within a *network* of aligned leaders, everything changes.

Suddenly:
- Your article appears in front of audiences who've never heard of you—but who trust the other leaders in the network
- Cross-references and connections emerge organically (other leaders linking to your work, building on your ideas)
- Search engines recognize the authority of the entire network, boosting every individual piece
- Readers discover you through related content, creating serendipitous connections

The research on network effects in digital publishing is clear: **A single author in a 100-person network can see 28x the reach** compared to publishing alone. At 1,000 users, that amplification grows to 500x.

This isn't theoretical. It's mathematical. Networks create compound visibility that individual platforms simply cannot match.

### 7. **You're Already Creating the Content**

Here's the thing that makes this especially urgent: **You're already doing the work.**

You're already:
- Preparing talks
- Writing sermons
- Drafting book chapters
- Crafting email newsletters
- Having conversations that surface important insights

The gap isn't that you need to create more content. The gap is that most of that content **disappears instead of being leveraged**.

A sermon becomes an article. A talk becomes a series. A book chapter becomes six published pieces that can be found independently. An email newsletter becomes the foundation for long-form content that will attract people who've never been on your list.

Digital publishing isn't about adding a new burden. It's about **capturing value from work you're already doing.**

### 8. **The Long Tail of Discoverability**

Let's talk about something most leaders don't think about: **long-tail search**.

When someone searches "incarnational community building across cultural lines" or "Christian response to gentrification" or "integrating contemplative practice in activist work," they're looking for something specific. Probably something you've written about, preached about, thought deeply about.

But if that content isn't published digitally, optimized for search, and part of a broader ecosystem of related content, they'll never find it.

Here's what the data shows:
- **Long-tail organic search** (specific, detailed queries) accounts for 70% of all search traffic
- These searches have **much higher engagement rates** because people found exactly what they needed
- Content that ranks well for long-tail queries can drive consistent traffic for **years without additional effort**

Every published article becomes a permanent beacon for people searching for that specific insight. This is how ideas find the people who need them most.

### 9. **Digital Publishing Is the Conversation**

We tend to think: "First the work, then the writing about the work."

But increasingly, **the writing IS the work.**

Thought leadership—real intellectual and spiritual leadership—happens in published discourse. The conversation that shapes how people think, how movements form, how paradigms shift occurs in written, shared, linked, referenced, built-upon content.

If you're not publishing digitally, you're not just missing an audience. You're missing the conversation itself.

The leaders who shape their fields are the ones consistently contributing to the ongoing dialogue. Not occasionally. Not only in books every five years. But regularly, thoughtfully, publicly.

### 10. **The Alternative Is Worse**

Finally, let's be honest about what happens if you don't publish digitally.

Your influence stays bounded by:
- The people who already know you
- The organizations that already book you
- The audiences that already subscribe to your newsletter

Your ideas remain:
- Difficult to discover
- Hard to share
- Locked in mediums that don't travel (events, podcasts, books)

Your organization:
- Looks less credible than it is
- Struggles to explain its value to new audiences
- Misses fundraising opportunities because donors can't see the depth of your work

And most tragically: **The people who need what you have to say never find it.**

Not because they're not looking. But because it's not there to be found.
