## Understanding Apostolic Leadership

**Apostolic leadership** refers to the pioneering, sentness, and systems thinking that extends the church into new contexts and creates systems for multiplication. This isn't about position or hierarchy—it's about the apostolic intelligence (AQ) that Jesus gave his church according to Ephesians 4:11. Apostolic leaders are pioneers who break new ground, systems architects who understand multiplication, and sent ones who extend the movement into new territory.

The term "apostolic" comes from the Greek word *apostolos*, which means "one who is sent." Apostolic leadership is fundamentally about sentness—understanding that we're sent by Jesus to extend his mission into new contexts. But it's also about pioneering—breaking new ground, establishing new works, and creating new possibilities. And it's about systems thinking—understanding how things connect, how systems multiply, and how movements spread.

Apostolic leadership isn't limited to a few special people. Every believer carries elements of apostolic intelligence, with some having it more dominantly. The question isn't whether you have apostolic intelligence—it's whether it's activated and expressed. When apostolic intelligence is present and active in leadership teams and communities, multiplication becomes possible.

### The Biblical Foundation

Apostolic leadership finds its foundation in the New Testament, where we see apostles functioning as pioneers, multipliers, and systems architects. Jesus himself is called "the apostle" (Hebrews 3:1), and he sent his disciples as apostles to extend his mission into the world.

In Acts 1:8, Jesus tells his disciples: "But you will receive power when the Holy Spirit comes on you; and you will be my witnesses in Jerusalem, and in all Judea and Samaria, and to the ends of the earth." This is apostolic vision—extending the movement from local to global, from familiar to new territory.

Paul's apostolic ministry demonstrates apostolic leadership in action. He didn't just plant churches—he created systems for multiplication. He established networks, trained leaders, and created reproducible patterns that enabled the movement to spread across the Roman Empire. He thought in terms of multiplication, not just addition. He saw the big picture and understood how systems connect and multiply.

The early church's explosive growth from 25,000 to 20 million in just three centuries wasn't accidental—it was the result of apostolic leadership that extended the movement into new contexts and created systems for multiplication.

### Apostolic Intelligence (AQ) Explained

Apostolic Intelligence (AQ) is the intelligence of sentness, pioneering, and systems thinking. It's one of the five forms of intelligence that Jesus gave his church according to Ephesians 4:11. AQ asks: "How do we extend into new territory? How do we maintain health as we multiply? What systems will support long-term movement?"

**Sentness** is the foundational characteristic of apostolic intelligence. Apostolic leaders understand that they're sent by Jesus to extend his mission into new contexts. This isn't about going where we want—it's about going where Jesus sends us. Sentness creates movement because it pushes us beyond our comfort zones into new territory.

**Pioneering** is the characteristic that breaks new ground. Apostolic leaders are pioneers who establish new works, create new possibilities, and extend into new contexts. They're not content with maintaining what exists—they're driven to create what doesn't exist yet. Pioneering creates movement because it expands the boundaries of what's possible.

**Systems Thinking** is the characteristic that understands how things connect and multiply. Apostolic leaders are systems architects who understand how to create reproducible patterns, establish networks, and build structures that enable multiplication. They think in terms of systems, not just programs. Systems thinking creates movement because it enables multiplication rather than just addition.

### The Characteristics of Apostolic Leadership

Apostolic leadership is characterized by several key qualities:

**Vision** - Apostolic leaders see what others don't see. They see possibilities where others see problems. They see multiplication where others see maintenance. They see movement where others see organization.

**Courage** - Apostolic leaders take risks that others won't take. They pioneer into new territory, break new ground, and create new possibilities. They're not afraid of failure because they understand that failure is part of the pioneering process.

**Multiplication Focus** - Apostolic leaders think in terms of multiplication, not just addition. They don't just want to grow—they want to multiply. They don't just want to add people—they want to multiply communities. They don't just want to expand—they want to reproduce.

**Systems Architecture** - Apostolic leaders understand how systems work. They know how to create reproducible patterns, establish networks, and build structures that enable multiplication. They think in terms of systems, not just programs.

**Long-term Perspective** - Apostolic leaders think beyond the immediate to the long-term. They understand that movements take time to develop, that multiplication requires patience, and that systems need to be built for sustainability.

## Framework Foundation: Apostolic Leadership in Context

Understanding apostolic leadership requires grasping how it functions within the larger framework of fivefold ministry and movement systems. This isn't about apostolic leadership in isolation—it's about apostolic leadership as part of the complete system Jesus designed.

### Apostolic Leadership and Fivefold Ministry

Apostolic leadership is one of the five ministry gifts Jesus gave his church. It works in dynamic tension with the other four gifts: Prophetic (challenge and transformation), Evangelistic (communication and gathering), Shepherding (care and community), and Teaching (formation and equipping).

Apostolic leadership needs the other gifts to function effectively. It needs prophetic challenge to stay aligned with God's purposes. It needs evangelistic communication to gather people. It needs shepherding care to nurture communities. It needs teaching formation to equip people. When all five gifts work together, complete movement capacity emerges.

The Western church has largely "exiled" apostolic leadership, operating primarily with Shepherding and Teaching. This truncation creates organizational equilibrium and stasis, making the church incapable of multiplication and missional engagement. Recovering apostolic leadership is essential for movement capacity.

### Apostolic Leadership and Movement Multiplication

Apostolic leadership is essential for movement multiplication. Movements multiply when they extend into new contexts, create reproducible patterns, and build systems for long-term sustainability. This is exactly what apostolic leadership does.

The early church multiplied because apostolic leadership extended it into new contexts across the Roman Empire. The Chinese underground church multiplied because apostolic leadership created systems for reproduction even under persecution. Contemporary movements multiply when apostolic leadership creates the conditions for multiplication.

Without apostolic leadership, churches can grow but they can't multiply. They can add people but they can't reproduce communities. They can expand but they can't create movements. Apostolic leadership is the difference between addition and multiplication.

### Apostolic Leadership and mDNA

Apostolic leadership connects to the mDNA framework through the Apostolic Environment element. The mDNA framework includes six elements that create movement capacity, and one of those elements is APEST Culture—the fivefold ministry environment that releases all of God's people.

Apostolic leadership creates the Apostolic Environment. When apostolic intelligence is present and active, it creates the culture and environment where mDNA can activate. Apostolic leadership isn't separate from mDNA—it's the leadership that enables mDNA to function.

## Practical Application: Developing Apostolic Leadership

Understanding apostolic leadership is one thing—developing it is another. How do we move from understanding to implementation? How do we develop apostolic leadership in ourselves and our communities?

### Step 1: Assess Your Apostolic Intelligence

The first step in developing apostolic leadership is to assess your apostolic intelligence. Do you think in terms of multiplication? Do you see the big picture? Do you understand how systems work? Are you willing to pioneer into new territory?

Apostolic assessment tools are available to help with this process. These tools help you understand your apostolic profile—where your strengths lie, where you need development, and how to activate your apostolic intelligence.

### Step 2: Develop Apostolic Vision

The second step is to develop apostolic vision—the ability to see possibilities where others see problems, to see multiplication where others see maintenance, to see movement where others see organization.

This involves asking apostolic questions: "What would multiplication look like here? How could we extend into new contexts? What systems would enable long-term movement?" It involves thinking beyond the immediate to the long-term, beyond addition to multiplication.

### Step 3: Practice Pioneering

The third step is to practice pioneering—taking risks, breaking new ground, and creating new possibilities. This might mean starting something new, extending into a new context, or creating a new approach to an old problem.

Pioneering requires courage because it involves risk. But it's essential for apostolic leadership. You can't develop apostolic intelligence without practicing pioneering. Start small, take risks, learn from failure, and keep pioneering.

### Step 4: Learn Systems Thinking

The fourth step is to learn systems thinking—understanding how things connect, how systems multiply, and how movements spread. This involves studying how successful movements work, understanding reproducible patterns, and learning to think in terms of systems rather than just programs.

Systems thinking is a skill that can be developed. Read about systems theory, study successful movements, and practice thinking in terms of systems. The more you understand how systems work, the better you can create systems for multiplication.

### Step 5: Build Apostolic Teams

The fifth step is to build apostolic teams—teams that include apostolic intelligence along with the other four intelligences. This doesn't mean you need a team full of apostles—it means you need teams where apostolic intelligence is present and active.

When building apostolic teams, look for people who bring apostolic intelligence. Create spaces where apostolic vision can be expressed. Make decisions that require apostolic thinking. The goal is to create teams where apostolic intelligence is valued and activated.

## Case Studies: Apostolic Leadership in Action

To understand how apostolic leadership works in practice, let's look at some historical and contemporary examples where apostolic intelligence was present and active.

### Paul's Apostolic Ministry

Paul's apostolic ministry demonstrates apostolic leadership in action. He didn't just plant churches—he created systems for multiplication. He established networks of churches, trained leaders who could reproduce the work, and created reproducible patterns that enabled the movement to spread across the Roman Empire.

Paul thought in terms of multiplication, not just addition. He saw the big picture—extending the movement from Jerusalem to Rome and beyond. He understood systems—creating networks, establishing relationships, and building structures that enabled multiplication. This is apostolic leadership at work.

### John Wesley's Apostolic Vision

John Wesley's Methodist movement demonstrates apostolic leadership in action. Wesley had apostolic vision that extended the movement into new territories across Britain and America. He created systems for multiplication—the class meeting system, the circuit rider system, the conference system—that enabled the movement to multiply.

Wesley thought in terms of multiplication, not just addition. He saw the big picture—transforming Britain and extending to America. He understood systems—creating reproducible patterns that enabled multiplication. This is apostolic leadership at work.

### The Chinese Underground Church

The Chinese underground church demonstrates apostolic leadership in action. Under severe persecution, apostolic leaders created systems for multiplication that enabled the church to grow from 2 million to 120 million in seventy years. They extended into new contexts, created reproducible patterns, and built networks that enabled multiplication even under persecution.

This is apostolic leadership at work—pioneering under pressure, creating systems for multiplication, and extending the movement into new contexts even when everything is against you.

### Contemporary Apostolic Movements

Today, we see apostolic leadership in action through contemporary movements that have activated apostolic intelligence. These movements demonstrate multiplication, extension, and systems thinking that enables long-term sustainability.

These movements have apostolic leaders who think in terms of multiplication, see the big picture, and understand how systems work. They extend into new contexts, create reproducible patterns, and build structures that enable multiplication. This is apostolic leadership at work.

## Common Challenges in Apostolic Leadership

Developing apostolic leadership isn't without challenges. Let's explore some of the common obstacles and how to overcome them.

### Challenge 1: Institutional Resistance

One common challenge is institutional resistance to apostolic leadership. Institutions are designed for stability, not multiplication. They resist pioneering because it creates change. They resist systems thinking because it challenges existing structures.

The solution is patient persistence. Apostolic leaders need to work within institutions while also creating space for apostolic vision and pioneering. This might mean starting new initiatives, creating parallel structures, or working to transform existing systems over time.

### Challenge 2: Risk Aversion

Another common challenge is risk aversion. Apostolic leadership requires taking risks, breaking new ground, and creating new possibilities. But many people and organizations are risk-averse, preferring safety over pioneering.

The solution is creating a culture that values risk-taking and learning from failure. Help people understand that failure is part of the pioneering process. Create safe spaces to experiment and take risks. Celebrate attempts, not just successes.

### Challenge 3: Lack of Systems Thinking

A third challenge is lack of systems thinking. Many leaders think in terms of programs, not systems. They focus on immediate results, not long-term multiplication. They don't understand how systems work.

The solution is education and development. Help leaders understand systems theory. Study successful movements and their systems. Practice thinking in terms of systems rather than just programs. The more people understand systems, the better they can create systems for multiplication.

### Challenge 4: Multiplication Barriers

A fourth challenge is multiplication barriers—structures, systems, and cultures that prevent multiplication. These barriers might be financial (dependence on central funding), structural (hierarchical control), or cultural (fear of multiplication).

The solution is identifying and removing multiplication barriers. This might mean restructuring finances, decentralizing control, or changing culture. The goal is to create systems that enable multiplication rather than prevent it.
