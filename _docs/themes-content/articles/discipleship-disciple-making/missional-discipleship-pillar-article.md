## Understanding Missional Discipleship

**Missional discipleship** refers to the integration of discipleship and mission that forms disciples who multiply and transform communities. It's not about adding mission to discipleship or discipleship to mission—it's about recognizing that authentic discipleship is inherently missional, and authentic mission requires discipleship. Missional discipleship forms disciples who are sent, who multiply, who transform—disciples who are missional by nature.

The integration of discipleship and mission is crucial. Discipleship without mission becomes formation without purpose—disciples who are formed but not sent. Mission without discipleship becomes activity without formation—missionaries who are sent but not formed. Missional discipleship integrates both—disciples who are formed and sent, who are shaped by Jesus and sent by Jesus, who follow Jesus and join Jesus's mission.

Missional discipleship creates multiplication capacity because it forms disciples who multiply. When disciples are missional, they naturally multiply—making disciples who make disciples, forming communities that form communities, transforming neighborhoods that transform neighborhoods. This multiplication capacity is what makes movements move—missional discipleship that creates exponential growth.

The Great Commission reveals the integration of discipleship and mission. Jesus commands us to "make disciples of all nations"—not just converts, not just church members, but disciples. We make disciples by sending them on mission. We don't first form people and then activate them. We form people by activating them. The Great Commission reveals missional discipleship—discipleship that is inherently missional.

The early church demonstrated this integration. They made disciples by going, teaching, and forming. They didn't separate discipleship from mission—they integrated them. Discipleship happened in mission. Mission required discipleship. This integration created multiplication capacity because disciples were formed to multiply—and they did.
