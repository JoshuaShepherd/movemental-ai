## Understanding Jesus is Lord Movement

**Jesus is Lord movement** refers to communities that make the lordship of Christ the absolute center from which all other themes radiate. This isn't about adding Jesus language to existing structures—it's about making Jesus's lordship the gravitational force that reshapes community identity, priorities, and systems. When Jesus is Lord becomes the orienting reality for a community, everything changes—discipleship becomes central, mission becomes natural, authority structures shift, and movement capacity activates.

The confession "Jesus is Lord" (Greek: *kyrios Iēsous*) was the foundational statement of the early church. It wasn't marginal or optional—it was absolute and central. In a world where Caesar claimed lordship, where competing authorities demanded allegiance, where multiple gods vied for worship, the early church declared: Jesus alone is Lord. This confession eliminated competing claims to authority. It reshaped community around Jesus's lordship. It created the conditions for movement.

When we speak of Jesus is Lord movement, we're talking about communities that understand lordship practically, not just propositionally. We're talking about churches where Jesus's authority shapes decisions, priorities, and structures. We're talking about communities where following Jesus defines identity and activity. We're talking about movements where Jesus centrality creates multiplication capacity.

The Jesus is Lord movement isn't a new innovation or modern strategy—it's the recovery of what has always been foundational. From the early church to historical movements to contemporary expressions, Jesus centrality has always been present whenever God's people have multiplied exponentially. What we're seeing in recent decades is the rediscovery of this centrality after centuries of institutional patterns that marginalized it.

### The Theological Foundation

The confession "Jesus is Lord" connects deeply to the Shema Yisrael—the foundational confession of Israel: "Hear, O Israel: The Lord our God, the Lord is one" (Deuteronomy 6:4). In declaring Jesus as Lord, the early church wasn't abandoning monotheism—they were claiming that Jesus is the one Lord that the Shema points to. This is what scholars call "christocentric monotheism"—the conviction that Jesus is the fulfillment of Israel's confession of the one God.

This theological foundation matters because it eliminates competing claims to lordship. If Jesus is Lord, then Caesar is not. If Jesus is Lord, then consumerism is not. If Jesus is Lord, then nationalism is not. If Jesus is Lord, then personal preference is not. The confession creates clarity about ultimate authority—and when ultimate authority is clear, movement capacity activates.

The biblical foundation for this confession is strong. Paul writes in Romans 10:9: "If you confess with your mouth that Jesus is Lord and believe in your heart that God raised him from the dead, you will be saved." In Philippians 2:11, he declares that "every tongue [will] confess that Jesus Christ is Lord, to the glory of God the Father." In 1 Corinthians 12:3, he states that "no one can say 'Jesus is Lord' except in the Holy Spirit." These passages show that lordship confession wasn't peripheral—it was central to Christian identity and practice.

In the early church, this confession had practical consequences. To declare "Jesus is Lord" was to simultaneously declare "Caesar is not Lord," which put you at odds with the entire power structure of the Roman Empire. Yet it was this costly discipleship that activated apostolic genius in the first centuries. The confession wasn't abstract—it was concrete and costly.
