## The Six Elements of the mDNA Framework

To understand the **mDNA framework** fully, we need to explore each of the six elements that comprise the genetic code. These aren't optional add-ons or enhancement programs—they're the genetic information itself.

### 1. Jesus is Lord: The Gravitational Center

At the heart of the **mDNA framework** lies the simplest and most profound declaration: Jesus is Lord. This isn't religious jargon or theological abstraction. It's the gravitational center that reshapes God's people into a movement.

When Jesus is Lord becomes the orienting reality for a community, everything changes. Discipleship becomes central because following Jesus is what defines us. Mission becomes natural because we're sent by the one who is Lord. Authority structures shift because Jesus is the head of his body. The entire system reorganizes around this single, powerful reality.

In the early church, this wasn't theoretical—it was practical and costly. To declare "Jesus is Lord" was to simultaneously declare "Caesar is not Lord," which put you at odds with the entire power structure of the Roman Empire. Yet it was this costly discipleship that activated the **mDNA framework** in the first centuries.

The Chinese underground church demonstrates the same reality. When everything else was stripped away—buildings, programs, professional clergy—what remained was Jesus. The **mDNA framework** condensed to its essence: Jesus is Lord. And this simple reality was enough to create exponential multiplication.

### 2. Disciple-Making: The Core Function

If Jesus is Lord defines who we are, disciple-making defines what we do. This isn't about running programs or classes—it's about multiplying followers of Jesus who learn to obey everything he commanded.

The **mDNA framework** activates when disciple-making becomes the core function of the community, not a peripheral activity. When every member understands their call to make disciples, when the structures prioritize multiplication over addition, when the culture values obedience over attendance, then the genetic code begins to express itself.

The early church multiplied disciples because making disciples was what they did—naturally, organically, continuously. They didn't have programs because they didn't need programs. The DNA of disciple-making was simply being expressed.

Every significant missional movement in history has been built on a foundation of rigorous disciple-making. The Methodist movement had class meetings and bands. The Chinese underground church had Life Transformation Groups. When movements lose this focus, they decline. When churches prioritize discipleship, they discover that it is the key to multiplication.

### 3. Missional-Incarnational Impulse: The Sent Nature

The **mDNA framework** requires that God's people understand themselves as sent. We're not gathered for our own benefit—we're sent for the sake of the world. This missional impulse, combined with an incarnational approach that engages culture deeply, creates the conditions for movement multiplication.

When the church sees itself as sent, it moves from attractional to incarnational. Instead of asking people to come to us, we go to them. Instead of expecting the culture to adapt to us, we adapt to the culture—just as Jesus did in his incarnation.

This sent, engaging, incarnational approach is encoded in the **mDNA framework**. It's what enabled the early church to engage diverse cultures across the Roman Empire. It's what enables movements today to reach people groups that institutional churches cannot.

The early church demonstrated this impulse. They went out—to synagogues, to marketplaces, to homes, to diverse cultures across the empire. They engaged deeply—learning languages, understanding customs, becoming native to contexts. This missional-incarnational impulse is part of the genetic code.

### 4. APEST Culture: Releasing All of God's People

The **mDNA framework** requires that all five functions of Ephesians 4—Apostle, Prophet, Evangelist, Shepherd, Teacher—are present and operational. This isn't about roles or offices—it's about functions that must be expressed for movements to emerge.

When the fivefold pattern is active, every member of the body is released to function according to their God-given design. Apostles multiply and extend. Prophets speak truth and call for change. Evangelists proclaim good news and gather people. Shepherds care and nurture. Teachers ground and instruct.

The early church didn't have a professional clergy class—they had a fivefold expression of ministry that released the entire body. This is encoded in the **mDNA framework**. Without it, movements cannot multiply because they depend on a few rather than releasing the many.

The Chinese underground church demonstrates this reality. Without professional clergy, every believer functions according to their fivefold design. The genetic code releases all people, not just some people. This is why movements multiply—they leverage the full capacity of the entire body.

### 5. Organic Systems: Decentralized Structures

The **mDNA framework** requires systems that enable multiplication rather than control it. Organic systems are decentralized, distributed, adaptive structures that allow movements to grow organically.

Organic systems distribute authority and enable initiative. They're fractal—the same patterns repeat at every scale. They're resilient—if one part is damaged, the whole continues. They're viral—they spread through relationship and replication rather than organization.

The early church organized organically—households, networks, relationships. The Chinese underground church organized organically—small groups, decentralized leadership, distributed authority. This organic structure is encoded in the **mDNA framework**.

Institutional systems centralize control and require permission. Organic systems distribute authority and enable initiative. This distinction is crucial for movement multiplication. When systems are organic, reproduction happens naturally. When systems are institutional, reproduction is controlled and limited.

### 6. Liminality & Communitas: Threshold Formation

The **mDNA framework** activates when God's people find themselves in liminal spaces—threshold experiences where the old certainties have passed away and the new hasn't yet fully emerged. In these spaces, communities form around shared mission rather than shared comfort—what we call communitas.

Liminality-communitas creates the conditions where the **mDNA framework** can activate. When we're pushed beyond our comfort zones, when we can't rely on old systems, when we're forced to depend on God and one another, then the genetic code begins to express itself.

The early church lived in constant liminality—persecution, marginalization, uncertainty. The Chinese underground church lives in constant liminality—illegality, persecution, risk. This liminal existence creates the communitas that enables the **mDNA framework**.

Regular community often forms around shared interests, comfort, and similarity. Communitas forms around shared purpose, challenge, and mission. This distinction is part of the genetic code. Movements form through communitas, not through comfortable community.

## Practical Application: Implementing the mDNA Framework

Understanding the **mDNA framework** is one thing. Implementing it is another. How do we move from theory to practice? How do we transition from a church that has the framework dormant within it to a church where the **mDNA framework** is actively expressing itself?

### The mDNA Assessment

The first step toward implementing the **mDNA framework** is honest assessment. We need to evaluate how well each of the six elements is currently expressed in our communities. This isn't about judgment or condemnation—it's about diagnosis so we can begin the process of activation.

An mDNA assessment helps us answer critical questions: Is Jesus truly Lord, or are other authorities competing for that position? Is disciple-making our core function, or is it peripheral? Are we missional-incarnational, or are we attractional? Is APEST culture active, or are we functioning with less than fivefold? Are our systems organic, or are they institutional? Are we experiencing liminality-communitas, or are we settled in comfort?

The assessment provides clarity about where we are and where we need to go. It reveals which elements are present and active, which are dormant, and which might be missing entirely. With this clarity, we can begin the work of framework implementation.

### Element Activation

Implementing the **mDNA framework** requires intentional work on each element. We can't skip steps or prioritize some elements over others—they all must be present for the system to emerge. But we can begin where we are, with the elements that are most accessible to us.

If disciple-making is weak, we begin there. We train every member to make disciples. We restructure our activities around multiplication. We create cultures where obedience is valued over attendance.

If mission is weak, we begin there. We shift from attractional to incarnational. We train people to engage their neighborhoods. We release initiatives that go to people rather than asking people to come to us.

If APEST culture is weak, we begin there. We identify and release apostles, prophets, evangelists, shepherds, teachers. We restructure leadership around the fivefold pattern. We create cultures where every gift is valued and expressed.

The key is intentionality combined with patience. The **mDNA framework** doesn't activate overnight. It requires consistent, persistent work on all six elements until they begin to function together as an integrated system.

### Framework Implementation

Implementing the **mDNA framework** requires more than programmatic changes—it requires paradigm shifts. We're not simply adding new activities to old structures. We're fundamentally reimagining how church operates when the **mDNA framework** is active.

This means addressing what I call the "operating system" level, not just the "program" level. Just as you can't run advanced software on an outdated operating system, so you can't activate the **mDNA framework** within institutional structures designed to prevent it.

Framework implementation requires us to examine our assumptions about church, leadership, structure, and mission. It requires us to let go of inherited patterns that keep the framework dormant. It requires us to embrace the forgotten ways that reactivate movement capacity.

### Movement Development

Once the **mDNA framework** begins to activate, it starts creating movement development—the emergence of communities that multiply, extend, and transform contexts. Movement development happens when all six elements work together, creating a system that possesses properties none of the individual elements could produce alone.

Movement development is marked by multiplication, not just addition. It's marked by decentralization, not centralization. It's marked by viral spread, not controlled growth. It's marked by transformation, not just information.

When the **mDNA framework** activates and movement development begins, we see the same patterns that marked the early church and the Chinese underground church: exponential growth, resilient structure, adaptive capacity, and transformative impact.

### Systemic Approach

Implementing the **mDNA framework** requires understanding it as a system rather than a collection of independent elements. This means working on all six elements simultaneously rather than focusing on one at a time.

The framework functions as a system—each element supports and enables the others. When Jesus is Lord becomes central, disciple-making becomes natural. ## Case Studies: mDNA Framework in Action

To understand the **mDNA framework** fully, we need to see it in action. Throughout history, whenever all six elements have been present and active, movements have emerged that transformed their contexts and multiplied beyond human capacity.

### The Early Church: 25,000 to 20 Million

Perhaps the most remarkable example of the **mDNA framework** in action is the early church itself. Starting with perhaps 120 people on the day of Pentecost, the early church grew to an estimated 25,000 by the end of the first century. By the year 310, despite persecution, marginalization, and illegality, the church had grown to approximately 20 million—a growth rate that defies human explanation.

What made this possible? All six elements of the **mDNA framework** were present and active. Jesus is Lord was the costly reality that oriented everything. Disciple-making was the natural activity of every believer. The missional-incarnational impulse sent the church into diverse cultures across the empire. APEST culture released every member to function according to their design. Organic systems enabled multiplication through households and networks. Liminality-communitas formed communities around shared mission in the face of persecution.

The early church didn't have programs, strategies, or professional clergy. They had the **mDNA framework**—and it was enough. All six elements worked together as an integrated system, creating Apostolic Genius that enabled exponential multiplication.

### The Chinese Underground Church: 2 Million to 120 Million

In the twentieth century, we witnessed another remarkable expression of the **mDNA framework**. The Chinese underground church, starting with perhaps 2 million believers in 1949, grew to an estimated 120 million by the turn of the century—all while operating illegally, facing persecution, and having no access to buildings, programs, or professional leadership.

How did this happen? Again, all six elements of the **mDNA framework** were present. Jesus is Lord meant risking everything. Disciple-making happened through relationships and obedience. The missional-incarnational impulse engaged Chinese culture deeply. APEST culture released every believer to function. Organic systems enabled decentralized multiplication. Liminality-communitas formed communities in the face of constant threat.

The Chinese church didn't have what we would consider "church" structures. They had the **mDNA framework**—and it was more than enough. All six elements worked together as an integrated system, enabling exponential multiplication despite persecution.

### The Methodist Movement: Wesley's Impact

The Methodist movement under John Wesley provides another example of the **mDNA framework** activating. Starting with small groups meeting in homes, the movement grew to transform Britain and influence the world. What made it work?

All six elements were present. Jesus is Lord was the orienting reality. Disciple-making happened through class meetings and bands. The missional-incarnational impulse engaged the working classes that the established church had abandoned. APEST culture released lay preachers and class leaders. Organic systems enabled multiplication through circuits and societies. Liminality-communitas formed communities among those on the margins.

The Methodist movement didn't work because of Wesley's brilliance alone—it worked because the **mDNA framework** was active. All six elements worked together as an integrated system, creating movement capacity that transformed contexts.

### Contemporary Movements: Church Multiplication Associates

Today, we're seeing the **mDNA framework** activate in contemporary contexts. Church Multiplication Associates (CMA) demonstrates all six elements working together as an integrated system. Simple churches multiply across contexts. Disciple-making happens through Life Transformation Groups. The missional-incarnational impulse engages diverse people groups. ## Common Challenges in Implementing the mDNA Framework

While the **mDNA framework** is latent in every church, implementing it isn't automatic. We face real challenges that can keep the framework dormant. Understanding these challenges helps us address them intentionally.

### Element Imbalance

One of the most common challenges is element imbalance—when some elements of the **mDNA framework** are active while others are dormant. Perhaps a church focuses heavily on disciple-making but lacks missional engagement. Or maybe mission is active but APEST culture is weak. Or organic systems exist but liminality-communitas is absent.

Element imbalance prevents the **mDNA framework** from activating because the system requires all six elements to function together. When elements are out of balance, the system cannot emerge. We must work intentionally to activate dormant elements while maintaining active ones.

### Missing Elements

A more serious challenge is when elements are missing entirely. Some churches function with only three or four of the six elements active. They might have Jesus is Lord and disciple-making, but lack missional impulse and APEST culture. Or they might have mission and APEST, but lack organic systems and liminality-communitas.

Missing elements prevent the **mDNA framework** from activating. We must identify which elements are absent and work intentionally to introduce them. This requires honest assessment and courageous action.

### Activation Barriers

Even when we understand what needs to happen, we face barriers to activation. Institutional structures resist organic systems. Comfortable communities resist liminality-communitas. Professional clergy resist APEST culture that releases all people. Attractional mindsets resist missional-incarnational impulses.

### Framework Complexity

Another challenge is framework complexity. The **mDNA framework** can seem overwhelming—six elements, each requiring attention, all needing to work together. Where do we start? How do we manage it all? What if we get it wrong?

The complexity is real, but it's also manageable. We start where we are. We work on one element at a time while keeping the system in view. We trust that as elements activate, they begin to support one another. We remember that the **mDNA framework** is about systems, and systems have emergent properties that help us along the way.

### Individual vs. Systemic

Perhaps the most significant challenge is treating the **mDNA framework** as a collection of individual elements rather than as an integrated system. When we focus on one element at a time without considering how they function together, we miss the systemic nature of the framework.

The **mDNA framework** functions as a system—each element supports and enables the others. We must work on all six elements simultaneously, understanding how they integrate and interact. This systemic approach is what enables Apostolic Genius to emerge.
