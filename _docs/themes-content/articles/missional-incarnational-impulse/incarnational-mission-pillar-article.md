## Understanding Incarnational Mission

**Incarnational mission** refers to Jesus's pattern of embodied presence and contextual engagement. It's not about adding incarnation to mission or making mission incarnational—it's about recognizing that authentic mission is inherently incarnational, following Jesus's pattern of taking on flesh and dwelling among us. Incarnational mission forms disciples who are present—present in their contexts, present with people, present as good news in flesh and blood.

The incarnational focus is crucial. Mission without incarnation becomes activity that happens from a distance, from outside, from above—mission that is foreign, inaccessible, disconnected. Mission with incarnation becomes activity that happens from within, from inside, from below—mission that is native, accessible, connected. This incarnational capacity is what makes movements move—incarnational mission that creates transformation.

Incarnational mission creates transformation capacity because it forms disciples who are present. When disciples are present in their contexts, present with people, present as good news in flesh and blood, they naturally transform—transforming neighborhoods, workplaces, communities. This transformation capacity is what makes movements move—incarnational mission that creates exponential impact.

### The Six Practices of Incarnational Mission

Incarnational mission involves six key practices: **Presence** (full identification with culture), **Proximity** (geographic and relational closeness), **Powerlessness** (humility and vulnerability), **Prevenience** (recognizing God's work already present), **Passion** (embracing redemptive suffering), and **Proclamation** (sharing the good news). These practices enable the church to engage authentically in the "nooks and crannies" of society, becoming good news in flesh and blood as well as word.

**Presence** means full identification with culture—being present in our contexts, not distant from them. Presence creates transformation capacity because disciples who are present naturally transform.

**Proximity** means geographic and relational closeness—being near people, not far from them. Proximity creates transformation capacity because disciples who are near naturally transform.

**Powerlessness** means humility and vulnerability—being weak, not strong. Powerlessness creates transformation capacity because disciples who are humble and vulnerable naturally transform.

**Prevenience** means recognizing God's work already present—seeing what God is already doing, not just what we want to do. Prevenience creates transformation capacity because disciples who recognize God's work naturally transform.

**Passion** means embracing redemptive suffering—suffering with people, not avoiding suffering. Passion creates transformation capacity because disciples who embrace redemptive suffering naturally transform.

**Proclamation** means sharing the good news—speaking the gospel, not just being the gospel. Proclamation creates transformation capacity because disciples who proclaim the good news naturally transform.

These six practices work together to create incarnational mission—mission that is present, native, connected. When all six practices are active, incarnational mission emerges—and transformation capacity is created.
