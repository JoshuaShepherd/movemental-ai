When Jesus said, "As the Father has sent me, I am sending you," he was speaking to all his disciples, not just a select few. We are all sent, and we are all missionaries in our contexts.

Sentness is not a special calling for a few but the normal calling for all. It is not an optional extra for the committed, but the essential identity of every follower of Jesus. We are the church before we do church. Our being sent is the basis of our doing church. This sentness creates the missional impulse that drives the church outward, the force that multiplies communities, the energy that transforms the world.

The early church understood this sentness. They didn't see themselves as a gathered community that occasionally did mission; they saw themselves as a sent people who gathered for encouragement and equipping. They were sent into their neighborhoods, their workplaces, their social spaces. They were sent into their cities, their regions, their world. This sentness created the multiplication that characterized the early church movement.

It is the recognition that we are all apostles in our contexts, all sent ones, all missionaries. ## The Framework Foundation: Sentness as Apostolic Identity

Sentness begins with a fundamental understanding of the missio Dei—the mission of God. Mission is not something the church does; it is something God does, and the church participates in it. God is a missionary God, and the church exists as a missionary people. This is not a secondary aspect of the church's identity—it is the church's identity.

When Jesus said, "As the Father has sent me, I am sending you," he was not only telling us where we are being sent, but also how we are to be sent. We are being sent here, but we are also being told how we are to be sent—incarnationally. Once again, as Christ-ian people, we are bound to follow in the way and pattern of Jesus.

The apostolic identity emerges from this understanding of sentness. The word "apostle" comes from the Greek word apostolos, which means "one who is sent." Every Christian is an apostle in their context—a sent one, a missionary, an agent of the King. This is not a special calling for a few but the normal calling for all. We are all sent, and we are all apostles in our contexts.

The early church demonstrates this apostolic identity at scale. They didn't see themselves as a gathered community that occasionally did mission; they saw themselves as a sent people who gathered for encouragement and equipping. They were sent into their neighborhoods, their workplaces, their social spaces. They were sent into their cities, their regions, their world. This sentness created the multiplication that characterized the early church movement.

Sentness is not something new to be created but something latent to be recovered. It is encoded in the very being of the church, a gift from God that has been forgotten but not lost. The future lies in the womb of the present; the potential for apostolic movement already exists within every expression of church, waiting to be awakened.

The missional impulse emerges from this understanding of sentness. We are sent by Jesus into the world, and this sentness creates the missional impulse that drives us outward. We don't just go—we are sent. We don't just do mission—we are mission. This sentness creates the missional impulse that multiplies movements.

## Practical Application: Living Out Sentness

How do we live out sentness in our communities? The practices of sentness are not complicated, but they are costly. They require us to shift from seeing ourselves as gathered communities to seeing ourselves as sent people, from doing mission occasionally to being mission always, from having a mission to being sent.

The first practice is understanding that we are sent. Every Christian is a sent one—a missionary in their context. This is not a special calling for a few but the normal calling for all. When Jesus said, "As the Father has sent me, I am sending you," he was speaking to all his disciples, not just a select few. We are all sent, and we are all missionaries in our contexts.

This understanding transforms how we see ourselves and our communities. We are not consumers of religious services but sent ones on mission. We are not attendees of church events but missionaries in our neighborhoods. We are not members of an institution but agents of the King in our contexts. We are the church before we do church. Our being sent is the basis of our doing church.

The second practice is identifying our sending contexts. We are all sent, but we are sent to different contexts. Some of us are sent to our neighborhoods, some to our workplaces, some to our social spaces. Some of us are sent to our cities, some to our regions, some to the ends of the earth. We need to identify where we are sent and live as sent ones in those contexts.

This identification requires prayerful discernment and relational engagement. We need to ask: Where has God placed me? What contexts am I called to engage? What people am I called to serve? What neighborhoods am I called to transform? Where is God sending me?

The third practice is living as sent ones in our contexts. Sentness is not just an identity we claim; it's a reality we live. We need to live as sent ones in our neighborhoods, our workplaces, our social spaces. We need to be present, engaged, and active in the contexts where we're sent.

This living requires intentionality and consistency. We need to be intentional about being present in our contexts, about building relationships, about engaging culture, about serving our neighbors. We need to be consistent in our presence, our engagement, and our service. We need to live as sent ones, not just visit as guests.

The fourth practice is gathering for encouragement and equipping. Sent people need to gather, not to escape from mission, but to be encouraged and equipped for mission. We gather to worship, to learn, to encourage one another, to be equipped for the mission we're sent to. We gather as sent people, not as escapees from the world.

This gathering requires a shift in our understanding of church. Church is not a place we go to escape from the world; it's a people we gather with to be equipped for the world. We gather as sent people, not as gathered people who occasionally go out. We gather to be sent, not to escape.

The fifth practice is multiplying sent ones. Sentness is not just about us being sent; it's about us sending others. We need to multiply sent ones, to raise up missionaries in every context, to activate apostolic identity in every member. We need to create environments where sentness is the norm, not the exception.

This multiplication requires intentional leadership development and apostolic activation. We need to identify and develop apostolic leaders who can activate and release sentness throughout the organization. We need to create pathways for every member to discover their apostolic identity and live as sent ones in their contexts.

The sixth practice is measuring sentness rather than attendance. Sentness measures success not by how many people come to our events, but by how many people are living as sent ones in their contexts. We measure by mission, not by maintenance. We measure by multiplication, not by addition.

This measurement requires us to rethink our metrics. We don't measure by attendance, buildings, or budgets. We measure by sentness—how many people are living as sent ones, how many contexts are being engaged, how many neighborhoods are being transformed. This sentness focus creates the multiplication that characterizes genuine movements.

## Case Studies: Sentness in Action

When we look at history, we see sentness in action. Jesus's ministry demonstrates this sentness at its finest. He was sent by the Father into the world, and he sent his disciples into the world. He didn't just teach them; he sent them. He didn't just equip them; he commissioned them. He didn't just gather them; he scattered them.

The early church demonstrates sentness at scale. When persecution scattered the church from Jerusalem, they didn't see it as a tragedy but as an opportunity. They were being sent into new contexts, new cultures, new regions. They understood that they were sent ones, apostles in their contexts, missionaries to their neighborhoods. This sentness created the multiplication that spread the gospel throughout the Roman Empire.

The Celtic missionary movement provides a powerful historical example. Celtic missionaries didn't wait for people to come to them; they went out into the world. They understood that they were sent ones, apostles to the cultures they encountered. They lived as sent ones, and the movement multiplied across Europe.

The Methodist movement under John Wesley demonstrates sentness at scale. Wesley didn't create a program—he started a movement. He didn't build an institution—he multiplied sent ones. He sent people into their contexts, and they lived as missionaries in their neighborhoods. By the end of Wesley's lifetime, one in thirty people in Britain had become Methodist, and the movement had spread to America. What was the key? Sentness—the understanding that we are all sent ones, all missionaries, all apostles in our contexts.

The Chinese underground church provides a powerful contemporary example. When persecution forced them underground, they discovered the essence of sentness. Every believer was expected to be a sent one, a missionary in their context. Every believer was expected to plant churches. Every church was expected to multiply. Sentness was not optional—it was the core identity that multiplied the movement.

Contemporary examples abound. We know of communities that have adopted sentness as their core identity. They understand that they are sent people, not gathered people. They live as sent ones in their contexts, and they multiply sent ones throughout their communities. They measure success not by attendance but by sentness, and they see exponential growth.

One community we know has embedded sentness into their organizational culture. Every member is expected to understand their identity as a sent one. Every member is expected to live as a missionary in their context. Every member is expected to multiply sent ones. Sentness is not optional—it is the core identity that multiplies the movement.

## Common Challenges to Sentness

The path of sentness is not without obstacles. Identity barriers represent perhaps the greatest challenge. We have tended to see sentness as a special calling for a few, not the normal calling for all. We have created a clergy-laity divide that makes sentness the domain of professionals, not the identity of all believers. This barrier makes sentness difficult because we're trying to activate something we don't believe is true of us.

The institutional resistance comes from our tendency to see church as a gathered community rather than a sent people. We have built institutions that gather people rather than sending them. We have created programs that attract people rather than activating sentness. This resistance makes sentness difficult because our structures work against it rather than for it.

The sentness transformation challenges our assumptions about what church looks like. We have measured success by attendance, buildings, and budgets. But sentness measures success by mission, multiplication, and transformation. This transformation requires us to rethink our metrics and our methods.

The apostolic activation challenge requires us to move beyond the clergy-laity divide to activate the ministry of all believers. We need to recognize that every member is a sent one, every member is a missionary, every member is an apostle in their context. This activation is essential for sentness because it enables every member to live as a sent one.

The measurement transformation challenges our assumptions about what success looks like. We have measured success by how many people come to our events. But sentness measures success by how many people are living as sent ones in their contexts. This transformation requires us to rethink our metrics and our methods.
