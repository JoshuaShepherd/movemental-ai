# Disciplism: Implementation Guide

**Concept**: Disciplism: Reimagining Evangelism Through the Lens of Discipleship  
**Purpose**: Step-by-step guide for implementing disciplism  
**Target Audience**: Church leaders, practitioners, implementers  
**Last Updated**: December 2025

---

## Overview

This guide provides practical steps for implementing disciplism in your church or ministry context. Disciplism represents a fundamental shift from treating evangelism as event-based activity to understanding evangelism as integrated within the lifelong process of making disciples. This implementation guide walks through assessment, planning, and step-by-step implementation, addressing common pitfalls and providing success indicators. Whether you're beginning from scratch or transitioning from a traditional approach, this guide offers a roadmap for making disciple-making central to your mission.

## Assessment

### Current State Assessment

**Questions to Consider**:

1. **How do we currently understand evangelism?** Is it primarily event-based, program-driven, or relationship-oriented? What's our primary method for sharing the gospel?

2. **What's the relationship between evangelism and discipleship in our context?** Do we treat them as separate activities? Is discipleship optional follow-up to evangelism?

3. **When does discipleship begin?** Do we only disciple people after they've made a decision? Or do we invite people into formation before conversion?

4. **What happens to new converts?** Do they enter a discipleship process? Is it mandatory or optional? How long does it last?

5. **Are we producing disciples who multiply?** Do our disciples make disciples of others? Or do we primarily add people through attraction and programs?

6. **How do we measure success?** Do we measure decisions, attendance, or discipleship depth? What metrics do we use?

7. **What's our approach to pre-Christians?** Do we invite them into our community and formation process? Or do we wait until they convert?

### Assessment Tools

#### Tool 1: Disciplism Readiness Assessment

**Purpose**: Evaluates current understanding and practice of disciplism principles

**How to Use**: 
- Complete individually or as leadership team
- Rate each statement 1-5 (1=Strongly Disagree, 5=Strongly Agree)
- Areas scoring 3 or below indicate areas needing development

**Statements**:
1. We understand evangelism as occurring within discipleship, not as separate activity
2. We invite pre-Christians into formation and community before conversion
3. Discipleship is central to our mission, not optional follow-up
4. We measure success by discipleship depth and multiplication, not just decisions
5. Our approach confronts consumerism rather than catering to it
6. We prioritize process-oriented formation over event-based conversion
7. Discipleship is relational and contextual, not programmatic
8. We expect and equip disciples to make disciples of others

**Scoring**: 
- 32-40: Ready for advanced implementation
- 24-31: Good foundation, ready for implementation
- 16-23: Some understanding, needs development before implementation
- 8-15: Significant work needed on understanding and foundation

#### Tool 2: Current Practice Inventory

**Purpose**: Documents current evangelism and discipleship practices

**How to Use**:
- List all current evangelism activities and methods
- List all current discipleship activities and methods
- Note how they relate (or don't relate) to each other
- Identify gaps and opportunities for integration

**Inventory Areas**:
- Evangelism activities: What do we do? When? How often?
- Discipleship activities: What do we do? Who participates? When does it begin?
- Integration points: Where do evangelism and discipleship connect?
- Gaps: What's missing? What's disconnected?

**Scoring**: Review inventory to identify integration opportunities and gaps

### Readiness Checklist

- [ ] Leadership team understands disciplism principles
- [ ] Current practices have been assessed and documented
- [ ] Team recognizes need for change (not just adding another program)
- [ ] Key leaders are committed to making discipleship central
- [ ] Resources are available for implementation (time, people, training)
- [ ] Clear communication plan for introducing change
- [ ] Examples and models have been studied (Soma, NewThing, etc.)
- [ ] Biblical foundations are clear (Great Commission, etc.)

## Step-by-Step Implementation

### Phase 1: Foundation and Understanding (Months 1-2)

**Duration**: 2 months  
**Goal**: Establish understanding and commitment to disciplism principles

#### Step 1.1: Leadership Development

- **Action**: Leadership team studies disciplism togetherâ€”read key resources, discuss principles, share observations
- **Resources Needed**: *Disciplism* book, related articles, time for discussion
- **Expected Outcome**: Leadership alignment on disciplism principles and commitment to implementation

#### Step 1.2: Current State Assessment

- **Action**: Complete assessment tools, document current practices, identify gaps
- **Resources Needed**: Assessment tools, time for reflection and documentation
- **Expected Outcome**: Clear picture of current state and areas needing change

#### Step 1.3: Vision Casting

- **Action**: Share disciplism vision with broader leadership and key stakeholders
- **Resources Needed**: Communication materials, meeting time, vision statement
- **Expected Outcome**: Broader understanding and buy-in for disciplism approach

#### Step 1.4: Study Examples

- **Action**: Learn from churches practicing disciplism (Soma, NewThing, 3DM, etc.)
- **Resources Needed**: Case studies, visits or conversations with practitioners
- **Expected Outcome**: Practical understanding of disciplism in action

### Phase 2: Pilot and Experimentation (Months 3-5)

**Duration**: 3 months  
**Goal**: Begin implementing disciplism in pilot contexts

#### Step 2.1: Identify Pilot Groups

- **Action**: Select small groups or contexts to begin implementing disciplism principles
- **Resources Needed**: Willing leaders, appropriate contexts (small groups, neighborhoods, etc.)
- **Expected Outcome**: 2-4 pilot contexts ready to experiment

#### Step 2.2: Train Pilot Leaders

- **Action**: Provide training on disciplism principles and practices for pilot leaders
- **Resources Needed**: Training materials, time for training, ongoing coaching
- **Expected Outcome**: Equipped leaders ready to practice disciplism

#### Step 2.3: Begin Pre-Conversion Discipleship

- **Action**: Pilot groups invite pre-Christians into formation process, begin journeying together
- **Resources Needed**: Relationships with pre-Christians, willingness to experiment
- **Expected Outcome**: Pre-Christians engaged in formation process

#### Step 2.4: Document and Learn

- **Action**: Regularly document what's happening, what's working, what's challenging
- **Resources Needed**: Reflection time, documentation system, learning mindset
- **Expected Outcome**: Clear learning about what works in your context

### Phase 3: Expansion and Integration (Months 6-12)

**Duration**: 6 months  
**Goal**: Expand disciplism throughout church, integrate with existing structures

#### Step 3.1: Share Learnings

- **Action**: Share pilot learnings with broader church, celebrate successes, address challenges
- **Resources Needed**: Communication channels, celebration venues, honest assessment
- **Expected Outcome**: Broader church awareness and interest in disciplism

#### Step 3.2: Expand to More Groups

- **Action**: Invite more groups/leaders to begin practicing disciplism
- **Resources Needed**: Trained leaders, support systems, ongoing coaching
- **Expected Outcome**: Growing number of groups practicing disciplism

#### Step 3.3: Integrate with Existing Structures

- **Action**: Align existing programs and structures with disciplism principles
- **Resources Needed**: Willingness to adjust, creative problem-solving, patience
- **Expected Outcome**: Existing structures serving disciplism goals

#### Step 3.4: Develop Multiplication Patterns

- **Action**: Focus on helping disciples make disciples of others, creating multiplication
- **Resources Needed**: Training on multiplication, accountability systems, celebration
- **Expected Outcome**: Disciples beginning to disciple others

### Phase 4: Deepening and Multiplication (Months 13-18)

**Duration**: 6 months  
**Goal**: Deepen practice and see multiplication

#### Step 4.1: Deepen Formation

- **Action**: Strengthen discipleship depth, address areas needing development
- **Resources Needed**: Advanced training, coaching, resources for deeper formation
- **Expected Outcome**: Increased discipleship depth and maturity

#### Step 4.2: Celebrate Multiplication

- **Action**: Recognize and celebrate when disciples make disciples
- **Resources Needed**: Celebration systems, storytelling, recognition
- **Expected Outcome**: Culture that values and expects multiplication

#### Step 4.3: Address Challenges

- **Action**: Honestly address ongoing challenges, adjust approaches as needed
- **Resources Needed**: Humility, problem-solving, support systems
- **Expected Outcome**: Ongoing improvement and adaptation

#### Step 4.4: Establish Sustainable Systems

- **Action**: Create systems that support disciplism long-term
- **Resources Needed**: Systems thinking, organizational development
- **Expected Outcome**: Disciplism embedded in church culture and systems

## Common Pitfalls

### Pitfall 1: Making It Too Complicated

**Description**: Over-programming disciplism, turning it into another complex system

**Why It Happens**: We're used to programs and systems, so we try to systematize disciplism

**How to Avoid**:
- Keep it simple: relationship, journey, formation
- Focus on principles, not programs
- Start small and organic
- Learn from examples but adapt to your context

**If You're Already There**: Simplify. Strip away complexity. Return to basic principles: make disciples who make disciples.

### Pitfall 2: Neglecting the Gospel

**Description**: In focusing on discipleship, some worry we're not sharing the gospel clearly

**Why It Happens**: Misunderstanding that disciplism de-emphasizes evangelism

**How to Avoid**:
- Remember: disciplism reframes evangelism, doesn't eliminate it
- Share the gospel naturally within relationships
- Train people to share the gospel relationally
- Trust the Holy Spirit to work through relationships

**If You're Already There**: Increase gospel clarity within discipleship relationships. Train people to share the gospel naturally.

### Pitfall 3: Being Too Rigid

**Description**: Applying disciplism principles rigidly without flexibility for different contexts

**Why It Happens**: Trying to replicate models exactly rather than adapting principles

**How to Avoid**:
- Focus on principles, not methods
- Adapt to your context
- Allow for different approaches
- Learn from mistakes and adjust

**If You're Already There**: Increase flexibility. Allow experimentation. Focus on outcomes (disciples making disciples) more than methods.

### Pitfall 4: Separating from Mission

**Description**: Disciplism happening only in safe Christian environments, not in mission contexts

**Why It Happens**: Comfort and safety can override missional impulse

**How to Avoid**:
- Keep disciplism connected to mission
- Practice disciplism in missional contexts
- Live among those who don't know Jesus
- Remember: missional-incarnational impulse is key

**If You're Already There**: Intentionally move disciplism into missional contexts. Connect formation to mission.

### Pitfall 5: Making It Optional

**Description**: Treating disciplism as another program option rather than central framework

**Why It Happens**: Difficulty changing existing structures and priorities

**How to Avoid**:
- Make disciple-making the organizing principle
- Align all activities with disciplism goals
- Make it clear this is central, not optional
- Lead by example

**If You're Already There**: Clarify priorities. Make difficult decisions. Align structures with disciplism principles.

### Pitfall 6: Expecting Immediate Results

**Description**: Expecting quick results and giving up when change takes time

**Why It Happens**: Cultural pressure for quick results, impatience with process

**How to Avoid**:
- Understand disciplism is long-term strategy
- Set realistic expectations
- Celebrate small wins
- Remember: formation takes time

**If You're Already There**: Adjust expectations. Celebrate progress. Focus on faithfulness over speed.

## Success Indicators

### Short-Term Indicators (3-6 months)

- Leaders understand and can articulate disciplism principles
- Pilot groups have begun practicing disciplism
- Pre-Christians are engaged in formation process
- Some people are being discipled who weren't before
- Conversations shifting toward discipleship language

### Medium-Term Indicators (6-12 months)

- Multiple groups practicing disciplism
- Clear examples of pre-conversion discipleship happening
- Disciples showing growth in formation
- Some multiplication beginning (disciples making disciples)
- Church culture shifting toward discipleship priority

### Long-Term Indicators (12-18 months)

- Disciplism is embedded in church culture
- Disciples are making disciples consistently
- Multiplication patterns emerging
- Depth of discipleship increasing
- Movement dynamics appearing (growth through multiplication)

### Quantitative Measures

- Number of people in discipleship relationships
- Number of pre-Christians in formation process
- Number of disciples making disciples
- Multiplication of groups/communities
- Growth through multiplication (not just addition)

### Qualitative Measures

- Quality of discipleship (depth, obedience, multiplication)
- Integration of evangelism and discipleship
- Alignment with disciplism principles
- Culture shift toward disciple-making
- Movement dynamics (multiplication, reproduction)

## Case Studies

### Case Study 1: Soma Communities (Tacoma, WA)

**Context**: Led by Jeff Vanderstelt, practices lifelong gospel integration

**Approach**: In pre-conversion phase, discipleship includes evangelism; after conversion, emphasis shifts but journey continues

**Results**: Deep formation, multiplication, missional engagement

**Key Learnings**: Process-oriented, relationship-based, gospel-integrated throughout

### Case Study 2: Community Christian Church & NewThing Network

**Context**: Founded by Dave and Jon Ferguson, Chicago area

**Approach**: View everyone as returning to God, design ministries for disciple-making, use "apprenticeship" language

**Results**: 50,000+ people, rapid growth, deep formation, multiplication

**Key Learnings**: Apprenticeship language, disciple-making design, multiplication focus

### Case Study 3: Early Church Catechumenate

**Context**: 100-300 A.D., early church practice

**Approach**: Seekers (catechumens) required to demonstrate commitment by living Jesus' ethics under sponsor guidance, often years before baptism

**Results**: Transformed societies, deep formation, multiplied movement

**Key Learnings**: Pre-conversion formation, commitment required, transformation focus
