## Executive Summary

The Starfish vs. Spider metaphor provides a powerful framework for understanding organizational structure and leadership models. The Spider represents centralized organizations with a clear head or hierarchy—if you remove the head, the organization dies. The Starfish represents decentralized networks where power is distributed—if you cut a starfish in half, it regenerates into two starfish. This metaphor, originally from Ori Brafman's work, has been applied to the church context to help leaders understand the difference between centralized (Spider) and decentralized (Starfish) expressions of church, and the importance of moving toward more Starfish-like structures for multiplication and movement.

At the heart of this concept is the recognition that the early church operated as a Starfish—decentralized, reproducing, resilient. Most contemporary Western churches operate as Spiders—centralized, dependent on leadership hierarchy, vulnerable to failure when key leaders are removed. The metaphor helps us see that multiplication requires decentralization, and that the locus of power matters deeply for organizational health and reproduction.

**Key Points:**
- Spider organizations have centralized power; remove the head and they die
- Starfish organizations have distributed power; they regenerate and multiply
- The early church was Starfish-like; most contemporary churches are Spider-like
- Multiplication requires decentralized structures and distributed leadership
- The continuum includes hybrids: Spiderfish and Starder organizations
- Starfish organizations are flexible, adaptive, and multiplication-oriented
- The center exists to empower the margins, enabling "good things to run wild"

### Alan's Definition

The Starfish vs. Spider metaphor distinguishes between two fundamentally different organizational structures. The Spider represents centralized organizations where power, decision-making, and information flow from a central head or hierarchy. Remove that head, and the organization collapses. The Starfish represents decentralized networks where power is distributed throughout the system. Damage one part, and the system regenerates and multiplies.

In the church context, Spider churches are oriented around one location, one congregation, one gathering, under one leadership head. The locus of power is centralized, flowing down like a strand of Christmas lights—if the top bulb goes out, everything below goes dark. Starfish churches are decentralized networks of multiplying disciples, leaders, and microchurches. Power is distributed like the internet, with multiple nodes and multiple directions of flow.

This distinction matters because multiplication requires decentralization. Centralized structures grow by addition; decentralized structures grow by multiplication. The early church was fundamentally Starfish-like, spreading through decentralized networks. Most contemporary Western churches are Spider-like, dependent on centralized leadership and vulnerable when key leaders fail.
