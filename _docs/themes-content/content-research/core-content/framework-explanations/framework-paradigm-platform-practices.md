# Paradigm-Platform-Practices Architecture: The Three Layers of Transformation

## Framework Overview

The Paradigm-Platform-Practices framework provides a three-level architecture for understanding and facilitating organizational transformation. It recognizes that change must happen at multiple levels simultaneously—in how we think (paradigm), how we feel and organize (platform), and how we act (practices). All three must be aligned for transformation to take root and endure.

This framework recognizes that transformation requires engaging the whole person and the whole organization. You cannot change practices without changing culture (platform), and you cannot change culture without changing thinking (paradigm). Conversely, paradigm shift without platform and practice changes will remain theoretical and unembodied.

The framework uses the metaphor of seed, tree, and forest:
- **Paradigm** is the seed (the mind) - contains the potential for the whole
- **Platform** is the tree (the soul) - contains the potential for the forest
- **Practices** are the forest (the will) - demonstrates the potential of the seed

In *On the Verge*, this framework is presented as Imagine (paradigm), Shift (platform/ethos), and Innovate (practices). The process recognizes that lasting change requires engaging mind, soul, and will—the whole person and the whole organization.

## Component Parts

### Paradigm: The Organizational Mind

Paradigm is the organizational mind—the identity, story, and defining ideas that shape how an organization understands itself and its purpose. Paradigms consist of:

**Mental Maps**: Meta-ideas, beliefs, competencies that guide thinking and behavior. These are the foundational concepts on which subsequent thinking is built. Meta-ideas are like levers that can change everything—they have high leverage in the system.

**Organizational DNA**: The genetics that replicate throughout the system. Like biological DNA, organizational DNA:
- Codes information for transmission of inherited traits
- Is self-replicating
- Carries vital information for healthy reproduction
- When it mutates, affects the integrity of the whole system

**Primary/Controlling Metaphors of Identity**: How the organization describes itself (e.g., "the body of Christ," "church as organism," "network," "movement").

**The Heroic Story**: The primary stories or origin stories that are central to identity—what the organization values and who it celebrates.

**The Defining Idea**: The core driver of the organization. For instance, the defining idea of MLC is that of an apostolic movement.

**Sense of Meaning**: The unique identity, purpose, and direction that gives the organization its ultimate sense of purpose (telos).

Paradigm shift requires:
- Leadership unity
- Apostolic imagination
- Perseverance
- Telling alternate stories
- Letting things die
- Seeing reality clearly

### Platform: The Organizational Soul

Platform is the organizational soul—the culture and structures that shape collective behavior. The platform is made up of two essential components:

**Culture Components:**
- **Theological Distinctives**: Core theological beliefs that shape identity and practice
- **Core Values**: What the organization truly values, made explicit and concrete
- **Shared Vocabulary**: Common language that carries meaning and shapes discourse
- **Heroes**: Exemplars worth following and imitating
- **Celebration of Behavior**: Recognizing and rewarding positive behavior
- **Branding**: How the organization is perceived externally

**Movemental Structures:**
- **Relational Structures**: How people connect, grow, and lead within the organization
- **Resource Structures**: How resources flow, including budgets, calendars, facilities
- **Measurement Structures**: What gets measured and rewarded—the metrics that indicate success

Platforms architect organizational behavior through the path of least resistance. Once a structure exists, energy moves through that structure by the path of least resistance. The structure of the organization determines the behavior of those that are part of the organization.

Platform alignment is critical. When culture and structures are aligned, they work together to shape behavior. When they are misaligned, the organization is out of sync with itself, leading to dysfunction.

### Practices: The Organizational Will

Practices are the organizational will—the observable, repeatable behaviors that embody values and create culture. Practices make transformation tangible and embodied.

**From Core Values to Core Practices**: Rather than simply writing, talking, teaching, and lecturing about culture and values, develop common practices that will give people pathways to act their way into a new way of thinking.

**Movemental Rhythms**: Repetitive actions that occur in a uniform and recurring pattern. Rhythms are fundamentally connected to time intervals (daily, weekly, monthly actions).

**Algorithms**: Built on repetitive actions like rhythms, but algorithms are fundamentally about learning from the repetitive action in order to accomplish a purpose. Algorithms:
- Posture the organization for ongoing metanoia
- Refine the organizational vision, mission, and strategy
- Help the organization learn and discern future decisions
- Help "what wants to happen" emerge
- Are fundamentally movemental

**Tools**: Physical, training, and communication tools that fractalize DNA throughout the organization. Tools:
- Build trust in the organization/platform
- Add value to members/participants
- Fractalize DNA and meta-ideas throughout the organization
- Remove friction, enabling scale
- Add capacity to the church/organization
- Foster innovation and partnership

**Habits and Muscle Memory**: Deeply ingrained rhythms in which culture becomes deposited in people in the form of dispositions, capacities, and inclinations. Habits consist of three elements: a cue, a routine, and a reward.

## System Dynamics

The three levels work together as an integrated system:

1. **Paradigm informs Platform**: The organizational mind (paradigm) shapes the organizational soul (platform). Culture and structures must align with the paradigm.

2. **Platform enables Practices**: The organizational soul (platform) creates the environment where practices can flourish. Practices must align with culture and structures.

3. **Practices embody Paradigm**: The organizational will (practices) makes the paradigm tangible and visible. Practices demonstrate what the organization truly values.

4. **All three must align**: Misalignment at any level undermines transformation. For example:
   - Paradigm says "missional" but platform structures are attractional = misalignment
   - Platform values "discipleship" but practices don't require it = misalignment
   - Practices try to be "missional" but paradigm is still institutional = misalignment

5. **Fractal organizing**: The DNA from the paradigm (seed) must fractalize through the platform (tree) and into the practices (forest). The pattern repeats at every scale.

6. **Sequential dependency**: Each phase is critical to the viability of the next. Inadequate framing of the paradigm will undermine the movement's platform. Failure to create the right platform will likewise undermine viability later on. Failure to create accessible practices will prevent the movement from going to scale.

## Activation Principles

The framework activates when:

1. **All three levels are addressed**: Transformation requires engaging paradigm, platform, and practices together.

2. **Alignment is maintained**: Culture and structures (platform) must align with thinking (paradigm), and practices must align with both.

3. **Sequence is followed**: Start with paradigm, then build platform, then develop practices. Each builds on the previous.

4. **Fractal organizing occurs**: The DNA from the paradigm must replicate through platform and practices.

5. **Wholehearted engagement**: The framework requires engaging mind (paradigm), soul (platform), and will (practices).

What blocks activation:
- Addressing only one or two levels
- Misalignment between levels
- Wrong sequence (e.g., trying to change practices without changing culture)
- Failure to fractalize DNA throughout
- Surface-level change without paradigm shift
- Platform that doesn't support the paradigm
- Practices that don't embody the values

## Diagnostic Tools

To assess alignment and health of the three levels:

1. **Evaluate paradigm clarity**: Is the organizational mind clearly articulated? Are meta-ideas identified? Is DNA named?

2. **Assess platform alignment**: Do culture and structures align with the paradigm? Are they working together or against each other?

3. **Examine practice embodiment**: Do practices truly embody the values and paradigm? Or are they disconnected?

4. **Check for misalignment**: Look for places where paradigm, platform, and practices don't match.

5. **Assess fractal organizing**: Is DNA replicating through all three levels?

Key questions:
- Is our paradigm clearly articulated?
- Do our culture and structures align with our paradigm?
- Do our practices embody our values and paradigm?
- Are we addressing all three levels, or just one or two?
- Is our DNA fractaling through platform and practices?
- Where do we see misalignment?

## Implementation Pathways

To implement the framework in your organization:

1. **Start with paradigm**: Articulate the organizational mind. Identify meta-ideas. Name the DNA.

2. **Build the platform**: Develop culture (theological distinctives, values, vocabulary, heroes, celebration, branding) and structures (relational, resource, measurement) that align with the paradigm.

3. **Develop practices**: Create observable, repeatable behaviors that embody the paradigm and are supported by the platform.

4. **Ensure alignment**: Regularly check that all three levels are working together.

5. **Fractalize DNA**: Make sure the DNA from the paradigm replicates through platform and practices.

6. **Iterate and refine**: As you learn, refine all three levels to maintain alignment.

Sequence and priorities:
- Foundation: Articulate paradigm (organizational mind)
- Infrastructure: Build platform (organizational soul) that aligns with paradigm
- Expression: Develop practices (organizational will) that embody paradigm and are supported by platform
- Alignment: Ensure all three work together
- Fractalization: Replicate DNA through all levels
- Iteration: Refine and improve

## Common Pitfalls

1. **Addressing only one level**: Trying to change practices without changing culture, or changing culture without changing thinking.

2. **Wrong sequence**: Building platform or practices before clarifying paradigm.

3. **Misalignment**: Having paradigm, platform, and practices that don't match.

4. **Surface-level change**: Making cosmetic changes without addressing the underlying paradigm.

5. **Failure to fractalize**: Not ensuring DNA replicates through all three levels.

6. **Institutional thinking**: Applying the framework through institutional paradigms rather than movemental ones.

7. **Quick fixes**: Trying to implement through programs rather than systemic change.

8. **Disconnected practices**: Developing practices that don't align with culture and paradigm.
