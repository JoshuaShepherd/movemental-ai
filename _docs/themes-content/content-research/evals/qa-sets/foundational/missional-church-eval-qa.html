Missional Church: Evaluation Q&A Set

Purpose: This Q&A set serves as comprehensive evaluation material for assessing AI agents' understanding of the missional church—a way of being the church that recognizes God's mission as primary and the church as sent by God into the world.

Complexity Levels: Questions progress from Level 1 (foundational understanding) to Level 5 (critical analysis and advanced synthesis).

Level 1: Foundational Understanding

Eval Question 1.1
Level 1Question: What is a missional church, and how does it differ from other church models?
Complexity: Level 1 - Foundational Definition
Expected Answer Length: 700-1200 words

Ideal Answer:

A missional church is a way of understanding and being the church that recognizes God's mission (*missio Dei*) as primary and the church as sent by God into the world to participate in his mission. If you've been observing different church models, you've probably noticed significant differences in how churches understand their purpose and how they engage with the world. The missional church represents a fundamentally different way of being.

The term "missional" describes the nature and purpose of the church itself—the church is missional by nature, not just engaged in mission as one activity among many. This is a crucial distinction. Many churches have mission as one of their activities—they do mission work, support missionaries, engage in mission programs. But missional churches recognize that mission is their identity, not just their activity. The church doesn't have a mission; the church is mission.

Let me show you how this differs from other models, because understanding the differences helps clarify what missional means:

Missional vs. Attractional

The attractional church focuses on attracting people to come to the church. It asks, "How can we get people to come to us?" The strategy is to create excellent programs, services, and experiences that draw people in. The assumption is that if we build it well enough, people will come.

The missional church goes to people rather than expecting them to come. It asks, "How can we go to people and join what God is already doing?" The strategy is to send people into their contexts—neighborhoods, workplaces, social spaces—to be the church where they already are. The assumption is that God is already at work, and we're called to join God's mission.

Missional vs. Institutional

The institutional church is organized around maintaining structures, programs, and organizations. It focuses on institutional stability and survival. Success is measured by institutional health: budgets, buildings, programs, attendance. The primary concern is keeping the institution functioning.

The missional church is organized around mission and is willing to change structures to fulfill mission. Success is measured by missional impact: Are people being sent? Are communities being engaged? Is the gospel being embedded? The institution serves mission, not the other way around.

Missional vs. Programmatic

The programmatic church is organized around programs and activities. People are expected to fit into programs. The church offers a menu of programs, and people choose which ones to participate in. Success is measured by program participation.

The missional church is organized around people and mission, creating flexible approaches that serve people and mission rather than the other way around. Programs might exist, but they serve the larger purpose of equipping people for mission. People aren't expected to fit programs; programs are designed to equip people.

Key Characteristics of Missional Church

Now let me describe what characterizes a missional church:

Sent-ness: The church understands itself as sent by God into the world, just as Jesus was sent by the Father (John 20:21). This is not just about missionaries going to other countries; it's about the whole church being sent into its local context. Every believer is sent, and the church exists as a sent people.

Incarnational Presence: The missional church goes deep into culture and context, becoming native to its context rather than remaining separate. Just as God became flesh and dwelt among us (John 1:14), the church is called to become native to its context. This means understanding culture, learning its language, and embedding the gospel deep within it.

Mission as Identity: Mission is not one activity among many; it's the identity and purpose of the church. The church doesn't have a mission; the church is mission. Mission flows from identity, not from programs. When you understand who you are as a sent people, mission becomes natural rather than programmatic.

Cultural Engagement: The missional church engages culture deeply, understanding that the gospel must be embedded in culture to be effective. This requires understanding culture, learning its language, and becoming native to it. The gospel must be contextualized—not diluted, but expressed in ways that make sense within the cultural context.

Missional-Incarnational Impulse: This combines going out (missional) with going deep (incarnational). The church extends outward into mission and embeds deep into culture simultaneously. These are not separate activities but two dimensions of the same reality.

Organic Rather Than Programmatic: The missional church operates organically, responding to context and need, rather than programmatically, running predetermined programs regardless of context. It adapts to local contexts rather than imposing uniform programs.

Multiplication Focus: The missional church focuses on multiplication—new expressions of church, new communities, new movements—rather than just addition to existing structures. It plants the gospel and allows new expressions to emerge organically.

Biblical Foundation

The missional understanding finds its foundation in: God's sending of Jesus (John 3:16, 20:21), Jesus' sending of the church (John 20:21, Matthew 28:18-20), the incarnation as model (John 1:14, Philippians 2:5-8), the early church as sent people (Acts 1:8, the book of Acts), and the Great Commission as sending (Matthew 28:18-20).

The early church understood itself as sent. Jesus' last words to his disciples were about sending: "As the Father has sent me, I am sending you" (John 20:21). The book of Acts shows the church being sent—first to Jerusalem, then to Judea and Samaria, then to the ends of the earth. The church is a sent people, a missionary people, by nature.

This is why missional isn't just a new strategy or program. It's a recovery of the church's essential identity as a sent people, a missionary people, called to participate in God's mission in the world. When the church rediscovers this identity, everything else shifts—how it organizes, how it engages culture, how it measures success. Mission becomes identity, and identity shapes everything.

Eval Question 1.2
Level 1Question: What is the missional-incarnational impulse, and what are the six P's of incarnational mission?
Complexity: Level 1 - Foundational Knowledge
Expected Answer Length: 800-1400 words

Ideal Answer:

The missional-incarnational impulse represents the twin movements that characterize authentic Christian engagement with the world: going out (missional) and going deep (incarnational). If you've been thinking about how the church engages the world, you've probably noticed that there are two movements needed: we need to go out, and we need to go deep. These aren't separate activities; they're two dimensions of the same reality, rooted in the missio Dei (the sending of God) and the incarnation of God in Jesus Christ.

Let me explain each movement, because understanding both is crucial for authentic engagement:

The Missional Impulse: Going Out

The missional impulse refers to the outward thrust, the sending nature of the church. Mission is not merely an activity of the church but the result of God's initiative, rooted in God's purposes to restore and heal creation. God is a missionary God, and the church exists as a missionary people. This impulse is a "sending" rather than an "attractional" approach—we go to them rather than expecting them to come to us.

The missional impulse can be visualized as seeds being scattered by the wind, spreading the DNA of the gospel into new contexts. It is the sneeze that cannot be suppressed, the outward movement that characterizes all authentic mission. When this impulse is blocked—when churches rely exclusively on attractional approaches—they cut themselves off from the very dynamic that enables cultural impact.

The Incarnational Impulse: Going Deep

The incarnational impulse refers to the deep identification with culture, the "enfleshing" of the gospel in specific contexts. Just as God became fully human in Jesus, taking on flesh and dwelling among us, so too must the church become native to its context rather than remaining foreign. The incarnation demonstrates that God takes culture seriously, and so must we.

This isn't about compromising the gospel or diluting truth. It's about embedding the gospel so deeply in culture that it becomes native—expressed in ways that make sense within the cultural context while maintaining the essential truth of the gospel. The gospel must be contextualized, not compromised.

The Six P's of Incarnational Mission

Incarnational mission involves six key practices that enable authentic engagement. These practices are drawn from the pattern of the incarnation itself—how God became human and engaged with the world:

Presence: Full identification with culture. This means being fully present in the context, not remaining separate or aloof. Just as Jesus became fully human, we become fully present in our contexts. This is about being there, really being there, not just visiting or parachuting in.

Proximity: Geographic and relational closeness. This means being close to people—physically close, relationally close, emotionally close. The incarnation shows that God didn't stay distant; God came close. We're called to proximity, not distance. This is about closeness, not separation.

Powerlessness: Humility and vulnerability. This means entering contexts with humility, not power. The incarnation shows that God entered human experience in weakness and vulnerability, not in power and dominance. We're called to enter contexts with humility, willing to be vulnerable, not trying to dominate or control.

Prevenience: Recognizing God's work already present. This means seeing that God is already at work in contexts before we arrive. The incarnation shows that God was already present in human experience. We're called to recognize and join what God is already doing, not to assume God is absent until we arrive.

Passion: Embracing redemptive suffering. This means being willing to suffer for the sake of mission, to embrace the cost of engagement. The incarnation shows that God was willing to suffer, and the cross shows the ultimate cost. We're called to embrace the cost of mission, not to avoid it.

Proclamation: Sharing the good news. This means actually sharing the gospel message, not just being present. The incarnation shows that God came not just to be present but to proclaim and demonstrate the good news. We're called to share the good news, not just to be good neighbors.

These six practices work together to enable authentic incarnational engagement. Presence without proximity doesn't create deep connection. Proximity without powerlessness creates dominance rather than service. Powerlessness without prevenience misses what God is already doing. Prevenience without passion avoids the cost. Passion without proclamation misses the message. All six are needed for authentic incarnational mission.

The missional-incarnational impulse is one of the most easily suppressed elements of mDNA, often replaced by what I call the "evangelistic-attractional" pattern. This pattern, while effective in certain contexts, blocks the church's capacity to engage beyond significant cultural barriers. The gospel must be proclaimed afresh in new ways to each generation, and the church is intrinsically incarnational—it must become native to its context rather than remaining foreign.

Level 3: Application

Eval Question 3.1
Level 3Question: How would you help a traditional attractional church transition to becoming missional? What are the key paradigm shifts needed, and what practical steps would you take?
Complexity: Level 3 - Application
Expected Answer Length: 1000-1800 words

Ideal Answer:

If you've been leading in a traditional attractional church, you've probably experienced the challenge of transition. The attractional model has worked in many contexts, but you sense that something more is needed. The world has changed, culture has shifted, and the old approaches aren't as effective. How do you help a church transition from attractional to missional?

Let me walk you through how I would approach this, because this is complex work that requires both wisdom and patience. The transition isn't just about changing programs; it's about shifting paradigms, changing culture, and developing new practices. This is metanoia work—radical change of mind that leads to changed life.

Key Paradigm Shifts Needed

Before you can change practices, you need to change thinking. Here are the key paradigm shifts that need to happen:

Shift One: From "Come to Us" to "Go to Them"

The attractional church asks, "How can we get people to come?" The missional church asks, "How can we go to people?" This is a fundamental shift in orientation. Instead of focusing energy on creating excellent programs that attract people, the focus shifts to equipping people to be the church where they already are.

This shift requires helping people see that mission isn't just something the church does; it's something the church is. Every believer is sent, and the church exists as a sent people. This changes how people think about their own role—they're not just consumers of church services; they're missionaries sent into their contexts.

Shift Two: From Programs to Presence

The attractional church is organized around programs. Success is measured by program participation. The missional church is organized around presence—being present in contexts, engaging with culture, embedding in communities. Success is measured by missional presence and impact, not just program attendance.

This shift requires helping people see that their primary ministry isn't in church programs but in their everyday contexts. Programs might exist to equip and support, but the primary action is in neighborhoods, workplaces, and social spaces where people already are.

Shift Three: From Addition to Multiplication

The attractional church focuses on addition—adding people to existing structures. The missional church focuses on multiplication—creating new expressions, new communities, new movements. This shifts focus from growing the existing church to planting the gospel and allowing new expressions to emerge.

This shift requires helping people see that multiplication is more important than addition, that planting is more important than maintaining, that extending is more important than accumulating.

Shift Four: From Attraction to Sending

The attractional church focuses on attraction—creating experiences that draw people in. The missional church focuses on sending—equipping and releasing people to go. This shifts energy from creating attractional experiences to equipping people for mission.

This shift requires helping people see that their calling isn't just to come but to go, that the church exists to equip and send rather than just to gather and maintain.

Practical Steps for Transition

Now, how do you actually facilitate these shifts? Here's what I would do:

Step One: Begin with Leadership Metanoia

Transformation starts with leaders. Leaders need to experience metanoia first—radical change of mind about what church is and how it functions. I'd work with leadership to help them understand missional church, to see the paradigm shifts needed, and to begin embodying missional principles themselves.

This might involve: teaching on missional church, reading together, visiting missional churches, conversations about paradigm shifts, and helping leaders begin to practice missional engagement themselves. Leaders can't lead where they haven't been. They need to experience metanoia before they can guide others through it.

Step Two: Create Missional Experiments

Rather than trying to change everything at once, I'd create spaces for missional experiments. Identify a few people who are interested in missional engagement and help them start small experiments—being present in their neighborhoods, engaging with their workplaces, embedding in their social spaces.

These experiments become learning laboratories where people can experience missional engagement, learn from it, and share their learning with others. They become stories that inspire others and models that can be replicated.

Step Three: Equip People for Mission

Shift from programmatic equipping (teaching people to participate in programs) to missional equipping (teaching people to engage missionally in their contexts). Help people understand the six P's of incarnational mission. Teach them to see their contexts missionally. Equip them with tools for engagement.

This might involve: missional training workshops, coaching people in their contexts, creating missional communities, and providing ongoing support and encouragement. The goal is to equip people to be missionaries, not just to participate in programs.

Step Four: Shift Measures of Success

Begin measuring missional engagement, not just program participation. Ask questions like: "How many people are engaged missionally in their contexts?" "What stories are we hearing of missional impact?" "Where are we seeing multiplication happening?"

This shifts the conversation from "How many came?" to "How many went?" from "What programs are successful?" to "Where are we seeing missional impact?" This changes what gets celebrated and what gets attention.

Step Five: Gradually Shift Culture

Over time, shift the culture from attractional to missional. This happens gradually as more people engage missionally, as stories of missional impact are shared, as the language shifts from "coming" to "going," as success is measured by missional engagement.

Culture changes through what people experience, what they see modeled, what they celebrate, what stories they tell. Create experiences of missional engagement, model missional practices, celebrate missional stories, and help people tell new stories about what church is and does.

Step Six: Maintain Both/And Approach

Recognize that this is a journey, not a flip of a switch. You can maintain some attractional elements while building missional capacity. The goal isn't to eliminate everything attractional; it's to shift from attractional-only to missional-primary. Some attractional elements might serve missional purposes—gathering for equipping and sending, for example.

This transition takes time. Be patient, be persistent, and trust the process. Metanoia doesn't happen overnight, and organizational metanoia takes even longer. But as people experience missional engagement, as they see the impact, as they tell new stories, the culture shifts. Gradually, the church moves from attractional to missional, from "come to us" to "go to them."
