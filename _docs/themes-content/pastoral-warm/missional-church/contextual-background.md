# Contextual background — Missional Church

Timeline or background items (3–8) for the Contextual Background section. Use for a timeline or vertical list with date/period, title, description, tag.

---

| Date / period | Title | Description | Tag |
|---------------|--------|-------------|-----|
| 1st–3rd c. | Early church expansion | A tiny, persecuted sect grows to an estimated 20 million in the Roman Empire. No buildings, no professional clergy; multiplication through discipleship, sentness, and organic networks. mDNA in action. | Historical |
| 4th c. onward | Constantine and institutional shift | Church gains legal and cultural power; gradual shift from movement to institution. Buildings, clergy, and centralized structure become normative. Many movemental instincts are suppressed or forgotten. | Historical |
| 18th c. | Methodist movement | Wesley and the Methodists reactivate disciple-making, organic systems (class meetings, circuits), and communitas. One in thirty in England become Methodists; movement spreads. | Historical |
| 20th c. | Chinese underground church | Under persecution, the church in China grows from ~2 million to ~120 million in seventy years. Essence distilled to “Jesus is Lord”; all six elements present despite no external resources. | Historical / Contemporary |
| Late 20th–21st c. | Missional church conversation | Recovery of missional theology and sentness; critique of attractional default. Books and frameworks (e.g. *The Forgotten Ways*, 2006) name mDNA and Apostolic Genius and call for recovery of forgotten ways. | Contemporary |
| 2006 | *The Forgotten Ways* published | Alan Hirsch articulates the six elements of mDNA and Apostolic Genius, with evidence from early church, China, and other movements. Becomes reference for missional and movement frameworks. | Publication |
| 2012 | *The Forgotten Ways Handbook* | Practical guide to implementing and assessing mDNA in local contexts. | Publication |
