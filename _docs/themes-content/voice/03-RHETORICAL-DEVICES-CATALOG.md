# Rhetorical Devices Catalog
## Complete Inventory of Alan Hirsch's Rhetorical Techniques

## Device Categories

### I. Figures of Speech

#### 1. Metaphor (Very High - Signature Device)
**Frequency**: 8.5 per 1000 words

**Primary Systems**:

**a) Movement/DNA Metaphors**
- Apostolic Genius (genetic code)
- Movement DNA / mDNA
- Dormant potential
- Activation/reactivation
- Expression of DNA

**Examples**:
> "Apostolic Genius lies dormant in you, me, and every local church."
> "The forgotten ways are coded into our DNA."
> "We must activate the latent potential within us."

**b) Organic/Biological Metaphors**
- Seeds and growth
- Living systems
- Embodiment (giving flesh)
- Viral spread
- Organic structures

**Examples**:
> "Every apple contains an orchard."
> "The seed of the future lies in the past."
> "The gospel spreads like a virus."

**c) Journey/Travel Metaphors**
- On the verge
- Hitting the road
- Path forward
- The way
- Journey of transformation

**Examples**:
> "We are on the verge of something new."
> "We must hit the road with Jesus."
> "The journey of transformation begins now."

**d) Light/Illumination Metaphors**
- Silver imagination
- Awakening
- Vision
- Blindness/seeing
- Revelation

**Examples**:
> "We need the silver imagination to see new possibilities."
> "Paradigm blindness prevents us from seeing."
> "This is an awakening to our true potential."

**e) Architectural/Structural Metaphors**
- Foundation
- Framework
- Building blocks
- Template
- Blueprint

**Examples**:
> "The foundation of all movements is Jesus is Lord."
> "We need a new framework for understanding."
> "This provides a template for transformation."

#### 2. Simile (Moderate Usage)
**Frequency**: 2.1 per 1000 words

**Patterns**:
- Clarifies complex concepts
- Creates vivid imagery
- Connects abstract to concrete

**Examples**:
> "Like a virus, the gospel spreads through relationships."
> "The church should be like yeast working through dough."
> "We are like seeds containing full potential."

#### 3. Extended Metaphor (Frequent)
**Characteristics**:
- Developed over multiple paragraphs
- Creates coherent systems
- Builds conceptual frameworks

**Major Extended Metaphors**:
1. Movement DNA system (entire framework)
2. Journey narrative (following Jesus)
3. Organic systems (church as organism)
4. Viral spread (gospel transmission)

#### 4. Personification (Moderate)
**Frequency**: 1.8 per 1000 words

**Examples**:
> "The church has forgotten its true nature."
> "Apostolic Genius seeks to express itself."
> "The gospel wants to spread."

#### 5. Hyperbole (Sparse but Strategic)
**Usage**: For emphasis, not exaggeration

**Examples**:
> "This is the most critical element."
> "Nothing could be more important."
> "This changes everything."

### II. Figures of Repetition

#### 1. Anaphora (Moderate Usage)
**Frequency**: 2.3 per 1000 words

**Patterns**:
- Repetition at beginning of phrases
- Creates rhythm
- Builds momentum
- Emphasizes points

**Examples**:
> "We must see it. We must get it. We must live it."
> "It is about... It is about... It is about..."
> "The church is... The church is... The church is..."

#### 2. Epistrophe (Rare)
**Usage**: Occasional for emphasis

**Examples**:
> "Jesus is Lord. Jesus is Savior. Jesus is our example."

#### 3. Parallelism (High Usage)
**Frequency**: 4.2 per 1000 words

**Types**:
- Structural: Repeated sentence structures
- Semantic: Parallel ideas, different wording
- Antithetical: EXCLUDED from voice profile

**Examples**:
> "Move out, move in, move alongside, move from."
> "See it, get it, live it."
> "Jesus is Lord, Savior, and example."

#### 4. Alliteration (Occasional)
**Usage**: Usually unintentional but effective

**Examples**:
> "Silver imagination"
> "Forgotten ways"
> "Missional movement"

#### 5. Assonance (Rare)
**Usage**: Occasional, usually unintentional

### III. Figures of Question

#### 1. Rhetorical Questions (High Usage)
**Frequency**: 3.2 per 1000 words

**Functions**:
- Engage readers
- Provoke thought
- Challenge assumptions
- Create dialogue

**Types**:

**a) Engagement Questions**
> "How do you think those early Christians did it?"
> "What would you do in this situation?"

**b) Provocative Questions**
> "What if we reimagined church as movement?"
> "What if we activated our latent potential?"

**c) Reflective Questions**
> "What does this mean for us?"
> "How does this apply to our context?"

**d) Challenge Questions**
> "Are we willing to pay the price?"
> "Will we step into this calling?"

#### 2. Hypophora (Moderate)
**Usage**: Question followed by answer

**Examples**:
> "How do we account for this growth? The answer lies in..."
> "What is the solution? We must..."

---

### IV. Figures of Emphasis

#### 1. Repetition (High Usage)
**Frequency**: 3.5 per 1000 words

**Types**:
- Key terms repeated
- Phrases for emphasis
- Concepts reinforced
- Creates memorability

**Examples**:
> "Jesus is Lord. Jesus is Lord. Jesus is Lord."
> "Movement. Movement. Movement."

#### 2. Enumeration (Moderate)
**Usage**: Numbered or listed items

**Examples**:
> "First, we must... Second, we need... Third, we should..."
> "One element is... Another is... A third is..."

#### 3. Amplification (Moderate)
**Usage**: Expanding on ideas for emphasis

**Examples**:
> "This is critical. This is essential. This is foundational."

---

### V. Figures of Comparison

#### 1. Analogy (High Usage)
**Frequency**: 3.8 per 1000 words

**Patterns**:
- Explains complex through familiar
- Creates understanding
- Builds connections
- Illustrates principles

**Examples**:
> "Just as a seed contains the tree, so every believer contains the church."
> "Like a virus spreads, so the gospel moves through relationships."

#### 2. Exemplification (Very High)
**Frequency**: 4.8 per 1000 words

**Types**:
- Historical examples
- Contemporary case studies
- Personal anecdotes
- Biblical narratives

**Examples**:
> "The early church grew from 25,000 to 20 million..."
> "The Chinese church demonstrates..."
> "Wesley's movement shows us..."

### VI. Figures of Address

#### 1. Direct Address (Very High - Signature)
**Frequency**: 18.7 per 1000 words

**Patterns**:
- "We" - inclusive, communal
- "You" - direct engagement
- "Us" - shared experience
- "Our" - collective ownership

**Examples**:
> "We must together discover..."
> "You are called to..."
> "Our challenge is..."
> "Let us consider..."

#### 2. Apostrophe (Rare)
**Usage**: Addressing absent person or abstract concept

**Examples**:
> "O church, awaken to your calling!"

---

### VII. Figures of Omission

#### 1. Ellipsis (Moderate)
**Usage**: Omission of words for effect

**Examples**:
> "We must... we will... we can..."

#### 2. Asyndeton (Occasional)
**Usage**: Omission of conjunctions

**Examples**:
> "Move out, move in, move alongside, move from."

#### 3. Polysyndeton (Rare)
**Usage**: Multiple conjunctions

---

### VIII. Figures of Substitution

#### 1. Metonymy (Moderate)
**Usage**: Part for whole, container for contained

**Examples**:
> "The church" for "the people of God"
> "The West" for "Western culture"

#### 2. Synecdoche (Occasional)
**Usage**: Part represents whole

**Examples**:
> "Every believer" represents "the whole church"

### IX. Figures of Sound

#### 1. Onomatopoeia (Rare)

#### 2. Cacophony/Euphony (Rare)

**Characteristics**:

- Diverse sources
- Thematically connected

**Source Types**:
- Biblical

#### 4. Anticlimax (Rare)
**Usage**: Very occasional

## Critical Constraint: Antithesis Exclusion

### Explicitly Excluded Devices

#### Antithesis (Prohibited)
**Definition**: Contrasting opposites (not X but Y, either/or)

**Status**: EXCLUDED from voice profile despite presence in source texts

**Rationale**:
- Creates binary thinking
- Excludes rather than includes
- Contradicts both/and approach
- Limits expansive vision

**Alternative Approaches**:
- Positive framing
- Additive language
- Inclusive formulations
- Both/and thinking

**Examples to Avoid**:
- ❌ "Not maintenance but mission"
- ❌ "Either authentic or inauthentic"
- ❌ "X rather than Y"

**Preferred Alternatives**:
- ✅ "Mission-focused approach"
- ✅ "Authentic engagement"
- ✅ "Both X and Y"
- ✅ "Inclusive of X and Y"
