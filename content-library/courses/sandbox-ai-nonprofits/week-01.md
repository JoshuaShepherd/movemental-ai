# Week 1 — Introduction and orientation

## video

**Opening video (script outline, ~4–5 minutes)**

Speaker: facilitator or executive sponsor.

You are not here to “catch the AI wave.” You are here to build **legitimate, constrained experimentation** so your mission does not get quietly reshaped by tools your board cannot inspect and your neighbors cannot trust.

This course is **eight weeks in the learning environment** and maps onto a **twelve-week operational season** described in the Sandbox Season Playbook. Each LMS week compresses one or more playbook weeks; your cohort may still run the full twelve sessions on the calendar. Week one produces the artifacts that make the rest of the season **safe**: a named Safety Owner, explicit in-scope and out-of-scope data, and a season charter everyone can see.

We hold together two truths from the Sandbox canon. First, value must be **legitimate**: grounded in real work, real people, and real outcomes—not theater. Second, it must be **constrained**: bounded sandboxes where failure is instructive instead of harmful. Those two words are the spine of every week ahead.

Name the three layers you are about to train—not as theory, but as a single sentence each: **pattern recognition** (seeing where value hides in work you already do), **structured experiments** (resolvable bets written in advance), and **judgment** (deciding what is worth graduating when scoring is green but humanity might still be red). The industry sells the middle layer as if it were the whole curriculum. In this room we refuse that compression.

Close with one question: *Who in your organization is willing to say “no” on ethical and relational grounds before a tool touches a vulnerable person or a sacred trust?* That person is not the enemy of innovation. They are the condition for it.

### Cohort norms

**Show the work.** If a score cannot be traced to an artifact, it does not count.

**One sandbox.** If it is not in the shared document, it did not happen.

**Name the Safety Owner early.** Their written flag is load-bearing.

**Refuse hero narratives.** The goal is repeatable judgment, not a single charismatic win.

### The three layers (why “twelve experiments” can still mean nothing)

The canon opens with a sobering scene: a team ran **twelve experiments in six weeks**, picked sensible use cases, did the setup work, and still struggled to say what had been learned. Leadership asked for a report; they received **anecdotes**. The staff had opinions; they did not yet have understanding that survives a hard board question.

The diagnosis is structural. Sandbox work stacks in **three layers**. Skip any one, and the failure mode looks like success from the inside.

**Layer one — pattern recognition.** The trained eye that sees where AI might produce value inside work your organization **already** does—not a general sense that AI could help, but the ability to walk through a week of work and name which pieces sit under which patterns of opportunity.

**Layer two — structured experiments.** Repeatable tests that determine whether a candidate use case is real: inputs, outputs, time, quality, risk—**written in advance**, run on real-enough work at low enough stakes, recorded where others can read them. An experiment without structure is an anecdote wearing a lab coat.

**Layer three — judgment.** The formed capacity to decide whether a working experiment is worth taking forward—holding ethical, relational, and formational questions alongside structural ones—so a senior leader can walk into a portfolio review and know **which greens are really green**.

The layers are sequential in logic and simultaneous in practice. Pattern recognition first, or you test the wrong things. Experiments next, or you defend opinions with no evidence. Judgment from the beginning, ripening across the season—or the portfolio is only a spreadsheet.

What each layer **alone** produces is instructive. Pattern without experiments yields **confident opinions** nobody can verify. Experiments without pattern yield **recipes nobody knows how to extend**—clean answers to questions that may not have been worth asking. Judgment without field evidence yields **careful paralysis**—risk named, nothing moved. Pairs are worse: pattern plus experiments without judgment produces **findings with no one to decide**; pattern plus judgment without data produces a **senior read with no floor under it**.

The market sells experiments as the whole curriculum because that layer packages neatly. Pattern recognition is slow to teach; judgment cannot be sold by the seat. Your organization pays for that compression a year later: staff who can prompt but cannot name the next use case, and cannot decide which use cases to take.

### A reader’s diagnostic (three questions)

Ask honestly:

1. Can a staff member, given an hour, produce **five candidate use cases grounded in this organization’s real work**, not in articles they read last week? If not, the weakness is **pattern recognition**.

2. Can that same person, given another hour, write an **experiment brief** another staff member could run without a phone call? If not, the weakness is **experiment structure**.

3. Can the senior team, handed **three finished experiments with green scores**, identify which is actually safe to graduate? If not, the weakness is **judgment**.

Most organizations flunk at least two. The identity of the flunks tells you where to start—usually not where the loudest voice in the room assumes.

> The industry is selling the experiments as if they were the whole curriculum. They are a third of it.

## chat_dissonance

**Context discovery — AI companion prompt (facilitator seeds this in the product)**

You are helping a nonprofit leader establish baseline context before a sandbox season. Your tone is warm, direct, and unhurried. You are not selling AI; you are securing the conditions for **legitimate, constrained** discovery. Ask **one question at a time**, wait for an answer, mirror back what you heard in one sentence, then move on.

Begin by naming the tension: most teams want to start with tools and prompts; this season starts with **roles, boundaries, and a shared document**. Ask what happened the last time the organization tried something “innovative” without a named owner—where did accountability go?

Then ask, in order: (1) What is your role, and what **real** weekly time budget do you have for this work—not the aspirational calendar? (2) Where does content and data live today—CRM, drives, email, paper, or only in someone’s head—and where is that inventory **wrong** or **dangerous**? (3) What is the best outcome you hope for in eight weeks, and what is the **failure mode** you worry about when nobody is looking—performative greens, donor drift, staff cynicism? (4) **Who** is willing to be named Safety Owner, and what authority do they have to stop an experiment without being overridden in the hallway afterward? (5) What populations, data classes, or workflows are **never** in play without board-level review—and has anyone written that down where the team can see it?

Close by producing **five short bullets** they can paste into the season charter under “context captured on [date],” each bullet tied to something they actually said—not generic advice. If they have not named a Safety Owner, your final bullet must be a clear next step: “Name Safety Owner by [date] or postpone start.”

## looking_ahead

Week two moves from paper to **pattern recognition**. You will run an eight-pattern scan across real work from the last ninety days—not hypotheticals—and you will leave with twelve to thirty **named candidates**, not solutions. Bring examples of repetitive work, translation work, synthesis-heavy reading, and places where personalization would be **high risk, high reward** and therefore demands senior review before it ever enters the sandbox.

Leave perfectionism at the door; bring specificity. The scan is not brainstorming; it is **looking at work you already have through eight lenses**.
