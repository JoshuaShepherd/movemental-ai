## reading

### What an experiment actually is

A sandbox experiment is a **specific bet, written down in advance, that can be resolved**—not “we tried the assistant on our newsletter,” but: *this input → this output → this time comparison → this named reviewer using pre-agreed criteria → this risk surface noted.* Written **before** the attempt.

Human memory reshapes attempts after the fact; advance writing is the immune system against **retroactive storytelling**.

> Most sandbox failures are not tool failures. They are experiment-design failures.

### The five required fields (each non-optional)

**1. Input.** Exact artifacts, named, respecting Safety tiers—not “some recent emails.” Without specific input, comparisons to human baseline are **stories with numbers**.

**2. Output.** Artifact shape, length, register, must-include elements—concrete enough that a reviewer could **grade** it. “A good version” is not a specification.

**3. Time comparison.** Realistic human baseline vs assisted path, including **review and correction** time—not assistant sprint vs human wrestling.

**4. Quality judgment.** A **named peer who did not run the experiment**, using **criteria agreed before** the output exists, producing a **written paragraph verdict**—not a lone score that abstracts what you need to preserve.

**5. Risk flag.** What surfaced that Safety should hear—near-miss confidentiality, quiet factual drift, tone sliding to sector median, prompt holding material it should not. Without this field, incidents masquerade as successes.

### Why “recipes are not cooking”

A well-run experiment produces a **recipe**: durable steps another staff member can follow—organizational capacity, not a hero moment. **Cooking** is what seniors do with recipes—which to use, when, with what overrides. Confusing the two graduates people with **tool literacy** and no **judgment**; six months later recipes apply where they were never tested.

### The shared artifact rule

Experiments live where the senior team can read them. Not private notes, not a lost thread. **Private experiments are not sandbox experiments**—they are personal workflow. The portfolio is built only from work others can **evaluate**.

## case_study

**Witness — housing nonprofit case notes**

A draft summarization experiment for internal case notes seemed low-risk. Midweek a program director **tweaked the prompt** five times when outputs felt off. The Portfolio Owner froze the run, labeled each tweak as a **separate run**, and required separate scoring. What felt like friction became data: the team learned their **evaluation rubric** was the weak link—not the model. They rewrote criteria **before** run two; the second recipe graduated with a peer paragraph a board member could follow. Without run hygiene, they would have merged five incompatible stories into one “sense” of success.
