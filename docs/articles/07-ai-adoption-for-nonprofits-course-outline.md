# AI Adoption for Non-Profits: A 4-Week Course for Leaders and Teams

A guided formation journey for non-profit leaders, staff, and stakeholders who want to adopt AI responsibly — finding real value (efficiency, cost savings, work quality) while documenting human concerns and building organizational trust along the way.

This is not a technology course. It is a change management course that uses technology as the catalyst.

---

## Who This Is For

- **Executive directors and board members** who need to make informed decisions about AI without hype or fear.
- **Program managers and team leads** who suspect AI could help but don't know where to start safely.
- **Operations, communications, and development staff** who are already experimenting informally and need structure.
- **Volunteers and stakeholders** who will be affected by AI adoption and deserve a voice in how it happens.

No technical background required. The only prerequisite is an honest willingness to experiment and reflect.

---

## Course Philosophy

Most AI training falls into two traps: breathless enthusiasm that ignores real concerns, or fear-based restriction that misses real value. This course avoids both.

The premise: **AI adoption is a human process first and a technology process second.** The tools are the easy part. The hard part is building shared understanding, identifying where automation helps and where it harms, creating psychological safety for experimentation, and documenting what your organization actually learns.

Each week follows a formation loop:

1. **Learn** — Understand a concept or capability.
2. **Experiment** — Try it in a controlled, real-world scenario from your own work.
3. **Reflect** — Document what happened, what surprised you, what concerned you.
4. **Discuss** — Share findings with your team; surface disagreements and insights together.

## Week 1: Orientation — Understanding What AI Actually Is (and Isn't)

### Learning Objectives

- Demystify AI/LLMs: what they do, what they don't do, where they fail.
- Establish a shared vocabulary across the team (no one left behind by jargon).
- Surface existing assumptions, fears, and hopes about AI within the organization.
- Create a team AI charter — a living document of values, boundaries, and goals.

### Session 1: What AI Actually Does

**Core content:**

- How large language models work — pattern completion, not understanding. The autocomplete metaphor, extended honestly: what it implies about reliability, hallucination, and confidence without comprehension.
- What AI is good at: drafting, summarizing, reformatting, brainstorming, translating, pattern recognition across large volumes of text.
- What AI is bad at: facts it wasn't trained on, math, legal/medical/financial advice, anything requiring judgment about your specific community, anything requiring empathy (it can mimic tone; it cannot care).
- The non-profit-specific landscape: donor communications, grant writing, program reporting, volunteer coordination, content creation, data analysis.

**Key distinction:** AI is a tool, not a colleague. It has no mission. It has no stake in your community. It produces output; humans produce meaning.

### Session 2: The Assumptions Audit

**Team exercise:**

Each team member writes down (anonymously if needed):

1. One thing they hope AI could help with in their role.
2. One thing they're afraid AI might change about their role.
3. One thing they don't understand about AI and wish they did.

Collect, read aloud, and cluster. This becomes the **Assumptions Map** — a living artifact the team will revisit in Week 4.

**Discussion prompts:**

- Where do our hopes overlap? Where do they conflict?
- Are our fears about the technology itself, or about how leadership might use it?
- What questions do we need to answer before we can experiment responsibly?

### Session 3: The Team AI Charter (Draft 1)

**Facilitated exercise — produce a one-page document covering:**

- **Our mission context:** What does our organization exist to do, and how does that shape how we should use AI? (A food bank and a counseling center will have very different boundaries.)
- **Our boundaries (for now):** What will we NOT use AI for during this course? (e.g., direct client communication, clinical decisions, donor relationship management without disclosure.)
- **Our disclosure stance:** When and how will we tell stakeholders that AI was involved in producing something?
- **Our data principles:** What organizational data are we comfortable putting into AI tools? What is off-limits? (PII, donor records, client case notes, financial data.)
- **Our experimentation rules:** How will we test AI tools safely? Who approves experiments? How do we document results?

This charter is a draft. It will be revised in Week 4 after the team has actual experience.

### Homework: First Contact

Each team member spends 30 minutes using a free AI tool (ChatGPT, Claude, Gemini) on a **low-stakes, non-sensitive task** from their actual work:

- Rewrite a paragraph from a recent newsletter.
- Summarize a long report into three bullet points.
- Draft five subject lines for an upcoming email.
- Generate a list of questions for a stakeholder interview.

**Document in the experiment log:**

- What you asked for (the prompt, verbatim).
- What you got back.
- How much editing the output needed before it was usable.
- How you felt about the process (not just the output).

## Week 2: Experimentation — Finding Where AI Actually Helps

### Learning Objectives

- Learn structured prompting techniques that produce usable output.
- Map the organization's workflows to identify high-value, low-risk AI opportunities.
- Run controlled experiments on real tasks and document results honestly.
- Begin building an evidence base (not an opinion base) for AI value.

### Session 4: The Prompting Workshop

**Core content:**

- Why "garbage in, garbage out" is the entire story of AI quality.
- The anatomy of a good prompt: role, context, task, format, constraints.
- Iteration as a skill: your first prompt is a draft, not a final answer. The conversation is the tool, not the single query.
- Non-profit-specific prompting patterns:
  - **Grant language:** "Rewrite this program description for a funder who prioritizes measurable outcomes. Keep it under 200 words. Use the active voice. Do not invent statistics."
  - **Donor communications:** "Draft a thank-you email for a first-time donor of $50. Warm but not effusive. Mention [specific program]. No more than 150 words."
  - **Report summarization:** "Summarize the following quarterly report into three key findings and two recommendations. Use plain language suitable for a board member who has 5 minutes."
  - **Content repurposing:** "Turn this 2,000-word blog post into a 280-character social media post, a 100-word newsletter blurb, and three bullet points for a slide deck."

**Live practice:** Each participant brings a real task and crafts prompts in real time. The group reviews outputs together — what worked, what didn't, what needed human revision.

### Session 5: The Workflow Mapping Exercise

**Team exercise — the Value/Risk Matrix:**

Map your organization's recurring tasks on two axes:

| | Low Risk | High Risk |
|---|----------|-----------|
| **High Volume** | **Start here** — newsletters, social posts, meeting summaries, data entry formatting | **Proceed with caution** — grant applications, donor reports, board communications |
| **Low Volume** | **Nice to have** — one-off research, brainstorming, event planning drafts | **Not yet** — client-facing communications, clinical documentation, legal/compliance, personnel decisions |

**For each "Start here" quadrant task, define:**

- The current process (who does it, how long, how often).
- What an AI-assisted version would look like.
- What "good enough" output means (not perfect — usable with reasonable editing).
- Who reviews the output before it goes anywhere.
- What could go wrong and how you'd catch it.

### Session 6: The Structured Experiment

**Each team member or pair selects ONE task from the "Start here" quadrant and runs a controlled experiment:**

**Experiment protocol:**

1. **Do the task the normal way.** Time it. Note the effort and quality.
2. **Do the same task with AI assistance.** Time it. Note the effort and quality.
3. **Compare honestly.** Document in the experiment log:
   - Time saved (or not).
   - Quality difference (better, worse, different).
   - Editing required to make the AI output usable.
   - Anything the AI got wrong that a human wouldn't have.
   - Anything the AI did that surprised you (positively or negatively).
   - Whether you would use AI for this task again.

**Critical rule:** Do not report what you think leadership wants to hear. Report what actually happened. Negative results are valuable results.

### Homework: The Efficiency Diary

Run your chosen experiment three more times over the week on different instances of the same task type. Track:

- Does the time savings hold up, improve, or disappear with repetition?
- Are you getting better at prompting? How?
- What patterns do you notice in what AI gets right vs. wrong for this specific task?

## Week 3: Concerns — Naming What Matters Before It Becomes a Problem

### Learning Objectives

- Identify and articulate the human concerns AI adoption raises for your specific organization.
- Understand the ethical, legal, and relational dimensions of AI in non-profit work.
- Build a Concerns Register — a living document of risks, mitigations, and open questions.
- Practice the hardest skill in AI adoption: saying "not yet" or "not this" with specificity.

### Session 7: The Concerns That Matter

**Core content — structured around five categories:**

**1. Data and Privacy**

- What happens to the data you put into AI tools? (Training, storage, third-party access.)
- Client confidentiality: even anonymized data can be re-identified in small communities.
- Donor data: trust is your most valuable asset. One breach — or one perception of misuse — damages it permanently.
- Practical guidance: use enterprise/team plans with data retention controls. Never paste PII into consumer AI tools. Establish a "clean prompt" practice.

**2. Accuracy and Accountability**

- AI hallucinations are not bugs — they are a fundamental feature of how the technology works.
- Who is responsible when AI-generated content contains an error in a grant application? A donor letter? A program report?
- The "automation complacency" trap: the more you rely on AI, the less carefully you review its output. This is when mistakes get through.
- Practical guidance: every AI output must have a named human reviewer. The reviewer is accountable, not the tool.

**3. Labor and Dignity**

- Will AI eliminate jobs at our organization? Which ones? Is that acceptable?
- The difference between "AI replaces tasks" and "AI replaces people." One is efficiency; the other is a values decision.
- Staff who fear for their jobs will not experiment honestly. Psychological safety is a prerequisite for useful adoption.
- The skills shift: AI may reduce time on production tasks but increase the need for editorial judgment, strategic thinking, and relationship work. Are you investing in that transition?

**4. Equity and Access**

- AI tools cost money. Are you creating a two-tier organization — those who have access and those who don't?
- AI reflects training data biases. If your organization serves marginalized communities, AI-generated content may default to language, framing, or assumptions that don't represent them.
- The digital literacy gap: not everyone on your team (or your board, or your volunteer base) is equally comfortable with new technology. Adoption speed should be set by the slowest essential participant, not the fastest enthusiast.

**5. Mission Alignment**

- Does AI adoption serve our mission, or does it serve our convenience?
- The efficiency trap: saving 10 hours a week on newsletters is only valuable if those 10 hours are redirected toward mission-critical work. If they're absorbed by more AI management, you've traded one overhead for another.
- Authenticity: your donors give because they believe in human beings doing human work. At what point does AI assistance cross into inauthenticity? That line is different for every organization, and it must be drawn intentionally.

### Session 8: Building the Concerns Register

**Team exercise:**

For each concern category, the team documents:

| Concern | Severity (1–5) | Likelihood (1–5) | Current Mitigation | Gap | Owner | Review Date |
|---------|----------------|-------------------|--------------------|-----|-------|-------------|
| *Example: Client data entered into ChatGPT* | 5 | 3 | *Verbal policy: "don't do it"* | *No written policy, no technical controls, no training* | *[Name]* | *[Date]* |

**Discussion prompts:**

- Which concerns are about the technology, and which are about how we manage change?
- Which concerns can we mitigate now, and which require more information?
- Are there concerns we're avoiding because they're uncomfortable? (These are usually the most important ones.)

### Session 9: The Stakeholder Perspective

**Preparation for external communication:**

- How will you explain your AI use to donors? Board members? Clients? Partner organizations? Regulators?
- Draft a **stakeholder FAQ** (3–5 questions and answers) that your team could use if asked about AI today.
- Role-play exercise: one person plays a skeptical board member or concerned donor. Another explains the organization's AI approach. The group provides feedback.

### Homework: The Concern Interview

Each team member has a 15-minute conversation with one stakeholder outside the core team (board member, major donor, volunteer, partner org leader, or client if appropriate) and asks:

- Have you heard about organizations using AI? What do you think about it?
- If we used AI to help with [specific task from Week 2], how would you feel about that?
- What would you want to know about how we use AI?

Document the conversation (with permission) and bring findings to Week 4.

## Week 4: Integration — Building Your Organization's AI Playbook

### Learning Objectives

- Synthesize four weeks of learning, experimentation, and concern-gathering into a concrete organizational plan.
- Finalize the Team AI Charter with evidence-based revisions.
- Create an AI Playbook — the practical document your team will actually use going forward.
- Establish a review cadence so the playbook stays alive and the organization keeps learning.

### Session 10: The Evidence Review

**Team exercise — revisit everything:**

**From Week 1:**
- Pull up the Assumptions Map. Which assumptions proved true? Which were wrong? Which are still open questions?
- Read the original fears aloud. Have they changed? Are there new ones?

**From Week 2:**
- Compile all experiment results. What tasks showed clear AI value? Which didn't?
- Calculate aggregate metrics: total hours tested, average time savings, quality ratings, error rates.
- Identify the "definite yes" tasks (clear value, low risk, team buy-in) and the "definite not yet" tasks.

**From Week 3:**
- Review the Concerns Register. Have any concerns been resolved by experience? Have new ones emerged?
- Share stakeholder interview findings. What surprised the team?

### Session 11: The AI Playbook (Draft)

**Produce a single document — the team's operational guide for AI use. Structure:**

**Section 1: Approved Uses**

For each approved task:
- What the task is.
- Which tool(s) to use (specific product, not generic "AI").
- The standard prompt template (from Week 2 experiments).
- Who reviews the output.
- What disclosure is required (if any).
- How to report problems.

**Section 2: Boundaries**

- Tasks where AI is not approved (with specific reasons, not just "feels wrong").
- Data that must never enter AI tools (specific categories, not just "be careful").
- Situations that require human-only handling (with escalation path).

**Section 3: Policies**

- Disclosure policy: when and how you tell stakeholders AI was involved.
- Data handling policy: which tools are approved, what data classifications apply, where credentials/API keys are managed.
- Review policy: who reviews AI output, what the review standard is, how errors are escalated.
- New use case policy: how a team member proposes a new AI application (who approves, what evidence is needed, how it's piloted).

**Section 4: Concerns and Open Questions**

- The active Concerns Register (transferred from Week 3).
- Open questions the organization hasn't resolved yet — with a plan for when and how to revisit them.
- A stakeholder communication plan: what you'll share, with whom, and when.

**Section 5: Review Schedule**

- Monthly: team check-in on what's working and what's not (30 minutes).
- Quarterly: review the full playbook, update approved uses, revisit concerns, assess whether AI is actually serving the mission.
- Annually: comprehensive review including stakeholder input, budget impact, and strategic alignment.

### Session 12: Charter Revision and Commitment

**Return to the Week 1 AI Charter and revise it based on four weeks of evidence:**

- What boundaries need to be tighter than you originally thought?
- What boundaries can be loosened because the risk proved manageable?
- What did the team learn about itself through this process — not just about AI?

**Closing exercise — each team member completes three sentences:**

1. "The most valuable thing AI can do for our organization is..."
2. "The most important boundary we need to maintain is..."
3. "The question I still want answered is..."

**Read aloud. Discuss. Record in the playbook.**

### Final Deliverables

By the end of Week 4, each participating organization should have:

- [ ] **Team AI Charter** (revised, signed by leadership)
- [ ] **AI Playbook** (operational guide with approved uses, boundaries, and policies)
- [ ] **Concerns Register** (active, with owners and review dates)
- [ ] **Stakeholder FAQ** (ready for external communication)
- [ ] **Experiment Evidence Base** (documented results from at least 4 controlled experiments)
- [ ] **Review Schedule** (monthly, quarterly, and annual check-ins calendared)

## Facilitation Notes

### On Pacing

This is a 4-week course, but it is not a rush. Each week has three sessions (roughly 60–90 minutes each) plus homework. Organizations that meet twice a week will finish in 4 weeks. Organizations that meet weekly will take 6. Both are fine. The homework — especially the experiments — needs real time between sessions.

### On Group Size

Ideal group: 4–12 people from the same organization. Larger groups should split into working pairs for experiments and reconvene for discussions. Cross-organizational cohorts work for Sessions 1, 4, and 7 (content delivery) but not for the exercises, which require shared organizational context.

### On Leadership Presence

Executive directors and senior leaders should be present for Sessions 2, 3, 8, 10, 11, and 12 at minimum. Their absence during the charter and playbook sessions signals that AI adoption is a staff problem, not an organizational commitment. That signal undermines everything.

### On Resistance

Some team members will be skeptical or resistant. That is not a problem to solve — it is a perspective to include. The most valuable voice in an AI adoption process is often the person asking "but should we?" Protect that voice. Build the course culture around genuine inquiry, not persuasion.

### On Tools

This course is tool-agnostic. It works with ChatGPT, Claude, Gemini, Copilot, or any general-purpose LLM. Do not let tool selection become a blocker. Start with free tiers. Evaluate paid plans only after Week 2 provides evidence of value.

---

## What This Course Does Not Cover

- **Technical implementation** (API integration, custom fine-tuning, building AI products). This is an adoption and change management course.
- **Sector-specific compliance** (HIPAA, FERPA, SOX). Organizations in regulated sectors need specialized legal guidance beyond this course's scope.
- **AI for fundraising automation** (automated donor outreach, predictive giving models). These are powerful but raise distinct ethical questions that deserve their own treatment.
- **Building AI tools** for beneficiaries or clients. Serving people with AI-powered tools is a different conversation from using AI internally.

Each of these is a valid next step — but only after the organizational foundation from this course is in place.
