# Adaptive leadership and AI adoption: stress-test for Movemental’s thesis (internal synthesis)

## Executive summary

**Ronald Heifetz’s** distinction between **technical problems** (knowable, delegable to expertise) and **adaptive challenges** (requiring learning, value conflict, and distributed responsibility) remains a **justified and legible** lens for how **generative AI** lands in mission-shaped organizations. It is **not**, however, a sufficient **exclusive** lens. Contemporary research and standards discourse frame enterprise and institutional AI as **sociotechnical**: irreducibly about **data, models, procurement, security, and law** *as well as* culture and courage.

**Recommendation:** Treat adaptive leadership as a **co-primary sensemaking frame** alongside **sociotechnical governance** — not as a replacement for technical strategy. For Movemental’s audiences, that pairing is both **more honest** and **more pastorally credible** than “anthropological, not technological” taken literally.

For **maturity models**, the field already offers **four- and five-stage** enterprise schemas (e.g., **MIT CISR** four-stage enterprise AI maturity; **Gartner** five-level framings). Movemental’s **five-stage** narrative is **competitive with industry habit** but **not uniquely validated** as a scientific stage theory. Credibility in sales and product copy requires **humility**: present stages as a **working heuristic** tied to observable behaviors and governance milestones, and avoid implying **empirical developmental psychology** without measurement.

On **Dunning–Kruger**, recent experimental work complicates a simple morality tale. **Fernandes et al.** (*Computers in Human Behavior*, 2026, DOI 10.1016/j.chb.2025.108779) find that with ChatGPT-class tools on demanding reasoning tasks, participants **gained performance** yet **lost calibration** — **overestimating** how well they did; the **classic DK pattern was disrupted**, and **higher AI literacy** was associated with **greater overconfidence** in the publicized interpretation. Movemental should **not** claim “DK exactly as in the textbooks” for AI; it *should* warn that **fluency and optimism** can **outrun judgment** — including for sophisticated users.

## 1. Is Heifetz the right primary lens?

**What Heifetz actually contributes.** In *Leadership Without Easy Answers* (1994) and *The Practice of Adaptive Leadership* (Heifetz, Grashow, Linsky, 2009), adaptive work is the learning required when **values, roles, or identities** must change — not merely when a new tool ships. That maps cleanly onto generative AI in churches and movements: questions of **truthfulness, attribution, pastoral presence, speed vs. depth, and who is displaced** are not resolved by a sharper IT roadmap alone.

**Where the lens strains.** AI adoption in real institutions is simultaneously:

- **Technical:** data quality, integration, evaluation of outputs, incident response, cybersecurity.  
- **Legal / ethical:** copyright, privacy, discrimination risk, record-keeping.  
- **Economic:** vendor contracts, lock-in, total cost of ownership.

Standards such as **ISO/IEC 42001** and policy frameworks (e.g., **NIST AI RMF**, EU regulatory conversation) presuppose **competence and documentation** — not “vibes.” If Movemental’s message implies leaders can **delegate all technical discernment**, it collides with **accountable oversight** norms.

**Verdict:** Heifetz is a **strong primary lens for the *anxiety, conflict, and identity work*** AI provokes — and a **secondary lens is mandatory** for the **sociotechnical** remainder. Public language should read: **“primarily adaptive in how it changes *us*; unavoidably technical in how it runs.”**

## 2. Maturity models: differentiation without overclaiming

**Established models.** MIT CISR’s **enterprise AI maturity** work (Weill, Woerner, Sebastian; see MIT Sloan and CISR publications) organizes progress across a **four-stage** trajectory grounded in **large surveys and interviews**. **Gartner** popularizes **five-level** maturity language for AI portfolios. **McKinsey’s** recurring “State of AI” reporting emphasizes **adoption breadth, value capture, and rewiring** more than a single canonical ladder in every edition.

**Movemental’s five-stage model (`C-F10`).** Five stages sit in the **industry-recognizable band** (especially vs. Gartner). Differentiation should **not** rest on “we discovered the true stages of AI.” It should rest on:

- **Audience fit:** formation-rich language for **movement leaders** vs. generic CIO stages.  
- **Ethical and public leadership** milestones: e.g., when an org is safe to **speak externally** about AI use, not only when it deploys copilots.  
- **Honest epistemology:** stages as **scaffolding for coaching**, not validated developmental psychology.

**Overclaiming risk.** Stage theories in **Kohlberg, Fowler, and Kegan** traditions are **contested** as predictive instruments for adults. Slapping a “stage 3” label on a small nonprofit may feel **evaluative** rather than **formative**. Product and marketing should prefer **capabilities unlocked** language over **identity ranking**.

**What would empirical validation require?** Longitudinal evidence that users **progress in order**, **rarely regress**, and that stage assignments **predict** responsible behaviors (verification habits, governance artifacts, incident reporting). Without that, say **“model”** or **“map”**, not **“science.”**

## 3. “Anthropological, not technological”: rhetorical power vs. literal truth

The headline **`book-003`** is **rhetorically powerful** for audiences tired of being shamed for not keeping up with Silicon Valley. It names a real phenomenon: AI reorders **attention, trust, craft, and vocation**.

Taken **literally**, the phrase is **too strong**. Movemental itself will presumably counsel on **data stewardship, vendor risk, and truthful representation** — all **technological or techno-legal** competencies. A disciplined public version:

- **Anthropological first** for *why this hurts and why we resist*.  
- **Technological enough** for *what we must never be naive about*.

That split preserves moral seriousness without inviting critics to accuse Movemental of **anti-intellectualism** or **consultant mystique**.

## 4. Experimentation vs. discernment (`book-005`, `book-043`)

**What research rewards.** Innovation and design-thinking literatures strongly support **cheap experiments** under uncertainty — consistent with “experimentation as prerequisite to discernment” when discernment means **discovering faithfulness in fog**, not **avoiding accountability**.

**What traditions rightly slow down.** Prudence, **authority of office**, and **communal discernment** exist partly to prevent **individualistic trial-and-error** on matters that touch **vulnerable people** (pastoral care, counseling, public teaching). “Permission to stay at experimentation level” **`book-043`** is pastorally necessary **if** it means **relief from shame and premature public posturing** — and dangerous **if** it becomes **permission to skip oversight** where harm is asymmetric.

**Synthesis line for copy:** Experimentation should be **structured** — **hypothesis, scope, review, rollback** — not **impulsive play**. That bridges **lean learning** with **ecclesial seriousness**.

## 5. “Leaders don’t need technical expertise” (`articles-027`)

**Strongest true version:** Leaders do not need to **train transformers** or **write Python** to **lead**.  
**False version:** Leaders need **no technical literacy** to **govern**.

Responsible leadership requires **enough literacy to commission the right questions**: data provenance, evaluation of failure modes, human-in-the-loop expectations, and **when to refuse automation**. Movemental should **keep** the encouragement for non-engineer leaders but **qualify** it with **minimum viable literacy** for their context tier.

---

## 6. Speed, hype, and “Dunning–Kruger” (`research-005`)

Do **not** lean on a pop-psych **DK** caricature for AI without nuance. The Fernandes et al. program suggests **metacognitive risk is widespread**, including among those who **think** they understand AI. That actually **raises** the stakes for Movemental’s **formation** positioning: the enemy is not only **incompetence** but **miscalibrated confidence** across skill levels.

## 7. Trade-offs of leaning hard into adaptive framing

| Upside | Downside |
|--------|----------|
| Dignifies slow, moral reasoning | Can **moralize resistance** unfairly |
| Explains why tool-first rollouts fail | Can **slow** adoption paths that Movemental as a vendor needs |
| Fits conflicted congregations | May **under-specify procurement and engineering** realities |
| Differentiates from hype merchants | Risks sounding like **another leadership seminar** |

**Business lens (`business-034`):** Position adaptive leadership as **why Movemental sells coaching and formation**, not as a denial that **platforms must be engineered well**.

## 9. Strong counter-arguments and the defensible thesis

**Counter-arguments to own:**

1. **AI is infrastructure**, not only culture — **scale, latency, and liability** follow technical facts.  
2. **“Adaptive” is consultant-coded** — audiences may glaze unless tied to **concrete practices**.  
3. **Elite frameworks** (Harvard Kennedy School lineage) may feel distant from **volunteer-led** churches unless **translated**.

**Strongest defensible thesis the evidence allows:**  
Generative AI is a **sociotechnical shock** that **presents as a tools conversation** but **forces adaptive work** on communities of truth and care. Movemental’s value is to **hold the adaptive conversation honestly** while **refusing two reductions**: neither **tech solutionism** nor **anti-tech romanticism**.
