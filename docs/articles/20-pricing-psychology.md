# Is $1,000 + 10% the right signal? Pricing psychology, comparables, and honest positioning

## Executive summary

The **$1,000 + 10%** structure is **coherent** as a story: it **anchors** against six-figure custom builds and against **publisher-heavy** economics while staying **below** premium creator-SaaS stacks on a **cash-outflow** basis for many leaders. The **risk** is not arithmetic alone; it is **psychological** and **relational**. **Percent-of-revenue** pricing maximizes **alignment** only when **measurement feels fair**, **definitions are legible**, and **trust** is already high. Otherwise it triggers **monitoring anxiety**, **mental accounting** (“every extra dollar hurts”), and **category confusion** (“Are you Stripe? A publisher? A church software vendor?”).

**Recommendations (internal):**

1. **Treat $1,000 as a credibility deposit**, not “the whole value,” and **surround it** with proof of build quality so it does not read as **too cheap to be real**.  
2. **Publish a plain-language revenue definition** (inclusions, exclusions, timing, refunds, pass-through donations, speaking, affiliate, etc.) before scaling sales conversations.  
3. **Pair the 10% with a cap, floor, or hybrid** for at least some segments if interviews show **acute loss aversion** among mission-first leaders.  
4. **Model Pareto concentration** explicitly: sustainability may depend on a **small number** of high earners; plan **retention** and **ethics** accordingly.  
5. **Separate** “AI lowered our build cost” (likely true) from “AI guarantees your upside” (not true)—pricing claims should track **cost of delivery**, not **promise of leader revenue**.

---

## 1. What pricing is doing in this category

For movement leaders, price is only partly about **liquidity**. It is also:

- A **signal of seriousness** (custom dev is expensive; “real” platforms look like ongoing subscriptions).  
- A **signal of identity** (“I am not a hobbyist” vs. “I steward donor money carefully”).  
- A **signal of relationship** (publisher advances feel like **patronage with strings**; SaaS feels like **rent**; revenue share feels like **partnership** or **surveillance**, depending on framing).

Behavioral economics gives a usable lens. **Anchoring** and **relative comparison** (Ariely; Kahneman & Tversky’s broader program) explain why juxtaposing **$1,000 + 10%** with **$100K builds** or **publisher-heavy splits** is rhetorically powerful: the brain resolves uncertainty by grabbing a nearby reference. The same mechanism creates a **skepticism trap**: if the anchor gap feels **too extreme**, buyers infer **hidden costs**, **missing scope**, or **future price shocks** rather than assuming genius operational efficiency.

**Transaction utility** (Thaler) helps explain why **10% of future revenue** can feel worse than **$399/month** even when discounted cash flows favor the percentage. A flat subscription is **predictable**; a percentage is **open-ended** and salient at the **moment of each sale**—especially for leaders whose theology or donor culture trains them to treat **growth** as **gratitude**, not **extraction**.

## 2. Is $1,000 “cheap,” “accessible,” or “expensive”?

The honest answer is **segment-dependent**:

- For a **working speaker or author** with even modest honoraria, **$1,000** is often **less than one engagement** or **one modest consulting week**. There, it reads **accessible** and may even feel **too low** unless quality cues are strong.  
- For a **small nonprofit** paying staff near median wages, **$1,000** can be a **board conversation**, especially if TechSoup-style **discount expectations** are in the background (see NTEN’s recurring “not enough budget” theme in nonprofit digital investment literature).  
- For a **bootstrap creator** comparing to **$39–$89/mo** Teachable or Kajabi entry tiers, **$1,000 upfront** is a **cash hurdle** even if lifetime cost favors Movemental.

**Professional services** research (Maister; Weiss) suggests that in **trust-based** sales, **clarity beats cleverness**. If **$1,000** is “setup,” say what happens if scope expands. If it is **partly offset** by the revenue share, say that plainly. Ambiguity plus low price is the **toxic** combination for perceived quality.

## 3. How “10% of revenue” lands in the mind

Revenue share is not exotic—**Mighty Networks** publishes tiered **transaction fees** roughly in the **sub–2%** range on higher plans; **Teachable Starter** carries a **high single-digit platform fee** on sales. Still, **10% of all revenue** (depending on definition) can be **materially larger** than those analogs if the base is broad.

Three psychological dynamics matter:

1. **Loss aversion:** Percent fees are experienced as **giving up** something you **earned**, whereas subscriptions are **budgeted overhead**.  
2. **Fairness scripts:** Leaders will compare Movemental to **payment processors** (~3%) + **email tool** + **web host**, not to **publishers**, unless you **earn** the publisher frame with real **distribution** comparables.  
3. **Agency under monitoring:** Even benign reporting can feel like **evaluation** unless norms are **cohort-shaped** (“we all share numbers”) rather than **hierarchical** (“platform audits you”).

Aligned incentives (the **90/10 story**) are **economically literate**, but **trust** is the prerequisite. Without it, the same structure reads as **rent-seeking on mission**.

## 4. Honest comparables in 2026 (public list pricing)

Movemental’s competitive set is **not one aisle** in the software store. It spans:

- **Creator suites** (Kajabi’s published bands now include a lower **Kickstarter** tier at **$89/mo** and reach **$399/mo** on **Pro**; Kajabi has communicated **2026 plan migrations** for some legacy customers).  
- **Course marketplaces** (Teachable’s **2025** restructure puts public monthly bands roughly **$39–$399** with **7.5%** platform fees on the entry tier and **0%** platform fees on higher tiers—plus card fees everywhere).  
- **Community platforms** (Mighty Networks lists **roughly** **$79 / $179 / $354** per month for standard tiers, with **tiered transaction fees** and **enterprise** “Pro” pricing that third parties cite as **five figures monthly** and up).  
- **Faith stacks** (Subsplash bundles are typically **quoted**; **giving** can be **$0/mo** with **processing fees**; **full branded app + web + media** stacks are often described in vendor comparisons as **hundreds to low thousands per month** equivalent—always **verify in procurement**).  
- **Custom builds** (serious **WordPress + LMS + membership + integrations** commonly land **five figures** build + ongoing care).

Net: **$1,000** upfront is **not** competing on the same **mental account** as **Squarespace**. It is competing with **“should I hire an agency?”** and **“should I rent Kajabi + a dozen plugins?”** That is good if the product story matches; bad if prospects mentally file you next to **$29/mo** tools.

## 5. Unit economics: does the math even ballpark?

Using the prompt’s toy assumptions: **100 leaders × $1,000** = **$100,000** one-time. If each leader generated **$50,000/year** in in-scope digital revenue, **10%** yields **$5,000 × 100 = $500,000/year** recurring **before** payment processing arguments, churn, refunds, and disputed classifications.

That **order of magnitude** can fund a **lean** SaaS with disciplined scope—**or not**, depending on support ratios, AI inference costs, compliance, and growth marketing. The important modeling point is **Pareto reality**: if a **small fraction** of leaders produces most digital revenue, the **10% leg** is **concentrated** in a handful of accounts. That is good for **short-term ARR** and dangerous for **concentration risk** unless retention is excellent.

**AI lowering build cost** is a credible **margin story** for Movemental itself. It is **not** a revenue story for the leader. Keep those claims **separated** in external messaging.

## 6. The “what if I never monetize?” objection

Revenue-share models live or die on **what happens at zero or low revenue**. If the leader’s fear is **“I will owe money I do not have,”** the contract must be **non-predatory** and the narrative **non-shaming**. If the fear is **“I will be deprioritized because I am not lucrative,”** the product roadmap and community design must **prove otherwise** with visible investment in **non-revenue success** (formation outcomes, convening quality, resource sharing).

Three patterns from the broader market:

- **Floor + share:** a small subscription guarantees baseline service; the percentage only kicks in above a threshold.  
- **Time-bound share:** a declining percentage after a revenue band (rare in SaaS, common in some agency earnouts).  
- **Pure share with explicit zero state:** simplest, but only works when **trust** is high and **support costs** for idle accounts are truly low.

Movemental should pick **one** story and **document** it, because prospects will ask in the first call.

## 7. Ethical positioning: monetization pressure vs. vocation

The prompt’s sharpest ethical question is whether **90/10** **nudges** leaders toward **monetizing everything**, including parts of their vocation that should remain **non-market**. Theology does not prescribe a commission rate, but it **does** privilege **truthfulness**, **generosity**, and **care for the vulnerable**. Revenue-share models can be **just**; they can also be **corrosive** if the platform’s survival **visibly depends** on squeezing the **long tail**.

Practical mitigations that stay on-brand:

- **Celebrate non-monetary outcomes** in community rituals and reporting, not only GMV.  
- **Scholarships, slower ramps, or waived rev-share periods** for credible mission-pure users.  
- **Hard clarity** on whether **donations** and **pass-through** funds count—this is both an **ethics** and a **sales-cycle** issue.

---

## 8. Verdict on “is $1,000 + 10% right?”

**As a strategic hypothesis:** **Plausible and differentiated**, especially when anchored honestly against **agency quotes** and **fragmented SaaS stacks**, and when the **10%** is sold as **shared upside** with **transparent rules**.

**As a finished, scale-ready price:** **Depends on trust artifacts** (definition of revenue, reporting UX, cohort norms) and on **segment-specific** payment psychology. If research interviews show persistent **“monitored”** language, consider **hybrids** (lower % with a modest floor subscription; or a **cap** after a threshold) without abandoning the **alignment story**.

**Editorial guidance:** Call the model **“aligned pricing”** or **“partnership economics”** rather than **“revolutionary”** unless you can **show receipts**—comparables shift yearly (Kajabi’s 2026 adjustments are a live example), and savvy buyers discount superlatives.
