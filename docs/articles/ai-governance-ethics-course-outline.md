# Declaring Our AI — A 4-Week Course for Non-Profit Leaders

**Subtitle:** Articulating, declaring, and codifying your organization's governance, ethics, and principles for AI

**Format:** 4-week cohort course (one session per week, ~90 minutes each)
**Audience:** Non-profit executive teams, board members, ministry leaders, and organizational decision-makers
**Cohort size:** 8–15 participants (ideally 2–3 people per organization, so governance work carries back to the team)
**Prerequisite:** None — the course meets leaders where they are on the AI maturity spectrum

---

## Course Promise

By the end of four weeks, your organization will have a **board-ready AI governance document** — not a theoretical exercise, but a living policy grounded in your actual mission, values, and operational reality. You will know where AI may serve your work, where it must not go, who owns what, and how to answer donors, accreditors, and staff with one clear voice.

---

## Why This Course Exists

Most non-profits are not in danger of adopting AI recklessly. They're in danger of **not deciding at all** — and letting ad hoc, ungoverned use fill the vacuum. When a staff member uses ChatGPT to draft a donor letter, when a program director pastes youth data into a public tool, when a board member asks "what's our position on AI?" and no one can answer — that's the real risk. Not that you'll move too fast, but that you'll never articulate what you stand for.

This course exists because **governance is stewardship**. It protects who you are, whom you serve, and the trust you hold — while freeing your team to use AI where it genuinely helps.

Organizations do not need more information about AI. They need **structured pathways to act, decide, and lead responsibly** within it.

---

## Pedagogical Approach

This course follows the **Dissonance → Action → Reflection → Community** formation scaffold:

- **Dissonance:** Each week opens with a tension — a real scenario that exposes the gap between "we should have a policy" and actually having one
- **Action:** Participants do governance work *during* the course — inventories, drafts, role assignments — not after it
- **Reflection:** Integration questions that connect the governance work back to mission, values, and organizational identity
- **Community:** Cohort processing where organizations learn from each other's contexts, constraints, and choices

The output is not a grade or a certificate. The output is a **document your board can adopt**.

## Course Architecture

### Week 0: Pre-Work (Asynchronous, ~30 minutes)

**Purpose:** Honest placement before the first session. No one walks in pretending.

**Pre-work assignments:**

1. **AI Maturity Self-Assessment** — Each participant locates themselves and their organization on the five-level maturity model (Awareness → Experimentation → Adoption → Integration → Leadership). No shame in being at Level 1. Honesty is the prerequisite.

2. **Shadow Inventory** — Before formal inventory work in Week 1, each participant answers three questions:
   - What AI tools do you personally use (even casually)?
   - What AI tools do you know or suspect staff in your area use?
   - Has anyone in the organization ever asked "what's our position on AI?" — and what happened?

3. **Values Pull** — Bring your organization's mission statement, values statement, and any existing technology or ethics policies to the first session. If you don't have written values, bring what you'd say if someone asked "what does your organization stand for?"

### Week 1: Governance Foundations — Why the Board Owns This

**Theme:** Governance is not about technology. It's about stewardship of mission.

**Session arc:** Dissonance → Teaching → Action → Reflection

#### Opening Dissonance (15 min)

**Scenario:** A well-meaning staff member at a youth-serving non-profit pastes de-identified but sensitive program notes into a public AI tool to "summarize themes for a grant report." The tool's terms of service allow the provider to train on inputs. No organizational policy exists. The executive director finds out two months later from a board member who saw a similar tool demo at a conference and asked: "Are we doing this?"

**Discussion:** What went wrong here? (Not the staff member — the system. No policy, no ownership, no boundaries. The staff member made a reasonable choice in the absence of guidance.)

#### Core Teaching (25 min)

**Why governance comes first:**
- Without a board-level AI stance, staff make ad hoc choices that may conflict with mission and values
- Donors, accreditors (ECFA and equivalents), and partners will increasingly expect a stated position
- Having "nothing" is a growing liability — not because AI is dangerous, but because ungoverned use erodes trust
- Governance is the way your organization says: *our mission is not negotiable, and technology serves it or it doesn't enter*

**What governance must cover (minimum viable policy):**
1. **Scope and limits** — Where AI may and may not be used (e.g., administrative efficiency vs. formation, pastoral care, decisions about vulnerable populations)
2. **Ownership and approval** — Who owns AI use in each functional area; what requires leadership or board sign-off
3. **Vulnerable populations** — Clear, non-negotiable rules for data involving minors, families, counseling, or care
4. **Documentation** — An inventory of current and intended AI use so policy is grounded in reality, not abstraction

**The ordering principle:**
- Governance and safety → first
- Efficiency and operations → second
- Revenue and growth → third
- Quality and content → fourth
- You cannot responsibly pursue the later stages without the earlier ones in place

**Concept: One owner per area.**
- Scattered, uncoordinated use is the actual threat — not AI itself
- Every functional area (programs, fundraising, content, operations, formation) needs a named person responsible for AI use in that domain
- The board adopts the framework; the executive team executes; area owners enforce

#### In-Session Action (20 min)

**Exercise 1: The Inventory**

Working from the Shadow Inventory pre-work, each participant completes a structured inventory for their organization:

| Area | Current AI Use | Planned/Desired Use | Who Uses It | Who Approved It | Touches Vulnerable Populations? |
|------|---------------|---------------------|-------------|-----------------|-------------------------------|
| Administration | | | | | |
| Fundraising/Development | | | | | |
| Communications/Marketing | | | | | |
| Programs/Ministry | | | | | |
| Content/Curriculum | | | | | |
| HR/Internal | | | | | |

**Key insight:** Most organizations discover that AI is already in use — just without governance. The inventory makes visible what was invisible.

**Exercise 2: The Values Test**

Using the values statement they brought, participants write one sentence for each value that completes: "Our commitment to [value] means that AI in our organization must / must never..."

*Example: "Our commitment to community means that AI in our organization must never replace the human relationships through which formation happens."*

#### Reflection Questions (10 min, journaled)

1. What surprised you about your inventory? What use did you not expect to find?
2. Where is the gap between your stated values and your current (or absent) AI governance?
3. If your largest donor called tomorrow and asked "what's your position on AI?" — what would you say right now? What do you wish you could say?

#### Cohort Discussion (20 min)

- Each organization shares one finding from the inventory and one values-test sentence
- Group identifies common patterns: what governance gaps appear across multiple organizations?
- Facilitator names the pattern: "The absence of policy is itself a policy — it just says 'anything goes.'"

#### Take-Home Assignment

1. Complete the full inventory (all areas) with input from at least one colleague who wasn't in the session
2. Draft a **one-paragraph AI stance** for your organization (3–5 sentences: what AI is for in your context, what it is not for, who owns it). Bring this draft to Week 2.

### Week 2: Ethics in Practice — Where the Lines Are

**Theme:** Legal is not the same as ethical. Possible is not the same as permissible. The question is not "can we?" but "should we — given who we are?"

**Session arc:** Dissonance → Teaching → Action → Reflection

#### Opening Dissonance (15 min)

**Scenario:** An AI consultant offers your organization a donor intelligence tool. It cross-references your family database against public records (political donations, property values, LinkedIn profiles) to identify high-capacity donors you already know. The financial case is strong — potentially $200K–$700K over three years. Everything is technically legal. The question: When a parent signed a camp registration form, did they reasonably expect that you might run their name through a wealth-screening pipeline?

**Discussion:** Where is the line between "good stewardship of fundraising" and "surveillance of the people who trust us with their children"? Is this different from traditional prospect research? If so, where does the difference start?

#### Core Teaching (25 min)

**Five ethical dimensions for evaluating any AI use case:**

1. **Consent and reasonable expectation** — Do the people affected know their data is being used this way? Would they be surprised? Legal ≠ ethical. Public data ≠ ethical use.

2. **Proportionality and necessity** — Is this scope proportionate to our actual need? Are we collecting data on populations with no relationship to us? Does the organization need a regional intelligence database, or better research on the families it already knows?

3. **Power asymmetry** — Does this create an information advantage over the people we serve? Are we serving "from true empathy and love" or "from a position of superiority"? When we know more about someone than they know we know, the relational dynamic shifts.

4. **The mosaic effect** — Individual data points are benign. A name in a public filing. A property record. But when AI aggregates these into a unified dossier, the result is qualitatively different from any single data point. Would the person feel comfortable if they knew what the organization had assembled?

5. **Mission integrity and witness** — Does this use case advance or undermine what we claim to stand for? If our mission says "everyone belongs," but our AI sorts people by economic value, what does that say about who we actually think matters?

**The NAES-100 Scoring Framework (simplified for session use):**

Five dimensions, each scored 0–20:
- Legal and compliance foundation
- Consent and transparency
- Proportionality and necessity
- Mission alignment and values integrity
- Human oversight and relationship primacy

Score interpretation:
- 85–100: Green — proceed
- 70–84: Yellow-green — proceed with guardrails
- 55–69: Yellow — caution, do not proceed without board discussion
- 40–54: Orange — serious concern, major restructuring required
- Below 40: Red — do not proceed

**The traffic-light framework for content and authorship:**

- **Red zone (prohibited):** AI originating ideas, theology, or positions the organization hasn't expressed; publishing AI-generated writing as human-authored; generating content that obscures who is responsible for the ideas
- **Yellow zone (discernment):** AI-assisted visuals, diagrams, UI; requires transparency about AI involvement
- **Green zone (permitted):** Organizing, structuring, adapting existing content; format translation; indexing, tagging, archiving; editing for clarity while preserving voice

#### In-Session Action (20 min)

**Exercise 1: Score a Real Use Case**

Each organization identifies one current or proposed AI use case from their inventory (Week 1). Using the simplified NAES-100 rubric, they score it across all five dimensions.

Facilitator walks through scoring together for the first dimension, then organizations score independently.

**Exercise 2: The Trust Test**

For the same use case, answer the single most important question:

> *"If the people we serve knew exactly what we were doing with their information and how we were using AI, would they trust us more or less?"*

Write the honest answer. If the answer is "less" — that's not a reason to hide it. It's a reason to change the approach.

#### Reflection Questions (10 min, journaled)

1. Where did your use case score lowest? What does that reveal about the gap between your practice and your values?
2. Is there an AI use you've been excited about that you now need to reconsider? What changed?
3. What ethical line is non-negotiable for your organization — the one you'd hold even if crossing it were profitable?

#### Cohort Discussion (20 min)

- Each organization shares their use case score and the trust test answer
- Group discussion: Where do organizations share common ethical boundaries? Where do contexts diverge?
- Facilitator names the principle: "The ethical north star is not 'is this legal?' or 'is this effective?' but 'does this protect the trust we've been given?'"

#### Take-Home Assignment

1. Score two additional use cases from your inventory using the NAES-100 framework
2. Revise your one-paragraph AI stance from Week 1 to include at least one explicit ethical boundary
3. Identify the **one person** in your organization who should be the "formation gate" — the person who must approve any AI use that touches the people you serve directly

### Week 3: Codifying the Document — From Principles to Policy

**Theme:** A principle without a policy is a wish. A policy without ownership is a poster on a wall. This week, you write the actual document.

**Session arc:** Dissonance → Teaching → Action → Reflection

#### Opening Dissonance (15 min)

**Scenario:** An organization wrote a beautiful AI ethics statement. It was posted on the website. It said all the right things about human dignity, transparency, and responsible use. Six months later, a staff member used AI to generate curriculum content for a youth program. Another used it to draft donor thank-you letters with fabricated personal details. A third built an automated email sequence for lapsed donors. When leadership was asked about it, they pointed to the ethics statement. But the statement didn't say who owned AI in each area, what required approval, or what the consequences were for going outside bounds. The statement was aspirational. The organization needed operational.

**Discussion:** What's the difference between a values statement and a governance document? Why do organizations often stop at the first and never build the second?

#### Core Teaching (25 min)

**The anatomy of a board-ready AI governance document:**

A governance document is not an essay. It is a **decision record** that answers six questions:

1. **Where may AI be used?** (Scope — specific areas and use cases that are approved)
2. **Where must AI not be used?** (Limits — explicit no-go zones, especially for vulnerable populations, formation, and pastoral care)
3. **Who owns AI in each area?** (Ownership — named roles, not departments)
4. **What requires approval, and from whom?** (Approval paths — distinguish routine use from high-stakes use)
5. **How do we document and review?** (Accountability — inventory, annual review, and triggers for ad hoc review)
6. **What do we say when asked?** (External posture — one-voice answer for donors, accreditors, partners, and the public)

**Document structure (template):**

```
I.   Organizational Identity and AI (2–3 sentences connecting AI governance to mission/values)
II.  Scope of Approved Use (table: area → approved uses → owner)
III. Prohibited Use (explicit list, especially vulnerable populations and formation)
IV.  Ownership and Approval Matrix (who owns each area; what escalates to leadership/board)
V.   Data and Privacy Safeguards (what data may/may not enter AI systems; vendor requirements)
VI.  Content and Authorship Standards (traffic-light framework or equivalent; human review gates)
VII. Ethical Evaluation Process (how new use cases are assessed — NAES-100 or simplified version)
VIII.External Communication (one-paragraph public stance; who speaks for the org on AI)
IX.  Review Schedule (annual minimum; triggers for ad hoc review)
X.   Adoption and Effective Date (board adoption record)
```

**Common failure modes:**
- Too aspirational, not operational (values without owners)
- Too restrictive, doesn't match reality (bans tools already in use without offering alternatives)
- Too technical, not readable by board or staff (jargon-heavy, no clear guidance)
- No review mechanism (written once, never updated, slowly becomes irrelevant)
- No external posture (can't answer donors or accreditors when asked)

**The "one-page test":** If you can't summarize your governance stance in one page that a board member, a staff member, and a major donor could all understand — it's not ready.

#### In-Session Action (30 min — extended for this session)

**Exercise: Draft Your Governance Document**

Using the template above, each organizational team drafts their governance document in session. Facilitator circulates. This is the core deliverable of the course.

**Specific guidance per section:**

- **Section I:** Use your values-test sentences from Week 1
- **Section II:** Use your inventory from Week 1 to populate the "approved uses" column
- **Section III:** Use your non-negotiable ethical lines from Week 2
- **Section IV:** Use the "one owner per area" principle; if you don't have enough leaders to fill every row, that's fine — one person can own multiple areas, but it must be named
- **Section V:** Start with one rule: "No data involving [your vulnerable population] in public or unvetted AI systems"
- **Section VI:** Adopt the traffic-light framework or write your own version
- **Section VII:** Include the simplified NAES-100 or adapt it to your context
- **Section VIII:** Write the paragraph you wish you could have said in Week 1's reflection question
- **Section IX:** "Annually, or when a significant new use case or risk emerges"
- **Section X:** Leave blank — this is what the board fills in

#### Reflection Questions (10 min, journaled)

1. Where did you get stuck in the drafting? What section was hardest — and what does that reveal?
2. Does this document match who your organization actually is — or who you wish it were? Where's the gap?
3. Who in your organization needs to see this draft before it goes to the board? What conversation do you need to have?

#### Cohort Discussion (15 min)

- Each organization reads their Section I (identity and AI) and Section VIII (external posture) aloud
- Group feedback: Is it clear? Is it honest? Would a donor trust it? Would a staff member know what to do with it?
- Facilitator: "The document is alive. It will change. The point is to have a starting position that your board can adopt and your team can follow — not a perfect one."

#### Take-Home Assignment

1. Complete all sections of the draft governance document
2. Have at least one person outside the course (a board member, a senior staff member, or a trusted advisor) read the draft and give feedback
3. Prepare a **5-minute board presentation**: "This is our AI stance, this is why it matters, and this is what we're asking you to adopt." Bring this to Week 4.

### Week 4: Declaring and Sustaining — From Document to Culture

**Theme:** A governance document adopted by the board is the beginning, not the end. The real work is building a culture where the stance is lived, reviewed, and adapted — so that AI serves mission rather than drifting into ungoverned territory again.

**Session arc:** Dissonance → Practice → Reflection → Commissioning

#### Opening Dissonance (15 min)

**Scenario:** Two organizations adopt identical AI governance documents on the same day. One year later:

- **Organization A** trained staff, assigned owners, reviewed quarterly, updated the document when a new tool was proposed, and told their donors: "Here's how we govern AI." When a staff member found a great new AI tool for program evaluation, they knew exactly who to ask and what the process was. The board reviewed the stance at the annual retreat. It evolved.

- **Organization B** filed the document, emailed it to staff, and moved on to the next urgent thing. Six months later, half the staff had forgotten it existed. A new hire in marketing adopted three AI tools without checking. The VP of programs heard about it and panicked. The board was never briefed. The document was technically in force but functionally dead.

**Discussion:** What made the difference? (Not the document. The culture around it.)

#### Board Presentation Practice (30 min)

Each organizational team presents their **5-minute board presentation** to the cohort. The cohort acts as the board — asking questions, probing, pushing.

**Presentation structure:**
1. **Why now** (1 min) — What's at stake; why the board needs to act
2. **What we're proposing** (2 min) — Summary of the governance document (scope, limits, ownership, safeguards)
3. **What we're asking** (1 min) — Specific board action: adopt this document and schedule an annual review
4. **One example** (1 min) — A concrete scenario showing how this document guides a real decision

**Cohort feedback after each presentation:**
- Was the "why" compelling?
- Was the document summary clear enough for a board member to act on?
- What question would a skeptical board member ask — and can you answer it?

#### Core Teaching (15 min)

**Sustaining governance — five practices:**

1. **Train, don't just distribute.** Every staff member who uses or might use AI needs 30 minutes on the governance document — what's in bounds, what's not, who to ask. New hires get it in onboarding.

2. **Review triggers, not just schedules.** Annual review is the minimum. Also review when: a significant new AI tool is proposed, a use case moves into a new area (especially formation or vulnerable populations), an incident occurs, or the regulatory/accreditor landscape shifts.

3. **Name the formation gate.** One person (or role) in the organization is the final authority on any AI use that touches the people you serve directly — youth, families, counselees, students. This person can say no, and that no is respected.

4. **Build the one-voice answer.** Every leader who might be asked about AI by a donor, partner, or accreditor should be able to give the same 30-second answer. Practice it. Write it on a card if needed. Consistency is credibility.

5. **Normalize "I don't know yet."** New AI capabilities will emerge faster than policy can anticipate. The governance document should create a culture where the right response to "should we use this new thing?" is not "sure, why not" or "absolutely not" — but "let me check with [owner] and we'll evaluate it." That pause is the governance working.

**The maturity path forward:**

The governance document moves your organization from unaware/ungoverned to **Adoption (Level 3)** on the maturity model. From here:

- **Integration (Level 4)** means AI becomes structural in approved areas — not occasional, but part of how you do approved work, with ongoing review
- **Leadership (Level 5)** means you're not just governing your own use — you're helping peer organizations navigate theirs. You become a resource, a model, a voice in the sector

Neither of those is required. Some organizations will stay at governed adoption for years. That's not failure — it's stewardship at the pace that fits their context.

#### Reflection Questions (10 min, journaled)

1. What is the single most important thing your organization gained from this process — the document, or the conversation that produced it?
2. Where are you most likely to let governance drift? What's the weakest link in your sustainability plan?
3. Who in your organization will be the champion of this work after the course ends? Is that you? If not, who — and what do they need from you?

#### Cohort Commissioning (20 min)

Each organization makes a **public commitment** to the cohort:

1. **The document:** "We will present this to our board by [date]."
2. **The owner:** "The person responsible for AI governance in our organization is [name/role]."
3. **The review:** "We will review our AI stance by [date — no later than 12 months from now]."
4. **The culture:** "The one practice we will implement to make this alive (not just a document) is [specific action]."

Facilitator closes: "You came in with a gap — between your values and your practice on AI. You're leaving with a document, an owner, and a commitment. That's not the finish line. It's the starting position. But it's infinitely better than silence."

## Course Delivery Notes

### Facilitation Requirements
- Facilitator should have direct experience with AI governance in non-profit contexts — not a technologist explaining AI, but a practitioner who has built governance with real organizations
- Co-facilitator or assistant recommended for cohorts with more than 10 participants
- Session recordings available for participants who miss a week, but live participation is strongly preferred (the cohort learning is not replicable asynchronously)

### Platform Integration
- Pre-work and take-home assignments delivered through the Movemental platform
- Governance document drafting can happen in-platform or in participants' preferred tools
- Cohort discussion continues between sessions via platform discussion threads
- AI Lab available as a reference tool (participants can explore it to understand what "bounded, transparent AI" looks like in practice)

### Pricing Alignment
This course maps to **Sprint 3: Governance and Ethical Framework Development** in the Transformation System ($5,000 per organization). Can also be offered as:
- Standalone cohort course ($1,500–$2,500 per seat, 2–3 seats per organization recommended)
- Part of a bundled engagement ($15,000–$20,000 for the full 5-sprint Transformation System)
- Adapted for denominational or network cohorts (8–12 organizations, custom pricing)

### Outcome Metrics
- **Primary:** Percentage of participating organizations that present a governance document to their board within 90 days of course completion
- **Secondary:** Percentage that complete an annual review within 12 months
- **Tertiary:** Percentage that report being able to answer stakeholder questions about AI with confidence (self-reported, post-course survey)
