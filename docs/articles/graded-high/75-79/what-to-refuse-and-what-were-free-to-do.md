# Chapter 13: What to Refuse—and What We Are Free to Do

**Refusal is not Luddism.** It is **love with lines**.

There are uses of AI that **trade away** the human thickness of this work: accountable presence, truth told in relationship, prayer as address to God, formation that costs time. There are other uses—boring, holy uses—that **free** leaders to show up where only a body can.

The boundary is **vocational**, not a superstition about electrons.

This chapter names **lanes**; chapter seven named the **seasons** of an organization’s learning curve. Some lines below are **permanent**—never simulate **intimacy that belongs to face-to-face care** or ship words nobody owns. Others work as **temporary fences** during structured experimentation: many teams pause **publishing** AI-affected finished voice until disclosure and review norms are real, and pause feeding **private** counseling-related, HR, or donor payloads into tools their policy and IT stack have not cleared. Permission to learn does not mean permission to **exploit surprise**. When a fence comes down, it should be because you **documented** what you learned—not because the calendar bullied you into looking “current.”

## Refusals (starting list, not closed canon)

**1. Publishing without a responsible human**  
Fully automated pipelines that ship words nobody has weighed are not “efficiency.” They are **moral outsourcing**.

**2. Simulating care you are not actually present to carry**  
If a reasonable reader thinks they are in **relationship with you** and they are mostly in relationship with a model, you have crossed from amplification into **impersonation**. For movement leaders, **approval** of AI-affected public voice belongs with the leader and whatever human circle they have deputized—not with the default settings of a vendor.

**3. Synthetic intimacy**  
Care, confession, and direction belong to **covenantal presence**. A chat window can **support** reflection; it cannot **carry** the full weight of a **person** alone.

**4. Theology without verification**  
Models hallucinate citations and flatten nuance. Traditioned humans must verify claims about God, Scripture, and the lives of neighbors—the **almost-right** failure (chapter eight) hits doctrine with unusual cruelty.

**5. Deception**  
Hiding material AI involvement where a fair reader would care violates **integrity**—the same integrity chapter ten traced when Americans punish **post-hoc** discovery of AI in political speech, news, and faith-shaped advice. The homework there is not “never touch a model.” It is **do not make people guess** what passed through one.

**6. Ungoverned data in the wrong container**  
Caseload notes, counseling summaries, personnel files, and donor histories are not generic “prompt material.” If your maturity season still has **no** clear boundary for what may leave your systems, the refusal is blunt: **do not treat consumer tools like a private vault** until governance says otherwise.

## Theological integrity (non-negotiable)

For movement leaders, **God-talk is never decorative**. When a model smooths a hard edge of Scripture, elides tensions your tradition has learned to hold honestly, or invents a citation that *sounds* learned, the failure is not only factual—it is **spiritual**. People learn what God is like from the sentences you let through.

That does not mean “never draft with assistance.” It means **claims about God, Scripture, and the lives of neighbors stay under human judgment** tethered to whatever rule of faith your community actually runs—**elders or board, supervisors, theological peers**—not under whatever completion the model favors this week.

When integrity slips, you rarely get a headline scandal first. You get the **almost-right** paragraph (see chapter eight): fluent enough to teach, wrong enough to misshape hope. The repair is not only “better prompts” but **accountable circles** willing to delete sentences that scan clean and still miss the mark.

## Freedoms (and some obligations)

You are **free**—often **obliged**—to use assistance for:

- **Scheduling, calendaring, first-pass transcription**  
- **Draft outlines you will rewrite**  
- **Literature sweeps you will verify**  
- **Translation support reviewed by bilingual leaders**  
- **Accessibility passes** (alt text, readability) **checked by humans**  
- **Documented internal experiments**—breaking prompts, stress-testing drafts in private sandboxes, logging “near misses”—so your team learns **before** those lessons become a public correction letter

Within the lanes above, you are also free—indeed wise—to **amplify** work you have already done: make corpus and teaching **findable**, invite **networked correction** (the scenius instinct from chapter nine), and **disclose** assistance where a fair reader would care (chapter ten)—so freedom does not smuggle in secrecy.

These are not “less spiritual.” They are **stewardship**—if they buy an hour with a teenager in crisis, buy the hour.

## Denominational weather (landscape, not unanimity)

Public Christianity is **arguing in real time**, not issuing a single FAQ. Useful **orientation points** for staff conversations—not **consensus proofs**—include bodies that have published AI-ethics language you can compare against your own polity (always pull **current** text before citing them in public):

- **Southern Baptist Convention** and related agencies—resolutions and guidance in recent cycles urging caution, disclosure, and human accountability with generative tools.  
- **World Council of Churches**—ecumenical process and statements on AI ethics (early-2020s onward).  
- **Rome Call for AI Ethics** (2020; Pontifical Academy for Life and partners)—a compact human-dignity frame many Catholic and secular institutions still reference.

Use them as **conversation starters**, not cudgels. Your **bishop, presbytery, board, or elders** remain the decisive voice for what your people are bound to hear as faithful.

## Nonprofits: money words need more fear of God, not less

Grant narratives, impact metrics, and donor letters are **high-trust genres**. AI may help outline—but **numbers and claims** pass through finance and program officers. The same **private-data** discipline that protects a **counseling or case-management** note protects a beneficiary story pulled from a case file: if it identifies a vulnerable person, it is not disposable training fodder. **Refuse** “pretty false.” **Freely** automate the meeting that used to steal the evening you needed to sleep.

## Refusal as formation

When a community practices **public refusals**—calmly, with reasons—it teaches **discernment** more than a policy PDF ever will. When it practices **public freedoms**—also with reasons—it teaches **stewardship** without shame. The capstone of the maturity picture in chapter seven is still rare: leaders who **demonstrate** mature use across an organization others can imitate. Refusals and freedoms named out loud are how you move from slides about ethics to **embodied** credibility.
