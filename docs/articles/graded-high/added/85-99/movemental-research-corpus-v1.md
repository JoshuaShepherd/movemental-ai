## 1. Executive summary

The verification pass produced a corpus that is in most cases **stronger** than what Movemental has been citing, with a small number of revisions and one important correction.

**What is verified.** The Virtuous and Fundraising.AI **2026 Nonprofit AI Adoption Report** holds up exactly as cited: **92%** nonprofit adoption, **7%** reporting major capability improvement, **81%** individual use without shared workflows, **47%** with no governance policy, **n=346**, released **February 16, 2026**. The **Forvis Mazars** February 2026 framing of AI governance as a board fiduciary responsibility is also verified, with a more precise reading available now. **FBI IC3** data on AI-enabled fraud and voice cloning is verified for **2025** and is materially larger than most of the sector understands (**$893M** in AI-attributed losses in 2025 alone, plus a **400%+** jump in voice-cloning fraud cited by industry sources).

**What needs correction.** The **"91% of church leaders use AI"** claim attributed to Lifeway Research April 2026 is **wrong as stated** and needs to be replaced. Lifeway's actual **September 2025** phone survey of **1,003** Protestant pastors (released April 21, 2026) found that **42%** of pastors use AI for ministry (**10%** as regular users, **32%** experimenting), with **56%** non-users. The **91%** figure does not appear in the Lifeway report. The closest comparable number comes from the **Barna/Pushpay State of Church Tech 2026** report: **60%** of church leaders personally use AI at least monthly, but only **33%** report their church using AI in operations. The **"9% have a formal AI policy"** figure also needs correction: the actual number from Barna/Pushpay is **5%** of churches with an established AI policy. The **"25% of churches encountered AI scams"** claim cannot be located in either Lifeway document and should be **dropped** or replaced with verified deepfake incident reporting.

**What is new and stronger.** Five sources should be added to the corpus immediately: the **Barna/Pushpay 2026 State of Church Tech** report (**n=1,306** church leaders); **Barna/Gloo State of the Church 2026** series on AI as spiritual authority (**n=1,514** U.S. adults, November 2025); the Vatican's **Antiqua et Nova** (January 28, 2025) as a primary theological source; the **BCG Widening AI Value Gap** (September 2025, **n=1,250**) and **McKinsey State of AI 2025** (**n=1,993**) for the cross-sector adoption-to-value gap; and **MIT NANDA The GenAI Divide** (July 2025), which documents a **95%** pilot failure rate. Together these sources turn Movemental's **"adoption without capability gain"** thesis from a single-study claim into a **converged finding** across nonprofit, enterprise, and faith-sector research.

**The strategic implication.** The data is stronger for Movemental's argument than the existing pitch suggests. The pattern is now documented across at least **four independent 2025–2026 studies**: widespread adoption, a **5–7%** high-performer cohort, and a governance gap that consistently predicts who lands in the high-performer cohort. The **"formation precedes deployment"** thesis sits well inside the empirical literature.

## 2. AI adoption rates by sector

### 2.1 Nonprofits

**Source 2.1.1: Virtuous & Fundraising.AI, *The 2026 Nonprofit AI Adoption Report*** | February 16, 2026 | **VERIFIED**

**Key findings.**

- **92%** of nonprofits use AI in some capacity
- **79%** report small to moderate efficiency gains

## 3. Governance and policy gaps

**Source 3.1: Forvis Mazars, *AI Governance for Nonprofit Boards*** | February 13, 2026 | **VERIFIED**

Authored by Karen Bodach and Dan Prater. Frames AI governance as a fiduciary expansion of the board's traditional duties of Care, Loyalty, and Obedience.

**Recommendations.**

- Resist the "efficiency trap"
- Address algorithmic blind spots through bias audits
- Codify responsibility using a framework such as NIST AI RMF
- Recruit board members with technology and data governance experience
- Require vendor accountability
- Incorporate AI risk into enterprise risk management

Cites NPQ ("How Nonprofits Can Resist the AI Efficiency Trap," October 28, 2025) and Council of Nonprofits ("Board Roles and Responsibilities," 2025) as supporting governance literature.

**URL.** https://www.forvismazars.us/forsights/2026/02/ai-governance-for-nonprofit-boards

**Source 3.2: Forvis Mazars, *AI Strategy: A Road Map From Readiness to Implementation*** | March 2026 | **NEW** supporting source

**Key findings.**

- Cites Financial Executives International 2026 report: **88%** of organizations regularly use AI in at least one business function
- Notes "major" AI concerns dropped from **50%** in 2024 to **36%**, suggesting maturing risk practices

**URL.** https://www.forvismazars.us/forsights/2026/03/ai-strategy-a-road-map-from-readiness-to-implementation

**Methodological note on the "47–76% no-governance" range.** Both endpoints are real but they come from **different surveys**.

- **47%** is the Virtuous/Fundraising.AI **2026** figure for nonprofits with **no AI governance policy** (**n=346**)
- **76%** is the TechSoup **2025** figure for nonprofits **without a formal AI strategy** (**n=1,321**), distinct from a governance policy

These should be cited **side-by-side** as a range of governance gaps, **not** as a single metric.

**Methodological note on "81% individual staff AI use without organizational policy."** The precise Virtuous wording is: **"81% of organizations report using AI individually and on an ad hoc basis."** Use this wording.

**Source 3.3: ECFA (Evangelical Council for Financial Accountability)** | Standards last formally revised pre-AI | **NEW** context

**Key context.**

- ECFA accredits ~**2,700** Christian nonprofit organizations against Seven Standards of Responsible Stewardship
- Founded 1979; standards focus on doctrinal integrity, board governance (minimum 5 members, majority independent), audited financials, transparency, fundraising integrity, conflicts of interest
- As of May 2026, ECFA has **not** published a binding AI standard or accreditation requirement
- This is a real gap and an opportunity for Movemental to position alongside or ahead of ECFA action

**URL.** https://www.ecfa.org/About.aspx

## 5. The capability gap

The pattern of widespread adoption with limited capability gain is now documented across at least **six** independent 2025–2026 studies, converging on a high-performer cohort of roughly **5–7%**.

| Source | Adoption / exposure | High-performer cohort | Sample |
| --- | --- | --- | --- |
| Virtuous / Fundraising.AI 2026 (nonprofits) | **92%** | **7%** major capability improvement | **n=346** |
| McKinsey State of AI 2025 (cross-sector) | **88%** | **6%** (>5% EBIT impact, significant value) | **n=1,993** |
| BCG Build for the Future 2025 | (varies) | **5%** future-built; **60%** no material value | **n=1,250** |
| MIT NANDA GenAI Divide July 2025 | **80%+** piloted | **5%** of pilots achieve revenue acceleration | 150 interviews, 350 surveys, 300 deployments |
| Barna/Pushpay 2026 (churches) | **60%** leader use, **33%** church use | **5%** with AI policy | **n=1,306** |
| TechSoup/Tapp 2025 (nonprofits) | **85.6%** exploring | **24%** with formal strategy | **n=1,321** |

**What predicts the high-performer cohort.** McKinsey identifies **workflow redesign** as the single strongest correlate of AI EBIT impact: high performers are **3.6x** more likely to pursue transformative change and **55%** have fundamentally redesigned at least some workflows, vs. roughly **20%** sector-wide. BCG's "future-built" cohort is differentiated by an AI-first operating model, business-IT co-responsibility, and modular architecture. Virtuous identifies the same pattern in nonprofits: organizations with shared workflows and outcome measurement are the ones reporting major capability gains, while **65%** are stuck in reactive individual use.

**The translation for missional organizations.** The data supports the claim that the gap between adoption and capability is primarily an **organizational, formation, and workflow problem** rather than a tools problem. This is the strongest empirical foundation available for Movemental's **"formation precedes deployment"** thesis.

## 6. Adult learning and formation research

### 6.1 Kolb's experiential learning theory

**Source 6.1.1: David A. Kolb, *Experiential Learning: Experience as the Source of Learning and Development*** | Original 1984; second edition 2014 | **VERIFIED** foundational source

Four-stage cycle: Concrete Experience → Reflective Observation → Abstract Conceptualization → Active Experimentation. Foundational text for "learning by doing" framing in adult and organizational learning.

**Source 6.1.2: Bergsteiner, Avery, & Neumann, *Studies in Continuing Education*** | 2010 | **NEW**

## 11. Methodological caveats and use notes

Three caveats to apply when citing this corpus.

**Sample composition.** The strongest empirical results come from samples that overrepresent larger and more digitally mature organizations. Virtuous's **346**-organization sample skews toward customers and prospects of a fundraising platform; Barna and Pushpay's **1,306** church leaders came roughly half from each organization's network. Both are valuable but are **not** random national probability samples. Lifeway's **1,003**-pastor sample is the cleanest methodologically (stratified random, weighted phone survey, ±3.3% margin of error) and should be the anchor for any specifically **Protestant-pastor** claim.

**Metric incommensurability.** Several of the most striking enterprise figures (the BCG **5%**, the McKinsey **6%**, the MIT NANDA **95%**) measure **different things**. BCG is measuring "value at scale" across **1,250** firms; McKinsey is measuring EBIT impact **>5%** across **1,993** firms; MIT NANDA is measuring revenue acceleration from individual pilots across a much smaller sample. They tell a consistent directional story but the specific numbers are **not interchangeable**. When citing, **name the specific metric**.

**Faith-sector incident prevalence.** The deepfake/voice-cloning incident reporting is documented in news sources (WIRED, VICE, Gizmodo, OSV News, ASIS, KnowBe4) and the AI Incident Database, but there is **not yet** a peer-reviewed or government tally of religious-sector incidents specifically. The qualitative case for harm is strong and growing; a specific national prevalence rate for the church sector is **not yet available**. Treat any single percentage claim about "what share of churches have encountered an AI scam" as **unverified** until a primary survey publishes one.

**General use principle.** Apply this corpus the same way a careful analyst would: cite the **primary source**, name the **specific metric**, and acknowledge **sample composition**. The thesis Movemental is making (that adoption is broad, capability gain is rare, governance is the bottleneck, and formation is the missing input) is supported by the strongest **2025–2026** research available across nonprofit, faith, theological, and enterprise sectors. The few specific statistics that need correction are corrected in Section 10.
