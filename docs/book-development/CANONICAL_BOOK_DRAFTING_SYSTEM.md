## SECTION 1 — KNOWLEDGE MAP SUMMARY

### Top 10 Core Claims

1. **Credibility is collapsing as a signal, not as a concept.** AI lowers the cost of producing plausible expertise to near zero. This raises the burden of verification for audiences and the burden of proof for genuine experts. The crisis is not that credibility doesn't matter — it's that the old signals (volume, polish, institutional backing) no longer reliably indicate it.

2. **AI is an anthropological challenge, not a technological one.** Drawing on Heifetz's adaptive/technical distinction: technical problems have known solutions; adaptive challenges require learning, loss, and cultural change. Movement leaders are better positioned for this than technologists — but only if they engage rather than abstain.

3. **Scenius is the proposed credibility solution.** Brian Eno's term for the collective genius of a scene. Applied to credibility: when multiple verified humans publicly cite, build on, and vouch for each other's work, the signal is exponentially harder for AI to replicate than any individual's output. This is Movemental's most distinctive intellectual contribution — but it is an analogical extension, not established theory. Peer-reviewed literature on "scenius" is nearly nonexistent (65 works in OpenAlex, mostly music/cultural studies).

4. **Amplification, not replacement.** The foundational operating principle. AI handles structure, formatting, distribution (the 70%); humans provide unique insight, lived experience, theological judgment, and voice (the 30%). The ratio is a governance heuristic, not an empirically validated optimum.

5. **Voice preservation is a non-negotiable priority, ranked above efficiency.** RAG + optional PEFT/LoRA heads + human gate is the honest technical stack. "Voice fidelity scores" are composite internal metrics, not clinical diagnostics. The core risk is the "almost-right" failure: doctrinal miscalibration that sounds fluent.

6. **Formation is not information.** Mezirow's transformative learning, Smith's cultural liturgies, and Palmer's pedagogy all support this conceptually. The Four Necessities (dissonance, action, reflection, community) are theoretically plausible but not validated as a specific battery. Eight weeks is a product design choice, not an empirically optimal window.

7. **Embodied leadership cannot be automated.** AI can simulate conversation but not create real relationship; generate prayer-like language but not pray; provide information about spirituality but not form people. This is the theological ceiling on AI use.

8. **Transparency about AI use builds trust — but disclosure architecture matters.** Pew 2025: 71% would view a political candidate less favorably on learning AI wrote a speech they liked. For religious content, disclosure norms should be tested with the specific audience, not assumed to follow entertainment patterns.

9. **Traditional publishing economics are extractive for authors, but the comparison must be precise.** Authors Guild 2023: median full-time author book income $10,000. Trade royalties run 10-15% of list. But "publisher keeps 85-90%" conflates royalty rate with operating profit. Substack charges ~10%, not 70-85%. The differentiation argument must center on ownership, infrastructure, and formation — not fee arithmetic.

10. **The convergence of credibility crisis + publishing disruption + scenius networks + movement infrastructure is a kairos reading, not an empirical law.** It is an integrated design thesis backed by separable evidence threads. The steel-man objection: four perennial Christian anxieties dressed as venture inevitability.

### Top 10 Risks / Overclaims to Avoid

1. **"68% can't distinguish AI content"** — No primary source found. Nearest: Pew 53% low confidence (different question), All About Cookies 77% (n=1,000 Prolific panel, not controlled task). Remove or replace immediately. A book about credibility cannot cite unverifiable statistics.

2. **"Substack/Patreon extract 70-85%"** — Directly contradicted. Both charge approximately 10%. This claim must be corrected before any external use.

3. **"28x-500x reach multiplier"** — Internal scenario model, not independently validated. Label as illustrative projection, not research finding.

4. **"Publisher takes 85-90% as profit"** — Conflates royalty rate with operating profit. Defensible as shorthand for "author's share of list price" but misleading if read as extraction.

5. **Scale vs. bounded network contradiction** — Strategy docs project 1,000+ users while positioning claims a 100-leader cap. These cannot coexist in public materials without explicit resolution.

6. **Voice fidelity scores presented as clinical diagnostics** — They are composite internal metrics. Authorship attribution (identifying which of k known authors wrote something) is solved NLP. Generative fidelity (does new text faithfully extend the author?) is not.

7. **Five-stage AI maturity model presented as uniquely validated** — MIT CISR and Gartner already publish comparable 4-5 stage models. Movemental's is a useful heuristic, not a scientific stage theory.

8. **Gender composition of candidate pool (82% male / 18% female)** — Mirrors the biased system Movemental claims to improve upon. Network homophily under a 100-person cap is self-reinforcing. Cannot be left unaddressed in credibility claims.

9. **"AI is an anthropological crisis, not a technological one"** — Rhetorically powerful but literally false. The platform Movemental builds is technological and carries procurement, security, and legal obligations. Frame as "primarily" or "fundamentally" anthropological, not exclusively.

10. **Convergence as Henry Jenkins means it** — Jenkins's "convergence culture" is a different, prior use of the same term. Risk of inherited academic authority without corresponding rigor. Define Movemental's usage explicitly.

## SECTION 2 — EDITORIAL & EPISTEMIC RULES

### Voice & Style

**Tone:** Prophetic-pastoral. Guide and fellow traveler. Calm, grounded, non-sensational. The author is inside the problem, not above it ("Dunning-Kruger on steroids. Including me.").

**Sentence structure:** Mix short declarative sentences with longer qualified ones. Short sentences land. Use "I want you to know / see / hear this" for key points. Name the reader's likely thought or feeling before answering it.

**Honesty markers:** "I don't know." "We're still learning." Self-correction in text ("About four weeks. No — actually, a week from start to finish"). Honest uncertainty is a credibility signal, not a weakness.

**Specificity:** Name "movement leaders, pastors, you" — not "stakeholders." Replace every abstract summary with a who/what/when. Concrete examples over conceptual frameworks whenever possible.

**Avoid absolutely:**
- Exaggerated claims or unsourced statistics
- Startup-style grandiosity ("revolutionize," "disrupt," "game-changing")
- Motivational filler ("You've got this," "The future is bright")
- AI tell-tales: "It's important to note," "In today's landscape," "When it comes to," "At the end of the day," "certainly," "indeed," "clearly," "leverage," "robust," "holistic," "deeply," "genuinely"
- Triplet/parallel-list overuse
- Vague intensifiers — replace with stronger nouns and verbs

**Format:** Short to medium paragraphs. Strong conceptual clarity. Minimal jargon. No fluff. A manuscript arguing for voice preservation cannot itself sound AI-generated — this sets a higher bar than most books.

### Epistemic Rules

1. **Never present a directional claim as universal.** "AI tends to lower verification costs" — not "AI destroys credibility."

2. **Replace vague claims with mechanisms, constraints, and conditions.** NOT: "AI destroys credibility." BUT: "AI lowers the cost of producing plausible expertise, which raises the burden of verification."

3. **If a claim has been weakened in the research docs, use the corrected version.** The research corpus contains explicit corrections. Honor them.

4. **Distinguish three epistemic tiers:**
   - **Known:** Peer-reviewed, replicated, or based on large-n primary data (Pew, Authors Guild, Ahrefs)
   - **Inferred:** Theoretically grounded, directionally supported, but not empirically validated as a specific claim (scenius as credibility mechanism, Four Necessities, 70/30 rule)
   - **Strategic belief:** A design choice or kairos reading backed by separable evidence but not provable (convergence thesis, 100-leader cap as optimal, eight-week formation window)

5. **Avoid fake precision.** No "70-80%" unless explicitly supported by a named study with stated methodology. Replace with "most," "a majority," or cite the actual figure with its source.

6. **Every statistic must have a traceable primary source.** If no source can be found, soften to qualitative language or remove. The following are verified and citable:

   | Claim | Figure | Source |
   |-------|--------|--------|
   | AI detection confidence | 53% not confident | Pew, Sep 2025, n=5,023 |
   | AI content provenance importance | 76% very/extremely important | Pew, Sep 2025 |
   | Multimodal synthetic detection | ~50% (near chance) | Cooke et al. CACM 2025, n=1,276 |
   | New web pages with some AI content | 74.2% | Ahrefs, Apr 2025, 900k pages |
   | Median full-time author book income | $10,000 | Authors Guild 2023, n=5,699 |
   | Clergy comfortable with AI-written sermons | 12% | Barna via NPR, Jul 2025 |
   | Clergy see merit in AI for sermon prep | 43% | Barna via NPR, Jul 2025 |
   | AI disclosure negative reaction (politics) | 71% less favorable | Pew, Jun 2025, n=5,023 |
   | Global epistemic anxiety | 58% concerned about true vs. false | Reuters DNR 2025 |
   | Religious audiobook growth | +17% | APA/Edison, Jun 2024 |

7. **Frame the author's own tools honestly.** Voice fidelity scores are internal rubrics, not clinical diagnostics. The 70/30 rule is governance design, not empirical optimum. The maturity model is a heuristic, not a validated stage theory.

8. **Acknowledge what the book cannot resolve.** "AI as both problem and solution" is a tension the book lives in, not resolves. Say so explicitly.

### Part I — The Crisis and the Reframe

#### Chapter 1: The Credibility Crisis
- **Purpose:** Establish that something fundamental has changed about how expertise is verified in public
- **Core argument:** AI lowers the cost of producing plausible expertise to near zero. The old signals — volume, polish, institutional affiliation — no longer reliably indicate genuine knowledge. This is not a future problem; it is the present condition.
- **Supporting concepts:** Pew 53% detection confidence, Ahrefs 74.2% AI-detected content prevalence, Cooke et al. near-chance detection, Reuters 58% epistemic anxiety, NewsGuard 3,000+ content farms
- **Risks to avoid:** "Credibility collapse" as if it's a scholarly term (it's coined); catastrophizing; unsourced "68%" statistic; implying that AI content is uniformly bad
- **Audiences:** Universal

#### Chapter 2: AI Is Not a Technological Challenge
- **Purpose:** Reframe AI from a tools problem to an adaptive leadership challenge
- **Core argument:** Technical problems have known solutions. Adaptive challenges require learning, loss, and cultural change. AI is the latter. This means non-technical leaders are not disqualified — they may be better positioned than technologists to lead the response.
- **Supporting concepts:** Heifetz adaptive/technical distinction, Fernandes et al. 2026 (AI users gain performance but lose calibration), "Dunning-Kruger on steroids"
- **Risks to avoid:** Dismissing technological competence as irrelevant; overstating the adaptive frame (the platform Movemental builds IS technological); claiming non-technical leaders are inherently superior
- **Audiences:** Universal

#### Chapter 3: Where We Are and Why Now
- **Purpose:** Situate the reader in the specific 2025-2026 moment — speed of adoption, audience posture shifts, SEO/GEO landscape change
- **Core argument:** The window for thoughtful engagement is narrowing. The audience has shifted from "overwhelmed" to "I get it, it's dumb" — both are risky postures. ChatGPT as fastest-adopted consumer technology in history creates urgency without panic.
- **Supporting concepts:** GEO research (Aggarwal et al., KDD 2024), SEO folded in as context (EEAT as alignment framework, not ranking score), speed-of-adoption data, audience posture analysis from pre-writing synthesis
- **Risks to avoid:** Date-specific predictions that will age poorly; treating GEO as a settled playbook (it's early research); "70-80% left on the table" without sourcing
- **Audiences:** Universal

### Part III — The Framework

#### Chapter 5: AI as Credibility Amplifier, Not Credibility Faker
- **Purpose:** Introduce the foundational reframe: AI can amplify genuine expertise rather than fake it
- **Core argument:** The same technology that enables cheap imitation also enables powerful amplification of real substance — but only when coupled with human judgment, voice preservation, and transparency. The distinction between amplification and replacement is the book's central operating principle.
- **Supporting concepts:** 70/30 governance heuristic, voice preservation priority ranking, corpus-trained agents, the "both problem and solution" tension
- **Risks to avoid:** Resolving the "both problem and solution" tension too neatly; presenting 70/30 as empirically validated; implying amplification is risk-free
- **Audiences:** Universal

#### Chapter 6: Amplification Not Replacement
- **Purpose:** Operationalize the principle — what does amplification look like in practice vs. what replacement looks like
- **Core argument:** Amplification preserves the human as source, decision-maker, and voice. Replacement delegates those functions to AI. The boundary is not about the quantity of AI involvement but about where agency sits.
- **Supporting concepts:** Orbit Media 2025 (full AI drafting correlates with weakest self-reported performance), Barna clergy split (12% comfortable writing sermons vs. 43% for prep), concrete workflow examples
- **Risks to avoid:** Making the boundary sound obvious when it's genuinely hard to maintain under production pressure; ignoring that "amplification" can slide into replacement gradually
- **Audiences:** Universal

#### Chapter 7: The Maturity Model and Why Experimentation Matters
- **Purpose:** Give readers a developmental framework for their own AI engagement — and insist that you cannot responsibly abstain without having experimented
- **Core argument:** Abstinence without experience is not wisdom; it's avoidance. The maturity model provides stages from awareness through responsible integration. You earn the right to set boundaries by first understanding what you're bounding.
- **Supporting concepts:** Movemental five-stage model (contextualized against MIT CISR and Gartner comparables), Fernandes et al. on calibration loss with AI use, green/yellow/red framework
- **Risks to avoid:** Presenting the five stages as uniquely validated (they're a heuristic); implying that more AI adoption is always better; dismissing concerns of those at early stages
- **Audiences:** Universal (expanded for churches, who may face institutional resistance to experimentation)

#### Chapter 8: Voice Preservation as Priority
- **Purpose:** Establish why preserving the leader's authentic voice matters more than efficiency
- **Core argument:** Voice is not a style preference — it is the carrier of earned authority. When AI produces fluent text that sounds almost right, the risk is doctrinal miscalibration wrapped in confident prose. Voice preservation is a theological commitment, not a technical feature.
- **Supporting concepts:** RAG + PEFT/LoRA technical stack (honest description), Pew 71% negative reaction to disclosed AI authorship, "almost-right" failure mode, Ippolito et al. on human vs. machine detection divergence, clergy comfort data
- **Risks to avoid:** Claiming voice fidelity is a solved problem; presenting internal metrics as industry-standard; conflating authorship detection (solved) with generative fidelity (unsolved)
- **Audiences:** Universal (expanded for movement leaders with large written corpora)

#### Chapter 9: Scenius as the Credibility Solution
- **Purpose:** Introduce the book's most distinctive contribution — network-embedded credibility as the answer to individual signal collapse
- **Core argument:** When AI can produce plausible expertise cheaply, the remaining hard-to-fake signal is years of substantive, cross-referenced, community-embedded work among verified humans. Scenius is the design pattern for this.
- **Supporting concepts:** Eno/Kelly origin, Wenger's Communities of Practice, Haas's epistemic communities, Ostrom's commons governance, citation graph theory, Sybil resistance as problem-kinship
- **Risks to avoid:** Treating "scenius" as established theory (it's an analogical extension with ~65 papers in OpenAlex); claiming networks are immune to gaming (they are not — the honest pitch is "costlier to fake, not impossible"); assuming the 100-leader bounded network automatically produces scenius

#### Chapter 10: Transparency, Disclosure, and Trust
- **Purpose:** Argue that transparency about AI use is a trust signal, not a liability — while acknowledging the complexity of disclosure
- **Core argument:** In a low-trust environment, proactive disclosure is a differentiation strategy. But disclosure architecture — when, how, and in what context — matters as much as whether you disclose. One-size-fits-all transparency policies are insufficient.

- **Core argument:** Here is what happened when I actually did everything this book recommends. Some of it worked. Some of it didn't. Here is what I learned, what surprised me, and what I got wrong.

### Iteration Plan: How Drafts Should Be Reviewed and Refined

**Round 1 — Internal consistency check (after each chapter draft)**
- Does this chapter use verified statistics only? Cross-check against the epistemic rules table.
- Does it avoid overclaims flagged in the Knowledge Map (Section 1)?
- Does it maintain the voice established in the Preface?
- Does it honor the "both problem and solution" tension without resolving it?

**Round 2 — Cross-chapter coherence (after each phase completes)**
- Scan for repeated arguments — if a point is made in full in one chapter, it should be referenced, not restated, in others
- Check transitions — does each chapter's closing setup match the next chapter's opening?
- Verify that the epistemic tier (known/inferred/strategic belief) is consistent across chapters for the same claim

**Round 3 — Contradiction resolution (after full draft exists)**
- The 100-leader cap vs. 1,000+ expansion phase — must be explicitly resolved or acknowledged
- The "primarily anthropological" claim vs. the technology being built — must be precisely qualified everywhere
- Fee comparison claims — must be verified against 2026 live pricing for all named competitors
- Gender composition (82/18) — must be addressed somewhere, not just in internal research

**Round 4 — Voice audit (after contradiction resolution)**
- Read each chapter aloud or with text-to-speech
- Apply the anti-slop checklist from BOOK_VOICE_AND_STYLE_GUIDE.md
- A manuscript arguing for voice preservation cannot sound AI-generated

**Round 5 — Remix production (after canonical is final)**
- Apply the modulation tables from Section 5
- Expanded sections should be drafted by re-engaging the relevant chapter prompt with the audience lens
- Minimized sections should be trimmed to 40-60% of original length with a summary reference

### Integration Plan: How Chapters Merge, Expand, and Adapt

**Canonical → Edition workflow:**
1. Finalize canonical text (all 21 chapters + preface)
2. Tag each paragraph with audience relevance: [universal], [ML-primary], [NP-primary], [CH-primary]
3. For each edition, execute the modulation table:
   - **Unchanged:** Copy directly
   - **Expanded:** Draft additional 1,500-3,000 words per expanded chapter using the chapter prompt's remix instructions
   - **Minimized:** Reduce to summary + reference to canonical (keep core argument, cut examples)
   - **Reframed:** Rewrite opening framing and key examples for the target audience; keep core argument intact
4. Each edition gets a unique foreword (500-800 words) addressing the specific audience
5. Appendices are edition-specific:
   - ML Edition: content workflow templates, agent configuration guide
   - NP Edition: board presentation template, governance framework, compliance checklist
   - CH Edition: congregational discussion guide, denominational position summary, liturgical integration examples

**Cross-edition consistency rules:**
- Core arguments must be identical across editions — only examples, emphasis, and depth change
- Statistics and sources must be identical (no edition-specific number rounding)
- The voice must be the same person in all editions — not a marketing persona adjusted for segment
- If a correction is made in one edition, it must propagate to all three
