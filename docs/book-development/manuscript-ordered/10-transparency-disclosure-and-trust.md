# Chapter 10: Transparency, Disclosure, and Trust

A donor opens your annual letter. The cadence is perfect—maybe too perfect. She wonders, not angrily, **what here passed through a model**. If she asks and you freeze, you have already answered.

Transparency is not a sticker for virtuous brands. In a low-trust information environment, **how you disclose** shapes whether people hear **integrity** or **damage control**. Chapter nine argued that credibility is partly **communal**; this chapter argues it is also **accountable**—a ledger readers can inspect.

## Disclosure can build—or burn—trust

Chapter eight already cited Pew’s June 2025 American Trends Panel work (*n* = 5,023) and the Barna figures summarized by NPR: post-hoc AI discovery in content people already liked **lands hard** on speech-like genres, and Protestant clergy **split** between comfort with AI for **prep** versus **writing** sermons. I will not replay every percentage here; the disclosure point is the same: **surprise reads like concealment**, even when nothing was intended.

The **same** Pew research season adds bruises that shape **architecture**, not only tone. In Pew’s broader science release on how Americans view AI’s impact, **76%** of U.S. adults call it extremely or very important to tell AI- from human-made pictures, video, and text, while **53%** are not too or not at all confident they can tell ([Pew Research Center, Sep 17, 2025](https://www.pewresearch.org/science/2025/09/17/how-americans-view-ai-and-its-impact-on-people-and-society/)). People are asking for **provenance** into a mirror they do not trust. Disclosure meets an **epistemic hunger** your comms can either feed or frustrate—not only a ethics checkbox.

In that same report, Americans draw a hard line on **faith-shaped** assistance: **73%** say AI should play **no role** in advising people about faith in God. However you draw that boundary in your tradition, your readers already live inside that instinct.

**Architecture matters as much as intent:**

- **Proactive** lane markers beat **gotcha** discovery.  
- **Specific** beats **vague** (“AI assisted with structure and citation checks on draft 2”).  
- **Accountable human** remains visible (“I approved every statistic”).  
- **Channel-aware** norms: what your board packet needs differs from a short-form social post.

**Experimentation vs. publish-tier:** Organizational learning can stay messy in private—but **publishing** AI-generated or undisclosed AI-affected content is a different risk class than drafting. One defensible pattern matches chapter seven: **moratorium on ship** while you document use cases, then **written principles** before the same tone scales across channels. That is not secrecy for cowards; it is **sequencing integrity** so your first public footprint is not an accident you later have to retract.

## A trust lens: Mayer, Davis, Schoorman

Mayer, Davis, and Schoorman’s classic integrative model (1995) defines **trust** under risk as grounded in perceived **ability, benevolence, and integrity**. In workshops I sometimes hear teams list **trust, expertise, character, platform**—that maps **unevenly** but usefully onto Mayer: **expertise → ability**; **character → integrity / benevolence**; **platform → governance artifacts** Mayer did not specify. Use canonical academic language when researchers are in the room; use your shorter list when you are teaching live—**so long as you never treat “platform” as a substitute for benevolence**.

Trust is also **contextual**: people trust differently on Instagram than in a therapy office. Disclosure should track those **gradients**. Where **integrity** is at stake, Mayer’s model rewards **inspectable** commitments—versioned policies, named approvers, repair when you miss—not vibes.

## Cautionary tale: persona without embodiment

In 2024, **Catholic Answers** launched a chatbot presented with a priestly persona (“Father Justin”) that collided with community norms around **sacramental language and clerical embodiment**; backlash was swift; the product direction shifted. The lesson for this book is not “never experiment.” It is: **when the voice implies spiritual authority, disclosure and role boundaries are not cosmetic**. Similar dynamics can hit **any** mission-driven brand that lets a model **wear** a personally intimate tone it cannot actually carry.

## Transparency is more than badges

Emerging work on **AI content labels** suggests labels can shift **attribution** without reliably fixing **persuasion** for some message types. Good disclosure therefore includes **inspectable evidence**: primary sources, named authors, process pages, and—where relevant—**who vouched** publicly and when.

For anything that functions like **scholarship or journalism** in your orbit, scientific publishing’s COPE posture is a useful import: **AI tools are not authors**; use should be **disclosed**; humans remain **accountable for every line** ([Committee on Publication Ethics, AI and authorship](https://publicationethics.org/guidance/cope-position/authorship-and-ai-tools)). You are not submitting to *Nature*—but your donors, boards, and younger staff increasingly expect **that** standard of traceability on high-stakes claims.

Parts of this manuscript were **drafted and structured with AI assistance**. I remain responsible for its arguments, its stories, and its statistics—and for correcting places where enthusiasm outran evidence. That sentence is not performative; it is **the minimum honesty** this chapter demands from others.

## Synthesis

Transparency is **ongoing practice**: update the policy when the tool changes; apologize when you miss; show the **repair**. If chapter nine named **communal credibility**, chapter ten names the **public accounting** without which communities sour. Build for readers who **want** provenance and **distrust** their own detection—and who treat surprise as a kind of betrayal. Remember sacred contexts where supermajorities want **no** simulated spiritual counsel. That is the weather; last quarter’s tool is not.
