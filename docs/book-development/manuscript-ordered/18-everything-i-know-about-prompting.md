# Chapter 18: Everything I Know About Prompting

Let me tell you how I began: **badly.**

Early prompts were **wishful haiku**—“warm, missional, prophetic, not cheesy.” The model obliged: **cheese with better vocabulary.** I learned on my own dime that **vague adjectives multiply platitudes**; **concrete constraints multiply usefulness**.

This is not a prompt-engineering certification. Models and interfaces keep shifting; tactic lists rot quickly. What follows are **principles**—ways of thinking that outlast a given release note.

## The best prompt is a clear mind

Before you type, answer three questions on paper:

1. **Audience** — Who reads this, and what do they fear?  
2. **Job** — Decide / draft / critique / compress / research—which one?  
3. **Done** — What would “good enough to edit” look like in **one sentence**?

If you cannot answer those, **no magic suffix** will save you.

That posture matches what I have said elsewhere in plain language: **good prompting is humane communication**—clarity, context, iteration, and a refusal to confuse cleverness with care. It also matches what **chapter seventeen** insisted about formation: the tool answers clearly only when **you** know what “formed” would look like for a real person in a real room.

## Discipline is not the same as performance

Here is the honest tension my own archive keeps surfacing.

I have spent real time turning a mountain of **LLM conversation exports** into something usable: **master documents**, **numbered extraction prompts**, **“check the processed list before you run this again”** rules, and **append-only evidence logs**. When I point a model at that work, I do not whisper poetry at it. I write like I am briefing an archivist: **scope** (only employers this run), **inputs** (these JSON files in context—do not assume others), **non‑negotiables** (no fabrication; every claim traceable; merge instead of overwriting fragile notes), and **outputs** (exact files, headings, bullet shape).

That is not the opposite of humane prompting. It is what humane prompting looks like when the stakes are **someone’s livelihood**, **public credibility**, or **a book that will be quoted back to you**.

So yes—sometimes I have opened with explicit meta-framing (“act as an expert prompt engineer…”) when I needed a **scaffold** I could then edit. The craft is not the swagger. The craft is **knowing when you are building a scaffold** and when you are **performing expertise for an audience of one**.

Another pattern my archive reflects: I move from fog to **structure** when fog is expensive—**taxonomies**, **routing**, **naming for outcomes**, even **visual schema** of where AI should and should not intervene. The prompt that mattered was often not a paragraph of tone words; it was **the diagram first**, then the instructions that guard the diagram.

The maturity map in **chapter seven** is the organizational mirror of the same instinct: **experiment** in bounded space, **document** what you learned, and keep **publish-tier** work under different rules than private drafting. Good prompts inherit that split—they do not pretend every message is a press release.

## Principles that keep helping

**Give stakes, not vibes.**  
Replace a vague request for “warm, caring tone” with “comfort someone whose parent just entered hospice; avoid clichés; do not offer explanations for suffering.”

**Ask for process visible to you.**  
“Show assumptions; mark uncertain claims with [VERIFY]; cite only sources I provide.”

**Single-purpose runs beat kitchen-sink prompts.**  
One job per message—extract employers here, achievements there—so the model is not secretly solving six problems and averaging them into mush.

**Iterate like a colleague, not a slot machine.**  
“Second paragraph is too confident—rewrite with hedged language and one question to the reader.”

**Force role separation.**  
“You are editor, not author: do not add new claims; only tighten my sentences.”

**Use negative space.**  
Ten lines of “do not” beat ten adjectives of “please be excellent.”

**Treat packaged intelligence as a product decision.**  
When I have configured **vendor-packaged assistants** (the kind of bounded “custom” workspace a platform lets you save), the work was less about mystical diction and more about **bounded jobs**, **clear user outcomes**, and language a tired leader could actually find.

## Judgment > templates

The skill that matters is **evaluation**: spotting **almost-right** teaching claims, **false consensus**, **hallucinated citations**. Practice by **red-teaming** your own outputs weekly—five minutes, ruthless friend, coffee paid for.

## Humility as technique

Say “I do not know” in the prompt when you do not. Models mirror confidence; **your uncertainty is data** the model can use to slow down.

## Why this chapter is near the end—not the start

If you began here, you might imagine AI is **primarily a wording problem**. After this book’s arc, you know it is **a credibility, vocation, and community problem** with wording symptoms.

Still: **clear language is love**. People parse sloppy prompts the way they parse **vague instructions that did not bother to be clear**—**they feel the disrespect**.

And still: **prompting is a kind of interface design**—not interface as “dark patterns,” but interface as **how intelligence meets obligation**. The words are where care becomes legible to a machine.
