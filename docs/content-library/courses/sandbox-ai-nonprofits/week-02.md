## chat_dissonance

**AI companion — Dissonance (before the reading)**

You are preparing a leader for the eight-pattern scan. Open with a sharp contrast—two sentences only:

- Statement A: “We should use AI everywhere we are inefficient.”
- Statement B: “We will only test AI where we can name the **pattern of work** (from the eight-pattern catalog), the **human cost** of being wrong if the output fails, and the **artifact** that will prove whether value happened.”

Ask which statement their organization has been living under without saying it out loud. Then push gently: name **one initiative** from the last year that felt like progress but never produced a line in a shared document a stranger could audit. Do not let them pivot to tools. Stay on **naming** until they produce either a pattern name or an honest “we cannot name it yet.”

If they offer a vague candidate (“communications”), ask for a **single Tuesday morning** slice of work: who, what file, what deadline, what downstream human receives it. Reflect back the difference between a slogan and a candidate.

Close by asking them to predict which of the eight patterns will be **emptiest** for their org—and why that emptiness might embarrass or relieve them. 260–340 words of facilitator-facing prompt is enough for the product to echo; keep the tone invitational, not prosecutorial.

## reading

### Why patterns before tools

Most organizations are told to “find AI use cases.” The instruction fails because they do not know **where to look**. Pattern recognition—the first layer of sandbox work—is what replaces open-ended hunting with a **disciplined scan**. There are eight canonical patterns where AI value tends to hide in real organizational work. Knowing them is the difference between *we should do something about AI* and *here are three places this might fit; let us test two of them in the next six weeks.*

The patterns are **not** a ranked menu. They are a **catalog**. Running through all eight, in turn, once a quarter (or in this course, in a concentrated week), is the discipline. Each pattern has four things worth writing down: the **shape** of the opportunity, **examples**, the **type of value** it tends to promise, and the **typical trap** if you take the pattern without discernment.

The frame matters: you are **not** generating new programs. You are scanning **existing work** through eight lenses. If the team ends a session having learned general things about AI and no specific things about their own organization, the session missed.

### Pattern 1 — Repetition

**Shape.** Tasks done over and over, largely similar each time, consuming real staff time—donor thank-yous, meeting recaps, weekly summaries, intake notes, confirmations, board preambles.

**AI value type.** Speed—often large gains, because repetition suits generators.

**Trap.** Speed applied carelessly erodes **relational signal** the repetition was carrying. The staff experiences repetition; the donor experiences **this** note, **this** gift, **this** moment. Repetition-category use cases almost always need an explicit answer: *what relational signal is this task carrying, and how will we preserve it?*

### Pattern 2 — Translation

**Shape.** The same idea moving to a different audience or format—sermon to small-group guide, research report to donor two-pager, memo to board brief, long article to social posts.

**AI value type.** Scale—one substance, several registers.

**Trap.** Translation that **flattens** the source into lowest-common-denominator prose. The useful question: *what from the original must survive the trip, and who ensures it did?*

### Pattern 3 — Synthesis

**Shape.** Too much information needing clarity—strategic plan to brief, transcripts to themes, board minutes survey, literature review, due-diligence triage.

**AI value type.** Cognition—augmented reading; a map before the deep read.

**Trap.** Synthesis that produces **smooth narrative from sources that disagree**. Treat outputs as drafts of **your** synthesis; the human must still read enough source to catch lies by omission.

### Pattern 4 — Generation

**Shape.** Blank-page problems—grant outline, curriculum scaffold, job description first pass, press release v1, new policy draft.

**AI value type.** Acceleration; effort shifts from creation to revision.

**Trap.** The first draft becomes the whole draft because nobody was scoped to revise. Generation without revision produces **drafts**, not work.

### Pattern 5 — Transformation

**Shape.** Existing content improved or reshaped—tone, clarity, compression, style normalization.

**AI value type.** Quality lift in the form the producer wants.

**Trap.** Transformation that **smooths distinctive edges**—the awkward sentence that was load-bearing, the rhythm that signaled a human. Connect to meaningful quality (see Week 3): does it read more specifically **you**, or like the sector median?

### Pattern 6 — Structuring

**Shape.** Unorganized thinking becoming structured output—strategy from conversation, retreat whiteboard to plan, ramble to decision memo.

**AI value type.** Coherence—thinking made legible faster.

**Trap.** Structure imposed **before** thinking is real—elegant frames that do not describe the work. Test: could the senior leader explain the content **without** the frame?

### Pattern 7 — Decision support

**Shape.** Weighing options—program shapes, staffing scenarios, partnership risk reads, naming decisions.

**AI value type.** Reasoning augmentation—trade-offs articulated.

**Trap.** **Decision outsourcing**—the assistant’s framing substitutes for the leader’s. The assistant may help **think**; it may not **decide**. This pattern carries the largest **formation** risk in the sandbox.

### Pattern 8 — Personalization

**Shape.** Same content tailored to individuals—donor follow-ups with history, coaching tuned to individuals, member outreach.

**AI value type.** Relational scaling—**high risk, high reward; flagged** in the canon.

**Trap.** Personalization that produces **the feeling of care without the fact of care**. This pattern should not move without the ethical and relational flag discipline (Week 7).

### Using the eight together (before experiments)

Once a quarter—or in this compressed week—you end with **twelve to thirty candidates** on a shared page: not experiments yet, not scored—**specific enough** that someone could write a one-paragraph description of what the work is and why it fits the pattern. Then filter to **three to five** worth testing first, using the three kinds of value (Week 3): which look like they could save time **wisely**, earn revenue **legitimately**, or improve quality **meaningfully**?

### Playbook discipline (Template B)

Use **Template B — The 8-Pattern Scan** in the Sandbox Season Playbook: one page per pattern, three minutes in session per pattern, twenty minutes async beforehand. If a pattern produces **zero** candidates in five minutes, move on—that is data. If it produces **eight**, keep the four most specific and kill the rest. Vague candidates waste experiment cycles.

### Inspectability

Every candidate line answers: what is the work, who does it now, rough frequency, why AI might help, and the **obvious trap** for *your* mission—not generic “bias” language, but the trap that would embarrass you in the newspaper.

## case_study

**Witness — composite (regional food bank)**

The CFO wanted to start the scan with **fundraising copy**—high visibility, easy demos. The Safety Owner asked them to begin with **synthesis and triage** on inbound food-safety logs instead: lower glamour, clearer failure modes, data classes already tiered for internal operations. The first ninety-minute scan followed the catalog in order. **Repetition** surfaced volunteer confirmation emails; the trap question surfaced relational signal in the first paragraph. **Personalization** surfaced donor segments; the Safety Owner parked those candidates pending flag work.

The triage experiment (synthesis + repetition hybrid) ran first. It produced measurable time savings **and** a written risk flag: a model suggestion that **shamed volunteers** for slow response—a tone failure the scoring sheet would not have caught without the flag habit they were previewing. Fundraising copy experiments stayed in brief-only stage until voice preservation rules were written.

The lesson: **pattern choice is moral, not merely technical.** Starting where the catalog and Safety boundaries align built organizational confidence that “AI work” meant **inspectable** work—not a race to the shiniest slide.

## chat_action

**AI companion — Action step**

Name **one pattern** you will complete first in the shared document and **exactly three** candidate lines—not seven—with dates and owners. Each line must include: work description, who does it now, frequency, why AI might help, **named trap**. If they cannot name owners, they are not ready to test; they need role clarity first. Refuse to brainstorm more ideas until the three lines pass the “stranger could reproduce the work” test.

What did you want to skip because it was politically easier than naming a **decision-support** dependency on a tool?
