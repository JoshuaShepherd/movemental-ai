## reading

### The four dimensions (none optional; none sufficient alone)

Every completed experiment gets scored on:

**1. Time saved.** Real minutes, net of review and correction, against a **fair** baseline—not “felt faster.” If they cannot produce a number, default **neutral**; unverifiable savings are where inflation starts.

**2. Quality improved.** Named reviewer (not the owner), criteria agreed **before** output, **written paragraph** verdict—not “acceptable,” but **meaningful improvement on human-only baseline** with evidence.

**3. Risk introduced.** Categorical honesty: confidentiality, accuracy, authorship, voice, relational. Did anything belong in an **incident register**? Asking five categories beats asking “any risks?” once.

**4. Repeatability.** Could another staff member follow the recipe? Artisanal brilliance is not organizational evidence.

Greens are **independent**—one must not pull the others along.

### Why self-scoring fails

Owners see savings and miss quality drift; they see intended quality and miss voice shift; they see “no obvious risk” and miss the category they were not looking for. Scoring needs **second eyes**: close enough to know good, far enough to have no stake in the verdict.

### Counteracting inflation

**Precommitted standards**—criteria written before the run, not chosen after to match output.

**Default-to-yellow** when uncertain; yellow is information, not failure.

**Out-loud-for-one:** for each green, someone asks: *what would have to be true for this to be wrong?* If nobody can answer, the score is too confident.

### What a low score means

A low score is the sandbox doing its job. The goal is not a wall of greens—it is a few **high-confidence** greens, honest yellows, and clear reds that close wrong directions **before** incidents.

> The goal is not a wall of green scores. It is a small number of high-confidence greens and a clear set of reds that closed the door on wrong directions.

### How scores aggregate

They feed the **portfolio** (what graduates, what returns, what stays parked), Safety’s **incident register** (early warnings), and later **Skills** (training on validated workflows). The sandbox is a **sensor**; scores are readings, not a report card.

Structural dimensions can all be green while **human** dimensions are red—that is why Week 7’s flag pass exists; scoring alone is necessary, not sufficient.

## case_study

**Witness — youth-serving org**

A use case touched narratives adjacent to minor-protected populations—not prohibited by a careless read of policy, but **wrong** for season scope. Peer scoring was enthusiastic about time saved on internal summaries. An external reviewer (program lead who did not run the experiment) wrote a yellow on **quality**—tone flattening—and a paragraph on **relational** risk the rubric had not named.

The Safety Owner **parked** the use case without debate. The board later heard not about “blocked AI” but about **judgment preserved trust** while adult-only operations experiments continued. The headline was restraint; the legacy was credibility.
