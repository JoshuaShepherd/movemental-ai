# AI Discovery Lab


Movemental’s Discovery Lab exists because most organizations are about to encounter a shift in capability that they are not structurally prepared to absorb. Artificial intelligence is not simply another tool to be learned; it is a general-purpose capability layer that can reshape how work is done across nearly every function of an organization. And yet, for most nonprofit teams, the current approach to AI is either passive consumption—reading articles, attending webinars—or fragmented experimentation, where individuals try things in isolation without structure, shared learning, or accountability.

The Discovery Lab is designed to replace that fragmentation with a coherent, practice-based system for developing AI capability across a team.

⸻

At its core, the Discovery Lab is not a course in the traditional sense. It is a guided environment for structured experimentation. The goal is not simply to teach people what AI can do, but to help them discover—through direct use—what it can do in the specific context of their role, their organization, and their actual work.

This distinction matters. AI maturity does not come from theoretical understanding. It comes from repeated, contextualized use—where individuals encounter real problems, attempt solutions, refine their approach, and gradually develop judgment. The Discovery Lab creates the conditions for that process to happen intentionally rather than accidentally.

⸻

The course begins by reframing AI as something closer to a thinking partner than a tool. Participants are introduced to the idea that AI can assist with:
	•	generating and refining written content
	•	analyzing data and identifying patterns
	•	structuring ideas and processes
	•	supporting decision-making
	•	automating repetitive tasks

But rather than overwhelming participants with possibilities, the Lab immediately moves into action.

Each participant identifies a set of candidate use cases within their own work. These are not abstract ideas, but specific, recurring tasks:
	•	drafting grant proposals
	•	summarizing meeting notes
	•	organizing donor data
	•	creating program materials
	•	responding to common inquiries

From there, the Lab introduces a structured approach to experimentation.

⸻

Every experiment follows a consistent pattern:
	1.	Define the task
What specific piece of work are you trying to improve or replace?
	2.	Establish a baseline
How long does this currently take? What is the quality like?
	3.	Design the AI interaction
What prompt or workflow will you use?
	4.	Run the experiment
Use AI to complete the task.
	5.	Evaluate the outcome
Compare time, quality, and effort to the baseline.
	6.	Refine and repeat
Adjust the approach and test again.

⸻

This structure does two things simultaneously. First, it ensures that experimentation is disciplined and measurable. Second, it creates a shared language across the team. Everyone is not just “trying AI”; they are running experiments that can be compared, discussed, and improved collectively.

⸻

Over time, these experiments begin to cluster into three primary categories of value:

⸻

1. Time Saved

These are use cases where AI significantly reduces the time required to complete a task.

Examples include:
	•	drafting first versions of documents
	•	summarizing long reports
	•	generating structured outlines
	•	automating repetitive communications

These are often the easiest wins. They do not require deep changes to how work is conceptualized; they simply make existing processes faster.

However, the Discovery Lab treats these not as trivial improvements, but as strategic signals. When an organization discovers that a task that once took two hours can now be completed in twenty minutes, the question is not just “how do we save time?” but:

“What should we do with the time we are reclaiming?”

⸻

2. Work Quality Improved

In many cases, AI does not just save time—it improves the quality of output.

Examples include:
	•	clearer, more structured writing
	•	better-organized data
	•	more comprehensive research synthesis
	•	more consistent tone and formatting

These improvements are often subtle but cumulative. Over time, they can raise the overall standard of the organization’s work.

The Lab encourages participants to evaluate quality explicitly. This is not about subjective impressions, but about asking:
	•	Is this clearer than what I would have produced?
	•	Is it more complete?
	•	Is it easier for others to use?

By documenting these improvements, organizations begin to see that AI is not just a productivity tool, but a quality amplifier.

⸻

3. Revenue Generated (or Potentially Generated)

This is the most strategically important category, and often the least immediately obvious.

In a nonprofit context, “revenue” may take multiple forms:
	•	increased donations
	•	more effective grant applications
	•	improved donor engagement
	•	new program offerings

The Discovery Lab helps participants identify where AI can influence these outcomes.

For example:
	•	using AI to draft and refine grant proposals more efficiently, increasing the number submitted
	•	analyzing donor data to identify high-potential relationships
	•	creating personalized outreach materials at scale
	•	developing content that attracts new supporters through search

These use cases require more creativity and intentionality. They are not simply about doing the same work faster, but about doing work that was previously not possible or not feasible at scale.

⸻

A key part of the Discovery Lab is helping participants learn how to find these revenue-generating use cases.

This involves asking:
	•	Where are we currently limited by time or capacity?
	•	Where do we know there is opportunity, but we are not acting on it?
	•	Where does better information or communication lead directly to increased funding or engagement?

By mapping these questions to AI capabilities, participants begin to identify experiments that have a direct connection to organizational sustainability.

⸻

As experiments accumulate, the Lab introduces a system for tracking and documenting outcomes.

Each experiment is logged with:
	•	the task
	•	the baseline time and quality
	•	the AI approach used
	•	the result
	•	the estimated time saved
	•	any observed impact on quality or revenue

Over time, this creates a growing body of evidence. Patterns emerge. Certain use cases prove consistently valuable. Others are discarded.

This documentation is critical. Without it, the organization is left with anecdotes. With it, they have data that can inform decisions about:
	•	workflow changes
	•	role design
	•	resource allocation

⸻

This leads to one of the most important moments in the Discovery Lab.

Organizations often begin to realize that hundreds or even thousands of hours of work could be saved annually through AI-assisted processes.

At that point, the question shifts from experimentation to strategy.

If large portions of technical, repetitive work can be handled more efficiently, what does that mean for:
	•	staffing
	•	role definitions
	•	priorities

The Lab does not prescribe a single answer, but it does create the space for these questions to be addressed intentionally rather than reactively.

In many cases, the most constructive response is not to reduce headcount, but to reallocate human effort toward areas that require:
	•	judgment
	•	relationship
	•	creativity
	•	leadership

In other words, AI handles the technical layer, while humans focus on the relational and strategic layers.

⸻

Alongside these practical considerations, the Discovery Lab places significant emphasis on human, ethical, and harm-related questions.

Every experiment includes a reflective component:
	•	What are the risks of using AI in this context?
	•	Could this introduce bias, error, or misrepresentation?
	•	Are there privacy concerns?
	•	Does this change the nature of the work in a way that affects people?

These questions are not treated as abstract ethics discussions. They are grounded in actual use cases.

For example:
	•	If AI is used to analyze donor data, what are the boundaries of acceptable use?
	•	If AI drafts communications, how do we ensure authenticity and accuracy?
	•	If tasks are automated, how does that affect staff roles and expectations?

By embedding these reflections into the experimentation process, the Lab ensures that ethical considerations are not an afterthought. They become part of how the organization learns and grows.

⸻

Another important outcome of this process is the development of organizational AI maturity.

AI maturity is not defined by how many tools an organization uses, but by:
	•	how well it understands where AI is useful
	•	how consistently it applies it
	•	how thoughtfully it navigates risks
	•	how effectively it integrates AI into workflows

The Discovery Lab accelerates this maturity by creating a shared, structured learning experience. Instead of a few individuals becoming “AI experts,” the entire team develops a baseline level of capability and understanding.

⸻

By the end of the course, organizations have:
	•	a documented set of AI use cases
	•	clear evidence of time saved and quality improved
	•	initial insights into revenue-generating opportunities
	•	a shared framework for ongoing experimentation
	•	a deeper understanding of ethical considerations

More importantly, they have shifted from a passive stance toward AI to an active one.

They are no longer asking:

“What is AI, and why does it matter?”

They are asking:

“Where should we apply this next, and how do we do it well?”

⸻

This is the deeper purpose of the Discovery Lab.

It is not simply to teach a set of tools or techniques. It is to help organizations develop the capacity to learn, adapt, and make informed decisions in a rapidly changing environment.

In a moment where AI capabilities are advancing quickly, that capacity may be more valuable than any single use case.

And for nonprofits in particular—where resources are constrained, stakes are high, and impact matters—the ability to systematically discover and apply new capabilities is not a luxury.

It is becoming essential.
