# Fundraising Course


Movemental’s Fundraising course exists because most nonprofits are attempting to raise money without a system that matches the relational reality of how funding actually works. They rely on scattered lists, partial knowledge of their donors, occasional campaigns, and a handful of key relationships that are often underdeveloped. Even when there is effort and goodwill, the process is inconsistent, opaque, and difficult to scale.

The course is designed to change that by helping organizations build a structured, intelligence-driven fundraising system—one that makes visible what is currently hidden, organizes what is currently fragmented, and enables action where there is currently hesitation.

⸻

At the beginning of the course, most organizations believe their primary problem is something like “we need more donors” or “we need better messaging.” While those may be partially true, the deeper issue is almost always this:

They do not have a clear, organized understanding of the relationships and opportunities already within reach.

Fundraising, especially at higher levels, is not primarily about broad outreach. It is about relational proximity—who knows whom, where trust already exists, and how those relationships can be engaged appropriately. The problem is that this relational layer is usually invisible or underutilized.

The first phase of the course focuses on making that layer visible.

⸻

Participants begin by gathering and consolidating their existing data:
	•	donor lists
	•	event registrations
	•	email contacts
	•	internal relationship knowledge
	•	board and staff networks

In many cases, this information exists but is scattered across spreadsheets, CRMs, inboxes, and personal memory. The course brings it into a single, structured environment.

From there, AI is introduced as a tool for organizing and enriching this data. Rather than simply storing names, the system begins to surface patterns:
	•	professional roles (e.g., CEOs, physicians, executives)
	•	potential capacity indicators
	•	shared connections between individuals
	•	geographic and social clusters

This is not about replacing human judgment, but about augmenting it. Leaders often have intuition about who might be a strong supporter, but that intuition is rarely supported by a comprehensive view of the data. The system provides that view.

⸻

One of the most powerful moments in the course often comes when participants see their data transformed into a map of relational opportunity.

For example, a list of hundreds or thousands of contacts can be analyzed to identify:
	•	individuals with significant financial capacity
	•	connections between those individuals and existing staff or board members
	•	clusters of influence within certain industries or communities

This does not create relationships where none exist, but it reveals relationships that were previously unnoticed or undervalued.

⸻

The next phase of the course focuses on activation.

Once high-potential individuals are identified, the question becomes:

How do we engage them in a way that is appropriate, effective, and aligned with our mission?

This is where many organizations struggle. Even when they know who they should be talking to, they are unsure how to initiate or deepen those conversations.

The course addresses this by helping participants develop:
	•	personalized outreach strategies
	•	clear narratives about their work and impact
	•	structured follow-up processes

AI plays a role here as well, assisting in drafting communications, organizing information about each contact, and ensuring consistency across outreach efforts. However, the emphasis remains on human interaction. AI supports preparation and clarity; it does not replace relationship-building.

⸻

Another key deliverable of the course is the creation of a fundraising dashboard.

This dashboard becomes the central system for managing fundraising activity. It includes:
	•	prioritized lists of potential donors
	•	notes on relationships and interactions
	•	suggested next steps
	•	tracking of outreach and responses

Rather than relying on memory or disconnected tools, the organization now has a living system that reflects its fundraising efforts in real time.

⸻

As the course progresses, participants begin to see fundraising not as a series of isolated actions, but as a coherent process:
	1.	Identify relational opportunities
	2.	Prioritize based on capacity and connection
	3.	Prepare thoughtful, informed outreach
	4.	Engage in conversation
	5.	Follow up consistently
	6.	Track and refine the process

This process is simple in principle, but difficult to execute without structure. The course provides that structure.

⸻

A critical aspect of the Fundraising course is helping organizations understand how to find revenue-generating use cases for AI within their context.

In general, revenue-generating AI use cases fall into three categories:

⸻

1. Increasing Volume of High-Quality Effort

AI allows organizations to do more of what already works.

For example:
	•	drafting more grant applications without increasing staff time
	•	reaching out to more potential donors with personalized messaging
	•	maintaining consistent follow-up across a larger set of relationships

The key insight is that many organizations are not limited by opportunity, but by capacity. AI expands that capacity.

⸻

2. Improving Targeting and Prioritization

AI helps organizations focus their efforts where they are most likely to succeed.

For example:
	•	identifying individuals with higher giving capacity
	•	recognizing patterns in past donations
	•	surfacing connections that increase the likelihood of engagement

This reduces wasted effort and increases the efficiency of fundraising activities.

⸻

3. Enabling New Types of Engagement

In some cases, AI makes it possible to do things that were previously impractical.

For example:
	•	creating tailored content for different donor segments
	•	building systems that nurture relationships over time through content and communication
	•	integrating fundraising with broader content and engagement strategies

This is where fundraising begins to connect with other parts of the Movemental system, particularly content and pathways.

⸻

Throughout the course, participants are encouraged to run structured experiments, similar to those in the Discovery Lab.

Each experiment might focus on a specific question, such as:
	•	Can AI help us identify higher-capacity donors more effectively?
	•	Can we increase response rates with more personalized outreach?
	•	Can we reduce the time required to prepare grant applications?

These experiments are documented, and their outcomes are evaluated in terms of:
	•	time saved
	•	response rates
	•	funds raised or influenced

Over time, this creates a clear picture of what is working and where to focus.

⸻

As with the Discovery Lab, the Fundraising course also integrates ethical reflection.

Fundraising involves sensitive data and personal relationships. The use of AI introduces additional considerations:
	•	How do we ensure privacy and data protection?
	•	Where is the line between insight and intrusion?
	•	How do we maintain authenticity in communication?
	•	How do we avoid reducing people to data points?

These questions are addressed directly, not as abstract concerns, but as practical considerations within the system being built.

⸻

By the end of the course, organizations have:
	•	a structured, enriched dataset of their contacts and relationships
	•	a clear prioritization of high-potential donors
	•	a functioning fundraising dashboard
	•	documented outreach strategies and processes
	•	initial evidence of what works through experimentation

More importantly, they have a shift in perspective.

They no longer see fundraising as:
	•	unpredictable
	•	dependent on a few individuals
	•	difficult to scale

Instead, they see it as:

a system that can be understood, improved, and sustained

⸻

One of the most important outcomes is confidence.

Leaders often hesitate in fundraising because they feel uncertain—about who to approach, how to approach them, or whether their efforts will be effective. The course reduces that uncertainty by providing clarity, structure, and evidence.

⸻

Ultimately, the Fundraising course exists because the resources organizations need are often closer than they realize. The challenge is not always finding new people, but understanding and engaging the people already connected to them.

By making those connections visible, organizing them into a system, and supporting action with both human judgment and AI capability, Movemental enables nonprofits to approach fundraising with a level of clarity and effectiveness that is rarely achieved through traditional methods.

It is not a shortcut. It is a restructuring of how the work is done.

And in a context where funding determines what is possible, that restructuring can be transformative.
