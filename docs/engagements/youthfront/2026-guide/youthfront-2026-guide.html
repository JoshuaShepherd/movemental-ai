1. Building and maintaining a single source of truth

A single source of truth (SSOT) for a nonprofit is not one PDF — it is a disciplined system: authoritative documents, clear ownership, version dates, and explicit separation between (a) what the organization says about itself, (b) what public records prove, and (c) what partners or vendors propose.

This guide demonstrates that pattern:

  • Layer public vs. internal research. Official statements and URLs sit beside Form 990, GuideStar, and ECFA. Internal research (stakeholder profiles, pipeline metrics) is labeled with last updated timestamps and may lag reality.
  • Prefer primary sources. Legal name, EIN, board composition, and fiscal data should be traced to IRS filings and the organization’s own pages — then rechecked periodically.
  • Name the compiler and method. Agentic research accelerates discovery; it does not replace staff review for personnel details, pastoral facts, or anything that could affect people’s privacy or reputation.
  • Track gaps explicitly. A healthy SSOT includes a “what we don’t know yet” register so leaders know where human input is required (see §8).

3. Mission, vision, values, and intention

Mission (stated). “Bringing youth into a growing relationship with Jesus Christ.” Wider vision language on Youthfront materials includes belonging across dividing lines; neighborhood programs are described as helping “unmake the dividing walls” and imagining community without walls.

Formation posture. Youthfront describes holistic, missional environments for Christian formation — emphasizing that transformation is the Spirit’s work, “not a formula,” and that staff create space, time, dialogue, hospitality, and community so young people can discover and experience God.

Church relationship. The organization presents itself as church-assisting: camps, training, resources, and initiatives are framed as assisting churches in contextualizing the gospel, not replacing local congregations.

Core values (strategic) as summarized in internal governance copy: Community, Trust, Theological Reflection, Playfulness, and Christian Formation, Prayer and Ministry — with practices such as shared prayer rhythms, communal life, and ministry “from true empathy and love.”

Sources: youthfront.com Our Story, Mission & Vision, Our Work, Our History; internal values and vim markdown in yf-np-dashboard _docs/_public/content/governance/.

4. Programs: Camp, Neighborhood, and Family

YF Camp

Two camp properties (historically Circle C / Camp West and L-Bar-C / Camp LaCygne) totaling more than 1,000 acres. Overnight and day camps for grades 1–12. Public materials describe camp as a “theological playground”: prayer rhythms, imaginative Scripture engagement, and viewing children and youth as participants in God’s mission. Scale cited in research compilation: 6,000+ youth and families annually; training and interaction with 200+ churches per year; regular engagement with theological scholars.

YF Neighborhood

Neighborhood-focused initiatives including shared meals, neighborhood-designed projects, and missional journeys. Something To Eat™ — meal packaging and distribution with partners such as Harvesters; monthly distribution cited at Franklin Center (KCK) for 350+ households. ImagineX and Snack Shack KC (Argentine) appear as entrepreneurship and safe after-school community examples. The Commons — meeting and rental space in Argentine.

YF Family

Support for parents and families; Presence-Centered Counseling as an in-house affiliate for therapy across ages; programs including Arise (juvenile justice), Teen Parent Connect, and related family resources.

Sources: youthfront.com Local Impact, Something To Eat, Our History; internal vim.md compilation (March 2026).

6. AI stewardship and executive ownership

Youthfront’s leadership-facing AI guide (March 2026) frames AI as already present in donor communications, drafts, program administration, and strategy — requiring a coherent answer for ECFA, major donors, and staff.

Ownership model (summary).

  • Board + CEO: adopt and speak to the organizational stance on AI.
  • Topher Philgreen (EVP Strategic Initiatives): strategy, roadmap, prioritization, and linkage to documented knowledge (e.g. “single source of truth” in systems like Base).
  • Lisen Tammeus Mann: fundraising and external communications — no donor PII in unvetted tools; human approval on donor-facing messages.
  • Natasha Nikkel (COO): operations and cross-cutting inventory of tools and uses.
  • Jim Newberry (VP Ministries): formation and program boundary — gate for youth-facing and formation-adjacent uses.

Ten leadership rules (condensed from the internal guide): (1) Board adopts stance; execs execute. (2) One named owner per domain. (3) Inventory before hardening policy. (4) No youth or vulnerable-family data in public or unvetted AI. (5) Donor-facing and formation-adjacent content requires human approval. (6) Deliberate adoption order: safety and governance first. (7) Development can answer donors/ECFA with a one-page stance. (8) Operations stay inside approved scope. (9) VP Ministries is the formation gate. (10) Annual (or event-driven) leadership review of stance and inventory.

Source: ai-core-leadership-youthfront/index.md in yf-np-dashboard (last updated 2026-03-08).

7. Donor intelligence dashboard and stakeholder knowledge base

The yf-np-dashboard repository (internal name “Donor Intel”) is a prototype prospect research and connection-mapping web application: authenticated workflows, prospect tiers, enrichment services, an AI assistant surface, and a Docs Library fed from markdown under _docs/_public/.

Documentation layout (SSOT pattern in code)

  • _docs/_public/ — reader-facing guides, governance copy, stakeholder research (indexed by the app’s /docs routes).
  • _docs/_build/ — agentic and technical artifacts (type notes, specs, harvest outputs) not shown in the public docs UI.
  • _docs/_prompts/ — executable build prompts.

Stakeholder registry (connection mapping)

A canonical registry (last updated 2026-03-22) documents board members, executives, and key staff with affiliations and LinkedIn-derived notes where available — to power warm-path donor introductions. Principle stated in that knowledge base: internal relationship data often outperforms purchased prospect lists for cultivation ROI.

Pipeline snapshot (example export)

One March 2026 pipeline summary in the dashboard repo listed approximately 19,649 identified prospects across scoring tiers (e.g. Tier 1 “Steward” ~48, Tier 4 long tail ~18k+), with most records not yet enriched — illustrating the difference between discovered names and cultivation-ready intelligence.

Privacy note. This PDF summarizes categories and public leadership roles. It does not reproduce full LinkedIn dossiers, private contact data, or prospect rows. Operational use of prospect data must follow Youthfront’s own data policies and consent rules.
