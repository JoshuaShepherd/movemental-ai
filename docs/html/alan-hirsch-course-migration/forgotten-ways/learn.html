Reflection — Jesus as Lord

Before anything else in the mDNA framework can activate, the community must rediscover what the first Christians risked their lives to declare: Jesus — not the institution, not the tradition, not even the pastor's vision — is Lord.

The confession "Jesus is Lord" was the earliest and most dangerous Christian declaration: a political claim in a world where Caesar held that title. It meant his kingdom comes before every other allegiance.

  1. Where is the gravitational center of your community right now? Is Jesus actually Lord of your church's decision-making — or have his claims been quietly replaced by the claims of the building, the brand, or the comfort of the congregation?
  2. What does allegiance to Jesus cost you this week? Name one thing you would need to release for Christ to be genuinely Lord of your ministry pattern.
  3. Whose lordship is easier to serve? Identify one competing lord — a donor, a strategy, a fear, a personality — that has been subtly steering formation in your community.

Bring to your next cohort call:

  • Share one specific decision your community made in the last year where "Jesus is Lord" was clearly operative — and one where it was not.
  • What would change if the weekly agenda of your leadership team began with the question, "What is Jesus saying to this community, and what will we risk to obey?"

Take 90 minutes this week for a Lordship inventory.

Walk through the rhythms of your community — staff meetings, preaching, budgets, discipleship, worship. For each rhythm, ask: what gets the last word? Note where the gravitational center is Christ, and where it has drifted. Bring the inventory to your cohort conversation.

  • Budgets and stewardship
  • Staff and team agenda-setting
  • Preaching and teaching selections
  • Discipleship pathways
  • Public witness and external partnerships

What's the difference between institutional lordship and movemental lordship?
Great question. Institutional lordship asks "how do we sustain this organization?" while movemental lordship asks "what is Jesus asking our community to risk for the Kingdom?" The first is maintenance logic; the second is movement logic.
