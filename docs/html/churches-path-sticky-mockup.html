Stage 01 of 04 Safety

Decide as a leadership team what staff are allowed to do with AI today, and put it in writing your people will actually read.

Safety checklist

The governance baseline.

Before staff use AI on real work, leadership needs a clear governance baseline. These are the essential pieces that make AI use safe enough to begin.

  1. Ownership is assigned
  2. AI posture is agreed
  3. Allowed and prohibited uses are written
  4. Sensitive data is classified
  5. Staff policy is published
  6. Review and update rhythm is scheduled
View full 14-item checklist 14 items
i. One leader and one decision group own this

An “A” grade looks like

One senior leader — your executive director, chief of staff, or COO — is officially the person responsible for AI at the organization. A small group (your senior team plus one or two board members) meets on a recurring schedule to make AI decisions together. Both are written down, both are on the calendar, and every staff member knows who they are.

ii. Leaders agree on what your organization believes about AI

An “A” grade looks like

Your senior leaders have spent real time talking through what your organization believes about people, truth, privacy, and what should never be handed off to a machine — and they have written it down on one page in plain English. Faith-based organizations name where their faith draws the line. Other organizations name the values that draw it. Either way, the page is in your own words, not a template.

iii. A short, signed list of “here is what we will and won’t do with AI”

An “A” grade looks like

Five to ten clear sentences, written in your own words and signed by the leaders who actually run the organization, that say what you will and will not do with AI. Short enough to read out loud at the start of a staff meeting, specific enough to settle an argument, and used as the standard for every AI decision from now on.

iv. A complete list of every kind of sensitive information you handle

An “A” grade looks like

A simple spreadsheet listing every type of sensitive information your organization touches: donor names and contact info, the people you serve, employee records, financial data, counseling or case notes, legal records, partner agreements, and so on. For each one, you have written down where it is stored, why you have it, and how long you keep it. Nothing is missing.

v. Information sorted into “safe to use” and “never use” groups

An “A” grade looks like

Every type of information from your inventory is sorted into clear groups — fine to use with AI, okay with care, restricted, or never enters AI tools. The “never” list is short, specific, and written down. Any staff member can look at a document or a piece of information and know which group it falls into.

vi. A short policy that tells staff what they can and can’t do with AI

An “A” grade looks like

One short document — readable in under ten minutes by a brand-new hire — that says clearly what staff are allowed to do with AI, what they are not allowed to do, and why. Plain English, not legal language. Where it borrows rules from your AI tool’s own policy (ChatGPT, Claude, Microsoft Copilot, etc.), it quotes them directly so staff have one trusted source to point to when a donor, parent, or partner asks.

vii. The AI tool is set up to follow your rules automatically

An “A” grade looks like

The AI tools you have approved are set up so your policy is built in, not optional. That means: staff sign in with their work account (no personal accounts on the side), accounts are removed when people leave, the tool keeps a record of how it is used, and any tool that connects to your other systems has a named owner. If you handle health, legal, or other regulated information, that work lives in a separate, locked-down environment with the right contracts in place. A written policy the tool itself ignores does not count.

viii. A clear plan for what to do when something goes wrong

An “A” grade looks like

A short, written plan that says exactly what to do at each level of problem — from a near-miss (“I almost pasted donor info into ChatGPT”) to a real exposure (“a draft with a beneficiary’s name was sent to a vendor”). The plan names who to tell, how fast, and what happens next. Staff who report a near-miss are not punished, and near-misses are tracked in a simple log so you can see patterns before they become incidents.

ix. All of these documents live in one easy-to-find place

An “A” grade looks like

Your beliefs page, AI policy, data list, “never” list, incident plan, and a short glossary all live in one folder or page that any staff member can find from the staff homepage in less than thirty seconds. If a new hire on day one cannot find it that fast, it does not yet exist.

x. A practice run of “what if something goes wrong”

An “A” grade looks like

Before any real incident happens, your senior team sits down together and walks through a realistic scenario — for example, “a staff member uploaded a donor list to an unapproved AI tool” — step by step, with the actual people who would handle it. The exercise exposes gaps you didn’t see on paper, and the plan gets fixed afterward, before a real incident teaches the same lessons more expensively.

xi. Every staff member has read and signed the AI policy

An “A” grade looks like

Every single staff member who has access to AI tools has signed your AI policy. The list of who signed is checked against your HR roster — not against memory, not “most people.” This is the clear gate: nobody starts using AI on real work until everyone has signed.

xii. A one-page public statement, ready before anyone asks

An “A” grade looks like

One page, written in your voice, that you can hand to your board, a regulator, a funder, a major donor, your denomination, an accreditor, or a key partner if they ask “how does your organization use AI?” It exists before the first time anyone asks the question, and staff use the same wording so the answer is consistent across the organization.

xiii. A regular schedule of check-ups, on the calendar

An “A” grade looks like

Every quarter, your decision group reviews the AI tool’s usage records, who has access to what, and whether the rules about how long data is kept are being followed. Every month, your senior team sees usage numbers. Each review is on the calendar with a name next to it. If it is not on the calendar, it does not happen.

xiv. A way to update the policy as you learn

An “A” grade looks like

Every incident, near-miss, or experiment that didn’t go as planned ends with one question: “What should we change in our training, policy, or tools so this is less likely next time?” The policy is dated and numbered (v1.0, v1.1, v1.2…) with a short list of what changed and when. A policy that has never been updated is a policy that has not yet met the real world.

Done when

  • Leaders agree on what is allowed, discouraged, and prohibited
  • Sensitive information has been classified clearly
  • Staff know the policy exists and can find it
  • There is a plan for what happens when something goes wrong

Watch for

If Safety is skipped, staff form habits before standards exist.

Stage 02 of 04 Sandbox

Run a small set of approved experiments without sensitive data, and capture what you learn somewhere leadership can actually see it.

The Sandbox pact

Sandbox is not “everyone try whatever you want.” It is structured experimentation inside boundaries. Two rules make it safe enough to actually learn from. If either one is missing, the experiment doesn’t run yet.

Rule 01

Nothing made in the sandbox gets published.

While experiments are running, AI-generated work doesn’t leave the organization. Not to donors, not to the public, not to the people you serve. The sandbox is for learning what works — not for shipping it. Anything that ships goes through a separate approval step after the experiment is done.

Rule 02

Private information stays private — by process, not by promise.

Before any experiment runs, you have a real, trained procedure for keeping sensitive information out of AI tools. Not a memo people skim — an actual process every staff member has been walked through. If a privacy breach is even possible, the experiment doesn’t run yet.

With both rules in place, your team is free to actually explore — to find where AI saves time on tedious work, helps generate revenue, or raises the quality of what you produce. Those three places are where most of the value lives.

How the experiment runs

An eight-week structured process for finding, documenting, and reviewing AI use cases that fit your organization.

  1. Step 01

    Recipes

    Movemental brings a starting list of proven AI use cases — across fundraising, communications, programs, and operations. Your team picks the ones worth testing for your organization.

  2. Step 02

    Experiment

    Each use case gets a designated owner and runs as a structured experiment, using anonymized examples or made-up data. No real donors, no real client records, no real financials.

  3. Step 03

    Log the value

    Every experiment is logged in one shared place — what was tried, what worked, and what kind of value it produced (time saved, revenue generated, or quality improved). AI helps assess the value; a human reviews every entry.

  4. Step 04

    Open visibility

    The whole organization can see the running list. Anyone on staff can flag an ethical or trust concern on any use case — no special training, no permission needed.

  5. Step 05

    Governance review

    At the end of the eight weeks, your decision group reviews each use case — its value, and any concerns staff flagged — and assigns one of three lights:

    Green light

    Clear value, no concerns. Ready to use across the team.

    Yellow light

    Real value, but only with specific guardrails — used this way, and only this way.

    Red light

    Off the table. Added to the “never” list, alongside the boundaries from Safety.

What you walk away with is a recipe book your organization actually agreed on.

Done when

  • Experiments avoid private or sensitive data
  • Each experiment has an owner
  • Learning is captured in one shared place
  • Leadership can see what is working, what is risky, and what should stop

Watch for

If Sandbox is skipped, experimentation goes private, scattered, and impossible to learn from.

Stage 03 of 04 Skills

Form your people — not just train them — so their judgment holds up when AI is in the room.

The deliverable

AI Wisdom & Maturity

Eight weeks · Live cohort · Customizable for your organization

Training teaches people how to use tools. Formation teaches people how to carry responsibility when the tool is in the room. This course gives staff practical AI skills while forming the judgment, review habits, and shared language needed to use AI responsibly.

What you are building is not a faster team. It is a wiser one.

The eight-week arc

  1. Week 01

    Orientation

    The course promise, the cohort, and the question every staff member already lives with: when should I use AI, and when shouldn’t I?

  2. Week 02

    Discernment

    How to tell plausible from good. The first thing staff need is the ability to see drift before it becomes a habit.

  3. Week 03

    Authorship

    Staying in control of the work. Using AI without letting it become the senior author in the room.

  4. Week 04

    Verification

    Checking facts, tone, and integrity. Treating every assisted output as unverified until it’s verified.

  5. Week 05

    Stewardship

    What AI doesn’t touch. Knowing where the lines are — and why some lines aren’t efficiency questions.

  6. Week 06

    Prompting as thinking

    Writing prompts that read like a well-written internal brief. (That’s the actual skill.)

  7. Week 07

    Workflow thinking

    Putting AI inside the actual shape of real work, so what one person learns is something the whole team can run.

  8. Week 08

    Synthesis & sending

    Pulling the whole arc together. A 30/60/90 day plan. A real sending into the work, not a graduation.

Inside each week

  • Opening teaching. The core question and framework for the week.
  • Guided AI conversation. A structured prompt experience that surfaces tension and applies the teaching.
  • Witness case study. A concrete example of the principle at work.
  • Cohort discussion. A facilitated 75-minute session where shared language forms.
  • Exit ticket. One sentence, one commitment, one practice to carry forward.

What people walk away with

  • The ability to recognize weak or risky AI output.
  • The instinct to revise without blindly rejecting assistance.
  • A shared language across roles.
  • Review practices that hold up under deadline pressure.
  • A workflow they can hand to a colleague.

Done when

  • Staff can explain what AI is appropriate for
  • AI-assisted work is reviewed before public or sensitive use
  • Teams know when human judgment has to lead
  • Leaders model responsible uncertainty

Watch for

If Skills is skipped, AI becomes a habit before it becomes a discipline.

Stage 04 of 04 Solutions

Build only after safety, experimentation, and staff capability are in place.

Solutions is the stage where the technology finally becomes central — but not first. By this point, your organization has clarified its boundaries, tested real use cases, trained its people, and learned where AI creates value without compromising trust.

Not: “What AI tool should we buy?”

But: “What should we build around the way our organization actually works?”

The custom AI build blueprint

A custom AI solution is designed around six things.

  1. 01

    Use cases

    Which approved experiments are valuable enough to repeat?

  2. 02

    Workflows

    Where does the tool sit inside real staff behavior?

  3. 03

    Data

    What information can the system use, protect, and never touch?

  4. 04

    Interface

    What do people actually see, ask, review, and approve?

  5. 05

    Governance

    Who owns the tool, reviews outputs, and keeps it aligned?

  6. 06

    Integration

    How does the system connect with the platforms, documents, and practices already in place?

What this can produce

  • Custom AI assistants
  • Internal knowledge tools
  • Workflow integrations
  • Training and resource platforms
  • Donor, communications, program, or operations support systems
  • Data and content structures that serve both people and AI

Why this matters

Tools built too early amplify confusion. Tools built after the first three stages amplify clarity.

By this point, AI is no longer a tool floating above the organization. It becomes infrastructure shaped around the people, mission, data, and decisions that already define the work.

Integration. Your people, information, use cases, and tools stop running as separate projects.

Activation. Knowledge buried in drives, reports, and individual heads starts moving to where decisions are made.

Transformation. Staff spend less time assembling and re-finding information, and more time on judgment, care, and mission-critical work.

Multiplication. What gets learned in one corner of the organization becomes available everywhere else.

When this works, the organization stops re-explaining itself to itself.

Done when

  • Tools follow the safety rules already established
  • Staff know how to use and review outputs
  • Workflow ownership is clear
  • The system reduces fragmentation instead of adding another tool

Watch for

If Solutions are rushed, the organization gets tools that look useful but don’t fit the people, the data, or the mission.

Case Study · Illustrative

How a growing church introduced AI without compromising voice, pastoral care, or trust.

Riverside Church (fictional, but based on real patterns) is a multi-staff congregation of about 1,200 people.

1,200
People
14
Staff
Ministry, communications, operations.
3+
Pastoral domains
Teaching, small groups, counseling.

The situation they were actually in.

AI didn’t arrive through a strategy meeting.

It showed up in staff workflows.

  • A communications staff member used ChatGPT to draft email announcements
  • A teaching pastor experimented with summarizing research notes
  • Someone on the youth team used AI to generate discussion questions

No one had made a decision about AI.

But it was already being used.

Quietly. Individually. Differently across roles.

From the outside, ministry looked normal.

From the inside, leadership had a growing question:

What happens to our voice, our people, and our responsibility if we don’t lead this?

Step 01 · Safety

They clarified what they believed.

Instead of starting with tools, Riverside started with a conversation:

What should AI never do here?

That question anchored everything.

What they did

A small leadership group formed:

  • Lead pastor
  • Executive pastor
  • Communications director
  • One elder

They worked through:

  • What is appropriate for AI to assist with?
  • What must remain human-led?
  • Where is spiritual responsibility involved?
  • Where is efficiency appropriate—and where is it not?

The most important line they drew was around teaching and pastoral voice.

They decided:

AI can assist preparation—but it cannot originate what is preached or personally spoken in pastoral care.

They wrote that down.

What changed

Within a month:

  • A short, clear AI guideline existed
  • Staff understood where AI fit—and where it didn’t
  • Sensitive areas (counseling, pastoral communication, prayer requests) were clearly off-limits
  • Leadership had agreed on a shared posture

Nothing about ministry changed externally.

But internally, something important shifted:

The church had named its responsibility before scaling its tools.

Step 02 · Sandbox

They experimented intentionally.

With boundaries in place, Riverside didn’t “roll out AI.”

They tried a few things—on purpose.

What they did

They approved a small number of experiments:

  • Drafting internal communications using anonymized content
  • Generating first-pass small group questions (with review)
  • Summarizing research notes for sermon prep (not writing sermons)

Each experiment had:

  • a clear boundary
  • an owner
  • a place where outcomes were shared

What changed

Some things helped.

Some didn’t.

Examples:

  • AI sped up internal communication drafts—but tone still needed human editing
  • Small group questions were useful as a starting point, but required discernment
  • Some generated content felt off—technically correct, but not pastorally appropriate

The key shift was this:

Staff stopped guessing individually and started learning collectively.

Step 03 · Skills

They trained for judgment, not just usage.

At this point, staff were already using AI.

The goal wasn’t to introduce it.

It was to help people use it wisely.

What they did

Training focused on:

  • When AI is appropriate in ministry contexts
  • When it should not be used
  • How to review and discern outputs
  • How to maintain pastoral voice and integrity

But the most important part wasn’t the training content.

It was what leadership modeled.

The lead pastor began openly sharing decisions:

  • When AI was helpful
  • When it wasn’t
  • Where he drew the line

This gave staff something more valuable than instructions:

A visible example of discernment.

What changed

Over time:

  • Staff became more thoughtful, not just more efficient
  • Conversations about AI became normal and open
  • Teams shared practices instead of experimenting in isolation

And something deeper became true:

The church was forming people who could think—not just use tools.

Step 04 · Solutions

They built tools that fit their ministry.

Only after the first three steps did Riverside build anything custom.

What they built

The tools reflected their context:

  • A communications assistant trained on their language and tone
  • A resource search tool for sermons, small group materials, and internal documents
  • A planning assistant for recurring ministry rhythms

What they did not build:

  • sermon-writing tools
  • pastoral-care automation
  • anything that replaced relational ministry

They built something different where care was at stake:

A discernment guide for staff and lay leaders—before using any tool at all.

What changed

The biggest shift wasn’t speed.

It was clarity.

  • Staff spent less time recreating resources
  • Communication became more consistent
  • Leaders weren’t answering the same questions repeatedly

But the core of ministry didn’t change:

People still pastored people.

What this actually produced.

Riverside didn’t become an “AI church.”

They became:

  • clearer about their convictions
  • more aligned as a team
  • more capable in their work

And most importantly:

They adopted AI without quietly weakening the things that matter most in a church.

Why this worked.

Nothing about Riverside was unusual.

The difference was the order:

  1. Clarify what matters
  2. Experiment carefully
  3. Form people
  4. Then build tools
