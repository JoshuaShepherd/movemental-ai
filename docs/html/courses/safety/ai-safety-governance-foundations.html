Stage 01 · Orientation

Safety: readiness & boundaries

An executive orientation — not a course, not a policy template alone. Safety is first in Safety → Sandbox → Skills → Solutions because the organization has to know what a responsible yes sounds like before anyone runs Sandbox Season, teaches Skills, or deploys Solutions.

Format Facilitated orientation for sponsors and boards
Typical arc Four to eight weeks of leadership work (iterative; absorbs Sandbox learning)
Audience Executive sponsors, boards, senior staff — people accountable for the mission
Gate Produces the frame every later stage operates inside

What Safety is

The organization’s explicit answer to questions it would be ashamed not to have asked.

Safety is not bureaucracy for its own sake. It is the work of naming, in language signed by real people, what you believe about the capability you are adopting — and what you will refuse to do with it, even when refusing costs speed. That framing is developed at length in Safety Before Speed and The Work of Safety.

Three tests tell you whether the baseline has landed:

  1. A tired executive director can explain the frame at 9 pm without someone else’s deck.
  2. A new hire in week one hears plain language about what may never be delegated to a model, what may be assisted only with review, and what may be explored internally before any external audience.
  3. A vendor’s timeline cannot compress your conscience — because boundaries were named before the vendor arrived.

Safety is not a ban dressed up as prudence. It is the positive picture of what yes means when yes is still a moral and missional yes.

Failure shapes

Three ways leadership teams recognize themselves.

Over-legalization

Counsel drafts before principals decide what they believe. The document is accurate, cautious, and unreadable. Staff sign without understanding; six months later they are doing what the policy would prohibit — because nobody could quote it in a hallway.

Avoidance

Leadership says “not yet a priority.” Shadow adoption fills the gap. Avoidance does not delay adoption; it delays governed adoption.

Delegating responsibility

One enthusiastic staff member owns “the AI portfolio” and de facto makes structural calls titles cannot carry. When something breaks, the org discovers governance was never actually shared.

Three layers

Governance, convictions, boundaries — held together.

Safety has three layers (see Safety Before Speed). Each fails without the others.

Layer one

Governance

Who may decide what, with what authority, under what review, and with what accountability when something goes wrong — a live map of decision rights, not only an org chart. Governance that is not on the calendar is not governance.

Layer two

Convictions

The deepest moral load-bearing beliefs the mission assumes — truth, personhood, care, speech, power. AI presses each category. The artifact is a short, first-person statement in leadership’s own voice — the lens every downstream decision is tested against.

Layer three

Boundaries

Plain-language yes / review / no lists for where assistance may operate. Ambiguity is not neutrality: a staff member guessing whether a model may touch a eulogy, a donor letter, or a child-safety protocol will eventually guess wrong.

Governance without convictions drifts. Convictions without governance stay private. Boundaries without either become arbitrary rules nobody can explain.

What “done” looks like

Concrete, quotable, enforced in the product.

Good Safety feels like clarity: clear yeses and noes readable in minutes; staff who can quote the spirit without a document open; alignment tests where three leaders give the same answer; configuration inside the product (SSO, audit logs, retention, connector inventory) — because policy in a PDF is decoration; policy enforced by the platform is governance.

If staff cannot quote the spirit of Safety in a hallway conversation, it is not Safety yet. It is archival fiction.

Artifacts

What Safety produces.

Three bundles, each short on purpose — expanded as the fourteen-move arc in The Work of Safety (sponsor + forum, moral reflection before policy, principles statement, data inventory and tiers, staff-readable AUP, product enforcement, incident playbook, published baseline, tabletop exercise, universal AUP sign-off, external posture statement, audit cadence, and explicit handoff criteria into Sandbox).

Governance document

  • Named sponsor and standing decision forum
  • One-page principles, first-person, signed by leadership
  • Data sensitivity inventory and tier map
  • AUP readable by a new hire in ten minutes
  • Incident-response playbook with tiered escalation
  • External posture statement for board, funder, regulator, partner

Boundary map

  • Plain-language yes / no / review lists with reasons
  • Glossary so staff, board, and partners share vocabulary

Decision rights

  • Who authorizes new use cases and tools
  • Who owns connector inventory and data boundaries
  • Who runs quarterly audit and board reporting
  • Who is on call for incidents — escalation without panic

Failure mode

Without Safety, the sequence fails quietly.

Ungoverned curiosity → normalized usage without vocabulary → an embarrassing incident handled quietly → retroactive policy staff sign like a software update. The staircase is merciful: skipped steps are reversible until reputation compounds the cost.
