Stage 02 · Program

Sandbox Season

Structured discovery — not a procurement pilot. This is the product shape Movemental publishes as Sandbox Season: twelve weeks, a small accountable cohort, and shared artifacts so Safety → Sandbox → Skills → Solutions stays a causal chain, not a slogan (The Purpose of Sandbox).

Format Cohort program, not self-paced coursework
Duration Twelve weeks, in a weekly learning cadence
Team A small accountable group — typically six to nine staff, one facilitator, one executive sponsor
Stage role Produces the evidence every later stage depends on

1 · What a Sandbox is

A bounded place where real work meets real tools under rules set in advance.

A Sandbox is not random experimentation, and it is not enthusiasm scattered across roles. It is a bounded space — five features held together — where the organization touches AI on tasks that are real enough to learn from and low enough in stakes to fail on.

  • Stated hypotheses. "We think summarization will save our program team four hours a week without flattening our voice." Named in advance, so the outcome can be compared to the claim.
  • Defined use cases chosen against the boundaries Safety already drew. The Sandbox does not ask "what can the tool do?" It asks, "what are we willing to learn about, under these limits?"
  • A learning loop. A rhythm, a small accountable team, and one shared document where observations accumulate in sentences other people can read later.
  • Shared artifacts. What we tried, what we saw, what we will try differently next week — and what we will not try again yet.
  • Real work, low stakes. Tasks where a bad output wastes time but does not wound a person outside the organization.

The Sandbox produces evidence. Not slide decks aimed at donors, not ROI projections — honest internal records of what happened when humans and models shared a desk. That evidence is the input every later stage of the sequence depends on.

A Sandbox is not a pilot. Pilots ask a procurement question — does this product work for us? Sandboxes ask a leadership question — what are we becoming as we use this?

2 · Why Sandbox comes before training

Training without evidence teaches people what they already wanted to believe.

The instinct, when AI arrives, is to train staff first. A consultant runs a workshop. Best practices are distributed. Tools are recommended. Staff leave energized, return to their desks, and discover that the workshop did not match the actual texture of their work. The training was theory-first; the work is context-first.

The Sandbox reverses this order for a reason. It grounds learning in evidence the organization generated itself, about its own writing, its own decisions, its own tone, its own donors, its own volunteers, its own partners. When Skills arrives in Stage Three, it is no longer training about AI in general. It is formation around the specific patterns the Sandbox already surfaced.

Three things happen when training precedes Sandbox:

  • Ideology wins over evidence. Staff defend whichever tool they liked in the workshop, not whichever tool produced better outcomes on the organization's actual work.
  • Vocabulary drifts. The organization borrows language from vendors and consultants instead of naming what it saw happen to its own voice.
  • The Sandbox becomes impossible to run honestly. Staff who have already been trained struggle to admit "we tried it and it did not work" — because admitting that feels like admitting the training was wasted.

Run Sandbox first, and training becomes formation. Run training first, and Sandbox becomes theater.

3 · What participants do

The work of a Sandbox cohort.

Participants in a Sandbox are not students. They are a small, accountable team chosen because their work is representative of the organization's actual production — not because they are the most enthusiastic about AI. Their work across the twelve weeks is made of four disciplined tasks.

Identify use cases

In the first two weeks, the team assembles a portfolio of candidate use cases from across the organization. Each candidate is named in plain language, sorted against the boundaries Safety drew, and scored honestly against the three value dimensions (below). The goal is not to pick winners yet. The goal is to surface enough candidates that the team is choosing against a real field, not defending the first idea that came up.

Run experiments

From the portfolio, a subset is promoted to experiment. Each experiment has a stated hypothesis, a bounded scope, a defined human-in-the-loop, and a time box. Staff do the real work twice — once with assistance, once without, or at two different levels of assistance — so the comparison is grounded. The experiment is written down before it starts, not after.

Track outcomes

Every experiment generates two artifacts: an outcome note (what happened, what surprised us, what we would try differently) and a value score against time saved, quality improved, or impact increased. Outcomes are never reported as averages. They are reported as narratives, because the surprises matter more than the numbers, and the numbers only matter when the narrative explains them.

Flag risks

Every experiment also generates a risk note — a short, unflinching description of where this kind of work could go wrong if it were scaled without supervision. Risk notes are the Sandbox's gift to Safety: they are the raw material for the next revision of the boundary map, the AUP, and the incident playbook. A Sandbox that finds no risks is not a Sandbox. It is a marketing exercise.

4 · The three value dimensions

What "value" means, scored honestly.

AI creates value in three measurable dimensions. Every Sandbox experiment is scored against them, separately, because conflating them is how organizations talk themselves into outcomes they did not actually see.

Dimension one

Time saved

How much human time the workflow takes before and after assistance, measured at the unit of work, not the unit of hype.

Example: A grant officer drafts five discovery emails to prospective foundations each week. With assistance, the drafting cycle drops from ninety minutes to thirty. That is sixty minutes, saved five times per week — named, counted, and verifiable from the calendar.

Honesty test: Does the time saved include the time the officer spent editing the draft back into the organization's voice? If not, the score is wrong.

Dimension two

Quality improved

Whether the output is demonstrably better than the non-assisted baseline on dimensions the organization actually cares about — clarity, accuracy, completeness, tone, faithfulness to the source.

Example: A communications director compares two internal memos — one drafted from scratch, one drafted with assistance and then edited. Three senior colleagues blind-rate the memos on a short rubric. The assisted draft scores higher on structure and lower on voice. That is not a win or a loss. That is a finding.

Honesty test: Is the quality score generated by the person who drafted the piece, or by a colleague who did not? The latter.

Dimension three

Impact increased

Whether the work reaches more people, moves more money, or produces measurably different outcomes in the mission metric the workflow serves.

Example: A volunteer coordinator tests a personalized follow-up flow after a recruitment event. The assisted flow responds within two hours instead of two days. Follow-up response rates are measured at thirty days. The difference is real, or it is not — and the Sandbox reports it either way.

Honesty test: Is the impact increase attributable to assistance, or to the fact that someone finally paid attention to the workflow? Often the answer is the second one — which is itself a finding.

Time saved, quality improved, impact increased. Three columns, one row per experiment. Organizations that score these separately generate evidence. Organizations that collapse them into a single "it helped" column generate testimony.

5 · The core loop

Five moves, repeated weekly.

Every Sandbox experiment runs through the same five-move loop. Short cycles beat heroic month-long bursts — they keep the emotional temperature low enough to admit failure, and they produce more learning per unit of calendar time.

Move 01

Choose use case

From the portfolio, pick a bounded task. One workflow, one team, one kind of output. "Draft five donor discovery emails," not "improve donor communications."

Move 02

Form hypothesis

State the expected outcome in a sentence. "We think assistance will cut drafting time by forty percent without a drop in voice fidelity." Name what would count as being wrong.

Move 03

Run experiment

Do the work. Assisted and unassisted where possible. Staff do the real task on real inputs, with the human-in-the-loop written down in advance.

Move 04

Capture result

Score against time, quality, and impact separately. Write the narrative — what happened, what surprised us, what we would change. One page, in plain sentences.

Move 05

Share & refine

Bring the result to the cohort. Other participants stress-test the finding. The loop begins again next week, either on the same case at a new scope or on the next candidate.

The facilitator's job is not to cheerlead. It is to keep the loop honest — same time, same document, same expectation that "we did not learn much this week" is a valid update if it is true. Churn without documentation is not a Sandbox; it is private practice with office Wi-Fi.

6 · What gets produced

The four artifacts a mature Sandbox leaves behind.

A Sandbox is successful when the organization can walk a new board member through four documents and explain what it now knows that it did not know twelve weeks ago.

Artifact 01

The use-case portfolio

Every candidate use case surfaced across the cohort, sorted into three tiers — ready for deployment under Safety, worth further experimentation, or not worth pursuing in this organization. Each entry is a short block with the workflow, the score across the three value dimensions, and a one-line verdict. The portfolio is the organization's map of where AI belongs in its work and where it does not.

Artifact 02

Experiment logs

The full set of weekly experiment records — hypothesis, setup, outcome, narrative, risk note. Not marketing; internal truth. Successful experiments and abandoned ones are recorded with equal care. Six months later, a new hire can read the logs and understand the organization's honest history with each use case, not its revised memory of it.

Artifact 03

Early wins, scored honestly

A short list of deployments the organization is ready to promote into governed workflows during Stage Four. Each early win includes its value scores, its named human-in-the-loop, and the conditions under which it should be paused. Early wins are what the Solutions stage will build on — but only after Skills has formed the judgment that makes them safe to run at scale.

Artifact 04

Risk patterns

A consolidated document of the risks the Sandbox actually saw — where the models flattened voice, where they confidently stated wrong facts, where they drifted toward generic competence, where staff were tempted to accept plausible output as good output. This document returns to Safety as a revision request. The boundary map, the AUP, and the incident playbook are all updated based on what the Sandbox learned, not on what the first draft imagined.

7 · Example use cases

Five concrete experiments a Sandbox cohort might run.

These are illustrative, not prescriptive — examples of the shape a real experiment takes. Each is written so a senior leader can see, at a glance, what AI is doing, where a human stays in the loop, and where the risk lives.

Use case 01

Donor communication drafting

A development officer tests whether AI can accelerate the first draft of donor discovery, cultivation, and stewardship emails — without flattening the relationship-specific voice these messages require.

What AI does
Produces a first-draft email from structured inputs: donor history, last conversation notes, current ask, the officer's preferred opener. Generates two or three tonal variants for the officer to choose between.
What the human checks
Every line. Voice fidelity against the officer's own recent correspondence. Any factual claim about the donor (amount given, relationships, interests) against the CRM. Nothing is sent without the officer's edit pass.
Where the risk is
Smoothing. Donor relationships live on specifics; assistance tends to drift toward generic warmth. Also — a fabricated donor detail reaching a major giver is not a mistake. It is a breach of trust. Any draft that leaves the sandbox without verification is outside the experiment.
Use case 02

Event follow-up emails

A volunteer coordinator tests whether AI can produce personalized follow-up messages to event attendees at speed — within hours of the event instead of days — while preserving the coordinator's voice.

What AI does
Drafts personalized messages from a roster plus per-attendee notes: which breakout they attended, who they spoke with, what they expressed interest in. Structures a short stack of next steps based on interest tags.
What the human checks
Each message before send. The coordinator's instinct about who needs a non-templated reply. Any attendee who opted out of future contact — which the system should never override.
Where the risk is
Scale without attention. A follow-up flow that goes out in bulk without meaningful personalization does worse than silence, because it signals that the organization only reached out because it was easy. The experiment measures response and unsubscribe rates together.
Use case 03

Internal summaries

A program team tests whether AI can produce first-pass summaries of long internal documents — board pre-reads, vendor proposals, research reports — that save reading time without eliminating the argumentative texture leaders need to engage the material.

What AI does
Produces structured summaries of internal documents: key decisions requested, positions held, open questions, numbers that matter. Flags the passages it found ambiguous.
What the human checks
The staff member who requested the summary reads the full document afterward if the summary surfaces a decision they will own. The summary is a map, not a replacement.
Where the risk is
Smoothing conflict. Summaries tend to average disagreements into a consensus tone, even when the underlying document is structured around a genuine debate. For board pre-reads and any document the executive team will decide from, this is dangerous. The experiment logs every place a summary blurred a tension that mattered.
Use case 04

Grant drafting support

A grants officer tests whether AI can accelerate the structural work of a proposal — fit narrative, logic model, budget narrative, evaluation plan — without claiming the parts of the grant that require organizational authorship.

What AI does
Drafts structural sections from structured inputs: the funder's priorities, the program's theory of change, the prior year's outcomes. Reformats existing content to match the funder's word limits and section labels.
What the human checks
Every number against the financials. Every outcome claim against prior reporting. Every reference to a partner or community against the actual relationship. The grants officer and program director both sign.
Where the risk is
Signature-level gravity. Grant attestations are trust instruments. A sentence that sounds true because it sounds like a grant is not the same as a sentence that is true. The Sandbox explicitly keeps the attestation paragraphs out of scope; only structural drafting is inside.
Use case 05

Volunteer coordination

An operations manager tests whether AI can reduce the coordination overhead of matching volunteers to roles — parsing interest signals, surfacing candidates, drafting role invitations — without replacing the relational judgment that makes placements stick.

What AI does
Cross-references volunteer profiles, stated interests, availability, and historical attendance to surface candidates for open roles. Drafts invitation emails and confirms logistics. Produces weekly coordination summaries for the operations manager.
What the human checks
Every first-time placement — no volunteer is matched to a role solely by automated suggestion. Any coordination message that touches a volunteer who previously had a negative experience. Any role that involves children, vulnerable adults, or confidential work.
Where the risk is
Efficiency replacing relationship. The experiment measures not just placement rates but retention — whether volunteers placed through assisted matching stay engaged as long as those placed through conversation. A placement efficiency gain that erodes retention is a loss the Sandbox will name.

8 · Failure modes

Three ways a Sandbox becomes theater.

Shadow experimentation

Staff across the organization quietly use AI on their own work, paying for personal subscriptions, mixing assisted and unassisted output, with no shared document. Leadership calls this "grassroots innovation." It is not. It is shadow experimentation — the condition where the organization accumulates habits it never chose, because nobody wrote anything down in common. A Sandbox requires a bounded cohort doing bounded work on record. Everything else is private practice with office Wi-Fi.

No logging

The cohort meets, runs experiments, and never produces shared artifacts. Insights live in heads, Slack threads, and a few half-finished Google Docs. Six months later, the organization cannot reconstruct what it learned, and staff who were not in the room have no way to inherit the judgment the cohort built. No logging is not a minor bookkeeping failure. It is the failure that turns Sandbox evidence into Sandbox folklore — which means Skills, in the next stage, will be built on guesses instead of findings.

"We tried AI"

The organization runs a short, unstructured burst of experimentation, produces a slide deck, and declares the Sandbox complete. The slide deck is glossy. The underlying learning is thin. Within a quarter, the same organization is back to "we need to try AI again" because nothing in the first burst was documented in a form that could compound. "We tried AI" is the executive summary of an organization that did not actually run a Sandbox. It will keep saying it until it runs one.

Each failure mode has a quiet version and a loud version. The loud version is easier to correct. The quiet version — shadow adoption that leadership believes is grassroots innovation, a Sandbox cohort that never produces written artifacts, a slide deck that stands in for learning — is how the organization ends up on Stage Four without having done Stages Two or Three.

9 · Outcome

Organizations leave knowing where value actually exists.

A mature Sandbox does not produce tools in production, and it does not produce ROI charts for the annual meeting. It produces people with grounded judgment — staff who can say what good looks like here, what bad looks like here, and which tripwires showed up often enough to merit a rule.

This is the input Skills is built from. Training without Sandbox evidence is guessing. Sandbox without Safety is noise. The sequence is doing its job when the third stage can say to real humans, in real work: here is what we already learned about ourselves on terrain where we could afford to learn.

By the end of a serious Sandbox season, the organization should be able to walk a new board member through a few pages of grounded notes and show, specifically, how its voice behaves under pressure, where assistants help, where they flatten, and which decisions still belong only to humans. That walkthrough is worth more than a hundred slide templates, because it is evidence of organizational mind.

If Safety answers what we will and will not do, Sandbox Season answers what we now know about how it feels when we try, in conditions we chose.
