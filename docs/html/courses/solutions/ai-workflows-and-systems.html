Stage 04 · Playbook

Solutions: production workflows

Not a tool catalogue. A disciplined way to turn earned Safety, Sandbox Season evidence, and formed Skills into deployed workflows the organization owns — the shape Movemental describes as Production advisory once a validated use case is ready to ship.

Format Playbook — reference document plus rollout templates
Duration Staged deployments, each sized to a specific workflow
Audience Operations leaders, workflow owners, and executive sponsors
Prerequisite Safety enforced in the product, Sandbox Season evidence in hand, Skills completed for workflow operators

1 · What Solutions actually means

Workflows, not tools.

The most common mistake organizations make in Stage Four is to treat "Solutions" as a synonym for "AI platform." It is not. A platform is a substrate. A workflow is work — inputs, outputs, people, and quality standards held together by a named owner. Organizations that shop first and design second usually end up with a tool that is more capable than their workflows are mature, and they spend the next year building workflows the tool has implicit opinions about rather than workflows the mission actually needs.

Solutions, in this framework, is the practice of taking specific workflows — identified and stress-tested during Sandbox, staffed by people formed during Skills — and deploying them inside the frame Safety already set. The tool is a variable. The workflow is load-bearing.

Three things follow from this definition.

  • Solutions is boring on purpose. It is where AI stops being a fascination and becomes infrastructure. Still watched, still accountable, still revisable, but no longer experimental. A deployed workflow should feel less exciting than a Sandbox experiment, because excitement inside production is usually a bug.
  • Solutions are revisable, not final. Every deployed workflow has a cadence for review against Safety, against measured outcomes, and against the risk patterns the Sandbox surfaced. Nothing is set and forgotten.
  • Solutions are owned by names, not titles. A workflow without a named owner is a workflow nobody will maintain. Every deployed solution is attached to a specific person, with the authority and calendar time to run it, and a specific escalation path when something goes wrong.

If your first serious organizational move was to select a platform, you did not begin with Solutions as the framework defines it. You began with shopping.

For the full argument, see Why Solutions Come Last and the AI Stewardship Sequence field guide on movemental.com.

2 · Three modes

Augmentation, automation, composition.

Every deployed Solution operates in one of three modes. The modes are not a ranking. They are a language for describing what kind of workflow you are building, so the expectations — and the guardrails — can be sized correctly.

Mode 01

Augmentation

A staff member does the work, and the model assists. The human is the author, the decider, and the reviewer. The model accelerates specific moves inside a workflow the human still owns.

Augmentation is the mode most mission-driven work should live in, because it preserves authorship, verification, and discernment at the point where they matter most — inside the hand of the person whose name will be attached to the output.

Typical pattern: a development officer drafts a donor email, with assistance generating variants and flagging cuts; the officer selects, rewrites, and sends.

Mode 02

Automation

The model performs a bounded task from structured inputs to a structured output, inside a workflow where a human supervises at defined checkpoints rather than at every step.

Automation is appropriate when the task is well-understood, when the inputs and outputs are standardized enough that deviation is detectable, and when the quality gate (what would cause this output to be paused, flagged, or sent back) is explicit. Automation is where efficiency compounds — and also where risk compounds if the checkpoints are skipped.

Typical pattern: structured parsing of intake forms into a CRM, with a weekly exception report reviewed by the operations lead.

Mode 03

Composition

Multiple assisted steps are chained into a longer workflow, with human judgment at designed handoff points. Each step's output is the next step's input, and the human is not reviewing every transformation — they are reviewing at the boundaries that matter.

Composition is the most powerful mode and the most dangerous. It is powerful because it is where AI begins to move organizational work faster than a single human could run it. It is dangerous because errors compound silently across steps. Composition is appropriate only after the individual steps have been run in Augmentation and Automation modes for long enough that the team knows exactly where the failure points are.

Typical pattern: an event-follow-up pipeline that parses attendee notes, drafts per-attendee messages, schedules meeting invitations, and surfaces flagged exceptions — with humans reviewing the exceptions list and a sample of the sends.

Most organizations try to start in Composition mode because Composition is the most demo-worthy. Organizations that do the sequence well start in Augmentation for six to twelve months, promote the patterns they trust into Automation, and only allow Composition where the underlying steps have earned that trust.

3 · Workflow structure

Four elements, named before deployment.

Every deployed Solution is described — in writing, before go-live — against four elements. This is not bureaucracy. It is the minimum description the organization needs to be able to operate, evaluate, and retire the workflow later. A workflow that cannot be written against these four elements is not ready to be deployed.

Element 01

Inputs

What information the workflow takes in, in what form, from what source, at what cadence. Whether the inputs are in approved data tiers. What happens if an input is missing or malformed.

Element 02

Outputs

What artifact the workflow produces, in what form, for what audience. Who receives the output and in what system. What is done with the output afterward.

Element 03

Owners

The named staff member accountable for the workflow's correct operation, the backup owner in their absence, the executive sponsor who owns escalations, and the last review date.

Element 04

Quality gates

The specific checks that must pass before an output ships — fact verification, tone review, integrity of claims, sensitive-data exclusion. What a failed gate triggers. How exceptions are logged.

Writing these four elements is how workflow thinking from the Skills course becomes organizational practice. A staff member formed in Stage Three can write a workflow description in an hour. An unformed staff member can spend a week on it and still leave the quality gate vague. The difference is visible in the document.

4 · Example workflows

Three deployed workflows, step by step.

The following examples show the shape of a fully-designed workflow. Each specifies inputs, outputs, owners, and quality gates, and each annotates every step with whether the model or the human is doing the work. These examples are illustrative — your organization's specifics will change the names, the data sources, and the thresholds — but the structure is the structure.

Example 01 · Augmentation

Donor Communication Workflow

A development officer drafts, reviews, and sends personalized donor updates and cultivation emails — with assistance accelerating the drafting and variant-generation steps while authorship, verification, and the send decision remain with the officer.

  1. Human

    Trigger and context assembly

    The development officer selects a donor from the CRM and confirms the communication purpose (discovery, cultivation, stewardship, ask, thanks). She reviews the donor's recent interaction history, last conversation notes, and current portfolio position. She confirms that the donor's data is inside the approved tier for assisted drafting.

  2. Assisted

    Draft generation

    The model receives a structured prompt: purpose, audience, two recent paragraphs of the officer's correspondence as voice calibration, boundaries (no fabricated claims, no specific phrases the organization does not use), and what good looks like. It returns a first draft and two tonal variants.

  3. Human

    Authorship pass

    The officer reads all three drafts. She selects the closest version or composes from parts. She rewrites any opening that has smoothed into genericity, any transition that has lost specificity, and any closing that has drifted from her own cadence. Authorship remains hers.

  4. Human

    Verification pass

    The officer checks every factual claim — amounts, dates, program outcomes, donor specifics — against the source in the CRM and the most recent reporting. Any number or claim she cannot verify is cut, not softened. If verification fails on a load-bearing claim, the draft is paused, not revised.

  5. Human

    Send decision

    The officer sends from her own email, logs the communication in the CRM, and notes any observations (e.g., tone drift she caught, claims she had to cut) in a weekly workflow log for the workflow owner.

  6. Human

    Weekly review

    The workflow owner (director of development) reviews the workflow log, looks for patterns in flagged tone drift or cut claims, and reports the aggregate findings to the workflow's executive sponsor monthly.

Inputs
Donor record, recent interactions, communication purpose, officer's voice samples
Outputs
A personalized donor communication, an updated CRM log, a weekly workflow observation
Owner
Director of development (sponsor: executive director)
Quality gates
Fact verification against CRM, tone read-aloud against prior correspondence, no send on unverified load-bearing claims
Mode
Augmentation
Review cadence
Monthly; paused if three consecutive weeks show rising tone-drift flags
Example 02 · Automation

Internal Reporting Workflow

A program team's weekly and quarterly reporting is compiled, drafted, and surfaced to leadership through a defined pipeline, with humans reviewing at checkpoints rather than at every step. Appropriate for a bounded, repeatable task with approved data and explicit quality gates.

  1. Human

    Input validation

    The program manager confirms that the week's program data — attendance, outcomes logged, incident notes — is complete in the operations system and in the approved data tier. The workflow does not run if a required field is missing; it alerts the manager.

  2. Assisted

    Structured summarization

    The model ingests the week's structured data and produces a standardized weekly summary — attendance by program, outcomes logged against targets, flagged exceptions, and a short narrative paragraph per program. The template for the summary is fixed; the narrative section has explicit constraints on what it may and may not claim.

  3. Assisted

    Exception detection

    The model flags any week where outcomes have dropped below a defined threshold, where incident notes indicate a safeguarding concern, or where attendance has fallen in a pattern matching prior attrition signals. Flags are logged to a shared exception list.

  4. Human

    Manager review

    The program manager reviews the exception list first, not the summary. Each flag is either accepted (requires follow-up action) or dismissed with a one-line reason. The manager then reads the weekly summary, confirms the narrative paragraphs match the underlying data, and approves for distribution.

  5. Human

    Leadership distribution

    The approved summary is distributed to the executive team and relevant board committee. The exception list and any accepted flags are distributed to the safeguarding lead and program director.

  6. Assisted

    Quarterly roll-up

    At the end of each quarter, the model compiles the thirteen weekly summaries and exception lists into a structured quarterly report draft. The draft is explicitly marked as provisional and enters an Augmentation workflow for the program director to author the leadership narrative.

  7. Human

    Quarterly authorship

    The program director writes the narrative sections of the quarterly report, using the assisted roll-up as source material but authoring the interpretation, the framing, and the recommendations. Verification pass against underlying weeklies is mandatory before the report leaves the director's hand.

Inputs
Weekly program data in the operations system, incident notes, outcome targets
Outputs
Weekly summary distributed to leadership, exception list to safeguarding and program leads, quarterly report draft to the program director
Owner
Program manager (weekly); program director (quarterly). Sponsor: COO.
Quality gates
Complete inputs validated before run, exception list reviewed before summary, narrative sections in quarterly always human-authored, verification pass mandatory before external use
Mode
Automation (weekly) transitioning to Augmentation (quarterly)
Review cadence
Quarterly workflow audit against Safety; annual full review
Example 03 · Composition

Event Follow-up Workflow

A multi-step, partially-chained workflow that parses attendee notes from an event, drafts personalized follow-up messages, schedules next steps, and surfaces flagged exceptions — with humans reviewing the exceptions list, a sample of the sends, and any first-time recipient. Appropriate only once the individual steps have been run in simpler modes for long enough to trust the composition.

  1. Human

    Event data intake

    The event coordinator uploads the event roster, the per-attendee notes (which session they attended, who they spoke with, stated interests, opt-in status), and the organization's current set of follow-up options. Every attendee must have explicit opt-in status; any ambiguous record is flagged out of the workflow.

  2. Assisted

    Interest parsing

    The model parses per-attendee notes into a structured interest tag set. Tags are drawn from a controlled vocabulary — no free-form tags are introduced. Attendees whose notes do not match any known interest cleanly are flagged to an exception list rather than forced into a tag.

  3. Assisted

    Message drafting

    The model drafts personalized follow-up messages from the interest tags and a library of coordinator-authored paragraphs. Drafts reference the specific session and stated interest. The drafts are saved to a review folder, not sent.

  4. Assisted

    Next-step proposal

    Based on interest tags, the model proposes next steps for each attendee — a meeting, a resource, a volunteer role, a returning invitation. Proposals are suggestions, not schedules.

  5. Human

    Exception triage

    The coordinator reviews the exception list first. Any attendee flagged for ambiguous opt-in, ambiguous interest, or any note matching a sensitive category (previous negative experience, vulnerable-population concern, pastoral flag) is removed from the assisted flow and handled manually.

  6. Human

    Sample review

    The coordinator reads a randomly-selected twenty percent of the drafted messages in full and the first message for every first-time recipient. Any draft that has smoothed beyond the coordinator's register, fabricated a specific, or lost the particularity the attendee expressed is pulled, edited, or rejected.

  7. Human

    Send approval and handoff

    The coordinator approves the remaining batch for send. Next-step proposals that require scheduling are handed off to the coordinator or the relevant staff; they are never auto-scheduled into anyone's calendar without human confirmation.

  8. Human

    Outcome tracking and review

    The coordinator logs response rates, unsubscribe rates, and next-step conversions per event. Monthly, the workflow owner reviews outcome trends alongside retention of attendees reached by the workflow versus those reached by the legacy hand-written process. If retention differs meaningfully, the workflow is paused and re-examined.

Inputs
Event roster with per-attendee notes, controlled-vocabulary tag set, coordinator-authored paragraph library, opt-in status
Outputs
Sent follow-up messages, scheduled next steps, exception list handled manually, monthly outcome report
Owner
Event coordinator (weekly); director of community engagement (monthly review). Sponsor: executive director.
Quality gates
Opt-in verified before send; exception list cleared before batch approval; 20% sample read in full; every first-time recipient reviewed; retention tracked against baseline
Mode
Composition — appropriate only after simpler modes earned the trust to chain the steps
Review cadence
Monthly outcome review; quarterly full audit against Safety; paused automatically on retention divergence

5 · What good Solutions look like

Durable, transferable, and measurable.

Durable

A good Solution survives staff turnover. The workflow is documented well enough that a new hire can be onboarded into it in a week, not a quarter. The institutional memory is in the document and the quality gates, not in a single person's head. Durability is the first and most neglected test — organizations build "solutions" that collapse the moment the staff member who understood them leaves.

Transferable

A good Solution can be operated by more than one person. The workflow's inputs and outputs are standardized enough that a backup owner, a new team member, or an adjacent department can run it without reinventing it. Transferability is what distinguishes a deployed Solution from a personal productivity hack. Personal hacks are fine; they are not Solutions.

Measurable

A good Solution reports its own outcomes against the value dimensions named in the Sandbox — time saved, quality improved, impact increased — and does so without the workflow owner needing to assemble a bespoke report. Measurement is built into the workflow, not tacked on afterward. Organizations that cannot answer "is this Solution still worth operating?" with numbers are operating on faith, which is fine for conviction but insufficient for infrastructure.

A fourth, quieter marker: good Solutions are revisable. They have explicit cadences for audit against Safety. When the boundary map changes, the Solutions that touch that boundary are re-examined — not because it is required, but because it is how the sequence stays alive instead of calcifying.

Solutions are not proof that you are “ahead.” The test of a good Solution is not that it is deployed — it is that it still holds six quarters from now.

6 · Failure modes

Three ways deployed Solutions erode.

Tool dependency

The organization's workflows become so attached to a specific platform that a platform change — a vendor acquisition, a pricing restructuring, a capability removal, a policy shift — becomes an existential event. The workflows never specified their inputs and outputs in platform-agnostic terms; they were always described as "we use Platform X to do Y." When X changes, Y breaks, and the organization discovers that it deployed tools, not workflows. The fix is to write the workflow against inputs and outputs first, and to treat the platform as an interchangeable piece of infrastructure — because, eventually, it will be.

Hero operators

A single staff member becomes the only person who can actually run a deployed workflow because they understand its exceptions, its quirks, and its quality gates by muscle memory rather than by documentation. The workflow appears to be humming. It is, in fact, a liability. When the hero leaves, the workflow collapses. When the hero is overloaded, the workflow skips its quality gates. When the hero is wrong, there is nobody on the team who can catch it. The fix is workflow transferability — documented to the point that a second person can operate it, and periodically run by that second person to confirm the documentation matches reality.

No ownership

A deployed Solution exists without a named, accountable owner. It was launched by a team, maintained by "whoever has time this quarter," and is now running on autopilot because nobody has the authority to pause it. Outputs drift. Quality gates atrophy. When something goes wrong, the escalation path is ambiguous. No-ownership is the failure mode that turns Solutions into shadow infrastructure — nominally governed, actually adrift. The fix is to refuse to launch any Solution without a named owner, a named backup, a named sponsor, and a calendared review date.

Each failure has the same root: treating deployment as the end of the work. Deployment is the beginning of the work. The organization that understands that is the organization whose Solutions still function three years on.

7 · Outcome

AI becomes part of the organization's actual work.

When Solutions is done well, there is no grand unveiling. AI stops being a topic. It becomes the way certain tasks get done — with named owners, written workflows, tested quality gates, and a review cadence that keeps the organization honest about which deployments are still worth operating.

The quiet signs that Stage Four has landed:

  • A new hire onboards into a workflow in a week, not a quarter, because the workflow is documented rather than tribal.
  • The board hears about AI not in a strategy slide but inside a routine operations report — a line item, not a narrative.
  • A deployed workflow is retired without drama because a better workflow, or a different organizational need, made it unnecessary. Retiring a Solution is as ordinary as deploying one.
  • When Safety is updated — a new boundary, a new incident learning, a new external requirement — the relevant Solutions are reviewed and revised within a quarter, because the connection between Safety and Solutions is operational, not aspirational.
  • Staff describe their own work in workflow language by default. The organization's vocabulary has absorbed the discipline.

When Solutions is timed well, the organization stops asking the model to rescue a broken workflow and starts asking the model to strengthen a workflow that already had integrity. That difference is not subtle. It shows up in donor trust, in staff morale, in the quality of disagreement inside teams, and in the quiet sense that the organization's public voice is still attached to a real body of conviction.

Impact that can compound without quietly trading away integrity. Solutions are where the mission meets the model on terms the mission chose.

Safety, Sandbox, Skills, Solutions. In that order. The order is the framework. Organizations that finish the staircase do not finish the work — the work continues in the review cadences, the revisions, the retirements, and the new experiments that feed back into Safety. The staircase does not end. It becomes the organization's ordinary way of holding a technology that keeps changing.
