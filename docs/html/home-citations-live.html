The argument

Adoption without capability.

Use is universal. 92% of nonprofits1 and 88% of organizations cross-sector10 already use AI. ~80% of university students use generative AI11. The same pattern that surfaces in McKinsey and BCG's enterprise data appears in nonprofit and faith-sector data: a 5–7% high-performer cohort, a long middle of organizations running pilots that don't move the metrics, and a steep gap between the two3.

What separates the cohorts is not a tool license. McKinsey identifies workflow redesign as the strongest correlate of AI EBIT impact: 55% of high-performers have fundamentally redesigned at least some workflows, vs. ~20% sector-wide10. Virtuous identifies the same pattern in nonprofits — organizations with shared workflows and outcome measurement land in the high-performer slice; 81% of the rest are using AI individually and ad hoc2.

In churches, 60% of leaders use AI personally each month, but only 33% of churches use AI in any part of operations8. Among Protestant pastors, 42% are using AI for ministry today9 — usually without a charter, a sandbox, or a stewardship frame.

This is not a tools problem. It is an organizational, formation, and workflow problem. The data supports the thesis you will hear us repeat throughout this site: formation precedes deployment.
