A note to nonprofit leaders

Are you called to lead in response to AI?

We're about to make a bold claim. It deserves your scrutiny. And if you find that you agree, it asks for urgent action. So we'll be exact about what that action is.

AI is already inside your organization. That single reality asks for one immediate move. Write and ratify your AI Safety Charter: five plain documents that set out your vision, your plans, your reality, your rules, and your response to AI. Nothing more complicated than that. Nothing less urgent.

Before you accept a claim that bold, you should see what it rests on. Here is what is already happening, in organizations like yours, right now.

Here's what's already happening None of this was planned. It is just true right now.

AI is already inside your organization.

Your staff are not waiting for a policy. Ninety-two percent1 of nonprofits already use AI in some form, and most of that use is one person, one personal account, one task at a time. The tools arrived before the decision did.

Sourced

Donor records sit in tools you do not control.

Someone pastes donor and beneficiary details into a personal AI account to clean a list or draft an appeal. No contract, no retention rule, no one decided this was allowed. It is what happens when 81%1 of the work runs through individual accounts with no shared place to do it safely.

Sourced

Your board has not met its duty yet.

Nearly half2 of nonprofits have no AI governance policy at all. The board's job to oversee how the organization handles risk now includes this3, whether or not anything has gone wrong so far. The duty applies before the harm, not after it.

Sourced

The time you are saving is not adding up.

Most teams report small gains and little that compounds. Only five to seven percent4 of organizations see real capability improvement, and almost all of them redesigned how the work flows instead of bolting AI onto the old steps. Individual use never adds up to organizational strength on its own.

Sourced

Your mission voice is drifting, quietly.

The loud risk is a data breach. The quiet one is worse, because no one notices it for a year. Appeals start to sound generic. Reports round off the inconvenient detail. The particular voice that made donors trust you gets sanded down a draft at a time, until your organization reads like every other organization.

A pattern leaders recognize

Every staff departure takes memory with it.

A mid-tier donor who gave steadily for seven years drifts away, because the only person who knew them left, and the relationship lived in that person's inbox, not in anything the organization kept. One donor, then another. A slow leak no dashboard catches. This is not a generosity problem. It is a memory problem.

The deeper problem

These aren't six problems. They're one.

Underneath the governance gap is fragmentation. Your program knowledge lives in scattered documents. Your donor relationships sit in separate systems. Your mission gets retold a dozen ways by a dozen people. None of it is connected, and none of it is held as one record the organization owns.

AI did not cause this. It raised the cost of it. A model asked about your work will fill the gaps with something fluent and wrong, faster than you can correct it. Faster drafts of the wrong story. Quicker research with no shared standard. Individual shortcuts that never become something the whole organization can use.

The quieter harm is not a headline breach. It is the slow erosion of mission integrity. Appeals that subtly mischaracterize the program. Reports that round off the inconvenient detail. Outreach lists that deprioritize the very people your mission was designed to serve.

The case

The full case, in one letter.

Here's the whole argument in plain words, written for your board. Read it here, or download it to send.

You carry a weight most organizations do not name out loud. The people you serve trust you. The donors who fund you trust you. And AI is now inside the work, whether or not anyone decided to let it in.

This is not a reason to panic, and it is not a reason to rush toward the newest tool. It is a reason to lead, in order. Safety first. Decide what your organization will and will not do with AI, and put your board's name on it. That is the AI Safety Charter, and it is the first move.

Then Sandbox, where your team tries AI on real work and learns what it is actually good for. Then Training, where you form your people by role. Then Tech, where we build the platform. Each step earns the next, and skipping one leaves the rest with nothing to stand on.

Here is the gift hidden in your situation. Do this work, and you protect more than donor and beneficiary trust. You turn individual AI use into shared strength, the thing most nonprofits never reach despite near-universal adoption.

Your constituents are watching whether you lead through this with care, or whether AI becomes one more invisible shortcut that slowly misrepresents the work you have been entrusted to do. Begin where it begins. Decide, in writing, what you will and will not do, before the moment decides it for you.

Why a platform

Isn't this just an expensive website?

A real choice. And the real question isn't the one you'd expect. Let's be honest about it.

You're not paying more for a website.

You're considering something you quietly ruled out years ago. A platform that publishes your work and runs tools built for how you operate used to cost a quarter of a million dollars and a software team. Building with AI collapsed that cost. The price didn't go up on a website. It came down on something you always needed.

Website builders are good. And cheaper.

If a simple site is all you need, use one. We mean that. But a tool built to make any website hands you an empty container and leaves the part that matters to you.

Formation

But tools alone aren't the answer.

Technology without formed people isn't progress. It's replacement. Boards that buy tools without forming staff get unapproved AI at scale, faster.

So the tools come with formation. Discernment for leadership. Authorship for anything that goes out under your name. Stewardship for donor and beneficiary trust. For a nonprofit, fiduciary duty and mission integrity depend on both.

Your constituents are watching whether you lead through this moment with care, or whether AI becomes one more invisible shortcut that slowly misrepresents the work you've been entrusted to do.

Formation isn't extra. It's the point.
