What's actually happening

Three things are converging in churches right now.

Your staff are using AI tools individually, without shared workflow or organizational guidance. Worship leaders are using AI for service planning. Communications staff are drafting newsletters with it. Associate pastors are using it for sermon prep, sometimes for pastoral correspondence. Each person has made their own decision about what's appropriate. Most of those decisions have not been discussed with anyone else.

Your elder team is starting to ask. Not aggressively yet, but the question has surfaced. What's our position? Do we have a policy? Are we disclosing AI involvement in communications to the congregation? If we got blindsided by an AI-related incident — a deepfake, a data exposure, an inappropriate AI-generated communication — would we know what to do? The questions are reasonable. Most churches don't have answers.

Meanwhile, the vendors selling AI tools to churches are not the people who can help leaders think through the harder questions. The category is full of companies that understand technology but not pastoral work, or that understand pastoral work but not technology. The result is that the practical decisions — what AI may and may not do in your context, how to handle the boundaries that protect pastoral relationships, what your church will refuse on principle — are getting made by individual staff members one tool at a time, with no shared framework.

The situation they were actually in

AI did not arrive at Riverside through a strategy meeting. It arrived in staff workflows, one person at a time. A communications staff member used ChatGPT to draft email announcements. A teaching pastor experimented with summarizing research notes. Someone on youth ministry used AI to generate discussion questions. No one had made a church-wide decision about AI — yet it was already in use: quietly, individually, and differently depending on the role. From the outside, Sunday mornings and midweek programs looked normal. From the inside, leadership felt a pressure that was hard to name until someone said it aloud: What happens to our voice, our people, and our responsibility if we don’t lead this?

That question is the hinge. Riverside’s story is not “we banned AI” or “we embraced AI.” It is: we stopped improvising in isolation and started leading with clarity.

Stage 01 — Safety

Riverside chose not to begin with tools. They began with a conversation anchored by a single question: What should AI never do here? That question turned out to be more productive than any vendor demo.

A small leadership group took ownership: the lead pastor, executive pastor, communications director, and one elder. They worked through practical and theological terrain together: What is appropriate for AI to assist with? What must remain human-led? Where is spiritual responsibility genuinely involved? Where is efficiency appropriate — and where is it not?

The line that mattered most was around teaching and pastoral voice. They decided explicitly:

AI can assist preparation — but it cannot originate what is preached or personally spoken in pastoral care.

They wrote that down — not as legalism, but as a shared vow the staff could cite when uncertainty appeared.

Within about a month, the shift was internal more than external: a short, clear AI guideline existed; staff understood where AI fit and where it did not; sensitive zones — counseling, pastoral communication, prayer requests — were clearly off-limits; and leadership shared a single posture rather than four private theories. Nothing about Riverside’s public ministry changed overnight — but something crucial changed backstage: the church had named its responsibility before scaling its tools.

Seven ratifiable artifacts (Stage 01)

At the end of the two-week Safety engagement, the working team received the full set of named documents for elder review:

  • Acceptable Use Statement
  • Care Boundaries
  • Disclosure Standards
  • Vendor and Tool Inventory
  • Data Handling Protocol
  • Incident Response Plan
  • Named Refusals

Stage 02 — Sandbox

Boundaries in place, Riverside still did not “roll out AI.” They ran a small set of intentional experiments: drafting internal communications using anonymized content; generating first-pass small group questions with human review; summarizing research notes for sermon prep — explicitly not writing sermons. Each experiment had three non-negotiables: a clear boundary, a named owner, and a place where outcomes were shared.

Some experiments helped. Some did not. AI sped up internal communication drafts, but tone still needed a human editor. Small-group question drafts were sometimes useful as a starting point — and sometimes pastorally thin. Occasionally the output was technically fine but spiritually “off.” The breakthrough was not the win rate of any single tool — it was cultural: staff stopped guessing individually and started learning collectively.

Stage 03 — Skills

By this point, staff were already touching AI; the goal was not introduction but wisdom. Training focused on when AI is appropriate in ministry contexts, when it should not be used, how to review outputs with discernment, and how to protect pastoral voice and integrity.

The curriculum mattered — but what mattered more was what leadership modeled. The lead pastor began naming decisions out loud: when AI had been helpful, when it had not, and where he drew personal lines. That visibility gave the staff something instructions alone cannot supply: a living example of discernment.

Over time, Riverside’s culture shifted: staff became more thoughtful, not merely faster; conversations about AI normalized; teams traded practices instead of hiding experiments. At a deeper level, the church was doing something formation-shaped: forming people who could think — not just use tools.

Stage 04 — Solutions

Only after Safety, Sandbox, and Skills did Riverside invest in anything custom. The tools reflected their actual context: a communications assistant trained on Riverside’s language and tone; a resource search tool spanning sermons, small-group materials, and internal documents; a planning assistant for recurring ministry rhythms.

They were equally explicit about what they refused to build: no sermon-writing tools, no pastoral-care automation, nothing that replaced relational ministry. Where care was at stake, they built something different in kind: a discernment guide for staff and lay leaders — before using any tool at all.

The payoff was not primarily velocity — it was clarity. Staff spent less time reinventing resources; communication grew more consistent; leaders fielded fewer repeating questions. Yet the heart of ministry stayed intact: people still pastored people.

What this produced — and spend on a comparable arc

Riverside did not rebrand itself as an “AI church.” It became clearer about convictions, more aligned as a team, and more capable in daily work — and it adopted AI without quietly weakening what matters most in a church.

Nothing about Riverside was unusual in scale or sophistication. The difference was sequence: clarify what matters, experiment carefully, form people, then build tools.
