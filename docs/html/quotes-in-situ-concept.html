In the margin

Movement isn't new. Movement is God at work.

Alan Hirsch On the Verge, ch. 9

01 Movement

The old order is ending. A newer, older one is stirring.

Every hundred years or so, the church's forms stop being useful. The scaffolding meant to carry the message becomes the thing preserved — and the message itself gets smaller and smaller to fit inside it.

Movements are God's way of starting again. They don't need permission. They don't wait for denominations. They multiply because everyone gets to play, not because a few professionals work harder. We think the West is on the edge of one, and the digital infrastructure leaders are using isn't ready for it.

Our culture is competent to implement almost anything and to imagine almost nothing.

Walter Brueggemann, The Prophetic Imagination (1978)

02 Platform

Platform is not furniture. It is the architecture of your influence.

Most leaders are digital sharecroppers — renting audience space from platforms that own the list, the economics, and the rules. The work compounds; the ownership doesn't. You can change the cushions forever and the house still isn't yours.

We build platforms leaders own. Custom publishing, AI-integrated workflow, a subscription economy aligned to kingdom work — all on an ownership model that pays back as the work does, instead of extracting on day one.

We live in capitalism. Its power seems inescapable — but then, so did the divine right of kings.

Ursula K. Le Guin, National Book Foundation Medal (2014)

03 Formation

Formation happens in practice, not in content.

Nobody became a disciple by watching a course. People become like the practices they repeat with people they trust — that's what a movement's formation engine is, and it looks almost nothing like the publishing industry's definition of content.

Movemental platforms are built for practice first: rhythms, prompts, small-group pathways, feedback loops, and corpus-aware AI that helps leaders assign formation work instead of just producing media to be consumed.

Attention is the rarest and purest form of generosity.

Simone Weil, letter to Joë Bousquet (1942)

04 Network

Movements move through networks, not through megaphones.

A thousand leaders connected by aligned practice and mutual introduction are stronger than a million followers on a shared feed. The network is the distribution, and it has its own ethics — you cultivate it, you don't extract from it.

We wire the collective the slow way: real relationships, honest cross-promotion, shared intelligence on what's actually working. The goal is scenius — the communal form of genius — not a louder individual.

Technologies are not simply inventions which people employ but are the means by which people are re-invented.

Marshall McLuhan, The Gutenberg Galaxy (1962)
