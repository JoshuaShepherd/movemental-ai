Executive Summary (1‑pager)
01 · Executive Summary

The frame for this proposal.

For faith-based non-profit organizations like Youthfront, AI is not primarily a technology challenge but a human, theological one. AI raises questions for how organizations communicate, plan, decide, relate, and build on the foundation of pre-existing trust they’ve already established with their community and constituents.

To do that well, there must be a foundation of clearly documented ground rules for Safety. My understanding is that Youthfront is developing this response. What comes next is thoughtful, wise experimentation in a safe sandbox so that valuable use cases can be discovered, while the human ethical questions each raises can be simultaneously flagged — for near-future resolution.

For mission-driven organizations like Youthfront, the best option is neither a full “No AI,” which is impossible, nor “Go AI” (as in full adoption without safety, training, and testing).

Safety is the first step, but it’s not the last. Once clear and complete safety documentation is in place, organizations must find out whether valuable AI applications do exist for them (wisely saving time, steward resources & revenue, and improving the quality of work and relationship) and what human concerns exist for each of them.

We provide a clear sequence and guidance through the process.

The sections that follow expand each part of this frame: Youthfront’s specific context, the underlying problem, the approach, the program structure (including our LMS cohort and a simple dashboard surface for use cases & ethical documentation), training, deliverables, timeline, investment, why Movemental, and next steps. A Closing reprises the frame, and a Further Reading section links the canon underneath.

02 · Youthfront Context

Eight decades of formation, entering a new operating environment.

Youthfront is not a typical AI adoption case. It is an 82-year-old, ECFA-accredited nonprofit that has carried youth formation work across Kansas City since 1943 — with a coherent theology (the Way of Prayer, the Way of Community, the Way of Ministry), a stable leadership team, and a body of work that predates nearly every tool currently under discussion in the AI conversation.

That history is the starting condition. It is also the reason AI must be approached as a formation question first, and a technology question second. The trust Youthfront has with its churches, camp families, donors, alumni, and pathways participants is not a marketing asset. It is the operating ground of the mission. Any use of AI either compounds that trust or quietly erodes it.

The operating surface, briefly

  • Five ministry lines — YF Camp, YF Neighborhood, YF Family, YF Pathways (Arise, Teen Parent Connect), and Presence-Centered Counseling.
  • Church-assisting posture, with 200+ partner churches and historic seminary relationships (including Nazarene Theological Seminary).
  • FY25 revenue of approximately $4.87M against $5.29M in expenses; FY24 revenue of $5.63M against $4.94M. Net assets ~$6.5M.
  • A declared statement of faith (the Apostles’ Creed) alongside a specific values vocabulary: Community, Trust, Theological Reflection, Playfulness, Christian Formation.
  • Leadership: Mike King (President/CEO), Lisen Tammeus Mann (VP Development), Jim Newberry (VP Ministries), Natasha Nikkel (COO), Topher Philgreen (EVP Strategic Initiatives).

03 · The Problem

Where Youthfront is, and what is missing.

Youthfront is not starting from zero.

You already have:

  • strong instincts around trust, formation, and relational responsibility
  • leadership awareness of data sensitivity and public credibility
  • internal momentum toward clarity
  • ongoing work to document safe AI usage and rules organization-wide

This is the crucial foundation.

What is missing—and what most organizations lack—is:

a structured way to discover value safely, while building shared skills & wisdom across the team

Underneath that gap, three pressures show up at the same time

In 2026, AI is already in the fabric for nearly every mature nonprofit. Youthfront’s situation is the sector’s situation. Without a structured way to discover value safely, three pressures compound.

Shadow usage

Individuals are already using AI. Without documented ground rules, sensitive content flows through consumer tools, authorship blurs, and no one knows what is actually happening across the organization.

Adoption before evaluation

Vendors sell solutions before the underlying use case is proven. Budget follows tools rather than value. The organization ends up paying for capability it cannot defend at the board level.

Training before context

Generic AI training produces generic confidence. Staff return from workshops able to describe AI in the abstract but unable to judge whether any specific use of it fits Youthfront’s mission.

Deep dive — why the two equal errors of “No AI” and “Go AI” both fail

The mirror of “adoption without safety” is “prohibition without learning.” Both produce the same outcome: an organization that does not know what AI can and cannot do inside its own work. The first is risky; the second is brittle. A Sandbox season is the escape from both errors.

See: The Two Equal Errors, Why Order Matters, Safety Before Speed, There Is a Way Through This.

04 · The Approach

What we propose.

A Sandbox-first engagement, with Skills layered in.

This is not a general training program.

It is a structured discovery system that produces real organizational work product and results (safely).

The engagement centers on three outcomes:

1. Use Case Portfolio (the core outcome)

  • Movemental provides a curated library of nonprofit-relevant AI use cases
  • Youthfront tests these through structured experiments
  • The organization produces a portfolio of as many validated, valuable use cases as we can explore and document together — starting from the original work of Movemental to get us started

Each use case is documented with:

  • purpose
  • owner
  • inputs and boundaries
  • estimated time savings, revenue potential, or other quantitative/qualitative metric
  • review requirements

Then

  • flags for concern about potential ethical and trust violations
  • a clear process for flagging and discerning what is allowed and under what conditions

This portfolio becomes:

  • the leadership decision surface
  • the board-level narrative
  • the foundation for any future adoption

2. Structured Evaluation (value + ethics together)

Each use case is evaluated through:

A. Practical value

  • time saved
  • quality improved
  • repeatability
  • risk introduced

B. Ethical and relational alignment

  • authorship and voice
  • trust and disclosure
  • appropriateness within Youthfront’s mission

This produces:

  • clear green / yellow / red decisions
  • documented reasoning
  • confidence in what moves forward—and what does not

3. Skills in Context (not abstract training)

Skills are built inside real work, not in isolation.

Staff learn:

  • how to work with AI without losing human intelligence, authorship & creativity
  • how to verify and review outputs
  • how to operate within clear boundaries

This ensures:

capability grows alongside judgment—not ahead of it

What this is — and what it is not

This is:

  • a Sandbox season
  • focused on discovery, evidence, and shared judgment

This is not:

  • a full AI rollout
  • a tools-first implementation
  • a generic training program

The underlying sequence: Safety, Sandbox, Skills, Solutions

The three outcomes above sit inside a deliberate sequence. Safety is the doorway Youthfront is already walking through. Sandbox is the engine of this engagement. Skills layer in as discovery requires. Solutions — integration of validated use cases — come last, and only when the evidence warrants.

Stage What it produces What it is not
Safety Documented ground rules: what AI is allowed to touch, under what conditions, with what review. A prohibition list.
Sandbox Structured experiments inside real work, producing the documented Use Case Portfolio described above. Playing with tools.
Skills Staff capability built inside real experiments — prompting, verification, voice preservation, boundary operation. Generic AI training.
Solutions Selective integration of validated use cases — only when the evidence warrants it. A platform rollout.

05 · Program Structure

Three surfaces: cohort, dashboard, facilitation.

The engagement runs across three connected surfaces. Each one produces something the others depend on. Together they form a working system the organization can continue to operate after the engagement ends.

1. LMS Cohort

A structured learning environment with modules, assignments, and discussion — delivered through Movemental’s existing LMS. Staff move together through formation-grade content, not drop-in videos.

2. Use Case Dashboard

A simple, shared dashboard surface for the live Use Case Portfolio — purpose, owner, boundaries, value metrics, ethical flags, review status, and green / yellow / red decisions. Visible to the cohort and to leadership.

3. Facilitated Sandbox

Regular working sessions with a Movemental facilitator — pattern recognition, experiment design, evaluation reviews, ethics discernment. Real work, reviewed in real time.

How the surfaces interact

Cohort learning seeds the pattern language the staff use in Sandbox. Sandbox experiments populate the Dashboard. The Dashboard surfaces the portfolio for leadership and board review. Facilitation keeps the loop honest — turning experiments into evidence rather than anecdote.

The Use Case Dashboard, specifically

The dashboard is deliberately simple. It is a shared surface — not a software purchase — that makes the Sandbox portfolio legible at a glance. It carries exactly the fields the Executive Summary names for each use case:

  • Purpose. What is the use case, in one sentence, in Youthfront’s own voice.
  • Owner. Who holds it inside the organization.
  • Inputs and boundaries. What goes in; what must never go in.
  • Value metric. Estimated time savings, revenue potential, or other quantitative/qualitative metric — scored honestly.
  • Review requirements. Who reviews outputs, and how often.
  • Ethical & trust flags. Flags for concern about potential ethical and trust violations, with the decision process attached.
  • Decision. Green approved — Yellow conditional — Red declined — with documented reasoning.

This dashboard is the leadership decision surface, the board-level narrative, and the foundation for any future adoption. It is also the deliverable Youthfront continues to operate after the engagement ends.

06 · Training

Skills built inside real work, not in a classroom.

Skills are built inside real work, not in isolation.

Staff learn:

  • how to work with AI without losing human intelligence, authorship & creativity
  • how to verify and review outputs
  • how to operate within clear boundaries

This ensures:

capability grows alongside judgment—not ahead of it

Four questions frame the training layer in practice: who does the training, how it is delivered, what the instruction modes are, and when each one happens.

Who

A Sandbox cohort of approximately 6–12 staff, selected together with Youthfront leadership. The shape we recommend:

  • One executive sponsor (likely COO or EVP Strategic Initiatives).
  • One representative from each active ministry line (Camp, Neighborhood, Family, Pathways).
  • Communications / storytelling.
  • Development / donor stewardship.
  • Operations / IT / data.
  • Optional: one board or advisory voice auditing, not operating.

How

Cohort-based, applied, and real-work-first. No abstract prompts. No sandbox copies of made-up scenarios. Every module produces something usable by the person who made it, on work they were already going to do.

  • Async LMS modules — short, substantive, formation-grade.
  • Live facilitated Sandbox sessions — weekly or bi-weekly.
  • Peer review inside the cohort — shared language, shared judgment.
  • Leadership checkpoints — portfolio walkthroughs with the executive team.

What — the instruction modes

Training is delivered in six layered modes. They are sequenced, not parallel; earlier modes are the substrate for later ones.

Mode Purpose Output
Orientation & Safety Shared language, ground rules, risk posture, and the Youthfront frame for AI. A shared safety handshake and a cohort charter.
Pattern Recognition Training the eye to see where AI might produce value inside real Youthfront work. Candidate use cases, owned by the people who would run them.
Experiment Design Writing a structured test before running it — inputs, outputs, success signal, risk flag. Experiment cards attached to candidate use cases.
Evaluation & Ethics Scoring value honestly and flagging the ethical & relational questions each use case raises. Green / yellow / red decisions with documented reasoning.
Applied Skills The concrete crafts — prompting, verification, voice preservation, output review, boundary operation, model selection. Staff who can work with AI without losing authorship.
Leadership Synthesis Turning the portfolio into language for board, donors, partner churches, and program staff not in the cohort. Executive briefings, board memos, partner-facing summaries.

When

Training runs across the engagement, not as a front-loaded workshop. The rhythm we recommend:

  • Month 1. Orientation & Safety; cohort launches LMS modules 1–2.
  • Month 2. Pattern Recognition; first candidate use cases identified and entered on the dashboard.
  • Month 3. Experiment Design & Evaluation; the first 6–10 experiments run and scored.
  • Month 4. Applied Skills & Leadership Synthesis; portfolio consolidation and executive briefing.
  • Months 5–8 (optional extension). Depth & scale — more ministry lines, deeper skills, selective Solutions.
Deep dive — skills as formation, not training

Movemental treats skills as formation — the slow building of judgment alongside capability — rather than as training in the corporate sense. The distinction is load-bearing for mission-driven organizations where authorship, voice, and relational trust cannot be mass-produced.

See: Skills as Formation, Not Training, The Skill of AI, Recipes Are Not Cooking.

07 · Deliverables

What Youthfront can expect to hold at the end of the season.

Outcomes Youthfront can expect:

  • A clear, defensible set of approved use cases
  • Reduced shadow experimentation and ambiguity
  • Staff capable of responsible, consistent AI use
  • Concrete work product & documentation for board, donor, and partner conversations
  • A Claude-centered approach to valuable, wise AI use at Youthfront

Most importantly:

Youthfront will be able to engage AI in a way that remains recognizable as Youthfront.

The specific deliverables

Those outcomes become specific, durable deliverables — each designed to be usable by leadership, defensible to the board, understandable to a donor, and reusable by staff who were not in the cohort.

  1. A scored Use Case Portfolio. Validated, documented use cases with value signal, ethical & trust flags, review requirements, and a green / yellow / red decision.
  2. A Youthfront-specific ethics log. The flagged questions, the reasoning behind each decision, and the conditions under which any yellow use case could move forward.
  3. A trained Sandbox cohort. 6–12 staff fluent in pattern recognition, experiment design, evaluation, and applied AI skills — carrying the posture into their teams.
  4. The Use Case Dashboard. The simple, shared surface Youthfront continues to operate after the engagement ends.
  5. Leadership & board materials. Executive briefings, board memo, and partner-facing summaries drawn directly from the portfolio.
  6. A decision framework for future AI choices. The criteria Youthfront will use for any new tool, vendor, or use case going forward.
  7. A Claude-centered posture. Platform guidance, model-selection logic, and skill definitions oriented around Claude Enterprise as the primary backbone.
  8. Year-two planning input. Recommendations for which validated use cases (if any) warrant Solutions-stage integration.
08 · Timeline

Engagement structure: a four-month core, with an optional extension.

  • Modular timeline (typically 4-8 months)
  • Sandbox as the primary engine
  • Skills layered in as needed
  • Solutions (integration) only if warranted by evidence

The core engagement is four months. Organizations that want to move deeper — adding ministry lines, carrying Skills further, or graduating specific use cases into Solutions — extend for up to four additional months.

Month 1 Orientation

Safety doorway and cohort kickoff

Safety handshake with leadership. Cohort selected and onboarded. LMS opens. Pattern language introduced. Dashboard stood up.

Month 2 Discovery

Pattern recognition and candidate use cases

Staff identify candidate use cases across ministry lines. First experiment cards written. Ethical & trust flags named honestly.

Month 3 Evaluation

Structured experiments and scoring

The first 6–10 experiments run inside real work. Value signals scored. Ethical flags adjudicated. First green / yellow / red decisions posted to the dashboard.

Month 4 Synthesis

Portfolio consolidation and leadership report

Portfolio stabilized. Applied Skills modules close. Executive briefing and board-ready materials delivered. Decision framework handed over.

Months 5–6 Depth

Deeper ministry-line coverage and selective Solutions

Additional experiments in under-covered ministry lines. Selective movement of high-confidence green use cases into light Solutions work — only where evidence warrants.

Months 7–8 Scale

Scale-out and year-two planning

Cohort alumni carry the posture into their teams. Second-ring staff onboarded via LMS. Year-two plan written with Youthfront leadership.

Deep dive — why Solutions come last

Most AI engagements begin with Solutions — tooling, platforms, automations — and back-fill the judgment later. The SSSS sequence inverts this deliberately. Solutions are the cheapest part of the stack once the earlier stages have done their work, and they fail expensively when they have not.

See: Why Solutions Come Last, Solutions Deployment, The Work of Safety.
