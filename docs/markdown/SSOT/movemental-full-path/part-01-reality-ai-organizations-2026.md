# Part one: the reality of AI inside organizations in 2026
Movemental tells the organizations we serve that AI built on top of a fragmented human and informational layer amplifies dysfunction rather than reducing it. We tell them that the work has a sequence — Safety, Sandbox, Skills, Solutions — and that Safety is the non-negotiable first stage. Underneath that language is a claim about the world. The claim is that AI is already inside the organizations we serve, that it is already creating consequences, and that the absence of governance is producing measurable harm to mission, trust, and operations right now. This part tests the claim against current research.

## Churches

The most authoritative current data on AI in churches comes from the 2026 State of AI in the Church Survey, conducted by Exponential AI NEXT and AIforChurchLeaders.com, drawing responses from church leaders across denominations, geographies, staff seniority, and functions. A separate Lifeway Research study of 1,003 Protestant pastors and 1,200 churchgoers, conducted in late 2025 and published in April 2026, provides a complementary view of pastor and congregant attitudes. Together they document a sector that has crossed a threshold in adoption while remaining almost entirely without governance. Adoption is widespread and accelerating. The 2026 Exponential survey found that 91% of church leaders support AI use in ministry. Sixty-one percent now use it weekly or daily, with 25% using it daily. That compares to a 43% weekly-or-daily adoption rate in 2024. Adoption nearly doubled in two years. Seventy-eight percent of church leaders use AI weekly or more. Sermon preparation is the most common use case. Sixty-four percent of pastors now use AI for sermon preparation. Other primary uses include text content creation (36%), research (22% to 26% across surveys), image generation (20%), and administrative work (16%). The Lifeway study found a more conservative adoption picture among rank-and-file Protestant pastors, but with a clear demographic skew: pastors under 55, in urban settings, with graduate degrees, and at larger churches are far more likely to be active adopters.

Governance has not kept pace with use. Only 9% of churches have a formal AI policy of any kind. Only 7% have an AI disclosure statement that informs their congregation about AI use. Seventy-three percent have nothing written at all. Fifty-one percent have not addressed AI use with their congregation in any way. The risks are not theoretical. They are arriving. Sixty percent of church leaders said they are "very concerned" about AI voice cloning being used to defraud their congregants. Twenty-five percent — one in four — said AI-generated scams or misinformation have already affected their church community. The pattern is documented in incident reports across multiple states (Alabama, Florida, Missouri, Nebraska, New York) and internationally. Scammers harvest publicly available sermon footage, livestream archives, and social media content to clone pastor voices and likenesses, then deploy those clones to solicit emergency donations from congregants. High-profile cases include Father Mike Schmitz and several megachurches. The FBI in late 2025 warned that AI-supercharged holiday scams had produced more than 9,000 AI-related complaints. Theological misalignment is the top concern. Seventy-five percent of church leaders named theological misalignment as their top ethical concern. The concern is well-founded. Generative AI tools are trained on enormous corpora of internet text, including theological material from traditions across the spectrum. The output can cite scripture, use pastoral language, and still be doctrinally inconsistent with a specific church's convictions. Sixty-seven percent of church leaders also worry that AI will gradually replace the human connection that pastoral ministry depends on. Congregations are watching. Eighty-seven percent of church leaders said their congregation brings up AI regularly. Only 13% said the topic has not surfaced. Congregants are forming opinions about church AI use whether or not the church communicates about it. The question for church leadership is not whether the congregation will think about AI. They are already thinking about it. The question is whether the church's leadership shapes that conversation or absorbs whatever conclusions form in its absence.

## Nonprofits

The most authoritative current data on AI in nonprofits comes from the 2026 Nonprofit AI Adoption Report, a benchmark study of 346 organizations conducted by Virtuous and Fundraising.AI and published in February 2026. A complementary view comes from the TechSoup 2025 State of AI in Nonprofits Benchmark Report and a February 2026 Forvis Mazars analysis framing AI governance as a fiduciary responsibility for nonprofit boards.

Adoption is near universal. Ninety-two percent of nonprofits use AI in some form. Seventy-nine percent of nonprofits report small to moderate efficiency gains from AI use — faster drafts, quicker research, improved content quality. The early-adopter question has been answered. The remaining questions are about how AI is being used and at what cost. The use is fragmented and individual. Eighty-one percent of nonprofits use AI individually rather than through shared workflows. The dominant pattern is one staff member experimenting with ChatGPT or a comparable tool — while the rest of the team continues with manual processes. Only 7% of nonprofits report major improvements in organizational capability through AI use, despite the 92% adoption rate. Governance is broadly absent. Forty-seven percent of nonprofits have no AI governance policy at all. The TechSoup benchmark found a more pessimistic figure: 76% of nonprofits have no formal AI strategy. Without a regulatory deadline or a major-funder requirement, AI policy work has been perpetually deprioritized. Staff find tools that help them, use them, and the organization catches up — often years later, sometimes only after an incident. Donor and beneficiary data is at risk. Nonprofit AI use overwhelmingly involves the organization's most sensitive data: donor giving histories, beneficiary records, program intake information, and sometimes protected health or financial details. When staff paste this data into consumer AI tools like the free tier of ChatGPT, the data may be retained by the model provider or used for model training. The Forvis Mazars February 2026 analysis explicitly frames this as a fiduciary issue. A board's traditional duties of care, loyalty, and obedience now include technological oversight. Mission integrity is in tension with AI output. AI-generated content reflects the statistical center of its training data, which is often misaligned with the mission, voice, theological commitments, or community standards of the nonprofit using it. Examples include AI-drafted donor appeals that subtly mischaracterize the program; AI-generated impact reports that round inconvenient details into a tidier narrative; AI-translated communications that lose the cultural specificity the original program was designed to serve. Each of these is an erosion of mission, often invisible to the staff producing it.

## Institutions: seminaries, training networks, and denominational bodies

Institutions face a different version of the AI question because they operate on two timelines simultaneously: their own institutional adoption, and what they teach the next generation of leaders about AI. Students are using AI heavily, with or without guidance. Generative AI use in higher education is functionally universal among students under 30. International benchmarks show 63% of students using AI chatbots for text generation, 58% for translation, 55% for explaining difficult concepts, and 52% for summarizing academic articles. Seminary students are using AI for sermon drafting, exegesis assistance, paper writing, theological research, and increasingly as a kind of pastoral conversation partner. Institutional policy varies wildly. Some seminaries have published thoughtful policies (Columbia Theological Seminary, Saint Meinrad, Puritan Reformed, Austin Presbyterian). Others have nothing. There is no association-wide standard from the Association of Theological Schools. Faculty within the same seminary often hold different policies because no institutional policy exists to align them. Curriculum has not yet caught up to the formative reality. Network responsibility is the unique institutional weight. Whatever an institution adopts internally becomes the implicit template their member churches, nonprofits, and partner organizations look to. A denominational AI charter influences hundreds or thousands of congregations. A seminary's AI policy influences every church its graduates lead. Institutional inaction is not neutral. It is a choice that propagates through the network as the absence of guidance.

## What is true across all three segments

Reading the church, nonprofit, and institutional data side by side, four patterns appear in all three: adoption has crossed a threshold no organization can opt out of; governance is the universal gap; the audiences these organizations serve are not unified about AI; and AI is already inside the organization in shadow form, below the line of formal organizational awareness. AI built on top of a fragmented human and informational layer amplifies dysfunction rather than reducing it. The data confirms the claim. The governance gap is the fragmentation. The amplification is already underway. The work of Safety is to close the gap before further amplification compounds it.
