# Part six: Skills Development
The Skills stage is the formational deepening of human capacity to lead AI work in this organization with wisdom. It is an eight-week engagement priced at $15,000 (cohort facilitation) or $4,800 per year per organization (self-paced license). The premise of the Skills stage is that running recipes is a starting point, not an end point. Organizations that complete Sandbox have demonstrated they can use AI productively. Organizations that complete Skills have developed the discernment to lead AI work — to evaluate new use cases, modify existing recipes, recognize when AI is the wrong tool, and form other staff in the same wisdom. The shift is from practitioner to discerner. From running well-designed recipes to making well-considered judgments about what to run, when, how, and whether.

### Six-A: The metaphor and the shift

We have used the language of recipe and chef in conversation about this stage. The metaphor captures something true — the shift from following someone else's instructions to making your own informed decisions — but a stronger frame for what Skills actually accomplishes is the shift from practitioner to discerner. A practitioner runs the recipe well. They follow the steps, attend to the inputs, produce the output. Their competence is measured by the quality of their execution. In the AI context, a practitioner uses Claude skills configured by someone else, applied to workflows they have been trained on, against data their Safety documentation has approved. They do good work. A discerner does all of that, and also knows when the recipe should not be run. When the workflow has shifted in ways the recipe no longer fits. When the data carries weight the recipe does not account for. When the constituent on the other side of the work would experience the recipe differently than the practitioner does. When the organization's mission is served by saying no rather than yes. The discerner reads the situation, weighs it against the values and convictions documented in Safety, considers the people involved, and chooses. Their competence is measured by the quality of their judgment. The Skills stage forms discerners. The work is not technological — though technology is the context. The work is formational — though competence is the result. Every module builds judgment as much as capability.

### Six-B: Learning outcomes for Skills Development

At the conclusion of Skills Development, participants are able to:

- Read an AI use case in context — understanding the workflow, the data, the people, the integrity considerations, and the organizational values that bear on the case — and articulate a position on whether and how to proceed, with reasoning their leadership can review and ratify.

- Modify an existing Claude skill or recipe to better fit a workflow that has evolved or a context the original recipe did not anticipate, including adjusting prompts, context windows, output structure, and integration with downstream systems where applicable.

- Identify the difference between a use case that needs technical refinement, a use case that needs procedural change, a use case that needs formational preparation of the staff using it, and a use case that should not proceed at all — and recommend the appropriate response for each.

- Evaluate AI output for quality, fit-to-context, alignment with the organization's voice and convictions, and absence of subtle errors that the staff member must catch before publication or deployment.

- Form other staff in beginning AI competence by selecting appropriate recipes, supervising initial use, providing feedback, and recognizing when a colleague has crossed from supervised practice into independent capability.

- Hold the human and theological dimensions of AI work — including questions of presence, formation, vocation, voice, and integrity — with sufficient depth to navigate situations the Safety documentation does not explicitly anticipate.

- Lead a use case adjudication conversation with peers and leadership — proposing a position, hearing pushback, modifying the proposal, and arriving at a decision the team can implement and the leadership can defend.

- Recognize the limits of one's own discernment and know when to escalate — to leadership, to

Movemental, or to a peer with more relevant expertise — rather than proceeding past one's actual capacity.

### Six-C: Course architecture

Skills Development is delivered as an eight-module course over eight weeks. Each module is structured around the same three-part rhythm: a formational reading or input, a practical exercise grounded in the participant's actual work, and a cohort or asynchronous discussion that sharpens the judgment the module is forming. Each module is approximately three to five hours of participant time per week. The course architecture is built on Movemental's existing LMS infrastructure (React, Next.js, Tailwind, Supabase, with agentic tutoring and feedback capabilities). The course can be delivered in three formats: cohort-facilitated (eight weeks with a Movemental facilitator running weekly sessions of a small group), self-paced (eight weeks of asynchronous progression with agentic tutoring and quarterly cohort moments), or hybrid (self-paced core with monthly facilitated cohort sessions for deeper discernment work).

Module 01: AI as human simulation: what we are actually working with LEARNING OUTCOMES

- Articulate why current AI tools present as human simulation rather than as conventional technology, and why this changes the nature of the competence required to use them well.

- Describe the specific ways AI output mirrors human language, judgment, and authority — and where this mirroring is reliable, where it is misleading, and where it is dangerous.

- Distinguish between using AI as a tool, using AI as a collaborator, and using AI as a substitute — and recognize which mode is operating in any given workflow.

Module one establishes the conceptual frame for everything that follows. Most AI training treats AI as a sophisticated tool. This module argues — and demonstrates — that current AI is better understood as human simulation: it speaks our languages, mimics our reasoning, produces our cultural artifacts, and presents itself through the same interface as another human collaborator. The competence required to work with human simulation is not technical training. It is discernment about when the simulation is helpful, when it is misleading, and when something other than simulation is needed. Practical exercise: each participant brings a recent piece of AI-generated work from their own role and analyzes it against this frame. Where did the AI succeed at simulating what was needed? Where did it fail? What did the participant's human judgment add that the simulation could not? Module 02: Reading a use case in context LEARNING OUTCOMES

- Identify the seven dimensions of context that bear on any AI use case — workflow, data, people, integrity, values, frequency, and downstream effect — and apply the framework to a use case from the participant's own work.

- Articulate why context is the irreducible human contribution to AI work, and what this means for how staff at every level should be formed.

- Recognize when a use case proposal is missing context the practitioner has assumed and the proposer has not surfaced.

This module trains the practitioner-to-discerner shift directly. A use case is never just "use AI to do X." It is always "use AI to do X, with this data, in this workflow, affecting these people, against these integrity standards, at this frequency, with these downstream effects." The participant who can read all seven dimensions of context produces dramatically better adjudications than the participant who reads one or two.

Practical exercise: each participant adjudicates three use cases from a curated library — one green-light, one yellow-light, one red-light — and writes the reasoning that supports each adjudication. Cohort discussion compares adjudications and surfaces where participants read context differently. Module 03: Modifying a recipe with discernment LEARNING OUTCOMES

- Modify an existing Claude skill or recipe to fit a workflow that has evolved, by adjusting prompts, context, output structure, and integration points.

- Distinguish between modification that improves the recipe and modification that compromises the recipe's alignment with Safety documentation.

- Recognize the limits of one's own modification authority and know when to escalate to a more capable collaborator or to Movemental.

Module three is the most technically substantive in the course, and is also the module where the formational dimension matters most. Modifying a recipe well requires both the technical capacity to make the change and the judgment to know whether the change is wise. Participants learn to modify recipes responsibly — making them better at the work the organization actually does, while keeping them aligned with the values the organization has documented. Practical exercise: each participant takes one recipe from their Sandbox engagement and produces a modified version tuned to a workflow that has shifted since the original Sandbox. The modification is reviewed by the cohort and the facilitator. Module 04: Evaluating AI output: catching what the simulation misses LEARNING OUTCOMES

- Evaluate AI output for quality, voice fit, contextual accuracy, subtle factual errors, and alignment with the organization's convictions.

- Describe the categories of error that current AI produces consistently, and the patterns that indicate output should not be used as-is.

- Develop a personal review discipline that catches the errors most relevant to one's own role.

AI output is fluent, plausible, and frequently wrong in ways that are difficult to catch without active discernment. Module four trains participants to read AI output with the kind of attention that catches what the simulation misses. The discipline is teachable, and the teaching is most effective when grounded in the specific kind of work the participant does.

Practical exercise: each participant is given a set of AI-generated outputs from their domain (for a pastor, AI-generated sermons and pastoral communications; for a development director, AI-generated donor appeals; for a faculty member, AI-generated theological writing). Each participant identifies the errors, the missing context, the voice mismatches, and the points where the output should not be used. Cohort discussion sharpens the eye. Module 05: The human dimensions: presence, voice, vocation, integrity LEARNING OUTCOMES

- Articulate the specific human dimensions of one's own work that AI cannot substitute for, and the reasons rooted in the organization's tradition and theology.

- Recognize the temptations created by AI assistance — toward efficiency over presence, output over relationship, scale over fidelity — and develop personal practices that resist them.

- Hold a conversation with a colleague who is over-using or under-using AI in ways that affect the work, in a way that deepens rather than strains the relationship.

This module is the formational core of the course. It is also the module where the segment-specific texture matters most — pastors process this material differently than development directors, who process it differently than seminary faculty. The course supports segment-specific cohorts where possible. The aim is not to argue against AI use but to develop the personal practices and theological clarity that make AI use sustainable in this kind of work over time. Practical exercise: each participant writes a one-page personal commitment articulating where they will and will not use AI in their work, and why, grounded in their own theology, vocation, and the people they serve. The commitment is shared with the cohort and revisited at the end of the course. Module 06: Forming others: from learning to teaching LEARNING OUTCOMES

- Select an appropriate recipe for a colleague who is beginning AI work, and supervise the initial use in a way that builds the colleague's discernment alongside their capability.

- Provide feedback on AI-assisted work in a way that develops the colleague's judgment rather than simply correcting the output.

- Recognize when a colleague has moved from supervised practice into independent competence, and adjust supervision accordingly.

Module six trains participants to do for others what the course is doing for them. This is the multiplier in the Skills stage: each participant becomes capable not only of their own competent practice but of forming others in the same competence. Organizations that complete Skills do not depend on Movemental for ongoing development. They develop their own people.

Practical exercise: each participant identifies a colleague (real or hypothetical, depending on organizational context) and develops a one-month formation plan for that colleague — recipes to start with, supervision rhythm, feedback approach, signs of readiness for independent work. The plan is reviewed by the cohort. Module 07: Leading adjudication conversations LEARNING OUTCOMES

- Lead a use case adjudication conversation that produces a decision the team can implement and the leadership can defend.

- Hear pushback on a proposed adjudication without becoming defensive, and modify the proposal in response to substantive challenge while maintaining substantive convictions.

- Document an adjudication decision in a way that future staff can read, understand, and apply.

Most AI work in organizations does not break because of bad technology. It breaks because of unmade decisions, unspoken disagreements, and undocumented adjudications. Module seven trains participants in the conversational and decisional discipline that keeps AI work on track at the team level. This is leadership work, regardless of the participant's formal role. Practical exercise: each participant facilitates a 30-minute adjudication conversation with their cohort on a use case the cohort has not yet considered. The conversation is recorded (or observed by the facilitator) and reviewed for the leadership skills the module names. Module 08: Knowing your limits: escalation and ongoing formation LEARNING OUTCOMES

- Recognize the situations that exceed one's current discernment, and know whom to escalate to and how.

- Develop a personal plan for ongoing formation in AI wisdom over the next 12-24 months as the technology and the field continue to evolve.

- Identify the resources, relationships, and rhythms that will sustain the participant's judgment as the conditions change.

Module eight closes the course by naming what the course cannot teach — and what the participant must develop on their own going forward. The conditions of AI work will continue to shift. The recipes will change. New use cases will emerge. New concerns will surface. The participant who has completed Skills is competent at this moment; staying competent requires ongoing formation. Module eight equips participants to lead their own formation rather than depending on Movemental indefinitely.

Practical exercise: each participant writes a personal ongoing formation plan — what they will read, who they will stay in conversation with, what rhythms of reflection they will maintain, when they will return to advanced learning. The plan is shared with the cohort and the facilitator. The course concludes with a closing conversation in which each participant names what they are taking with them.

### Six-D: Skills technological supports

LMS infrastructure. The course is delivered through Movemental's existing LMS (React, Next.js, Tailwind, Supabase). Each module is structured with reading inputs, video or written explanations, practical exercises with submission, cohort or asynchronous discussion, and assessment. The infrastructure supports cohort-facilitated, self-paced, and hybrid delivery from the same source. Agentic tutoring and feedback. Self-paced and hybrid participants receive agentic tutoring throughout the course. The agent reads participant submissions, provides initial feedback, surfaces patterns the participant should attend to, and escalates to a Movemental facilitator when human judgment is required. The agent does not replace the facilitator; it ensures that participants in self-paced learning still receive substantive engagement at scale. Recipe sandbox environment. Module three (recipe modification) and several practical exercises require participants to actually modify Claude skills. The course provides a configured sandbox environment in which modifications can be tested without risk to live organizational systems. The sandbox connects to the participant's organization's Safety documentation so modifications are pre- screened for alignment. Cohort interaction tools. For cohort-facilitated and hybrid delivery, the LMS supports synchronous video sessions, asynchronous threaded discussion, peer review of practical exercises, and the seventh-module facilitated adjudication conversations. The infrastructure is built to handle cohorts of 8-15 participants with one facilitator.

- Which delivery format fits our organization — cohort-facilitated (highest engagement, fixed schedule), self-paced (highest flexibility, more individual responsibility), or hybrid (balanced)?

- Who from our organization will participate? (Skills is most valuable for staff who will lead AI work in the organization. Typically 5-12 participants per cohort. Choose for leadership trajectory and capacity for formation, not just current AI enthusiasm.)

- What is our intended outcome for the cohort? (Some organizations want capacity-building across many staff; others want a small group of deep practitioners who will lead the rest. The intent shapes who participates and how the course is delivered.)

- How will the cohort's learning be integrated back into the organization? (Skills is wasted if it stays inside the participants. The integration plan — how participants share learning, lead adjudications, form others — is itself a leadership decision.)

- What investment of participant time is realistic? (3-5 hours per week for 8 weeks per participant.

Confirm before enrollment.)

- What is our cadence for ongoing formation after the course? (The eighth module produces a personal formation plan, but the organization should also have a collective rhythm — quarterly use case reviews, annual cohort reunions, ongoing facilitator engagement, etc.)

MOVEMENTAL'S ROLE BY LEVEL At Level 1 (Self-Paced License, $4,800/year per organization): The full course is licensed to the organization for self-paced delivery. Up to 25 participants per organization per year. Agentic tutoring throughout. Quarterly cohort moments where licensed- organization participants from across the network gather virtually for facilitated discussion. Annual update of course content as the field evolves. This is the volume tier — how Movemental serves organizations that want substantive formation at scale. At Level 2 (Cohort Facilitation, $15,000):

A Movemental facilitator runs an eight-week cohort for 8-15 participants from a single organization (or for multi-organization cohorts at the institution level). Weekly synchronous sessions, asynchronous discussion between sessions, facilitator review of practical exercises, and a closing conversation that integrates the cohort's learning into organizational planning. The standard offering for organizations engaging Movemental for Skills. At Level 3 (Network Engagement Skills, from $60,000 for institutions): Skills can be delivered as part of a Network Engagement for institutions whose member organizations need formation across the network. Typical structure: institutional cohort plus 3-10 member-organization cohorts, with shared resources, cross-cohort learning moments, and a culminating institution-level integration. The institution becomes a teaching artifact for its network. Certified facilitators can run the Skills course inside their own institution under license, with annual recertification.
