Part one: the reality of AI inside organizations in 2026
Movemental tells the organizations we serve that AI built on top of a fragmented human and 
informational layer amplifies dysfunction rather than reducing it. We tell them that the work has a 
sequence — Safety, Sandbox, Skills, Solutions — and that Safety is the non-negotiable first stage. 
Underneath that language is a claim about the world. The claim is that AI is already inside the 
organizations we serve, that it is already creating consequences, and that the absence of governance is 
producing measurable harm to mission, trust, and operations right now. This part tests the claim against 
current research.
Churches
The most authoritative current data on AI in churches comes from the 2026 State of AI in the Church 
Survey, conducted by Exponential AI NEXT and AIforChurchLeaders.com, drawing responses from church 
leaders across denominations, geographies, staff seniority, and functions. A separate Lifeway Research 
study of 1,003 Protestant pastors and 1,200 churchgoers, conducted in late 2025 and published in April 
2026, provides a complementary view of pastor and congregant attitudes. Together they document a 
sector that has crossed a threshold in adoption while remaining almost entirely without governance.
Adoption is widespread and accelerating.
The 2026 Exponential survey found that 91% of church leaders support AI use in ministry. Sixty-one 
percent now use it weekly or daily, with 25% using it daily. That compares to a 43% weekly-or-daily 
adoption rate in 2024. Adoption nearly doubled in two years. Seventy-eight percent of church leaders 
use AI weekly or more.
Sermon preparation is the most common use case. Sixty-four percent of pastors now use AI for sermon 
preparation. Other primary uses include text content creation (36%), research (22% to 26% across 
surveys), image generation (20%), and administrative work (16%). The Lifeway study found a more 
conservative adoption picture among rank-and-file Protestant pastors, but with a clear demographic

skew: pastors under 55, in urban settings, with graduate degrees, and at larger churches are far more 
likely to be active adopters.
Governance has not kept pace with use.
Only 9% of churches have a formal AI policy of any kind. Only 7% have an AI disclosure statement that 
informs their congregation about AI use. Seventy-three percent have nothing written at all. Fifty-one 
percent have not addressed AI use with their congregation in any way.
The risks are not theoretical. They are arriving.
Sixty percent of church leaders said they are "very concerned" about AI voice cloning being used to 
defraud their congregants. Twenty-five percent — one in four — said AI-generated scams or 
misinformation have already affected their church community. The pattern is documented in incident 
reports across multiple states (Alabama, Florida, Missouri, Nebraska, New York) and internationally. 
Scammers harvest publicly available sermon footage, livestream archives, and social media content to 
clone pastor voices and likenesses, then deploy those clones to solicit emergency donations from 
congregants. High-profile cases include Father Mike Schmitz and several megachurches. The FBI in late 
2025 warned that AI-supercharged holiday scams had produced more than 9,000 AI-related complaints.
Theological misalignment is the top concern.
Seventy-five percent of church leaders named theological misalignment as their top ethical concern. The 
concern is well-founded. Generative AI tools are trained on enormous corpora of internet text, including 
theological material from traditions across the spectrum. The output can cite scripture, use pastoral 
language, and still be doctrinally inconsistent with a specific church's convictions. Sixty-seven percent of 
church leaders also worry that AI will gradually replace the human connection that pastoral ministry 
depends on.
Congregations are watching.
Eighty-seven percent of church leaders said their congregation brings up AI regularly. Only 13% said the 
topic has not surfaced. Congregants are forming opinions about church AI use whether or not the 
church communicates about it. The question for church leadership is not whether the congregation will 
think about AI. They are already thinking about it. The question is whether the church's leadership 
shapes that conversation or absorbs whatever conclusions form in its absence.
Nonprofits
The most authoritative current data on AI in nonprofits comes from the 2026 Nonprofit AI Adoption 
Report, a benchmark study of 346 organizations conducted by Virtuous and Fundraising.AI and 
published in February 2026. A complementary view comes from the TechSoup 2025 State of AI in

Nonprofits Benchmark Report and a February 2026 Forvis Mazars analysis framing AI governance as a 
fiduciary responsibility for nonprofit boards.
Adoption is near universal.
Ninety-two percent of nonprofits use AI in some form. Seventy-nine percent of nonprofits report small 
to moderate efficiency gains from AI use — faster drafts, quicker research, improved content quality. 
The early-adopter question has been answered. The remaining questions are about how AI is being used 
and at what cost.
The use is fragmented and individual.
Eighty-one percent of nonprofits use AI individually rather than through shared workflows. The 
dominant pattern is one staff member experimenting with ChatGPT or a comparable tool — while the 
rest of the team continues with manual processes. Only 7% of nonprofits report major improvements in 
organizational capability through AI use, despite the 92% adoption rate.
Governance is broadly absent.
Forty-seven percent of nonprofits have no AI governance policy at all. The TechSoup benchmark found a 
more pessimistic figure: 76% of nonprofits have no formal AI strategy. Without a regulatory deadline or 
a major-funder requirement, AI policy work has been perpetually deprioritized. Staff find tools that help 
them, use them, and the organization catches up — often years later, sometimes only after an incident.
Donor and beneficiary data is at risk.
Nonprofit AI use overwhelmingly involves the organization's most sensitive data: donor giving histories, 
beneficiary records, program intake information, and sometimes protected health or financial details. 
When staff paste this data into consumer AI tools like the free tier of ChatGPT, the data may be retained 
by the model provider or used for model training. The Forvis Mazars February 2026 analysis explicitly 
frames this as a fiduciary issue. A board's traditional duties of care, loyalty, and obedience now include 
technological oversight.
Mission integrity is in tension with AI output.
AI-generated content reflects the statistical center of its training data, which is often misaligned with the 
mission, voice, theological commitments, or community standards of the nonprofit using it. Examples 
include AI-drafted donor appeals that subtly mischaracterize the program; AI-generated impact reports 
that round inconvenient details into a tidier narrative; AI-translated communications that lose the 
cultural specificity the original program was designed to serve. Each of these is an erosion of mission, 
often invisible to the staff producing it.

Institutions: seminaries, training networks, and denominational bodies
Institutions face a different version of the AI question because they operate on two timelines 
simultaneously: their own institutional adoption, and what they teach the next generation of leaders 
about AI.
Students are using AI heavily, with or without guidance.
Generative AI use in higher education is functionally universal among students under 30. International 
benchmarks show 63% of students using AI chatbots for text generation, 58% for translation, 55% for 
explaining difficult concepts, and 52% for summarizing academic articles. Seminary students are using AI 
for sermon drafting, exegesis assistance, paper writing, theological research, and increasingly as a kind 
of pastoral conversation partner.
Institutional policy varies wildly.
Some seminaries have published thoughtful policies (Columbia Theological Seminary, Saint Meinrad, 
Puritan Reformed, Austin Presbyterian). Others have nothing. There is no association-wide standard 
from the Association of Theological Schools. Faculty within the same seminary often hold different 
policies because no institutional policy exists to align them. Curriculum has not yet caught up to the 
formative reality.
Network responsibility is the unique institutional weight.
Whatever an institution adopts internally becomes the implicit template their member churches, 
nonprofits, and partner organizations look to. A denominational AI charter influences hundreds or 
thousands of congregations. A seminary's AI policy influences every church its graduates lead. 
Institutional inaction is not neutral. It is a choice that propagates through the network as the absence of 
guidance.
What is true across all three segments
Reading the church, nonprofit, and institutional data side by side, four patterns appear in all three: 
adoption has crossed a threshold no organization can opt out of; governance is the universal gap; the 
audiences these organizations serve are not unified about AI; and AI is already inside the organization in 
shadow form, below the line of formal organizational awareness.
AI built on top of a fragmented human and informational layer amplifies dysfunction rather 
than reducing it. The data confirms the claim. The governance gap is the fragmentation. The 
amplification is already underway. The work of Safety is to close the gap before further 
amplification compounds it.

Part two: the minimum viable Safety checklist
The Safety stage exists to do a specific, concrete, urgent piece of work: to bring an organization's AI use 
out of the shadows and into governed daylight, in the smallest set of artifacts that responsibly address 
the documented reality. It is not a full risk-management program. It is not a multi-month consulting 
engagement. It is the minimum that protects the organization from the harms the data shows are 
already arriving.
The MVP Safety checklist contains seven items. Six are written artifacts. The seventh is a single act of 
organizational communication. Together they take roughly two weeks to draft and ratify when an 
experienced facilitator is involved, and somewhat longer when the organization works through them 
independently.
The seven items in summary
•Item 1. AI Use Charter — the principles and posture document
•Item 2. Acceptable Use Policy — the operational rules for staff
•Item 3. Data Handling Standards — what data may touch which tools
•Item 4. Disclosure and Attribution Standard — when AI involvement is named publicly
•Item 5. Pastoral / Programmatic / Educational Care Boundaries — where AI must not go
•Item 6. Voice Cloning and Impersonation Response Plan — the protocol for the active criminal 
threat
•Item 7. Communication to the Constituent Base — the public act
What this list deliberately does not include
Several items that appear on more elaborate AI governance frameworks are not on this MVP list: vendor 
evaluation frameworks, formal AI risk registers, full incident response protocols beyond impersonation, 
vendor data processing agreements, and annual review processes. These are appropriate for 
organizations that have completed the MVP work and want to mature beyond it. They are not 
minimum-viable.

Item 01: AI Use Charter
What this is
A short document — typically two to four pages — that names the organization's posture on AI. It 
declares the principles guiding AI use, the lines AI should not cross in this organization, and the values 
the leadership wants the staff to apply when AI questions arise that the charter does not anticipate. The 
charter is not a policy. It is the page a board ratifies and a senior leader can hand to a new staff member 
to communicate "this is how we think about this."
Cost of not doing it. Without a charter, the organization has no shared frame for AI decisions. Every 
staff member operates from their own theology, ethics, or vendor enthusiasm. The communications 
director, the pastoral care lead, and the executive director all hold different assumptions, and the 
inconsistency surfaces as small mistakes that erode trust over time. In the 2026 data, this is the gap 
behind the 91% of churches and 47% to 76% of nonprofits with no governance: there is no shared 
starting point for the rest of the work.

A1. BOILERPLATE TEXT
The following is the templated language for an AI Use Charter. Square-bracketed fields are organization-
specific and require completion. Italicized parenthetical notes are guidance for the customizer and are 
removed in the final document.
AI Use Charter for [Organization Name]
Adopted by [Board / Elder Council / Leadership Team] on [Date].
Why this charter exists. Artificial intelligence tools have entered the daily work of [Organization 
Name] whether we have authorized them or not. Members of our staff, our volunteers, and our 
community are using AI tools to draft communications, conduct research, generate content, and 
increasingly to seek counsel. We believe these tools can serve our mission well when used with 
discernment, and we believe they can damage our mission when used without it. This charter 
names the principles by which we will use AI in our work, the lines we will not cross, and the 
values we expect our staff and partners to apply when situations arise that this charter does not 
anticipate.
Our mission is [insert mission statement or short framing of organizational purpose]. AI is a tool. 
Our mission is the measure. Where AI serves the mission, we use it. Where AI threatens the 
mission, we do not.
Principles guiding our use of AI.
First: the human comes first. AI does not replace human discernment, presence, or relationship 
in the work that matters most. We use AI to serve people, not to substitute for the humans 
those people came to be in relationship with.
Second: integrity is non-negotiable. [For churches: The theological integrity of our teaching, 
preaching, and pastoral care.] [For nonprofits: The integrity of our mission, our beneficiary 
relationships, and our donor trust.] [For institutions: The integrity of our formation work and our 
accountability to the leaders we are forming.] AI-generated or AI-assisted content that touches 
these areas is reviewed by a human responsible for that integrity before it reaches the people 
we serve.
Third: data is held with care. The data of our [members / donors / beneficiaries / students] is 
sacred. We do not expose it to AI tools that do not meet our data handling standards. We treat 
our data with the same care our people have a right to expect.

Fourth: transparency builds trust. We tell our [congregation / donors / community / students] 
what role AI plays in our work. We do not pretend AI is not present. We do not pretend AI is 
more central than it is. We are honest about how we use it, and we invite questions.
Fifth: discernment over efficiency. When efficiency and discernment pull in different directions,

Lines we will not cross.
We will not use AI as a substitute for [pastoral care / direct beneficiary care / spiritual

We will not expose [member records / donor data / beneficiary information / student records /

We will not use AI in ways that are deceptive to the people we serve.

our staff to apply the principles above and to err on the side of [discernment / caution /

We will review this charter [annually / every two years]. We will revise it as our understanding

Item 02: Acceptable Use Policy
What this is
A working document that names which AI tools are approved for use in the organization, what they are 
approved for, what they are not approved for, and what the consequences are for violating the 
boundaries. This is the operational artifact staff actually consult. It should be specific enough to answer 
real questions: Can I paste donor data into ChatGPT? Can I use AI to draft a sermon and not disclose it? 
Can a youth pastor use an AI counseling app with a student?
Cost of not doing it. Without an acceptable use policy, staff make individual judgments under time 
pressure, and those judgments accumulate into organizational practice without anyone deciding it 
should. The most common failure mode in the 2026 nonprofit data — 81% of nonprofits using AI 
individually without shared workflows — is downstream of this gap. Without a policy, donor data 
ends up in consumer AI tools. Pastoral notes get processed through models that retain training data. 
Beneficiary records get pasted into chatbots for "summarization." Each is a real incident pattern, and 
each is preventable with one written page.

Item 03: Data Handling Standards
What this is
A specific document — usually one to two pages — that names what categories of organizational data 
may and may not be exposed to AI tools, and under what conditions. The categories are sector-specific. 
The handling rules are mostly standard. The mapping of which data the organization actually has and 
where it lives is genuinely organization-specific.
Cost of not doing it. Without data handling standards, staff treat all data as equivalent and make 
the worst kind of mistake: pasting the most sensitive information into the least controlled tool. The 
2026 nonprofit data documents this directly — donor records and beneficiary data routinely flowing 
through consumer AI tools without contractual data protection. Major-donor confidence, legal 
exposure, and beneficiary safety can be compromised in seconds, and the organization often does 
not know until much later.

Data Handling Standards for AI Tools at [Organization Name]
Effective [Date]. Reviewed [Cadence].
Purpose. This document classifies the data our organization holds, defines which categories of 
data may be processed by AI tools, and specifies the conditions under which any such processing

Data classification. Our organization holds data in the following categories. Each category has a 
specific handling standard.
Category A — Public Information. Information that is already publicly available about our 
organization: published sermons, public newsletters, public-facing program descriptions, public 
board members, public financial summaries. Handling: may be processed in any approved AI 
tool (paid tier) without further restriction.
Category B — Internal Operational Information. Information used in the course of organizational 
work but not generally protected: internal meeting notes, draft communications, project plans, 
internal training material. Handling: may be processed in approved AI tools with paid-tier 
accounts that do not retain inputs for training. Free-tier consumer AI tools are not approved for 
this data.
Category C — Constituent Identifying Information. [Member directory data / donor names and 
giving histories / beneficiary intake records / student records and academic information]. 
Handling: may only be processed in AI tools with explicit contractual data protection (Enterprise-
tier accounts with signed Data Processing Agreements, on-premises tools, or organizational AI 
tools we have specifically vetted). Consumer AI tools — even paid tiers — are not approved 
unless their data terms have been verified by [Designated Leader].

Stage 02: Sandbox
Part five: Sandbox Discovery
The Sandbox stage is the exploratory, disciplined, locally-tested discovery of where AI actually creates 
value in this organization, what use cases are wise to pursue, what use cases need revision before 
pursuit, and what use cases are off the table. It is a four-week engagement priced at $15,000 in its 
facilitated form.
The premise of the Sandbox stage is that AI is not a technological challenge first. The interface to current 
AI tools is human communication. Cutting-edge AI tools do not present as conventional technology — 
they present as human simulation. The way to evaluate AI in any organization is therefore not to study it 
from a distance but to put real recipes in the hands of real staff doing real work, observe what happens, 
and adjudicate the findings against the values and constraints documented in the Safety stage. The 
Sandbox is not a training program. It is a structured exploration with documented outcomes.
Five-A: What Sandbox produces
Sandbox Discovery produces three connected deliverables.
Deliverable one: a Use Case Portfolio.
A working document containing every use case explored during the four-week engagement, with for 
each: a description of the workflow, the AI tool or recipe applied, the staff member or team that tested 
it, the time the test took, the value the use case promises (time saved, revenue generated, work 
improved), the human or ethical concerns surfaced, the integrity considerations relative to the 
organization's Safety documentation, and a final adjudication of green light, yellow light, or red light. 
The portfolio is the document the organization keeps and uses to make decisions about which use cases 
to deploy, modify, or set aside.
Deliverable two: a Discernment Memo.
A short narrative document — typically four to eight pages — that names the patterns the Sandbox 
surfaced. What kinds of use cases consistently produced value in this organization. Which kinds of use 
cases consistently raised concerns. What the team learned about itself in the process. What the 
leadership now sees that they did not see before. The Discernment Memo is the document that informs 
the decision about whether to proceed to Skills, to Solutions, or to neither.
Deliverable three: a Readiness Assessment.

The Movemental AI Path  ·  Full Source of Truth
A brief evaluation of whether the organization is ready to proceed to Skills (deepening human capacity 
to lead this work) or directly to Solutions (technological deployment). Not every organization needs 
every stage. Some are well-served by Sandbox and the Discernment Memo, then a focused Solutions 
deployment. Some need substantial Skills work before any further technology is added. The Readiness 
Assessment names which path is right for this organization based on what the Sandbox surfaced.
Five-B: The traffic-light system
Every use case tested in the Sandbox receives one of three signals. The signals are only assigned in the 
context of discovered potential value — the Sandbox does not adjudicate use cases the team did not 
pursue, did not test, or did not surface. Adjudication happens for use cases that have demonstrated 
some promise, with the integrity question evaluated against that promise.
GREEN LIGHT.  The use case produces clear value, raises no significant concerns under the 
organization's Safety documentation, can be performed by current staff with reasonable 
training, and serves the mission rather than warping it. Green light means the use case is ready 
for deployment in Solutions or for immediate productive use within the boundaries already 
documented.
YELLOW LIGHT.  The use case produces value but requires documented revision before 
deployment. The revision could be technical (the recipe needs adjustment), procedural (the 
workflow needs review or oversight added), formational (the staff using it need additional 
preparation), or ethical (a concern surfaced that requires a leadership decision). Yellow light 
means promising but not yet ready. The Discernment Memo captures what specifically must 
change before the use case is deployed.
RED LIGHT.  The use case cannot be pursued under the organization's current Safety framework, 
mission integrity, or constituent commitments. The reason is documented and named clearly. 
Red light is not a negotiation. It is the sober recognition that not every promising-on-paper use 
case is wise in practice for this specific organization. Red light use cases are valuable to 
document because they protect the organization from later attempts to deploy what should 
have been declined.
Five-C: The Sandbox process, week by week
Week 0: Pre-engagement assessment.
Before the engagement formally begins, the organization completes a broad assessment that maps 
where each staff member currently stands. The assessment is delivered as a Claude artifact — a 
structured interactive intake — and asks each participant about their current workflow, their team 
context, their feelings about AI, the tools they currently use, their experience with AI to date, and the

friction points in their work that they wish were different. The output is a personalized starting point for 
each participant. This is the moment Movemental meets each person where they are, not where the 
organization wishes they were. The assessment respects that AI work is most usefully tested as locally as 
possible — down to the user — because each staff member's relationship to AI, their tasks, their fears, 
and their existing tools is specific to them.
Week 1: Recipe deployment and value mapping.
Each participant is given a small set of pre-built Claude skills — recipes — adapted to their specific role. 
The recipes are configured against the organization's Safety documentation and are pre-screened to 
operate within Data Handling Standards and Care Boundaries. Participants run the recipes against real 
workflows: a communications director drafts donor appeals with a Donor Letter recipe; a pastor 
researches a sermon series with a Sermon Research recipe; an executive director summarizes board 
materials with a Board Briefing recipe; an operations lead drafts policies with a Policy Drafting recipe. 
Each participant logs their experience: time taken, time saved relative to baseline, output quality, 
concerns observed.
The point of this week is value discovery. Not training. Not theory. Real recipes against real work, with 
the friction and the value both made visible. Participants do not need to understand prompt engineering 
or model selection to run a recipe well. The recipe handles the technical complexity. The participant 
brings the contextual judgment. This honors what the AI moment actually is — a frontier we need to 
navigate immediately rather than a topic we need to study before approaching.
Week 2: Concern surfacing and use case expansion.
Participants reconvene, having now lived with the recipes for a week. The conversation in week two 
surfaces what worked well, what produced unease, what surprised the participant about their own 
response to AI assistance, and what new use cases the participant would now like to explore based on 
what they have already tried. New recipes are configured for the second-week tests. Participants who 
saw promising patterns extend their use; participants who encountered concerns slow down and 
explore those concerns more deliberately.
The facilitator (Movemental staff) attends to several specific signals during week two. Where is AI-
assisted work landing back in the participant's human workflow well? Where is it creating new friction? 
Where are participants beginning to over-trust the output? Where are participants under-trusting it and 
missing real value? The facilitator's job is not to push participants harder toward AI use but to help each 
participant calibrate accurately.
Week 3: Adjudication and revision.
The facilitator works with participants and leadership to adjudicate each tested use case. Some receive 
green-light status immediately. Others need a working session to determine what revision would

red-light status with documentation of why. The Use Case Portfolio takes shape during this week.
Week 4: Discernment Memo and Readiness Assessment.
The facilitator drafts the Discernment Memo and the Readiness Assessment based on what the four 
weeks revealed. The leadership reviews and refines. The engagement concludes with a leadership 
conversation that names what the organization has learned, what it now believes is true about its 
relationship with AI, and what it will do next. The conversation is structured but not scripted. It is the 
moment the organization moves from exploring to deciding.
Five-D: Sandbox technological supports
The Sandbox is the first stage at which Movemental's technological capability becomes directly visible to 
the organization. Three technological supports are central.
Pre-built Claude skills (recipes). Movemental maintains a growing library of Claude skills configured for 
the work organizations in our segments actually do. These are not generic prompts. They are skills 
configured against the organization's Safety documentation, adapted to the role of the staff member

from scratch.
The assessment artifact. A Claude artifact delivered in week zero that surfaces each participant's

person actually is, and produces a personalized starting profile that informs which recipes are

logs their tests; the facilitator adjudicates each entry; the portfolio accumulates as a living document 
throughout the engagement and is exported as the deliverable at week four.

Stage 03: Skills
Part six: Skills Development
The Skills stage is the formational deepening of human capacity to lead AI work in this organization with 
wisdom. It is an eight-week engagement priced at $15,000 (cohort facilitation) or $4,800 per year per 
organization (self-paced license).
The premise of the Skills stage is that running recipes is a starting point, not an end point. Organizations 
that complete Sandbox have demonstrated they can use AI productively. Organizations that complete 
Skills have developed the discernment to lead AI work — to evaluate new use cases, modify existing 
recipes, recognize when AI is the wrong tool, and form other staff in the same wisdom. The shift is from 
practitioner to discerner. From running well-designed recipes to making well-considered judgments 
about what to run, when, how, and whether.
Six-A: The metaphor and the shift
We have used the language of recipe and chef in conversation about this stage. The metaphor captures 
something true — the shift from following someone else's instructions to making your own informed 
decisions — but a stronger frame for what Skills actually accomplishes is the shift from practitioner to 
discerner.
A practitioner runs the recipe well. They follow the steps, attend to the inputs, produce the output. 
Their competence is measured by the quality of their execution. In the AI context, a practitioner uses 
Claude skills configured by someone else, applied to workflows they have been trained on, against data 
their Safety documentation has approved. They do good work.
A discerner does all of that, and also knows when the recipe should not be run. When the workflow has 
shifted in ways the recipe no longer fits. When the data carries weight the recipe does not account for. 
When the constituent on the other side of the work would experience the recipe differently than the 
practitioner does. When the organization's mission is served by saying no rather than yes. The discerner 
reads the situation, weighs it against the values and convictions documented in Safety, considers the 
people involved, and chooses. Their competence is measured by the quality of their judgment.
The Skills stage forms discerners. The work is not technological — though technology is the context. The 
work is formational — though competence is the result. Every module builds judgment as much as 
capability.

Six-B: Learning outcomes for Skills Development
At the conclusion of Skills Development, participants are able to:
•Read an AI use case in context — understanding the workflow, the data, the people, the integrity 
considerations, and the organizational values that bear on the case — and articulate a position on 
whether and how to proceed, with reasoning their leadership can review and ratify.
•Modify an existing Claude skill or recipe to better fit a workflow that has evolved or a context the 
original recipe did not anticipate, including adjusting prompts, context windows, output structure, 
and integration with downstream systems where applicable.
•Identify the difference between a use case that needs technical refinement, a use case that needs 
procedural change, a use case that needs formational preparation of the staff using it, and a use 
case that should not proceed at all — and recommend the appropriate response for each.
•Evaluate AI output for quality, fit-to-context, alignment with the organization's voice and 
convictions, and absence of subtle errors that the staff member must catch before publication or 
deployment.
•Form other staff in beginning AI competence by selecting appropriate recipes, supervising initial 
use, providing feedback, and recognizing when a colleague has crossed from supervised practice 
into independent capability.
•Hold the human and theological dimensions of AI work — including questions of presence, 
formation, vocation, voice, and integrity — with sufficient depth to navigate situations the Safety 
documentation does not explicitly anticipate.
•Lead a use case adjudication conversation with peers and leadership — proposing a position, 
hearing pushback, modifying the proposal, and arriving at a decision the team can implement and 
the leadership can defend.
•Recognize the limits of one's own discernment and know when to escalate — to leadership, to

The course architecture is built on Movemental's existing LMS infrastructure (React, Next.js, Tailwind,

Module 01: AI as human simulation: what we are actually working with
LEARNING OUTCOMES
•Articulate why current AI tools present as human simulation rather than as conventional 
technology, and why this changes the nature of the competence required to use them well.
•Describe the specific ways AI output mirrors human language, judgment, and authority — and 
where this mirroring is reliable, where it is misleading, and where it is dangerous.
•Distinguish between using AI as a tool, using AI as a collaborator, and using AI as a substitute — 
and recognize which mode is operating in any given workflow.
Module one establishes the conceptual frame for everything that follows. Most AI training treats AI as a 
sophisticated tool. This module argues — and demonstrates — that current AI is better understood as 
human simulation: it speaks our languages, mimics our reasoning, produces our cultural artifacts, and 
presents itself through the same interface as another human collaborator. The competence required to 
work with human simulation is not technical training. It is discernment about when the simulation is 
helpful, when it is misleading, and when something other than simulation is needed.
Practical exercise: each participant brings a recent piece of AI-generated work from their own role and 
analyzes it against this frame. Where did the AI succeed at simulating what was needed? Where did it 
fail? What did the participant's human judgment add that the simulation could not?
Module 02: Reading a use case in context
LEARNING OUTCOMES
•Identify the seven dimensions of context that bear on any AI use case — workflow, data, people, 
integrity, values, frequency, and downstream effect — and apply the framework to a use case 
from the participant's own work.
•Articulate why context is the irreducible human contribution to AI work, and what this means for 
how staff at every level should be formed.
•Recognize when a use case proposal is missing context the practitioner has assumed and the 
proposer has not surfaced.
This module trains the practitioner-to-discerner shift directly. A use case is never just "use AI to do X." It 
is always "use AI to do X, with this data, in this workflow, affecting these people, against these integrity 
standards, at this frequency, with these downstream effects." The participant who can read all seven

dimensions of context produces dramatically better adjudications than the participant who reads one or 
two.
Practical exercise: each participant adjudicates three use cases from a curated library — one green-light, 
one yellow-light, one red-light — and writes the reasoning that supports each adjudication. Cohort 
discussion compares adjudications and surfaces where participants read context differently.
Module 03: Modifying a recipe with discernment
LEARNING OUTCOMES
•Modify an existing Claude skill or recipe to fit a workflow that has evolved, by adjusting prompts, 
context, output structure, and integration points.
•Distinguish between modification that improves the recipe and modification that compromises 
the recipe's alignment with Safety documentation.
•Recognize the limits of one's own modification authority and know when to escalate to a more

Module three is the most technically substantive in the course, and is also the module where the 
formational dimension matters most. Modifying a recipe well requires both the technical capacity to 
make the change and the judgment to know whether the change is wise. Participants learn to modify 
recipes responsibly — making them better at the work the organization actually does, while keeping 
them aligned with the values the organization has documented.
Practical exercise: each participant takes one recipe from their Sandbox engagement and produces a 
modified version tuned to a workflow that has shifted since the original Sandbox. The modification is 
reviewed by the cohort and the facilitator.
Module 04: Evaluating AI output: catching what the simulation misses
LEARNING OUTCOMES
•Evaluate AI output for quality, voice fit, contextual accuracy, subtle factual errors, and alignment 
with the organization's convictions.
•Describe the categories of error that current AI produces consistently, and the patterns that 
indicate output should not be used as-is.
•Develop a personal review discipline that catches the errors most relevant to one's own role.

adjudication documentation, and ongoing formation plans, all building on Sandbox findings and Safety 
documentation. Solutions produces the deployment, the deployment governance integration document,

constitute its AI infrastructure: governance documents, adjudicated use cases, formed staff, deployed 
capability, and a documented integration that connects all of it. This infrastructure is durable. It survives 
staff turnover. It survives technology changes. It survives the organization's next season of work. It is, 
quite literally, the foundation Movemental promised at the beginning of the path.
