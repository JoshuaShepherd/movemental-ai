## Editorial bio

Alan Hirsch is the missiologist who coined the term "movemental" — the namesake of this platform — and built the intellectual architecture much of the contemporary missional church now runs on. Born in Johannesburg in 1959, raised in apartheid-era South Africa, he emigrated to Australia in 1983 and pastored the South Melbourne Restoration Community for fifteen years among addicts, sex workers, and the chronically housed before turning that ministry's lessons into theory.

His magnum opus, The Forgotten Ways (Brazos, 2006), introduced the mDNA framework and the concept of Apostolic Genius; 5Q (2017) and The Permanent Revolution (Jossey-Bass, 2012, with Tim Catchim) have made APEST one of the most widely adopted readings of Ephesians 4 in the Western church, with more than 150,000 assessments taken through his platforms. Across twenty books published with seven publishers — including The Shaping of Things to Come with Michael Frost, ReJesus, Untamed with Debra Hirsch, and Metanoia with Rob Kelly — he has built a coherent line from Christology through missiology to ecclesiology.

He founded 100Movements, co-founded the Forge Mission Training Network and 5Q Collective, and co-founded the M.A. in Missional Church Movements at Wheaton College. He teaches as adjunct faculty at Fuller, George Fox, and Asbury, and works as embedded consultant for Redeemer City to City and movement mentor for NewThing International.

## Unique Contribution

### What only this author says

- **Apostolic Genius / mDNA as a systematic, named framework**: While others have written about missional church, organic church, or movement dynamics, Hirsch is the only thinker who has articulated a comprehensive, named, six-element operating system (mDNA) that integrates Christology, discipleship, mission, leadership, organizational theory, and communal formation into a single coherent model. The concept that every church carries dormant movement DNA that can be "reactivated" is distinctively his.

- **APEST as ecclesial intelligence for all believers, not just leaders**: Others have written about fivefold ministry, but Hirsch's distinctive claim is that APEST describes callings latent in every believer (not just leaders) and that the Western church has systematically "exiled" three of the five (Apostle, Prophet, Evangelist), creating a structurally imbalanced institution incapable of movement. The 5Q framework -- measuring ecclesial intelligence like IQ or EQ -- is his unique contribution.

- **"Jesus is Lord" as a system-level governor**: The idea that a church's Christological maturity caps the performance of every other dimension of its life -- that if "Jesus is Lord" is at Level 2, the whole mDNA system is capped at Level 2 -- is a distinctive systems-theoretic insight applied to theology.

### Where they disagree with peers

| Topic | Their Position | Mainstream Position |
|-------|---------------|-------------------|
| APEST scope | All five gifts are for all believers, distributed across the entire Body; the church has exiled APE functions | Most evangelical traditions limit APEST to leadership offices or argue apostles/prophets ceased (cessationism) |
| Institution vs. movement | Institutionalization is deviation from the apostolic norm, not maturation toward it | Mainstream ecclesiology views institutional development as healthy maturation; 9Marks and Reformed traditions defend institutional church |
| Attractional church model | "Come and see" is a Christendom-era artifact; "go and be" (incarnational) is normative | Many evangelical and megachurch leaders defend attractional methods as legitimate gospel strategy |
| Sacred-secular divide | Hellenistic dualism has distorted Western Christianity; Hebraic holism is the biblical norm | Traditional Reformed and Catholic theology maintains valid sacred-secular distinctions (e.g., two-kingdoms theology) |
| Apostolic ministry today | Active apostolic ministry is essential and ongoing; the church needs apostles now | Cessationist traditions argue apostolic office ended with the first century; Reformed traditions argue "apostle" is a completed office |

## Hirsch affinity rubric

# Hirsch Affinity Rubric (0–100)

## Purpose

This rubric measures the **conceptual and relational affinity** of any published author to Alan Hirsch—i.e., how closely their thought, practice, and positioning align with Hirsch’s body of work. It is designed to be completed **without reading Hirsch’s original books**, using organic search and agentic knowledge: patterns discoverable through web search, citations, affiliations, and observable language.

**Use cases:**
- Ranking authors by Hirsch affinity for content discovery
- Search-and-classification pipelines for movement-leader research
- Mapping author clusters in missional/APEST/movement space
- Identifying hidden affinities (authors who think like Hirsch but don’t cite him)

---

## Design Principle: Search-Completable Without Book Access

Each dimension is scored from evidence that can be found via:

- Web search (articles, bios, interviews)
- Citation / reference analysis
- Organizational affiliations and conference rosters
- Co-authorship and endorsement networks
- Observable vocabulary and conceptual patterns
- AI / agent summaries of publicly available content

**Not required:** Direct access to *The Forgotten Ways*, *Shaping of Things to Come*, *5Q*, or other Hirsch books.

---

## Hirsch Signature Concepts (Reference for Assessors)

Use these as the conceptual anchor. Affinity is higher when an author references, builds on, or independently expresses these ideas.

| Concept | Observable Signals |
|--------|---------------------|
| **APEST / 5Q** | Fivefold ministry (Apostle, Prophet, Evangelist, Shepherd, Teacher); apostolic environments; Ephesians 4 leadership |
| **mDNA / Apostolic Genius** | Missional DNA; six elements (Jesus is Lord, disciple-making, apostolic environment, missional-incarnational impulse, organic systems, communitas); “reactivating latent” language |
| **Centered set vs. bounded set** | Community defined by movement toward Jesus rather than boundaries; drawing people closer vs. defining in/out |
| **Incarnational mission** | 6 P’s (Presence, Proximity, Prevenience, Passion, Powerlessness, Proclamation); “you can’t commute to mission”; embodied witness |
| **Organic movement vs. institution** | Small, reproducible, networked structures; multiplication over addition; movement DNA over church-growth formulas |
| **Communitas** | Community forged in liminality, shared mission, risk; beyond comfort-based community |
| **Disciple-making systems** | Obedience-based, reproducible, relational discipleship; not information transfer |
| **Missional church** | Church as sent, not gathered-to-consume; mission-shaped ecclesiology |

## Movemental Audience Implications

- **Content format priority**: Books and structured courses should be the anchor content on the platform. Hirsch's audience is accustomed to deep, framework-driven content and will pay for it. The 150,000+ APEST assessment users represent a built-in audience for digital training products. Video content is a major gap -- centralizing his dispersed video footprint (38+ videos across 20+ channels) onto Movemental would provide immediate differentiation.

- **Topic focus**: APEST/5Q content should be the lead offering -- it has the broadest appeal, the most engaged audience (150K+ assessments), and the most practical application. The Forgotten Ways / mDNA framework is the prestige content that establishes credibility. Discipleship and organizational transformation (Metanoia) content should follow. Communitas/liminality is a growing topic that resonates with post-COVID church leaders.

- **Cross-pollination**: Michael Frost shares the largest audience overlap and would be the highest-value companion author on the platform. Brad Brisco (if already on Movemental) would cross-link naturally through Forge and Exponential networks. Neil Cole, Hugh Halter, and Alan Roxburgh share significant audience segments. Dave Ferguson bridges into the megachurch/multisite audience that Hirsch alone does not fully reach.

- **Growth opportunity**: The biggest growth opportunity is digital -- Hirsch's audience is underserved online. No personal YouTube channel, no major free online course, no centralized video library. The 150,000+ APEST assessment users are a warm audience that could be converted to platform subscribers. International growth (UK, Australia, New Zealand, South Africa) is also significant given his origins and existing conference relationships in those markets.

- **Monetization signal**: Strong. Hirsch's audience already pays for content: books across 7 publishers, paid masterclasses on Ephesiology, paid Forge residencies, paid APEST assessments, conference registration fees ($200-500+), and consulting engagements with megachurches (Future Travelers). Seminary students pay tuition for programs he teaches in. The audience is comprised of church leaders with organizational budgets who regularly invest in leadership development. The 150,000+ APEST assessment completions at paid or registration-gated access is the strongest monetization signal -- this audience is accustomed to paying for frameworks and tools, not just consuming free content.
