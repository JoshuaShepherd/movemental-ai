## Editorial bio

Brad Brisco is one of the leading voices in the North American missional church movement. As Director of Multiplication Strategies for Send Network (a ministry of the North American Mission Board), he shapes church-planting strategy at a national level, with a particular emphasis on missional communities, covocational ministry, and incarnational presence. His distinctive position is the seam most missional writers avoid: he translates post-Christendom missional theology — Newbigin, Bosch, Frost, Hirsch — into the working language of a Southern Baptist denominational system that has not always reached for it. His path to ministry was unconventional. He worked in the restaurant industry before seminary, an experience that later grounded his conviction that the marketplace is not secondary to ministry but an essential context for mission. He holds an undergraduate degree in health care administration, a Master of Divinity, and a doctorate in missional ecclesiology (dissertation on helping existing congregations transition toward a missional direction); as of 2025 he is pursuing a Doctor of Ministry at Kairos University.[^bio][^idverify]

His written corpus moves in stages alongside Lance Ford — from the small-group curriculum *Missional Essentials* (2012), through *The Missional Quest* (InterVarsity Press, 2013, foreword by Ed Stetzer) and *Next Door As It Is In Heaven* (NavPress, 2016), to the free e-book *ReThink* and his solo synthesis *Covocational Church Planting* (Missional Press, 2021). His intellectual awakening came in 2003, when he read *The Shaping of Things to Come* by Michael Frost and Alan Hirsch — a book he credits with fundamentally changing the way he thought about church, mission, and church planting.[^bio][^theology]

His current institutional footprint centers on NAMB/Send Network (Director of Multiplication Strategies, evolved from an earlier "Director of Bivocational Church Planting" role), the Missional Church Network blog he founded, and a co-founded grassroots ecosystem — Forge Kansas City, KC Sentral, and the Sentralized Conference — plus a seat on the Forge America national leadership team. He now lives in Treasure Island, Florida, with his wife and three children; the family was impacted by Hurricane Helene in late 2024. It is a deliberately distributed footprint that mirrors the institutional and grassroots circles he has always tried to hold together.[^bio][^committedvoice]

## Frameworks

### Covocational Church Planting

| Field | Value |
|-------|-------|
| Introduced | 2021 (*Covocational Church Planting*); earlier articles at NAMB/Send Network |
| Components | Bivocational = a second job to supplement a church salary, with intent to go full-time; Covocational = the marketplace calling IS the primary vocation, church planting is integrated, with no intent to leave the marketplace |
| Visual / model | — (conceptual distinction; no published diagram located) |
| Adoption evidence | Adopted by NAMB/Send Network (free bilingual e-book), Missio Alliance, multiple podcast hosts who frame conversations using the term; on the Wheaton Billy Graham Center church-planting reading list |
| Source | [Amazon — Covocational Church Planting](https://www.amazon.com/Covocational-Church-Planting-Aligning-Marketplace/dp/1736282115), [missioalliance.org](https://www.missioalliance.org/the-welcome-rise-of-co-vocational-church-planters/) |

The traditional "bivocational" label carries an implicit hierarchy — pastoral ministry is the real calling, marketplace work a necessary compromise. Brisco's "covocational" reframing eliminates that hierarchy: marketplace presence is a strategic and sacred missionary advantage, not a limitation. The reframing from "bi-" to "co-" carries the entire theological argument.[^contentanalysis]

| Field | Value |
|-------|-------|
| Introduced | "What is a Missional Community?" blog post; NAMB article on missional communities and church planting |
| Components | A committed group of 12–25 Jesus followers, Spirit-empowered, place-based, mission-oriented |

The basic unit of incarnational mission — larger than an inward-focused small group, smaller than a program-driven congregation — sized to the "extended family" social unit and organized around a shared place or network rather than a shared program.[^contentanalysis]

| Field | Value |
|-------|-------|

### Antithesis prohibition (non-negotiable)

Do not overuse contrastive negation as the *default* move. While Brisco's signature reframing pattern is "X is not simply Y; it is really Z" (e.g. "Bivocational church planting is not simply about having two or more jobs; it is really about aligning one life…"), his own voice guide names "Antithesis Overuse — 'Not X, but Y' as the default move" as a failure mode, preferring "additive, forward-building language ('We also need…,' 'Building on that…')." His consistent correction of readers is that mission must be grounded in theology *first* — he refuses the "Corporate Consultant" register ("optimize your missional engagement," "leverage your congregational assets"), the "Program Jockey" register (tactics without missional framing), and "Vague Encouragement" ("just be missional" with no grounding or next step). The order is always why → what → how. Derived from [profile/voice-analysis.md] and the "Failure Modes" section of [BRAD_BRISCO_VOICE_IDENTITY.md].

## Theological positioning

| Domain | Position |
|--------|----------|
| Tradition | Evangelical (works within the Southern Baptist Convention via NAMB/Send Network) in theology; post-evangelical / missional in methodology; draws on missio Dei and Anabaptist-influenced praxis (via Frost/Hirsch) |
| Ecclesiology | Low-church, missional-incarnational; the church is a missionary community before it is an institution; critique of church as "vendor of religious goods and services" |
| Missiology | Strongly incarnational; mission begins with presence in a place, not invitation to a place; North America as a post-Christendom mission field |
| Leadership | Distributed / APEST-leaning; diminishes the clergy-laity divide; covocational model inherently disperses ministry beyond professional clergy |
| Christology | A "robust understanding of Jesus" is essential for church life and missional engagement |
| Anthropology / formation | Every believer is a sent person; centered-set (orientation toward Jesus) over bounded-set (in/out) discipleship |
| Culture | Theology of place — where you live matters theologically; neighborhood engagement is participation in the missio Dei, not optional outreach |

### Distinctive claims

- **The covocational distinction**: marketplace calling and church-planting calling are *co-equal* vocations, not primary and secondary — the terminological reframing from "bi-" to "co-" carries the whole argument.
- **Missional theology inside SBC structures**: a missional-incarnational voice operating from within one of the largest evangelical denominational apparatuses (NAMB/Send Network), translating post-Christendom missiology into mainstream church-planting language.
- The church is missionary **by nature, not merely by activity** — the church IS the missionary, it does not just send them.
- Church plants should **begin with missional-community life and incarnational presence**, not with a Sunday worship launch.
- A **systematic, sequential** set of named paradigm shifts (9/12) deployable for denominational and network training — pedagogical structuring most missional writers do not provide.
