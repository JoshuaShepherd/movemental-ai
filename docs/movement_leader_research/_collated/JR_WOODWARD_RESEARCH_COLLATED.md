## Editorial bio

JR Woodward (legal name Gailand Woodward Jr.) is a church planter, missiologist, and movement leader who has spent more than three decades establishing missional-incarnational communities across the United States. He founded New Life Christian Fellowship (NLCF) at Virginia Tech in 1989 as part of Great Commission Ministries, then co-planted Kairos Los Angeles out of East Hollywood around 2002–2003, living in the neighborhood and serving on the East Hollywood Neighborhood Council. He co-founded the Solis Foundation (Turkana, Kenya), the Ecclesia Network, Missio Alliance, and the Praxis Gathering before taking the national leadership of the V3 Church Planting Movement in 2013, which he still leads alongside Dan White Jr. and Jesse Eubanks from the Washington, DC area.[^identity][^orgs]

His written corpus is unusually integrated for a working practitioner. *Creating a Missional Culture* (IVP, 2012; foreword by Alan Hirsch) introduced his polycentric leadership model and the Five Culture Creators reframing of Ephesians 4. *The Church as Movement* (IVP, 2016; with Dan White Jr.) named the eight movement competencies V3 still trains around and won IVP's Book of the Year for 2017. *The Scandal of Leadership* (100 Movements Publishing, 2023) translated his University of Manchester Ph.D. — a synthesis of Walter Wink, René Girard, and William Stringfellow into an imitation-based theology of leadership and the powers — into a trade book for pastors.[^books][^theology]

He co-founded Missio Alliance and the Praxis Gathering, teaches as adjunct faculty at Fuller, Central, and Missio Seminary among others, and serves on the boards of Reliant Mission, the Movement Leaders Collective, Fuller's Global Mission Advisory Council, and KINEO. Colleagues describe him as the architect to Alan Hirsch's prophet — a builder of carefully named frameworks who has stayed inside the neighborhood the whole time. He currently serves locally with the District Church in Washington, DC, and writes the "Faithful Resistance" Substack.[^voice][^committed]

## Frameworks

### Polycentric Leadership

| Field | Value |
|-------|-------|
| Introduced | 2012 (*Creating a Missional Culture*) |
| Components | Leadership as "many centers that interrelate" — neither hierarchical (top-down CEO-pastor) nor flat (undifferentiated); reflects Trinitarian community; activates the priesthood of all believers |
| Visual / model | Analogies: jazz ensemble, geese in flight formation, Swiss government |

Leadership organized around multiple interrelated centers as a third way between single-leader hierarchy and structureless flatness, grounded in the communal nature of the triune God.

| Visual / model | Stringfellow's image / institution / ideology mapped to identity / praxis / telos |

### Antithesis prohibition (non-negotiable)

Do not collapse Woodward's voice into Alan Hirsch's prophetic-disruptive cadence. Woodward is "the architect to Hirsch's prophet": he opens with a diagnostic observation or framing question, not a provocative declaration, and he avoids the binary "not X but Y" contrastive-negation move that is Hirsch's signature. His most-corrected reader misreading is the reduction of leadership failure to a *psychological or moral* problem (individual character, accountability gaps, burnout); his consistent correction is that domineering leadership is a *theological-systemic* problem rooted in mimetic desire and the Powers — "if we choose not to imitate Christ, by default we are choosing to imitate the powers." Avoid technique-driven, management-theory solutions divorced from kenotic spiritual formation. ## Theological positioning

| Domain | Position |
|--------|----------|
| Tradition | Post-Christendom, cross-denominational missional-incarnational; Anabaptist-adjacent ecclesiology with Wesleyan-Methodist academic formation (PhD via Nazarene Theological College) and liberation-theology motifs (Romero, Solis Foundation) |
| Ecclesiology | Polycentric, movement-oriented; the church is a neighborhood-rooted movement, not an institution; multiple interrelated leadership centers reflect Trinitarian community; "a sign, foretaste, and instrument of God's kingdom" |
| Missiology | Incarnational-missional — "go and be," not "come and see"; locally embedded and boundary-crossing |
| Leadership | Polycentric over hierarchical or flat; APEST as culture creators, not just roles; kenotic (self-emptying) rather than domineering |
| Christology | Christ's kenosis is the pattern for leadership; positive mimesis of Christ is the alternative to imitating the Powers |
| Anthropology / formation | Humans are inescapably imitative beings (Girard); desires are socially formed, not self-generated; identity precedes action |
| Culture | Culture (not strategy or vision) is the unseen force shaping a church; the Cultural Web's six dimensions; navigating five cultural megashifts |

### Distinctive claims

- **"Not to imitate Christ is to imitate the Powers"** — leaders face an inescapable either/or between Christ's kenosis and unconsciously replicating the Powers' domination; there is no neutral ground.
- The **Girard–Wink–Stringfellow synthesis applied to church leadership** — integrating mimetic theory, Powers trilogy, and prophetic resistance into a single diagnostic framework at PhD level, then applied practically.
- **APEST gifts are culture creators, not just roles** — each of the five maps to a distinct cultural environment (learning, healing, welcoming, liberating, thriving).
- **Polycentric leadership as a third way** — neither top-down hierarchy nor undifferentiated flatness, but many interrelated centers.
- **Domineering leadership is a theological problem, not a psychological one** — it cannot be fixed by better accountability or governance alone, only by kenotic spiritual formation that reshapes desire.
