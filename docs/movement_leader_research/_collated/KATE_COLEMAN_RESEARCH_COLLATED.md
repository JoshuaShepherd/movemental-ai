## Editorial bio

Kate Coleman is a British Baptist minister, leadership consultant, womanist theologian, author, and speaker, best known for her book *7 Deadly Sins of Women in Leadership* and for a series of institutional "firsts" in British church life. Born in Ghana in 1964 and raised in England, she came to Christian faith as a university student while studying biochemistry and pharmacology at the University of Southampton — in a church context that, by her own account, "didn't believe in women in leadership. And neither did I."[^eauk] She went on to become the **first Black woman accredited as a Baptist minister in the UK** (during her first pastorate at Chalk Farm Baptist Church, London), planted **The Regeneration Centre** in Birmingham in 2000, and completed a PhD at the University of Birmingham in 2005 with a thesis on "métissage" — a theological anthropology of Black women's subjectivities in postcolonial Britain.[^baptist][^wtc]

Her denominational and movement leadership compounded those firsts: **first Black woman President of the Baptist Union of Great Britain (2006–2007)**, under the presidential theme "Stay Focused," and **first Black female Chair of the Evangelical Alliance Council (2012–2014)**.[^baptist] Out of that platform she founded **Next Leadership**, a Birmingham-based leadership-development social enterprise she co-directs with Rev. Cham Kaur-Mann (the first South Asian woman minister in the Baptist Union), serving leaders across church, charity, voluntary, public and business sectors in the UK and internationally — including work with the United Nations on a leadership-development curriculum for women leaders.[^next][^baptist]

What she built around her flagship message is a small ecosystem of book, programme, and platform: the 2010 self-published *7 Deadly Sins of Women in Leadership* (later issued in a revised Zondervan edition with a foreword by Elaine Storkey), the Ellevation and Reimagine women's-leadership programmes, the "7DS" course and self-assessment, and — most recently — *Metamorph: Transforming Your Life and Leadership* (2024, Alan Hirsch's 100 Movements Publishing imprint), which extends her work from women's leadership into a general "metamorphic leadership" model drawn from biblical figures. Her recurring thesis is that the obstacle to women's leadership "is not an issue of calling — it's an issue of recognition," and that "existing leadership systems, processes, and cultures were never designed with women or people of color in mind."[^metamorph-review][^insights]

## Frameworks

### 7 Deadly Sins of Women in Leadership

| Field | Value |
|-------|-------|
| Introduced | 2010 (*7 Deadly Sins of Women in Leadership*) |
| Components | Seven internal, self-defeating behaviours: (1) limiting self-perceptions, (2) failure to draw the line / establish appropriate boundaries, (3) inadequate (God-inspired) personal vision, (4) an unhealthy/destructive work–life rhythm, (5) the "disease to please," (6) colluding instead of confronting, (7) neglecting family matters — with a remedy framed around three "E"s: re-**education**, **empowerment**, **encouragement** |
| Visual / model | — (no formal published diagram located) |
| Adoption evidence | Revised Zondervan edition; "7DS" programme (book + video study series + book-club materials + "Breakthrough Day" event + "My Deadly Sins" online self-assessment); reviewed by CBE International and *Ministry* magazine |
| Source | [cbeinternational.org](https://www.cbeinternational.org/resource/7-deadly-sins-women-leadership-2/), [ministrymagazine.org](https://www.ministrymagazine.org/archive/2012/01/7-deadly-sins-of-women-in-leadership), [nextleadership.co.uk/7ds](https://www.nextleadership.co.uk/7ds) |

The framework names seven *internal* behaviours by which women (and men) sabotage their own leadership before external systems do, and argues that "reeducation is necessary to empower and equip women (and men) to become the leaders God designed them to be."

### Antithesis prohibition (non-negotiable)

Do not frame the barrier to women's leadership as a question of *calling, permission, or ability* — and do not reduce it to external systems alone. Coleman's most-corrected reader misreading is the assumption that the question is "are women called/allowed to lead?" Her consistent correction is that "it's not an issue of calling — it's an issue of recognition," paired with the insistence that women also carry *internal*, self-defeating behaviours that must be named and re-educated before systems are blamed. Her voice holds both at once: structural critique ("existing leadership systems... were never designed with women or people of color in mind") **and** personal responsibility ("God didn't call me in spite of who I am but because of who I am"). Flattening either side — pure victim narrative or pure self-help — breaks the voice. Derived from the Next Leadership "Go where you're really needed" essay and the *Metamorph* reviews.[^insights][^metamorph-review]

## Theological positioning

| Domain | Position |
|--------|----------|
| Tradition | Baptist / evangelical (accredited Baptist minister; Baptist Union president 2006–07; Evangelical Alliance Council chair 2012–14); womanist theology stream |
| Ecclesiology | Movemental and anti-institutional instinct — "The Church as it was is no longer going to cut it"; warns that clinging to a "small story" produces an institution; leadership that "can live everywhere in the organization" |
| Missiology | Mission as the third domain of metamorphic transformation (identity → community → mission); "disruptive mission mindset" drawn from the Acts disciples |
| Leadership | "Leadership as a verb" — practice over title; distributed ownership; re-education, empowerment, encouragement; internal-behaviour accountability |
| Christology | Leading "from rootedness in Christ"; Jesus as the model who held leadership, mission, and diversity together ("Stay Focused" theme) |
| Anthropology / formation | Spiritual formation and leadership held together, not assumed apart; identity rooted in "because of who I am, not in spite of"; the "full-disclosure friend" as key to sustainability |
| Culture | Names structural exclusion and intersectional barriers for BAME women; treats organizational platforms as sacred ("pulpits"); racial-justice champion |

### Distinctive claims

- The barrier to women's leadership is **recognition, not calling** — a reframing she repeats as a near-thesis statement.
- Women carry **internal, self-defeating behaviours** ("the seven deadly sins") that must be named and re-educated, not only external systems blamed.
- "When the Spirit is just left to move, it's the women who rise up to lead" — so a theology that excludes women's leadership may need to **question itself against the evidence of the Spirit's work**.
- Existing "leadership systems, processes, and cultures were never designed with women or people of color in mind" — structural exclusion is the default, not the exception.
- Sustainable leadership depends on a **"full-disclosure friend"** — radical relational candor as the single most important sustainability factor.
