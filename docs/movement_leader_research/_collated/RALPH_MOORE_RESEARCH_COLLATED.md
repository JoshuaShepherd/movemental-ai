## Editorial bio

Ralph Moore is an American church planter and disciple-making strategist best known as the founder of the **Hope Chapel** movement — a decentralized, low-cost, multiplication-first model that has propagated into a reported **2,300–2,600+ churches** worldwide. Formed in the Southern California **Jesus Movement** of the early 1970s (a "humble hippie" and former surfer who began in youth ministry at 19), he and his wife **Ruby** planted Hope Chapel in 1971, starting with twelve people in a vacated church building, before settling the congregation into a converted bowling alley in **Hermosa Beach** in 1976. The church was chartered Foursquare, though Moore later operated outside Foursquare's denominational control "because they wanted too much control over how things were done."[^story][^seven]

In 1983 Moore left Hermosa Beach to plant **Hope Chapel Kaneohe Bay** in Hawaii (now Anchor Church, led by his son Carl), and later **Hope Chapel Honolulu** (2013), retiring from pastoring in 2017. His own three churches launched **70-plus** congregations through his hands-on, mini-church-based disciple-making — offspring that have multiplied across several generations into the movement's headline church count. His signature maxim captures the thesis: *"It's not how many churches you plant, it's how many of the churches you plant, plant churches that plant."*[^seven]

In retirement Moore serves as a **Church Multiplication Catalyzer for Exponential**, travels globally teaching multiplication, and has authored a long shelf of practitioner books — *Let Go of the Ring* (the Hope Chapel story, 1983), *Starting a New Church* (2002), *How to Multiply Your Church* (2009, foreword Ed Stetzer), *Making Disciples* (2012; revised 2024 with Bobby Harrington), and *How Nearly Anyone Can Start a Microchurch* (2022) — plus a set of free Exponential ebooks. His resources are stewarded through **NewBreed Training** (Peyton Jones) as "The Ralph Moore Multiplication Pathway," and he is a faculty voice in the Discipleship.org / RENEW.org disciple-making network.[^exp][^newbreed]

## Frameworks

### MiniChurch model

| Field | Value |
|-------|-------|
| Introduced | Hope Chapel (1971 onward); described in *Let Go of the Ring* and later works |
| Components | Decentralized weekly small groups that are the basic unit of discipleship and the incubator for planters; reflect on the sermon around "soul-searching questions, much like those of Wesley's class meetings" (e.g., "What did the Holy Spirit say to you this week?") |
| Visual / model | — |
| Adoption evidence | The primary engine behind 70+ first-generation plants and 2,300–2,600+ total churches |
| Source | [exponential.org/7lessons](https://exponential.org/7lessons/), [effectivechurch.com](https://effectivechurch.com/seven-lessons-from-an-interview-with-ralph-moore-by-bill-easum-december-2016/) |

Hope Chapel's signature decentralized small-group system — the discipleship and planter-incubation vehicle from which leaders who start three mini-churches are identified as potential church planters (and sent after ~5–7 years).

| Field | Value |
|-------|-------|
| Introduced | Recurring across his teaching and books |
| Components | "It's not how many churches you plant, it's how many of the churches you plant, plant churches that plant"; a simple, decentralized plan + "a red-hot passion for the Great Commission"; explicit anti-megachurch posture |
| Visual / model | — |
| Adoption evidence | Core of his Exponential "multiplication catalyzer" teaching and the Hope Chapel generational count |
| Source | [exponential.org/7lessons](https://exponential.org/7lessons/) |

The thesis that movements are measured by multiplying generations of reproducing churches, not by the size or count of a single planter's churches.

### Mega → Multi → Micro

| Field | Value |
|-------|-------|
| Introduced | ~2019 (*Mega Multi Micro*, Exponential ebook); *How Nearly Anyone Can Start a Microchurch* (2022) |
| Components | A paradigm shift from megachurch thinking to multiplying micro-congregations; bi-vocational, no-seminary, "plant a church without leaving your job"; microchurches in homes, parks, cafés, boardrooms |
| Visual / model | — |
| Adoption evidence | Exponential resource line; "New to Five" Level-5 multiplication framing (with Jeff Christopherson) |
| Source | [exponential.org/product/mega-multi-micro](https://exponential.org/product/mega-multi-micro/) |

A reproducibility framework moving the church's imagination from scale ("mega") toward decentralized, low-cost, "nearly anyone can" microchurch multiplication.

> Note: "Hero Maker" leadership (give away power; make heroes of others) is a posture Moore exemplifies within the Exponential / Dave Ferguson framework rather than a model he coined. "FAT (Faithful, Available, Teachable)" is **not** his coinage (Navigators tradition).

### Antithesis prohibition (non-negotiable)

Do not frame church success in terms of size, attendance, or the megachurch ideal, and do not present church planting as the work of credentialed professionals. Moore's most-corrected misreading is that bigger is better: "We got wrapped up in being big, and we forgot about making disciples," and "The U.S. puts too much emphasis on megachurches… it costs a lot of money to be mega." His consistent correction is that the metric is reproducing generations of disciple-makers and churches — done by ordinary believers with "no formal theological training" — not the scale of any single congregation. Keep the register plain and practitioner-level, never abstract or jargon-laden. Derived from his Exponential "Seven Lessons" interview and Foursquare interview.[^seven][^foursquare]

### Distinctive claims

- The true measure of a movement is generational: churches that plant churches that plant churches.
- Effective multiplication needs only "a simple, decentralized plan and a red-hot passion for the Great Commission" — not money, buildings, or seminary credentials.
- The American overemphasis on megachurches takes time and money away from disciple-making.
- "Nearly anyone" can start a reproducing microchurch without leaving their job.
