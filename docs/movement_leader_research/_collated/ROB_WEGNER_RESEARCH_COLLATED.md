## 3. Editorial bio

Rob Wegner is one of the most networked figures in the contemporary North American microchurch and church-multiplication conversation — a megachurch insider who became a microchurch evangelist. For roughly a quarter-century at **Granger Community Church** in northern Indiana, under founding pastor Mark Beeson, he served as Teaching Pastor / Pastor of Life Mission, co-led the EnterMission community-transformation initiative, co-founded the **Future Travelers** coaching program with **Alan Hirsch**, and made repeated trips to India with his wife **Michelle Wegner** to document a Granger-seeded grassroots church-planting movement that grew to over 120,000 disciples across 950+ churches in southern India. That India experience reshaped his ecclesial vision and produced *Share the Well*, the photo-essay book he and Michelle co-authored.[^bio][^id]

Around 2015 he moved to Kansas City to serve four years as a teaching pastor at **Westside Family Church**, whose team — by Westside's own account — "trained leaders who planted over 700 churches internationally in four years." In **2019**, with a starter group of about 80 disciples and partnering with **Brian Johnson** and Michelle, he founded **Kansas City Underground (KCU)** — "a mission agency and decentralized network of microchurches and missional leaders committed to filling Kansas City with the beauty, justice, and Good News of Jesus." KCU's stated 40-year horizon is 21,000 microchurches arising from 42,000 missionaries; as of 2025–26 it operates ~95 microchurches across 7 Collectives, 110+ activated missionaries (300+ in training), 5 Hubs, and 120+ Discovery Bible Studies, built on an explicit two-entity structure (mission agency + decentralized network).[^bio][^kcu]

Concurrent with KCU he holds at least three national-scale roles: **Microchurch NEXT Co-Director / Mobilization & Practice Catalyst at Exponential**, **Global Leadership Team member of NewThing** (Dave Ferguson's multiplication network, integrated into Exponential April 1, 2025), and co-host (with **Lance Ford**) of *The Starfish and the Church* podcast and the joint *Starfish yoU* Substack — the public faces of **Starfish Initiatives**, a coaching brand listing Wegner, Ford, Hirsch, and Rich Robinson. His corpus is larger than the public conversation suggests: eight co-authored books across ~15 years, culminating in *The Starfish and the Spirit* (Zondervan, 2021), co-authored with Ford and Hirsch with a foreword by **Ori Brafman** — the bridge work that places Wegner inside the Hirsch–Ford–Brafman lineage and connects the Forge/100Movements wing to the Exponential/NewThing/Underground wing.[^bio][^net]

### Antithesis prohibition (non-negotiable)

Do not write Wegner in attractional-megachurch, celebrity-pastor, or "deliver-curriculum-and-leave" register. His most-corrected misreading is that the church is an event attended and led by paid professionals; his consistent correction is that the church is "an extended spiritual family led by ordinary people, not paid professionals," and that mission, not the institution, is the organizing principle ("It's not that the church has a mission. It's that a mission has the church"). Two further prohibitions follow from his own corpus: (1) do not voice him as the singular hero — the collaborative co-author/co-host byline is itself the message, so avoid solo-authority framing; (2) do not let leadership voice drift into *telling* rather than *inviting* — the October 2024 "Navigating Change" confession ("You're doing it again. You're telling everyone else what you want to create… This is not inviting us to be that family of families") names telling-over-inviting as the failure mode he publicly repented of. ## 8. Theological positioning

| Domain | Position |
|--------|----------|
| Tradition | Non-denominational evangelical; post-megachurch missional evangelicalism (non-Reformed, non-mainline, charismatic-friendly but not Pentecostal-distinctive) |
| Ecclesiology | Missional-incarnational; microchurch as "extended spiritual family led by ordinary people"; decentralized/starfish; mission (not institution) is the organizing principle |
| Missiology | Multiplication via disciple-making; Indian DMM/DBS imported as normative; "a missionary on every street" |
| Leadership | Servant-equipper / Hubology; anti-celebrity-pastor; family-of-families over founder-of-organization |
| Christology | Functionally christocentric in form — Jesus' own relational pattern and selectivity as the fixed model |
| Anthropology / formation | Practice over information; habit/rule-of-life; transformation into the likeness of Jesus as the outcome |
| Culture | Reproducible gospel language; de-exoticizes the global South; engages power-redistribution (MLC + Arrabon consulting) |

### Distinctive claims

- The church is not an event attended; it is a missionary network embodied — what happens when ordinary people carry the gospel into their existing relational webs.
- Ordinary people, **not paid professionals**, lead the church (the microchurch definition is structurally anti-clergy).
- Decentralization is not a tactic but closer to the New Testament grain; the Spirit empowers the starfish (the title's central addition to Brafman's secular thesis).
- Megachurch leadership and microchurch leadership are different vocations — and a decentralized network can have *better* oversight than a megachurch.
- Definitions matter ecclesially: the American church cannot multiply because it cannot define the disciple it is multiplying.
- Reproducible gospel language is itself a multiplication input.
- Family-of-families over founder-of-organization — including public self-correction of founder-team drift toward control.
- India (DMM/DBS) is normative, not exotic.
