## Executive Summary

Alan Hirsch is the foremost theorist of apostolic movement in the contemporary Western church. His distinctive contribution is the articulation of a phenomenology of Jesus movements -- naming the latent "Apostolic Genius" (mDNA) encoded in every Christian community and providing a systematic framework for reactivating it. Across 20 books, 30+ articles, 38+ videos, and 7 training programs spanning 2003-2024, Hirsch has built an integrated intellectual architecture that moves from Christology (Jesus is Lord) through missiology (incarnational mission) to ecclesiology (the church as movement). His second major contribution -- the APEST/5Q framework -- has become one of the most widely adopted models for understanding fivefold ministry, with over 150,000 assessments taken globally. No other thinker has so comprehensively integrated movement theory, organizational science, and missional theology into a single coherent system.

## Named Frameworks & Models

### mDNA (Missional DNA)

| Field | Value |
|-------|-------|
| Introduced in | *The Forgotten Ways* (2006) |
| What it describes | The six interdependent elements that constitute "Apostolic Genius" -- the operating system of Jesus movements |
| Components | (1) Jesus is Lord, (2) Disciple Making, (3) Missional-Incarnational Impulse, (4) Apostolic Environment (APEST), (5) Organic Systems, (6) Liminality-Communitas. The Holy Spirit is the atmosphere in which all six operate. |
| Visual | Yes (hexagonal diagram with "Jesus is Lord" at center; each element interconnected) |
| Adoption | Widely cited -- adopted by Exponential, Forge, Fresh Expressions, Forming Lutherans, Tampa Underground, and numerous church planting networks globally |

**How it works**: Like biological DNA, mDNA is found in every living cell of the church, is transferable, self-replicating, and carries vital information for healthy reproduction. "Jesus is Lord" gates overall maturity -- no element can rise above the church's Christological depth. When all six are present and interacting, Apostolic Genius emerges as a system greater than the sum of its parts. When one or more are suppressed (as in institutional Christendom), the system defaults to maintenance mode. The task of leadership is to create environments where the dormant code can be reactivated. Hirsch argues: "The possibility of movement lies at the heart of every church" -- the code is already there.

### APEST / 5Q

| Field | Value |
|-------|-------|
| Introduced in | *The Forgotten Ways* (2006) as one mDNA element; developed fully in *The Permanent Revolution* (2012) and *5Q* (2017) |
| What it describes | The fivefold ministry pattern from Ephesians 4:11 as the "original intelligence and capacity" of the Body of Christ |
| Components | Apostles (extend/guard), Prophets (covenant faithfulness), Evangelists (recruit/communicate), Shepherds (nurture/protect), Teachers (understand/transmit) |
| Visual | Yes (multiple diagrams -- pentagonal model, hand metaphor, body systems analogy) |
| Adoption | Widely cited -- 150,000+ APEST assessments taken through 5Q Central; adopted by denominations (LMC Anabaptist Churches, Churches of Christ), Exponential network, Redeemer City to City, multiple seminaries |

**How it works**: Christ himself perfectly embodies all five functions. The ascended Christ distributes these callings to the entire Body -- not just leaders -- so the church can grow into maturity. Western churches have typically reduced ministry to Shepherd-Teacher (the "ST" default), effectively "exiling" Apostolic, Prophetic, and Evangelistic functions. This truncation produces a maintenance-oriented church incapable of movement. Reactivating all five creates what Hirsch calls "5Q" -- a measure of ecclesial intelligence analogous to IQ or EQ. When all five are present and integrated, the church has the environment for missional movement. The framework has evolved from a leadership model (2006) to a comprehensive ecclesiology with its own assessment tools, training programs, and organizational applications (2017-present).

### Christology > Missiology > Ecclesiology

| Field | Value |
|-------|-------|
| Introduced in | *ReJesus* (2008/2009, with Michael Frost) |
| What it describes | A cascading theological sequence: who Jesus is determines how we do mission, which determines what the church looks like |
| Components | Three-step logical chain; get the first wrong, everything downstream distorts |
| Visual | Yes (arrow diagram: Christology -> Missiology -> Ecclesiology) |
| Adoption | Widely cited in missional theology circles; referenced by Ed Stetzer, Missio Alliance, Verge Network |

**How it works**: Most churches start with ecclesiology (what kind of church do we want?) and try to fit mission and Jesus into it. Hirsch inverts this: start with Jesus, let Jesus shape your mission, let mission shape your church. "Those who start with the Church and then try to fit mission into it will never find mission. Those who start with mission and then ask what kind of Church is needed to fulfill it will find the Church will be found." This framework is the theological engine beneath the mDNA and APEST systems.

### Metanoia (Paradigm-Level Transformation)

| Field | Value |
|-------|-------|
| Introduced in | *Metanoia* (2023, with Rob Kelly) |
| What it describes | Repentance understood as fundamental paradigm shift -- not merely moral reform but cognitive-spiritual revolution that transforms individuals, churches, and organizations from the inside out |
| Components | Mind (paradigm), Soul (pathos/culture), Will (practice) -- "wholehearted metanoia" engages all three |
| Visual | Yes (three-dimensional model of transformation) |
| Adoption | Emerging -- featured at Missio Alliance Awakenings 2023; growing uptake in church leadership circles |

**How it works**: Hirsch argues that Jesus' first public word -- "metanoia" (repent) -- is far more radical than the Western church has understood. It means a complete reorientation of consciousness. Human transformation requires the uniting of mind, soul, and will in a single act of turning. For organizations, this means addressing paradigm (how we think), platform (the culture/soul of the organization), and practice (what we actually do). Metanoia is both instantaneous and ongoing. This framework complements the earlier structural focus of mDNA/APEST by addressing the interior mechanism of how change actually happens.

### Reframation

| Field | Value |
|-------|-------|
| Introduced in | *Reframation* (2019, with Mark Nelson) |
| What it describes | The process of changing mental frameworks to transform perception of God, people, and mission -- re-enchanting a disenchanted worldview |
| Components | Drawing on C.S. Lewis, Martin Buber, Hans Urs von Balthasar; calls for "remystifying, repoeticizing, and remythologizing" |
| Visual | No (more literary/philosophical than diagrammatic) |
| Adoption | Niche but growing -- particularly resonant with post-evangelical and creative audiences |

**How it works**: The Western world has undergone a process of disenchantment -- reducing reality to the measurable and the material. The church has participated in this reduction, losing the capacity for wonder, imagination, and encounter. Reframation is the deliberate process of re-enchanting our frames -- seeing God as bigger, people as more sacred (imago Dei), and mission as more beautiful than our current paradigms allow. It represents Hirsch's most literary and philosophical work, marking a shift from organizational frameworks toward imaginative renewal.

### Movemental Thinking (Movement vs. Institution)

| Field | Value |
|-------|-------|
| Introduced in | Throughout all works; the term "movemental" coined by Hirsch |
| What it describes | The fundamental paradigm distinction between institution (centralized, controlling, maintenance-focused) and movement (organic, multiplicative, network-structured, virus-like) |
| Components | Not a formal model but a pervasive lens; characteristics include: grassroots networks, adaptability, viral reproduction, mission as organizing principle, edge-of-chaos dynamics |
| Visual | Yes (multiple -- starfish vs. spider, life cycle of movements, network diagrams) |
| Adoption | Widely cited -- "movemental" has entered the vocabulary of missional practitioners; the Movemental AI platform takes its name from this concept |

**How it works**: Hirsch argues that the NT church was a movement, not an institution. Movements spread like viruses through decentralized networks; institutions centralize and control. The church's default drift is from movement to institution -- from the "edge of chaos" (where innovation happens) to equilibrium (which, in living systems, is death). Leadership's task is to maintain movement dynamics: simplicity, reproducibility, apostolic sending, networked structure, and mission as organizing principle. "Phenomenal Jesus movements grow precisely because they do not have centralized institutions to block growth through control."

### Has their thinking shifted?

Hirsch's core convictions have been remarkably consistent since 2003: the church is a movement, not an institution; it carries latent apostolic DNA that must be reactivated; Jesus is Lord gates everything. What has evolved is the depth and breadth of the system.

The most significant intellectual development is the trajectory from external to internal. The 2003-2012 phase focused primarily on structures, systems, and organizational paradigms -- what the church should look like and how it should be organized. The 2018-2023 phase turned inward -- how people actually change at the level of cognition, imagination, and will. *Metanoia* and *Reframation* represent Hirsch's recognition that structural change without interior transformation is insufficient. The "operating system" needs not just new code (mDNA) but a new processor (metanoia) running on a re-enchanted worldview (reframation).

A secondary evolution is the development of APEST from one element among six (2006) into a comprehensive framework with its own ecosystem of assessments, training, and organizational theory (2017-present). This represents the single framework that has scaled most broadly beyond Hirsch's own audience.

## Unique Contribution

### What only this author says

- **Apostolic Genius / mDNA as a systematic, named framework**: While others have written about missional church, organic church, or movement dynamics, Hirsch is the only thinker who has articulated a comprehensive, named, six-element operating system (mDNA) that integrates Christology, discipleship, mission, leadership, organizational theory, and communal formation into a single coherent model. The concept that every church carries dormant movement DNA that can be "reactivated" is distinctively his.

- **APEST as ecclesial intelligence for all believers, not just leaders**: Others have written about fivefold ministry, but Hirsch's distinctive claim is that APEST describes callings latent in every believer (not just leaders) and that the Western church has systematically "exiled" three of the five (Apostle, Prophet, Evangelist), creating a structurally imbalanced institution incapable of movement. The 5Q framework -- measuring ecclesial intelligence like IQ or EQ -- is his unique contribution.

- **"Jesus is Lord" as a system-level governor**: The idea that a church's Christological maturity caps the performance of every other dimension of its life -- that if "Jesus is Lord" is at Level 2, the whole mDNA system is capped at Level 2 -- is a distinctive systems-theoretic insight applied to theology.

### Where they disagree with peers

| Topic | Their Position | Mainstream Position |
|-------|---------------|-------------------|
| APEST scope | All five gifts are for all believers, distributed across the entire Body; the church has exiled APE functions | Most evangelical traditions limit APEST to leadership offices or argue apostles/prophets ceased (cessationism) |
| Institution vs. movement | Institutionalization is deviation from the apostolic norm, not maturation toward it | Mainstream ecclesiology views institutional development as healthy maturation; 9Marks and Reformed traditions defend institutional church |
| Attractional church model | "Come and see" is a Christendom-era artifact; "go and be" (incarnational) is normative | Many evangelical and megachurch leaders defend attractional methods as legitimate gospel strategy |
| Sacred-secular divide | Hellenistic dualism has distorted Western Christianity; Hebraic holism is the biblical norm | Traditional Reformed and Catholic theology maintains valid sacred-secular distinctions (e.g., two-kingdoms theology) |
| Apostolic ministry today | Active apostolic ministry is essential and ongoing; the church needs apostles now | Cessationist traditions argue apostolic office ended with the first century; Reformed traditions argue "apostle" is a completed office |

### What would be lost without their work

Without Hirsch, the missional church conversation would lack its most comprehensive theoretical architecture. While Newbigin provided the theological foundation and practitioners like Neil Cole provided organic models, Hirsch uniquely bridged theory and practice by creating a systematic, named framework (mDNA/APEST) that could be taught, assessed, and implemented across diverse contexts. The vocabulary of the missional movement -- "missional DNA," "Apostolic Genius," "movemental," "5Q" -- would not exist. The 150,000+ people who have taken the APEST assessment would lack the framework to understand their callings. And the bridge between movement theory and established church practice (via Future Travelers and the Exponential network) would be significantly weaker.

## Content Interconnections

### How themes connect across works

Hirsch's books form a tightly integrated system, not a disconnected catalog. The architecture works as follows:

1. **Foundation**: *The Shaping of Things to Come* (2003) lays the missional-incarnational groundwork. *The Forgotten Ways* (2006) builds the comprehensive mDNA/Apostolic Genius system on that foundation.

2. **Christological center**: *ReJesus* (2008) deepens the "Jesus is Lord" element -- the first and governing element of mDNA. Without this book, the mDNA system lacks its theological anchor.

3. **APEST deep dive**: *The Permanent Revolution* (2012) and *5Q* (2017) take one element of mDNA -- Apostolic Environment -- and develop it into a comprehensive framework. *Activating 5Q* (2018) provides the practical toolkit.

4. **Liminality and communitas**: *The Faith of Leap* (2011) develops another mDNA element into a full theology of risk and adventure.

5. **Practical application**: *The Forgotten Ways Handbook* (2009), *Right Here, Right Now* (2011), and *Untamed* (2010) translate the theoretical system into everyday practice.

6. **Established church bridge**: *On the Verge* (2011) and *The Starfish and the Spirit* (2021) apply movement principles to existing large churches and organizational structures.

7. **Interior transformation**: *Reframation* (2019) and *Metanoia* (2023) address how the paradigm shifts described in earlier books actually happen at the cognitive and spiritual level.

8. **Applied disruption**: *All Change* (2024) applies the lifetime of frameworks to the current moment of cultural disruption.

## Movemental Content Strategy Implications

- **Pillar mapping**: Hirsch's content serves every major Movemental content pillar: Missional Theology (mDNA, incarnational mission), Leadership Development (APEST/5Q), Organizational Transformation (movement vs. institution, organic systems), Discipleship (disciple making, formation), and Spiritual Formation (metanoia, reframation). He is the most cross-pillar author on the platform.

- **Cross-author connections**: Brad Brisco (neighborhood mission, bivocational ministry -- applies Hirsch's frameworks at grassroots level); Michael Frost (incarnational mission, 3 co-authored books -- the closest intellectual partner); Dave Ferguson (church multiplication, megachurch bridge); Neil Cole (organic church, simple church movements); Ed Stetzer (missional ecclesiology, evangelical mainstream). Any author in the missional space will reference or respond to Hirsch.

- **Content gaps**: (1) Digital-native expressions of APEST and mDNA -- Hirsch has begun exploring this (Church Digital podcast) but has not written substantively on it. (2) APEST for marketplace/vocational contexts beyond church leadership. (3) Shorter-form, high-accessibility content -- Hirsch's writing tends toward complexity; there is an opportunity for "Hirsch Simplified" content on the platform. (4) Women in APEST -- the framework is gender-neutral in theory but most examples and co-authors are male.

- **Series potential**: (1) "The Six Elements of mDNA" -- a 6-part series, one per element, drawing from the book corpus. (2) "APEST in Practice" -- 5-part series on each fivefold calling with assessment integration. (3) "Movement vs. Institution" -- a diagnostic series helping churches locate themselves on the spectrum. (4) "Metanoia Journey" -- a formation series on paradigm-level transformation. (5) "Reading Order" -- a curated learning path through Hirsch's works. (6) "The Forgotten Vocabulary" -- short explainers of Hirsch's coined terms (mDNA, communitas, movemental, Apostolic Genius, reframation).

- **Searchable concepts**: mDNA, missional DNA, Apostolic Genius, APEST, 5Q, fivefold ministry, five-fold ministry, missional church, incarnational mission, missional-incarnational, communitas, liminality, movemental, movement vs. institution, organic systems, Jesus is Lord, Christology missiology ecclesiology, metanoia, reframation, disciple making, shepherd-teacher dominance, apostolic ministry, theo-genetic codes, Forgotten Ways, post-Christendom, Hebraic thinking.
