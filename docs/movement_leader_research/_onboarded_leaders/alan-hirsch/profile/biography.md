## Narrative Biography

Alan Hirsch is one of the most influential voices in the global missional church movement -- an Australian missiologist, author, serial entrepreneur, and movement catalyst who has fundamentally reshaped how Western churches understand mission, leadership, and apostolic movement. Through more than seventeen published books, multiple organizations (100Movements, Forge Mission Training Network, Future Travelers, 5Q Collective), and extensive teaching at seminaries across the United States, Hirsch has become the leading theorist and practitioner of what he calls "movemental" thinking -- the paradigm shift from church-as-institution to church-as-movement.

His magnum opus, *The Forgotten Ways: Reactivating the Missional Church* (2006), introduced the concept of "Apostolic Genius" and the mDNA (missional DNA) framework -- six interdependent elements that characterize every significant Jesus movement in history. His APEST framework (Apostles, Prophets, Evangelists, Shepherds, Teachers), developed most fully in *5Q* (2017) and *The Permanent Revolution* (2012), has become a widely adopted model for understanding church leadership and organizational health.

Born into a Jewish family in apartheid-era Johannesburg, South Africa, Hirsch's journey to Christian faith came through an unexpected encounter during military service. That conversion, combined with his multicultural background and experience leading a church among the marginalized in inner-city Melbourne, forged a distinctive voice -- one that combines prophetic urgency, theological depth, and practical accessibility. Today he lives in the Los Angeles area, where he continues to write, teach, and catalyze movements through 100Movements and the 5Q Collective.

## Origin & Formation

Alan Hirsch was born on October 24, 1959, in Johannesburg, South Africa, into a Jewish family. When he was four, his family moved to Cape Town, where he spent most of his childhood and adolescence. Growing up under apartheid had a profound impact on him; by age thirteen, he was politically active against the regime, having decided early on that apartheid was "profoundly wrong and morally reprehensible."

Though his family was not particularly religious, Hirsch's Jewish heritage deeply shaped his worldview. He later described having "an illicit fascination with Jesus" as a young Jewish teenager and said he could not remember a time when he ever doubted God's existence. After completing a mandatory two-year stint in the South African military -- where he was "first introduced to drugs and then to Jesus" -- he attended university in Cape Town, studying business and marketing. At about age twenty, one of his drug buddies was born again and shared the gospel with him, leading to what Hirsch describes as "the defining moment of my life." He emigrated to Australia in 1983 with his family, shortly after meeting his wife Debra.

## Career & Ministry Arc

In Australia, Hirsch pursued theological education at the Australian College of Theology, graduating in 1989. That same year, he and Debra began leading the South Melbourne Church of Christ (later renamed South Melbourne Restoration Community) -- a declining congregation in inner-city Melbourne made up largely of people "on the fringes of society": homosexuals, prostitutes, schizophrenics, drug addicts, and other marginalized individuals. They led this community for fifteen years, planting additional churches and initiating outreach agencies and missional orders. In 1994 he was also appointed Director of Mission, Education, and Development for the Churches of Christ in Victoria and Tasmania.

This grassroots experience became the laboratory for Hirsch's theoretical work. In 1996, he, Debra, and Michael Frost co-founded the Forge Mission Training Network, dedicated to developing innovative missional leaders. His first major publication, *The Shaping of Things to Come* (2003, co-authored with Frost), established the foundational ideas that would mature into *The Forgotten Ways* (2006) -- a book widely regarded as the definitive statement on the church as dynamic missional movement.

From 2009 onward, Hirsch's influence expanded dramatically. He co-founded Future Travelers (helping megachurches transition toward missional movements), co-created the M.A. in Missional Church Movements at Wheaton College (2010), and took adjunct professorships at Fuller Seminary, Asbury Seminary, and George Fox Seminary. In 2016, he founded 100Movements, dedicated to reanimating transformative missional movements globally, and the 5Q Collective, focused on activating five-fold ministry in church contexts.

## Current Focus

Hirsch's current focus centers on 100Movements and the 5Q Collective, both aimed at catalyzing authentic missional movements through the mDNA and APEST frameworks. His formal US sponsor and employer is Church Resource Ministries (CRM), with his role in missional leadership development. He serves as associate faculty at Wheaton College, continues adjunct teaching across multiple seminaries, and works as a movement mentor for NewThing International and an embedded consultant for Redeemer City to City in New York. He lives in the Los Angeles area and is an active participant in The Tribe of LA, a Jesus community among artists and creatives. He continues to write, speak at conferences internationally, and consult with churches and organizations on missional transformation.
