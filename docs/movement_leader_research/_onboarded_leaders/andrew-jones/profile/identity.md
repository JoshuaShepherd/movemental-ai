# Identity: Andrew Jones (Tall Skinny Kiwi)

## Vocational Identity

Andrew Jones is, first and most durably, a chronicler and connector of movements. Where many leaders in the emerging and missional conversation produced books and frameworks, Jones produced a living archive — a blog written in real time across two decades that narrated a movement as it was forming. His vocation has been bivalent throughout: on one side, thirty years of embodied, third-sector mission work across more than seventy countries as an aid worker, church developer, pastor, and mission consultant; on the other, an unusually early and prolific digital presence that began in 1997 and never stopped. He is a practitioner who writes, and a writer who has actually planted, traveled, and built. He has consistently described his interest not as institution-building but as discovering and empowering "impact entrepreneurs" — people doing social transformation at the grass roots.

## Core Convictions

Several convictions recur across his corpus. First, that the church is better understood as a rhizome than as a hierarchy — spreading wide rather than building high, sharing ideas, values, beliefs, and action across a network rather than scaling a single center. Second, that mission begins in the local and the ordinary, and that "home" countries are mission fields no less than overseas ones. Third, that emerging church was never primarily a style or a worship aesthetic but "a radically different mindset, value system and worldview" — and that people inside emerging culture often neither want nor need a tidy definition of it. Fourth, that the internet and digital culture are not threats to be managed but native terrain for the gospel, to be inhabited generously and creatively. His 2022 Greenbelt talk, "In The Metaverse As In Heaven," is a direct extension of this conviction.

## What Makes His Voice Distinct

Jones's voice is distinct in three ways. He is a centrist and a bridge-builder — repeatedly positioned as a non-partisan connector who could hold relationships across factions of a fractious movement, and who narrated its history (including its decline) candidly, as in his much-discussed "Emerging Church Movement (1989-2009)?" post and his 2010 departure from Emergent Village. He is a born storyteller with a global eye, writing from a campervan in Europe, a flat in San Francisco, or an island in Orkney, always alert to the people and projects most others miss. And he is, by temperament, curious and mischievous rather than dogmatic — quick to point readers toward someone else's work, quick to play, and allergic to taking himself too seriously. His authority comes not from a credential or an institution but from having been present, online and in person, for the whole arc of a movement.
