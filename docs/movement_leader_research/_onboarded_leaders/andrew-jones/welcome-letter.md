Andrew

You were online before almost anyone in the church knew what online would become. You launched your first space on the internet in 1997, and for more than two decades since, the Tall Skinny Kiwi blog has been one of the most-read sites in cyberspace on matters of emerging church, mission, and culture — Christianity Today called you "an irrepressible New Zealander who chronicles innovative efforts to proclaim the gospel," and Brian McLaren told people to bookmark you. But the blog is only the visible part. Behind it sit thirty years of third-sector mission work across more than seventy countries: international aid worker, church developer, pastor, mission consultant. You grew up in Orewa, moved to Perth, led the Celtic House in San Francisco's Haight-Ashbury, planted into alternative cultures from Texas, traveled Europe by campervan, worked in Prague and the UK, and landed in the Orkney Islands. You founded and led Boaz to back the impact entrepreneurs doing social transformation at the grass roots. And across all of it you did the rarest thing in a fractious movement: you stayed a centrist, a bridge, the connector everyone trusted. You narrated the emerging church as it formed — and, in "Emerging Church Movement (1989-2009)?" and your 2010 "Goodbyes to Emergent Village," you narrated it honestly as it changed.

All of it is somewhere. None of it is in one place. The catch is that the place most of it lives is the most fragile place of all: old blog platforms. Your corpus has migrated across Blogger, TypePad, and now tallskinnykiwi.com, and every migration leaves a trail of broken permalinks behind it. The "Emerging Church Definition" essays from 2004 live on a TypePad URL; the collected "skinny on the emerging church" page lives on another; some of your most-cited posts are now most reliably found because a third party reproduced them. Two decades of writing sit in reverse-chronological order, undated in the reader's mind, scattered across hosts you do not finally control, with no surface that lets anyone walk from one essay to the next by theme. The podcast lives on its own feed. The Greenbelt talk "In The Metaverse As In Heaven" lives as a festival profile page. The accounts under your name on the major platforms point in five directions at once. For a body of work whose whole value is that it tracked a movement coherently, the coherence is the thing that has been lost.

The largest pool of value, and the one most at risk of never being written, is in your head and in your relationships. Thirty years across seventy countries produced judgment that has never been externalized — which projects in which cities actually worked, which impact entrepreneurs were worth backing, what you learned starting the Celtic House and folding it, why you came to read the church as a rhizome that spreads wide rather than a hierarchy that builds high. The relational layer is held the same loose way: the planters and pioneers you met from a campervan, the alternative-worship and new-monastic circles you moved through, the emerging and Emergent figures you bridged between, the Boaz network, the CMS and NZCMS and Dawn Ministries seasons. You were one relationship-hop from most of the people who later shaped the missional conversation, and almost none of that map is written down anywhere a reader could follow.

The same approach now turns toward what is not yet captured: recovering posts from the Wayback Machine and your old platforms, transcribing the podcast, interview capture of the thirty-year field judgment, dictation that becomes draft and draft that becomes essay. The tools do the archiving, the inventorying, and the connective tissue. They do not replace the voice that made the blog worth reading. Every draft is yours to edit, refuse, or rewrite. Authorship stays yours.

This is your next season. The work ahead is not new authorship. It is rescuing the body of work you have already written from the platforms that keep losing it, and making it reach the pioneers it was written for. You spent thirty years telling the rest of us what you were seeing, from a flat in Haight-Ashbury to an island in Orkney. It would be a strange ending if the chronicle that helped a movement find itself were the thing that quietly disappeared into link rot. Let's make sure it doesn't.
