## The Story

Brad Brisco has been teaching church planting and missional ecclesiology for **more than 20 years**, logging **18+ years of college-level instruction** in History of Christianity, Religion in America, Life of Paul, Discipleship and Evangelism, Theology of Place, Launching Apostolic Movements, and Worship. He co-founded **Forge Kansas City**, co-founded the **Sentralized Conference**, and currently serves as **Director of Multiplication Strategies for the Send Network / North American Mission Board** — a role that evolved out of his previous title as Director of Bivocational Church Planting for NAMB. He earned a **D.Min. in Missional Ecclesiology** with a dissertation on helping existing congregations transition toward a missional direction. He has co-authored **five books** — *Missional Essentials* (a 12-week small-group curriculum), *The Missional Quest* (IVP, with Lance Ford), *Next Door As It Is In Heaven* (NavPress 2016, with Lance Ford), *ReThink: 9 Key Paradigm Shifts for Activating the Church*, and *Covocational Church Planting* (Missional Press 2021, a free ebook available in English and Spanish). He is a regular contributor to [New Churches](https://newchurches.com/contributor/brad-brisco), [ChurchLeaders.com](https://churchleaders.com/author/brad-brisco), and his own [missionalchurchnetwork.com](https://missionalchurchnetwork.com).

That is a striking amount of codified, durable work. And yet the picture that emerges from the research is of a career pioneer whose **embodied teaching runs multiple magnitudes larger than the digital record of it**. The 18+ years of college-level teaching produced syllabi, lectures, readings, and assessment material for seven distinct courses — none of which is publicly indexed anywhere. The Sentralized Conference has run annually in Kansas City for years; conference recordings from it are not systematically hosted anywhere under his name. Forge Kansas City is a training center whose workshops have no public archive. NAMB's Send Network training happens inside institutional systems that the public cannot see. Brad's work sits in three concentric containers — **the college LMS, the denominational platform, and the conference room** — each of which is designed to deliver content to a specific audience and then close the door behind them.

And even the public-facing layer is scattered. His five books sell on Amazon and Christianbook — including *Covocational Church Planting* as a free bilingual ebook, which is one of the most accessibility-forward moves in his entire catalogue — but none of them are cross-referenced from a single author hub. He writes regularly for at least three platforms (missionalchurchnetwork.com, newchurches.com, churchleaders.com) plus 5Q Central, but the article inventory has never been catalogued. He maintains a Blogger profile ([blogger.com/profile/13260894325252150384](https://blogger.com/profile/13260894325252150384)) whose relationship to his current blog is unclear. His podcast footprint is five confirmed guest appearances — **Shifting Culture #225, The Church Digital #251, H2 Leadership #058, Ephesiology #199, Into the Harvest #242** — with topics ranging from covocational ministry to digital ecclesiology to the future of church planting in North America. He has never hosted his own podcast. His social media handles (Twitter/X, LinkedIn) are flagged as "likely present" but not URL-verified in the research — meaning the audit could not establish with confidence which accounts are his. There is no newsletter on record. There is no personal YouTube channel on record. There is no course on a mainstream learning platform on record.

In short: Brad Brisco has built one of the most practically useful bodies of church-planting training in North America, and almost all of it is locked behind institutional walls, spread across partner publications he doesn't own, or embedded in a conference format that expires at the end of each session.

## Synthesis

Brad Brisco's fragmentation is a **three-walled institutional problem**: NAMB owns one wall, the colleges own another, Sentralized owns the third, and the public can see through none of them. His five books are indexed on Amazon and Christianbook. His articles appear on three partner platforms. His podcast footprint is five confirmed guest appearances. Everything else — the 18+ years of teaching, the annual Sentralized sessions, the Forge KC cohort curriculum, the NAMB multiplication strategy, the D.Min. dissertation — lives inside institutions that are not designed to release it to a general audience. The single largest pool of trapped value is the **18+ years of college teaching content across seven distinct courses**, because it represents durable, structured, sequenced material that already exists — it simply does not exist in a form the public can see. The second-largest is the **Sentralized Conference archive**, which has accumulated years of high-quality session content with no public home under his name. The basics of a personal platform — a newsletter, verified social handles, a unified author hub, a personal YouTube or podcast presence — are not filled out; that gap is real, but the actual content reserve behind the scenes is the headline.
