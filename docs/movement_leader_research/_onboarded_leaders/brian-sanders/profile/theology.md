# Theological Profile: Brian Sanders

**Status**: Complete
**Last updated**: 2026-06-05

---

## Core thesis

Brian Sanders' theological center is **mission-organized ecclesiology** — the conviction that the church becomes itself when ordinary disciples carry the gospel into their actual neighborhoods, and that mission, not the weekly gathering or the leader's vision, is the church's organizing principle. His load-bearing claim, developed across two decades of inner-city intentional community in Tampa and articulated most sharply in *Underground Church* (Zondervan, 2018) and *Microchurches: A Smaller Way* (Underground Media, 2019), is that the microchurch is not merely the smallest expression of the church but the most common, most reproducible, and most potent. Worship is the lordship of Jesus — "not necessarily about singing or liturgy." Leadership serves the callings already present in ordinary believers rather than recruiting people to the leader's vision. A lightweight network exists to incubate and resource many small mission-organized churches, inverting the attractional assumption that the central church runs programs people attend.

## Theological tradition

**Primary**: Evangelical Protestant, formed in the collegiate-mission stream (InterVarsity Christian Fellowship) and the missional / simple-church movement. Sanders holds an MTh in Applied Theology (University of Wales / Spurgeon's College) alongside an MA in Religious Studies (University of South Florida) and a BA in Communication (University of Florida). He is non-denominationally networked — the Underground Network is a city-wide microchurch movement, not a denomination — and his ecclesial conviction sits outside attractional, program-centered church logic while remaining recognizably evangelical in Christology and Scripture.

**Formation influences**:

- **InterVarsity Christian Fellowship / Tampa Urban Project** — collegiate and urban mission, empowerment of ordinary believers in every context, years as Florida State Director (1999–2025).
- **Missional / simple-church stream** — Hirsch, Cole, Frost, and the Exponential ecosystem; Alan Hirsch wrote the foreword to *Underground Church*; Neil Cole and Michael Frost endorsed it.
- **Inner-city intentional community** — more than twenty years living in Tampa's poorest urban neighborhood with his wife Monica and their six children; the theological soil beneath the microchurch experiment.

## Key frameworks and models

| Framework | Theological core | Where developed |
|-----------|-----------------|-----------------|
| **Microchurch ("a smaller way")** | The church's basic unit is small — worship, community, and mission coalescing — and is "not just the smallest expression of the church, it is the most common." | *Microchurches: A Smaller Way* (2019) |
| **The ecclesial minimum** | Worship (Jesus' lordship), community (shared accountable life), and mission (outward witness) are the irreducible core when every cultural and programmatic accretion is stripped away. | *Microchurches* (2019); Microchurches podcast |
| **The Underground model** | Church "structured and streamlined for mission" rather than for a weekly gathering; a network of relationship and resources serving many small churches; priority of the poor, the lost, and biblical justice. | *Underground Church* (2018); Underground Network practice since 2006 |

## Distinctive positions

- **The microchurch is primary, not preliminary.** Sanders inverts the assumption that small groups are stepping-stones to a larger gathering. The microchurch is the most common and most reproducible expression of the church.
- **Mission organizes the church, not the reverse.** A church can be structured and streamlined for mission rather than for a weekly service and still be the church "in its most potent form."
- **The network serves the small.** The proper ecclesial relationship is a lightweight network incubating many mission-organized microchurches — not a central church running programs people attend.
- **Leaders serve callings, not visions.** Leading people means releasing their God-given callings rather than recruiting them to the leader's agenda.
- **Anonymity is incompatible with witness.** "Any place where we can go consistently and remain anonymous is not a witnessing church."
- **Every disciple carries formation responsibility.** "My prayer is that every committed disciple would imagine themselves responsible for the future formation of the church."
- **Justice and the poor are ecclesial, not peripheral.** The Underground was built with explicit priority for the poor, the lost, and biblical justice — not as a social appendix but as church identity.

## Christology and worship

Sanders' Christology is evangelical and lordship-centered. Worship is defined by the lordship of Jesus rather than by liturgical form or musical performance — a distinction that keeps ecclesiology from collapsing into event attendance. The kingdom is proclaimed when "every Christian in every context" is called to carry responsibility for the church's future formation.

## Missiology

Mission is the organizing center. Sanders argues the Underground had to affirm "that every Christian in every context is being called by God to proclaim the coming of his kingdom." Ministry is organized around individuals' callings to outward mission — to the poor, the lost, and biblical justice — rather than around a central gathering that people attend. This is practitioner missiology: the ten-year Tampa experiment narrated in *Underground Church* is the evidential base, not a thought experiment.

## Ecclesiology

Sanders' ecclesiology is microchurch-and-network. The church's irreducible unit practices worship, community, and mission simultaneously. A city-wide network provides coaching, finance, media, and incubation without replacing the microchurch as the locus of discipleship. Sister movements in fifteen to thirty-plus cities worldwide (Kansas City Underground, Ignite Dublin, and others) treat the Tampa model as a reference architecture.

## Leadership and formation

Leadership is empowering and calling-serving. Formation happens in intentional community and outward mission — the inner-city household Sanders has shared with Monica and their children for more than two decades is the lived substrate. *The 6 Seasons of Calling* extends the "callings of individuals" theme across the lifespan, treating vocation as developmental rather than as a single vocational decision.
