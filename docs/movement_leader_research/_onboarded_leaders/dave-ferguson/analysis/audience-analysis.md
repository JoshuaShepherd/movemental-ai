## Audience Profile

### Who Is the Audience?

**Primary audience**: Church planters and multiplication-minded senior pastors actively working to move their churches from addition to reproduction (Levels 3→4 on the Five-Level framework). Typically ages 30–50, evangelical, denominationally diverse (Exponential serves 100+ networks). They use Ferguson's frameworks — Hero Maker's five practices, the BLESS missional rhythm, the Five-Level church typology — as practical implementation tools. Many encountered his work first through the Exponential conference or through the NewThing network. They are practitioners, not scholars: they want accessible, reproducible frameworks they can deploy with their leadership teams next Sunday.

**Secondary audience**: Denominational executives and network leaders (directors of church multiplication for the SBC, AG, Wesleyan, C&MA, Vineyard, and similar bodies) who use Exponential as a collective convening platform. They engage Ferguson primarily through the conference, through free Exponential eBooks (*Becoming a Level Five Multiplying Church*, *Keeping Score*, *Good, Great, Greater*), and through book adoptions for cohort-based training. Ages 40–60. They think at the systems level and are drawn to the Five-Level framework's diagnostic clarity and the 16% Mission's aspirational target.

**Tertiary audience**: Everyday Christians seeking a concrete, non-awkward framework for sharing their faith with neighbors. This is the *BLESS* and *Finding Your Way Back to God* audience — broader than the church-leader base, more general-market, less theologically specialized. These readers arrived through WaterBrook's distribution (PRH general Christian/inspirational channel) rather than through Exponential. Ages 25–55, evangelical, looking for practical spiritual formation tools they can use without being a pastor or theologian.

**Emerging audience**: Leaders across the for-profit sector encountering Ferguson's hero-making and multiplication frameworks through his Global Leadership Network articles, the Carey Nieuwhof podcast, and the Workplaces.org platform. This audience reads Ferguson as a leadership-development voice, not primarily a church voice. The *Multiplier* (2026) book and RPMS framework, with its language of sustainable health, resonates in organizational contexts beyond the church.

## Audience Personas

### Persona 1: Marcus — Multi-Site Pastor Moving from Growth to Multiplication

| Field | Value |
|-------|-------|
| Role | Lead pastor of a 1,200-member suburban evangelical church with two campuses |
| Age | 41 |
| Education | MDiv from a Southern Baptist seminary |
| Tradition | Non-denominational / broadly evangelical |
| Discovery path | Attended Exponential Orlando 2019; bought *Hero Maker* at the conference bookstore; sent the book to his entire elder board |
| Primary format | Books + Exponential conference talks + podcast appearances (Carey Nieuwhof) |
| Use case | Trying to shift his church from Level 3 (growing) to Level 4 (reproducing); using the Hero Maker five practices to restructure staff performance reviews around who each leader is apprenticing |
| Pain point | His board celebrates attendance growth and budget surpluses; he needs language and evidence for why reproducing a daughter church is more faithful than adding a third campus |

**What Marcus values in Ferguson**: The Five-Level framework gives him a simple, non-threatening vocabulary for explaining why his current success metrics are theologically insufficient. *Hero Maker*'s five practices are concrete enough to present in a board meeting. Ferguson's own journey at Community Christian — from a single church to a multi-site to a movement catalyst — is exactly the trajectory Marcus wants to model. The ICNU concept gave Marcus language for a leadership culture shift he had been trying to articulate for years.

**What Marcus struggles with**: He bought the book; his elders didn't. He needs more than a single book — he needs a cohort, a curriculum, an ongoing community of practice with other leaders on the same journey. He wishes Ferguson had a coaching network or a digital course he could put his whole team through.

| Field | Value |
|-------|-------|
| Role | Financial advisor and small-group leader at a mid-size evangelical church |
| Age | 36 |
| Education | Business degree |
| Tradition | Non-denominational evangelical |

| Use case | Applying the five BLESS practices to his relationships with five specific neighbors; using the framework to guide his small group's outreach efforts |

**What James values in Ferguson**: The BLESS framework is genuinely simple enough to remember, natural enough to actually use, and theologically grounded enough to feel meaningful rather than gimmicky. No digital community of practitioners, no ongoing curriculum, no place to report back on how the framework is going. The book ends and the conversation ends. He would engage with a BLESS digital community or small-group video course if it existed.

### Positioning Summary

Dave Ferguson's competitive advantage is not academic depth (that's Hirsch or Woodward) or prolific volume (that's Hirsch or Frost). It is **institutional infrastructure at maximum scale combined with practitioner credibility**. He co-founded the world's largest church-planting conference and personally oversaw a network that planted 6,373+ churches. When he writes about multiplication, he is not describing an ideal — he is reporting from three decades of building the largest multiplication infrastructure in North American evangelicalism. No other voice in the missional movement has this particular credential.

## Movemental Audience Implications

- **Content format priority**: The Exponential conference and the books are the dominant format anchors. Ferguson's audience is practitioner-heavy and time-compressed — they want frameworks they can deploy in ministry next week, not long academic treatments. Online courses built around the Five-Level framework, the Hero Maker five practices, the BLESS framework, and the RPMS gauges would map directly onto what his audience already pays for (conference registration, book purchases, free Exponential eBooks they trade emails for).

- **Owned audience urgency**: This is Ferguson's most significant strategic gap relative to his stature. His personal Substack has 511 subscribers after 4 months. Exponential's newsletter list, conference registration database, and podcast audience are institutionally owned — if he left Exponential, he would not take that list. Movemental's highest-value contribution is helping Ferguson build an owned audience that is his personally, not Exponential's. The Substack URL problem (daveferguson3.substack.com — the "3" suffix is a permanent brand friction point) should be addressed on any new platform.

- **Cross-pollination with Alan Hirsch**: Ferguson's co-authorship of *On the Verge* with Hirsch, his integration of APEST language, and Hirsch's endorsement of *Multiplier* make these two the highest-value natural pairing on the platform. Hirsch provides the meta-framework; Ferguson provides the practical deployment. A co-produced "How to Build a Multiplying Church" learning track would serve both audiences and amplify each author's reach.

- **BLESS as a wider-market entry point**: The BLESS and *Finding Your Way Back to God* content reaches a significantly broader audience than Ferguson's church-leader content. These titles were published by WaterBrook (Penguin Random House) and aimed at the general Christian market. A Movemental-hosted BLESS digital study or video course could expand his reach into general-Christian consumer audiences well beyond the church-planter niche.

- **Monetization signal**: Strong. Ferguson's audience already pays: Exponential conference registrations are a paid product (hundreds of dollars per attendee), books across four publishers sell consistently, and audiobooks (7 titles) indicate a listening audience willing to consume his content in multiple formats. The audience is primarily professional church leaders and denominational executives with ministry budget access — they can expense training resources and conference registrations. This is not a budget-constrained audience; it is an underserved audience that will pay for structured, ongoing formation if the access mechanism exists.
