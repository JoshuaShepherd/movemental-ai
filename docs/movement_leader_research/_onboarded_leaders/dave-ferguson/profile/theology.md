# Theological Profile: Dave Ferguson

**Status**: Complete
**Last updated**: 2026-05-27

---

## Core Thesis

The mission of Jesus is fundamentally a multiplication mission, and the church fulfills that mission not by growing larger but by reproducing disciples, leaders, groups, churches, and networks at every level. A leader's primary calling is not to be the hero — not to accumulate platform, congregation, or influence — but to make heroes of others by calling out their God-given gifts and releasing them into apprenticeship-based ministry. Healthy, reproducing leaders and healthy, reproducing churches are the engine of kingdom movement; the church does not advance through the addition of attendees but through the multiplication of sent, mission-shaped people. This multiplication vision must be held together by relational collaboration ("the Great Collaboration") and grounded in incarnational neighboring practices (BLESS) that make the mission concrete and accessible for every ordinary Christian.

---

## Theological Tradition

**Primary**: Evangelical missional theology, rooted in church-planting practice and the reproducing-church movement.

**Denominational heritage**: Independent Christian Church / Churches of Christ (Restoration Movement). Ferguson grew up in a Restoration Movement household (his parents planted a church in this tradition when he moved from Missouri to Chicago at age 4). Lincoln Christian University — his undergraduate institution — is a flagship Restoration Movement school. Community Christian Church operates in the independent Christian Church tradition, emphasizing non-creedal, Scripture-centered Christianity, baptism, and ecclesial simplicity. The Restoration Movement's historic concern for returning to New Testament Christianity maps closely onto Ferguson's emphasis on apostolic church planting and movement ecclesiology.

**Secondary influences**:
- **Missional-incarnational movement** (Alan Hirsch, Michael Frost): Ferguson co-authored *On the Verge* (2011) with Hirsch; his practical ecclesiology draws heavily on Hirsch's "mDNA" (missional DNA), "Apostolic Genius," and APEST (Apostles, Prophets, Evangelists, Shepherds, Teachers) framework, applying these to the American multi-site and church-planting context.
- **Church-growth movement (adapted and critiqued)**: Ferguson employs church-growth language (metrics, growth stages, scoreboards) but redirects it toward multiplication rather than accumulation. His five-level church typology (decline, plateau, growing, reproducing, multiplying) critiques the dominant Level 3 "growing" model as insufficient.
- **Evangelical missiology**: Engages the broader evangelical church-planting conversation through Exponential, where he convenes leaders from 100+ networks and denominations spanning Baptist, Wesleyan, Reformed, Pentecostal, and non-denominational traditions.

**Theological method**: Practitioner-inductive. Ferguson develops theology from the ground up — from lived church-planting experience at CCC and the NewThing network — and then frames it in accessible biblical terms. He is not a systematic theologian but a theological practitioner: he reads Scripture through the lens of what he has seen work in church planting, and he reads church-planting practice through the lens of what he finds in Jesus's ministry and the book of Acts.

Each step progressively releases the apprentice toward independent multiplication. Ferguson applied this model to his own pastoral succession: Ted Coniaris moved through these steps over an 18-month process before becoming Lead Pastor in May 2025. | [unseminary.com](https://unseminary.com/embracing-the-future-with-humility-community-christians-leadership-succession-with-ted-coniaris/); *Hero Maker* (2018); *Exponential* (2010) |
| **BLESS (Five Missional Practices)** | A five-practice framework for everyday incarnational neighboring: **B**egin with prayer (daily intercession for five specific neighbors); **L**isten (ask questions, understand their lives without immediately talking); **E**at (share meals; build relationship over food); **S**erve (respond to the needs discovered through listening); **S**hare (tell your personal story of faith when the relationship is ready). Rooted in Genesis 12:2 ("I will bless you... and you shall be a blessing") and illustrated by a Thai missionary study where "blessers" led ~100 people to faith versus 2 for information-distributors. | *B.L.E.S.S.* (2020, with Jon Ferguson); [christianpost.com](https://www.christianpost.com/news/megachurch-pastor-dave-ferguson-shines-spotlight-on-winning-souls-one-by-one.html) |

Designed for spiritually wandering people and for churches doing outreach and evangelism. | *Finding Your Way Back to God* (WaterBrook/Multnomah, 2015, with Jon Ferguson) |
| **The Big Idea** | One laser-focused theme per week, aligned across all church ministries — preaching, children's ministry, small groups, hospitality, social media — through creative collaboration among church staff and volunteers. | **The Great Collaboration** | The three great commands of Jesus synthesized: Great Commission (Go) + Great Commandment (Love) + Great Collaboration (Together). Reproducing church networks are not optional accessories but the infrastructure for kingdom movement. | *Together: The Great Collaboration* (Exponential, 2020, with Patrick O'Connell) |
| **RPMS (Leader Health Gauges)** | Four diagnostic "gauges" for monitoring leader health and preventing drift: Relational (Am I connected to life-giving relationships?), Physical (Am I stewarding my body?), Mental (Is my mind clear and grounded?), Spiritual (Am I abiding in Christ?). Drawn from Luke 2:52 ("Jesus grew in wisdom [mental] and stature [physical], and in favor with God [spiritual] and men [relational]"). Journaling-based practice, rated 1–10 daily. | *Multiplier* (Zondervan Reflective, 2026); [globalleadership.org](https://globalleadership.org/articles/leading-yourself/do-this-every-day-to-combat-drift/) |
| **The Drift Trio** | Three corrosive forces that cause leaders to drift from health and integrity: (1) The World — surrounding leaders with empty, fleeting values; (2) The Flesh — corrupted desires and disordered passions; (3) The Devil — spiritual adversary working behind the scenes. Ferguson frames drift as incremental and unconscious: "leaders drift one day at a time." | *Multiplier* (2026); [jeffmclain.com](https://www.jeffmclain.com/a-life-worth-multiplying-a-look-at-dave-fergusons-multiplier/) |
| **Six Permission-Giving Levels** | A spectrum for releasing leadership: (1) Watch and discuss; (2) Plan together; (3) They propose, you discuss; (4) They plan, you give feedback; (5) They handle independently, report results; (6) Complete autonomy. Leaders can calibrate where a given person sits on this spectrum and progressively move toward greater release. | [exponential.org/hero-maker-2-permission-giver](https://exponential.org/hero-maker-2-permission-giver/) |
| **Four-Stage Movement Framework** (from *On the Verge*, with Hirsch) | (1) Imagine — develop missional perspective; (2) Shift — reframe ecclesial concepts; (3) Innovate — implement genuine innovation; (4) Move — generate sustained apostolic momentum. Applied to established large churches seeking to become apostolic movements. | *On the Verge* (Zondervan, 2011, with Alan Hirsch) |

## Distinctive Theological Positions

**Multiplication over growth**: Ferguson makes a consistent and sharp distinction between churches that grow (Level 3) and churches that reproduce and multiply (Levels 4–5). Growth without reproduction is, in his framework, a theological failure — it keeps kingdom resources within one organization rather than investing them in the next generation of disciples and churches. "Church growth is good but it's only good if it leads to multiplication, which is best." ([churchleaders.com](https://churchleaders.com/podcast/319174-dave-ferguson-multiplication-mission-jesus.html))

**Every leader reproduces who they are**: Leadership formation is ontological, not merely behavioral. "You reproduce who you are and what you do." (*Multiplier*, 2026) This is why internal health — relational, physical, mental, spiritual — is a theological issue, not merely a personal wellness concern. A spiritually unhealthy leader will inevitably replicate that unhealthiness in those they disciple.

**Hero making over heroism**: The American church-leader culture prizes the "hero pastor" — the gifted speaker and visionary leader who builds a large platform. Ferguson argues this is a theological distortion. Jesus spent 73% of his time with the Twelve (the apprenticeship minority) rather than the crowds. The leader's primary calling is to make other leaders great, not to be great themselves. "Am I trying to be the hero, or am I trying to make heroes out of others?" (*Hero Maker*, 2018)

**Mission belongs to every believer**: BLESS is deliberately designed to be practiced by "everyone — young and old" (book subtitle). The New Testament vision is of a kingdom of priests (1 Peter 2:9) in which every follower is sent on mission. Ferguson consistently pushes against a clergy-lay distinction in mission, framing all Christians as missionaries within their existing relational networks.

**Prevenient grace shapes neighborly evangelism**: In the BLESS framework, prayer comes first because Ferguson holds that God is already at work in every neighbor before the Christian shows up. The task is to listen and discern what God is already doing, then join it — rather than launching an "outreach program" that treats the neighbor as a passive target.

**Kingdom metrics, not church metrics**: Ferguson argues that churches measure the wrong things — attendance and budget — rather than the right things: disciples made, leaders released, churches planted. His concept of the "kingdom scoreboard" tracks who is currently in apprenticeship and how many leaders have been developed and released, making multiplication visible and accountable.

**Collaboration as kingdom infrastructure**: The merger of NewThing into Exponential in 2025 was framed by the principle "Kingdom over Castle." Organizations that hold their structures too tightly diminish the kingdom; leaders who are willing to subordinate institutional interests to kingdom mission enable movement-scale impact.

**Spiritual health as multiplication prerequisite**: *Multiplier* (2026) makes the argument that sustainable kingdom multiplication requires internal health first. "You can't multiply something that's not first alive in you." The leader who neglects the RPMS gauges will eventually fail publicly in ways that harm the communities they serve.

## Ecclesiology

Ferguson's ecclesiology is best characterized as **apostolic-reproducing**: the church is a sent, reproducing organism, not a gathered, attracting institution. Its primary task is not to grow one congregation but to generate a movement of disciples and churches across every context.

Key ecclesiological commitments:
- **Multi-site as reproducing vehicle**: CCC's early expansion into multiple campuses was not merely a growth strategy but an expression of the church's identity as a reproducing community.
- **Networked churches over independent churches**: NewThing's model of interconnected church-planting networks reflects Ferguson's conviction that the kingdom advances through collaboration ("Together"), not through the isolation of independent congregations.
- **The local church as movement catalyst**: Community Christian Church has understood itself not as a destination but as a launching pad — an "impact church, reproducing church, and catalyst for global church movement" simultaneously.
- **Restoring New Testament patterns**: The Restoration Movement heritage shows in Ferguson's appeal to Jesus's ministry and the book of Acts as the normative pattern for how the church should plant, reproduce, and multiply.

## Intellectual Genealogy

**Primary theological influences**:
- **Alan Hirsch** — Ferguson's most significant intellectual conversation partner. The co-authorship of *On the Verge* (2011) brought Hirsch's APEST framework, mDNA, "Apostolic Genius," and Forgotten Ways missiological vision into direct integration with Ferguson's reproducing-church practice. Ferguson is self-described as "high on the apostolic gifting" — language drawn directly from Hirsch's APEST typology.
- **The Restoration Movement tradition** — Non-creedal, Scripture-centered, baptism-emphasizing, and ecclesially pragmatic. Ferguson's distrust of denominational gatekeeping and his appeal to New Testament simplicity trace to this heritage.
- **Jesus's discipleship model** — Ferguson reads the Gospels primarily through the lens of Jesus's time-allocation: 73% with the Twelve (apprenticeship), 27% with the masses. This becomes the hermeneutical key for his hero-making theology.

**In conversation with**:
- Todd Wilson (Exponential co-leader; Level Five church framework)
- Warren Bird (research collaborator; *Hero Maker*)
- Patrick O'Connell (NewThing implementation; *Together*)
- Ed Stetzer, Craig Groeschel, Christine Caine, Mark Batterson, Carey Nieuwhof, Derwin L. Gray (endorsers of *Multiplier*)

**Pushes back against**:
- **The hero-pastor model**: The celebrity-leader culture that prizes platform above reproduction. Ferguson's "hero maker" language is a deliberate inversion of this model.
- **Attractional ecclesiology**: Churches designed to attract and retain — "come and see" — rather than to send and multiply — "go and be." The BLESS framework is explicitly designed to move everyday Christians out of a consumer posture into a missionary posture.
- **Attendance-and-budget scorecards**: The dominant metrics of American church health, which Ferguson argues incentivize growth at Levels 1–3 and make multiplication invisible.
- **Organizational castle-keeping**: Leaders and organizations that prioritize institutional self-preservation over kingdom collaboration. "Kingdom over Castle" is the counterproposal.

## Theological Evolution

Ferguson's theology has developed across three broadly identifiable phases:

**Phase 1 — Church strategy and the Big Idea (2007–2011)**: The earliest books (*The Big Idea*, *Exponential*, *On the Verge*) focus on ecclesial structure and methodology — how a single church can become a movement, how creative collaboration aligns ministries, how the apostolic-movement framework maps onto contemporary American church planting. This phase is primarily strategic and ecclesiological.

**Phase 2 — Mission activation and spiritual journey (2012–2015)**: *Discover Your Mission Now*, *Keeping Score*, and *Finding Your Way Back to God* mark a turn toward the individual believer's missional identity and the spiritual journey of the un- or de-churched. The theology here is more pastoral and evangelistic — how does every Christian participate in mission? What does the journey back to God look like? This phase broadens the audience from church leaders to ordinary Christians and spiritual seekers.

**Phase 3 — Leader health and multiplication sustainability (2018–2026)**: *Hero Maker* (2018), *B.L.E.S.S.* (2020), and *Multiplier* (2026) represent Ferguson's mature integration of multiplication theology with leader formation and personal health. The question is no longer simply "how do churches multiply?" but "how do leaders sustain the inner life that makes multiplication possible over decades?" This phase introduces the RPMS framework and the concept of the Drift Trio — arguably Ferguson's most theologically searching contribution, because it takes the multiplication imperative and grounds it in a realistic anthropology of human limitation and the possibility of leadership failure.

Throughout all three phases, Ferguson's core commitments — reproducing disciples, releasing leaders, networking churches, measuring by kingdom metrics — remain constant. The progression moves from ecclesial methodology to personal missional formation to integrated leader health.
