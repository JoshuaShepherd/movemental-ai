Dhati,

We've spent real time with your work — not skimming it, but reading it the way you'd want a fellow practitioner to: *Among Wolves* with its eight movements through Matthew, *Advocates* and its narrow path through Philemon, the *Where Life Exists* conversations, the "Why I Stay" series, the Saturate essay where you insisted the church "is not like family; it is family." What follows isn't a pitch. It's what we saw, and what we think it would take to put it all in one place.

The first thing we noticed is how *consistent* you are. Most leaders drift; you don't. From *In Plain Sight* in 2013 to *Among Wolves* in 2017 to the urban-missionary work of MyBLVD now, it's one conviction worked out over a decade: disciple-making, not church-planting, is the engine — "if you make disciples the logical result is a church." You've said you want Blueprint to be part of "the last generation to leave the urban context for sound discipleship." That line is a thesis, and almost everything you've made is a footnote to it.

The second thing we noticed is that your work doesn't live anywhere as one thing. *Among Wolves* is at B&H; the Bible study and leader kit are at LifeWay; *In Plain Sight* — the seed of all of it — has no retail page at all, no ISBN we could find, effectively gone. *Where Life Exists* is on your site and YouTube; the MyBLVD Podcast with Missie Branch is on Podbean and iHeart; *We Are Send Network* is on Apple. Your articles are scattered across ERLC, Saturate, Outreach, and Christian Post — and some of those archives won't even load for a search engine. A pastor in a hard city who wants "the Dhati Lewis way to make disciples among wolves" has to assemble you from fragments.

The third thing: your frameworks are doing the heavy lifting, and not one of them has a home. The Advocate-versus-Aggravator triad — rely on Christ, run to the tension, respond with dignity. The eight movements in Matthew. The church as family. The "7 Stops of an Urban Missionary" — which, as far as we can tell, isn't fully written down anywhere public at all. These are the things people quote you for, and they live buried inside long-form works instead of standing as references someone can find, link, and build on.

So here's what we'd do. We'd give the whole corpus one searchable home — the books, the studies, the podcast catalogue pulled together from its four platforms, the articles rescued from archives that don't crawl — and we'd make the frameworks first-class: each one a page a planter in Atlanta or Memphis or anywhere "life exists" can actually find. We'd capture the "7 Stops" out of your head and the podcast and into something durable. The goal isn't more content; you have enough. The goal is that the disciple-making vision you've spent a decade on becomes something others can disciple-make *from*.

We'd also hold your relational world in one place — not to commodify it, but because it's part of the story. The network you led at Send, the one you're building at MyBLVD, the peers who vouch for the work: that map matters, and right now it only exists in scattered traces.

We know the tension here, because it's your own tension — you'd rather run *to* the people than build infrastructure that sits between you and them. You've warned that gifts can take us where our character can't handle, and that overhead can mean "a lot of overhead and little impact." We're not asking you to become an institution. We're asking whether the work could be gathered so the next urban missionary doesn't have to leave to find sound discipleship — which is, after all, the thing you said you wanted.

If that's worth a conversation, we're ready when you are.
