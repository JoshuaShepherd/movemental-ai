# Calling Profile: Hugh Halter

Hugh Halter's vocation has always been lived on the ground before it was written down. For more than thirty years he and his wife Cheryl have planted churches and served as missionaries in North America, moving from Portland to Denver to Alton, and at each stop the calling has taken the same shape: to embody the kingdom of God in a particular place rather than to build a program that could be exported anywhere. The arc of his work runs from pioneering congregations to forming leaders to, most recently, regenerating a whole city.

In Denver that calling produced Adullam, which he founded and pastored not as a single Sunday gathering but as a network of incarnational communities — small, sent expressions of church woven into neighborhoods. Out of that practice came his role co-directing Missio, an apprenticing network where he and Matt Smay taught the next generation of missional leaders, and his service as U.S. director of Forge America. His early books — *The Tangible Kingdom*, the *Tangible Kingdom Primer*, and *AND* — were the written distillation of what he was already doing: making the case that the church is most faithful when it is both gathered and scattered, present in homes and tables and ordinary places.

The calling deepened as he turned toward the economics and ethics of how missionaries actually live. *BiVo* named the bi-vocational life as a calling rather than a compromise. *Sacrilege*, *Flesh*, and *Brimstone* pressed into the unorthodox ways of Jesus, the down-to-earth reality of the incarnation, and the discipline of holy nonjudgment — a posture toward outsiders that he has practiced as much as preached. *Righteous Brood*, written with Dave and Sue Ferguson, extended the mission into the family itself.

In Alton, the calling moved from forming leaders to rebuilding a place. He founded Lantern Network in 2016 and in 2018 took a derelict federal post office and turned it into Post Commons — the living room of the city, where connection, enterprise, and mission meet over coffee. That work has become the seedbed for his most recent vocation: through Brave Cities, co-led with Taylor McCall, he now coaches apostolic leaders to build "kingdom ecosystems" — whole-city expressions of the gospel where homes, reconciliation, discipleship, worship, enterprise, and justice coalesce. Across every season, the through-line is the same: incarnational presence, embodied first, then named.
