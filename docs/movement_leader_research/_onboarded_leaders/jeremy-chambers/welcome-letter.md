Jeremy

You and Monica have built one of the most genuinely integrative bodies of work in the missional church — the rare corpus that refuses to choose between intimacy with God and engagement with neighbors and instead holds them as a single rhythm. It began in Richmond in 2013, when you founded the Arise RVA Church Network and grew it into a web of microchurch communities affiliated with the V3 Movement under the Baptist General Association of Virginia. It carried west in late 2019, when you moved to Denver to help Rowland Smith launch the Denver expression of The Pando Collective. It now runs through your role as Training Director of Forge America, where you steward the formation of the next generation of practitioners. Along the way you and Monica wrote two books in the 100 Movements Publishing orbit — *Kingdom Contours*, the six-paradigm planting toolkit that extends Hirsch's mDNA, and *The Art of Missional Spirituality*, your thirty-one daily practices that fuse contemplative discipline with neighborhood mission. You coined "Grativangelism." You named the difference between calling and assignment. You have taught that distraction, not opposition, is the gravest danger to the inner life. That is a remarkable inventory for a practitioner who still plants and still coaches.

And almost none of it lives in one place that belongs to you. *The Art of Missional Spirituality* sits on Amazon, on the 100 Movements page, and on Goodreads. The revised *Kingdom Contours* sits on its own Amazon listing; the 2020 original sits on Everand under a different imprint and a different subtitle. Your essays scatter across the V3 Movement blog, where eleven posts ran between 2018 and 2021, across two Renovaré articles, and across a single Missio Alliance piece from 2025 — eleven years of writing with no shared surface a reader can walk through. Your podcast footprint is real and uncollected: the Renovaré *Life With God* conversation on the Lord's Prayer, *Everyday Disciple* with Caesar Kalinowski, *Dandelion Resourcing* with Alex Absalom, *Pivotal People*, and *Shifting Culture* Episode 197 — all of them on other people's feeds. You host no podcast of your own. There is an Instagram handle at @jemochambers and a personal site that gathers the books, but no newsletter, no Substack, no place that turns a reader into someone you can reach again. The reach is genuine; the rails belong to other people. The relational layer is held the same way, and it is the layer your whole thesis depends on. The Arise communities you reproduced in Richmond, and the Denver micro-expressions you have helped launch through Pando, are connected to those networks, not to you. The Forge practitioners you train nationally are formed through Forge's pipeline and its Teachable courses, credited to the team rather than to your name. The endorsers and co-laborers who orbit your work — Rowland Smith, Terry Ishee, JR Woodward, Brian Sanders, Carolyn Arends, the Hirsches, Michael Frost — are one conversation away, but there is no surface where the people you have formed can find their way back to you once a cohort ends or a conference closes.

The largest pool of value, and the one most at risk of going unwritten, sits in your head and in your marriage. The judgment behind the thirty-one practices — why Praying the Hours, why secret acts of kindness, why immersion in Ecclesiastes — exists in fuller form than any book has captured. The Cycle of Grace as you actually teach it, the lived mechanics of calling versus assignment, the pastoral wisdom of refusing microchurch absolutism, the dyad method by which you and Monica write together — these are the things practitioners most need and the things least likely to make it onto a page on their own. The Forge training you deliver in rooms and on calls is taught a hundred times before it is ever written down once.
