# Competitive Landscape Analysis: Meghan Larissa Good

**Research Date**: 2026-05-27
**Subject**: Meghan Larissa Good — Resource Development Manager, Jesus Collective; AMBS DMin Faculty; author (*The Bible Unwrapped*, *Divine Gravity*)
**Primary Niche**: Jesus-centered hermeneutics, Anabaptist theology, movement ecclesiology, narrative preaching, post-evangelical deconstruction/reconstruction
**Confidence Level**: Medium-High

## 1. Direct Competitors

These authors operate in substantially overlapping territory — Jesus-centered Christianity, accessible biblical interpretation, progressive/post-evangelical theology — with overlapping audiences.

### Pete Enns / The Bible for Normal People

- **Overlap**: Very High. Enns is the dominant voice in the accessible, intellectually honest, post-fundamentalist biblical scholarship space. His podcast "The Bible for Normal People" (co-hosted with Jared Byas) is the most-listened-to biblical studies podcast in the progressive-evangelical market. Both Good and Enns address: how to read difficult texts, how to hold a high view of Scripture while engaging its complexity, how to help people who were hurt by fundamentalist approaches. Both are accessible without being shallow.
- **Distinction**: Enns is an academic scholar (PhD Harvard, Westminster Theological Seminary faculty, Eastern University) writing from within and then against the Reformed tradition. Good is a narrative theologian-preacher writing from within the Anabaptist tradition. Enns has become more skeptical of traditional evangelical categories over time ("The Sin of Certainty"); Good maintains a more robustly Christocentric and orthodox framework. Enns' Christocentrism is assumed rather than foregrounded; Good's "Jesus is the fullness of God" is the explicit organizing claim of everything she does. Their audiences overlap substantially in the "left-of-evangelical, still Christian" demographic, but Good speaks more directly to pastors and ministry leaders who want to remain orthodox while engaging intellectual honesty.
- **Books**: 10+ including *The Bible Tells Me So* (2014), *The Sin of Certainty* (2016), *How the Bible Actually Works* (2019), *The Heart of Christianity* (2023).
- **Digital Presence**: Very Strong. Active Substack ("Contemplative"), Podcast (The Bible for Normal People — estimated 100K+ downloads/episode), YouTube, Twitter/X (~65K followers estimated), Instagram. One of the most developed digital platforms in the space.
- **Audience**: Large (100K–300K estimated). Post-evangelical, mainline curious, former fundamentalists, progressive Christians.
- **Unique Angle**: Academic scholarship made maximally accessible; permission to doubt without losing faith; warm irreverence toward fundamentalism.
- **Overlap with Good**: **Very High on audience** — similar demographics and felt needs. **Moderate on theology** — Enns is less Christocentric as organizing principle; Good is more orthodox in her Christological commitments. **Good's distinct advantage**: The "Jesus as gravitational center" reframe is a more constructive, actionable vision than Enns' primarily deconstructive approach. For pastors who need something to give their congregation after deconstruction, Good is the better resource.

### Brian McLaren

- **Overlap**: High. McLaren is the most prominent progressive-evangelical reform voice of the last two decades. Both Good and McLaren write for post-evangelical Christians seeking a more hopeful, generous, Jesus-centered faith; both use "better story" language; both diagnose Western Christianity as having gotten the story wrong; both are published in trade Christian books rather than academic presses.
- **Distinction**: McLaren operates at much larger scale (15+ books, major publishers including Convergent and Jericho Books, international speaking profile). He has moved further from traditional evangelical categories than Good — his christology and soteriology are more pluralist/universalist in trajectory. Good maintains a more robustly orthodox Christocentric framework: "Jesus is the fullness of God" is a confessional claim, not a metaphor. McLaren endorsed *Divine Gravity* ("The world needs to see congregations of people who are actually following Christ"), signaling recognition of Good as a more constructively Jesus-centered voice in the same general space.
- **Books**: 15+ including *A New Kind of Christian* (2001), *A Generous Orthodoxy* (2004), *The Secret Message of Jesus* (2006), *We Make the Road by Walking* (2014), *Do I Stay Christian?* (2022).
- **Digital Presence**: Strong. Active across social media, website, and speaking circuit.
- **Audience**: Large (100K–300K estimated). Progressive Christians, post-evangelicals, seekers.
- **Overlap with Good**: **High on audience** (post-evangelicals seeking a better story). **Moderate on theology** (Good is more confessionally Christocentric; McLaren more pluralist). **Good's distinct advantage**: Anabaptist peace-church tradition gives Good a more historically grounded "better story" than McLaren's more conceptual frame; her pastoral credential (16 years) grounds her in local congregation reality; her hermeneutical framework (*The Bible Unwrapped*) is more practically useful for Scripture engagement.

### Sarah Bessey

- **Overlap**: High. Bessey is the most prominent progressive-evangelical woman's voice in the "better story" and "deconstruction-to-reconstruction" space. Both Good and Bessey write accessible theology for women and men who have been hurt by patriarchal/fundamentalist Christianity; both emphasize the person of Jesus over doctrinal systems; both write with personal confession, narrative grace, and accessible prose.
- **Distinction**: Bessey is primarily a personal essayist and memoirist (*Jesus Feminist*, *Out of Sorts*, *Field Notes for the Wilderness*) rather than a systematic theologian or hermeneutician. Good provides more theological and hermeneutical rigor; Bessey provides more personal testimony and spiritual companionship. Bessey's *Out of Sorts* and *Field Notes for the Wilderness* overlap with *Divine Gravity*'s deconstruction/reconstruction audience. Bessey founded Evolving Faith (a conference for the deconstructing); Good is building infrastructure through Jesus Collective.
- **Books**: 4 — *Jesus Feminist* (2013), *Out of Sorts* (2015), *Miracles and Other Reasonable Things* (2019), *Field Notes for the Wilderness* (2023).
- **Digital Presence**: Strong. Large Instagram following (350K+ estimated), active newsletter, Substack presence, Evolving Faith conference platform.
- **Audience**: Large (200K–400K estimated). Progressive Christian women (primary); post-evangelical men and women (secondary).
- **Overlap with Good**: **High on audience** (post-evangelical women; deconstruction survivors). **Low-Moderate on theology** (Bessey is more personal/narrative; Good is more theologically systematic). **Good's distinct advantage**: Theological rigor and hermeneutical framework give Good a resource Bessey's audience needs but Bessey herself does not provide. *The Bible Unwrapped* fills exactly the gap Bessey's readers feel after reading *Out of Sorts* — "now what do I do with the Bible?"

### Bridget Eileen Rivera

- **Overlap**: Moderate-High. Rivera writes for progressive/post-evangelical Christians navigating deconstruction, emphasizing the Jesus of the Gospels over doctrinal systems. Both Good and Rivera are women writing for the deconstruction-to-reconstruction audience with Christocentric orienting claims.
- **Distinction**: Rivera is primarily a blogger and memoirist at a smaller scale; Good is a formally credentialed theologian (DMin, Buechner Award), an ordained pastor (16+ years), and a trade-published author. Rivera's audience is younger and more skeptical of institutional Christianity; Good's audience is more pastorally-oriented and open to denominational identity.
- **Overlap with Good**: **Moderate** — similar audience demographics; different scale and different credential level. Good's institutional credibility (Mennonite ordination, AMBS, Herald Press) is a differentiator.

## 2. Adjacent Voices

These authors cover related territory but with a different primary angle, tradition, or institutional context.

### Greg Boyd (ReKnew Ministries / Woodland Hills Church)

- **Relationship**: Closest senior collaborator. Boyd wrote the foreword for *The Bible Unwrapped* (2018); Good wrote the foreword for Boyd's *God Looks Like Jesus* (2025). Both are connected to Jesus Collective. Boyd hosted Good in a two-part *Divine Gravity* interview (ReKnew podcast, May 2025). Their Christocentric hermeneutic ("God looks like Jesus") is nearly identical in content.
- **Distinction**: Boyd is a formally trained philosopher-theologian (Yale PhD) and the most prominent open theist in evangelical Christianity. His scale (20+ books, Zondervan publisher, international reach) is substantially larger than Good's. His audience is broader but his Anabaptist/peace-church denominational specificity is lower. Good's narrative preaching voice and accessible literary prose are distinctively hers; Boyd's is more systematic-philosophical.
- **Books**: 20+ including *Letters from a Skeptic* (1994), *God at War* (1997), *The Myth of a Christian Nation* (2005), *Cross Vision* (2017), *Inspired Imperfection* (2023), *God Looks Like Jesus* (2025).
- **Digital Presence**: Strong. ReKnew.org, active podcast/YouTube, Twitter/X (37K+ estimated), Herald Press and other publishers.
- **Audience**: Large-Established (100K–200K estimated). Progressive evangelical, open theism adherents, Jesus Collective network.
- **Overlap with Good**: **Very High on theology** (shared Christocentric hermeneutic; mutual forewords). **High on audience** (Jesus Collective network; progressive-evangelical). **Complementary voices**: Boyd is the philosopher-theologian providing systematic depth; Good is the narrative preacher providing accessible, pastoral entry. On Movemental, this is the highest-value pairing — Boyd's audience is a ready-made distribution network for Good's books and courses.

### Rachel Held Evans (Legacy)

- **Relationship**: Adjacent model and movement predecessor. Evans' death in 2019 left an unclaimed audience — progressive-evangelical women seeking theological honesty, accessible scholarship, and a Jesus-centered alternative to fundamentalism. Good's voice, books, and audience profile parallel Evans' in meaningful ways: accessible prose, personal confession, Christocentric orientation, willingness to name what the church has gotten wrong without abandoning it.
- **Distinction**: Evans was more confessional-memoir oriented (*A Year of Biblical Womanhood*, *Searching for Sunday*, *Inspired*); Good is more movement-manifesto and hermeneutically systematic. Evans built a large Twitter/blog following in the 2010s digital moment; Good is building in a different (YouTube, newsletter) moment. Evans' death created a genuine gap that no single voice has fully filled.
- **Overlap with Good**: **High on audience** (progressive-evangelical women; deconstruction survivors; people wanting to "stay Christian"). Good does not replace Evans but does serve a meaningful portion of the audience Evans served. This is a strategic consideration for Movemental positioning.

### Richard Rohr (Center for Action and Contemplation)

- **Relationship**: Adjacent contemplative Catholic voice. Both Rohr and Good address a post-evangelical audience seeking spiritual depth without fundamentalist constraints. Both emphasize a Jesus-centered Christianity that has been distorted by power and cultural accommodation.
- **Distinction**: Rohr is a Franciscan friar writing from Catholic contemplative tradition; Good is a Mennonite pastor writing from Anabaptist peace-church tradition. Rohr's Christology (Universal Christ framework) differs substantially from Good's (Jesus as the specific historical revelation of the fullness of God). Rohr's "Universal Christ" framework has been critiqued by evangelical and Anabaptist voices for collapsing the distinction between Christ and creation; Good's framework is more robustly particular.
- **Audience**: Very Large (500K+ estimated). Contemplative Catholics, progressive Christians, spiritual-but-not-religious seekers.
- **Overlap with Good**: **Moderate on audience** (spiritual seekers; post-evangelical); **Low on theology** (different Christologies, different tradition). Not a direct competitor but an adjacent voice whose audience contains potential Good readers.

### Nijay Gupta

- **Relationship**: Good's DMin dissertation committee member (expert advisor). Both are accessible New Testament scholars writing for theologically literate lay and pastoral audiences. Gupta's *Tell Her Story* (2023) and *Paul and the Language of Faith* address similar audiences in overlapping institutional contexts (seminary faculty-to-practitioner).
- **Distinction**: Gupta is primarily an academic NT scholar with public-facing reach; Good is primarily a pastor-preacher-theologian with movement framing. They serve overlapping audiences at different points on the scholarly-popular spectrum.
- **Overlap with Good**: **Moderate** — shared audience in theologically educated pastors; different primary mode (academic-popular vs. pastoral-movement).

### Scot McKnight

- **Relationship**: Endorsed *The Bible Unwrapped*: "accessible, witty and theologically sound." Both occupy the accessible-NT-scholar-for-pastors space. McKnight's "Jesus Creed" framework (love God + neighbor as Jesus' interpretive hermeneutic) resonates directly with Good's Jesus-lens approach.
- **Distinction**: McKnight is a prolific academic (40+ books) at a much larger scale. His audience is broader and more evangelical. He writes from a Baptist/evangelical academic context; Good from Anabaptist pastoral ministry.
- **Overlap with Good**: **Moderate** — similar audience of theologically serious pastors and ministry leaders; Good is more narrowly focused on the Jesus-lens hermeneutic and movement ecclesiology. McKnight's endorsement of *The Bible Unwrapped* implies recognition of a peer voice.

## 4. Differentiation: What Makes Meghan Larissa Good Unique

### The Anabaptist Christocentric Voice in the Post-Evangelical Conversation

Good is the only voice in the deconstruction/reconstruction space who holds together:

1. **Anabaptist peace-church tradition** (MC USA ordination, AMBS affiliation, Herald Press publishing relationship) — providing denominational depth and historical grounding
2. **Christocentric hermeneutic applied with the rigor of a DMin dissertation** (*Between Breaths*; *The Bible Unwrapped*) — not just "Jesus is great" but a systematic, dialogic, genre-sensitive method
3. **Award-winning narrative preaching voice** (inaugural Frederick Buechner Award) — a literary-aesthetic register that no other theologian in this space matches at this level
4. **Movement-scale manifesto** (*Divine Gravity*) — a "better Christian story" framework with eight specific theses and a new-reformation vision
5. **Daily devotional content engine** ("Reading Jesus" YouTube — 54+ episodes in 2026) — consistent, accessible, Jesus-centered biblical accompaniment

No single peer holds all five. Enns has the hermeneutical accessibility but not the Anabaptist depth, the preaching award, or the movement framing. McLaren has the movement scale but not the Anabaptist specificity, the preaching credential, or the hermeneutical framework. Bessey has the narrative voice but not the theological rigor or the daily devotional content engine. Boyd has the Christocentric depth and the Jesus Collective connection but not the pastoral 16-year Anabaptist pastorate or the preaching award.

### The Hermeneutics-to-Movement Arc

Good has completed an arc that few voices manage: she has written the definitive accessible introduction to Jesus-centered hermeneutics (*The Bible Unwrapped*) AND the movement manifesto that shows where the hermeneutic leads ecclesially (*Divine Gravity*). Most peers do one or the other. Pete Enns explains how to read the Bible; he does not provide a movement vision for what the church should become. Brian McLaren has the movement vision but not the hermeneutical curriculum. Good provides both — making her uniquely useful for churches, seminaries, and movements that need to answer "how do we read Scripture?" AND "what kind of church should we become?"

### The Preaching Dimension

Good is the only voice in this peer group who is also a formally recognized award-winning narrative preacher. This distinction creates an audience (preachers, homiletics students, preaching instructors) that none of her peers can credibly address in the same way. It also shapes the quality of her writing: where Enns is an academic writing accessibly, where McLaren is a pastor-activist writing urgently, Good is a preacher writing — and the difference in how theological claims are embodied in specific images, stories, and moments is audible throughout her prose.

### The Centered-Set Ecclesiology

Good's "divine gravity" model — defining the church by trajectory toward Jesus rather than by boundary position — is the most practically useful ecclesiological framework in the deconstruction/reconstruction conversation. The question "am I still a Christian?" is answered not by checking doctrine boxes but by asking "am I moving toward Jesus?" This reframe has immediate pastoral utility for the deconstructing believer and the ministry leader trying to hold a polarized congregation together. No other voice in this space offers this specific model at this level of clarity.

## 8. Key Takeaway

Meghan Larissa Good's competitive advantage is not breadth of output or platform size — on those metrics, she trails Enns, McLaren, Bessey, Boyd, and McKnight significantly. Her advantage is **a unique combination of Anabaptist theological tradition depth, award-winning narrative preaching craft, a coherent Jesus-centered hermeneutical framework (proved by two books and a DMin), a movement manifesto (*Divine Gravity*)**, and a daily devotional content engine ("Reading Jesus" YouTube) — positioned at a career inflection point where all of these assets are converging for the first time.

The white space she occupies is specific and real: the progressive/post-evangelical reconstruction conversation is well-stocked with voices that help people deconstruct (Enns), process the emotional journey (Bessey), or articulate a broadly generous faith (McLaren). What it lacks is a voice that offers a theologically robust, Christocentrically grounded, hermeneutically systematic, and pastorally warm framework for *rebuilding* faith around Jesus — one that draws on a historically serious tradition (Anabaptism), carries the credential of award-winning preaching, and provides both the how-to-read-Scripture and the what-kind-of-church frameworks in accessible form. That is Good's lane. It is underoccupied. And she has been building toward it for the better part of two decades.

Movemental's opportunity is to give Good the digital infrastructure that matches her theological stature and her career inflection — a unified content hub, course delivery for the curriculum implicit in her two books, audience-building tools, and network amplification that connects her work to the Boyd/Jesus Collective ecosystem and the broader progressive-evangelical conversation. The gap between her actual contribution (Buechner Award winner, 16 years pastoral ministry, 2 Herald Press books with exceptional endorsers, Jesus Collective Theology Circle Chair, 54+ YouTube devotionals in a single year) and her digital visibility is one of the most correctable in the Movemental research pipeline. Closing that gap is the opportunity.
