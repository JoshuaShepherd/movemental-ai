**Description**: Good addresses the questions that 21st-century readers bring to the Bible — questions about biblical authority, literary genre, difficult texts, and how to interpret Scripture without falling into either knee-jerk fundamentalism or dismissive skepticism. The book guides readers toward "faithful reading, communal discernment, and deep and transformative wonder about Scripture." Three sections span biblical basics, interpretive methods across different literary genres (including Old Testament violence, poetry, prophecy, apocalyptic), and practical application to daily life, including the role of the Holy Spirit and community discernment. Each chapter concludes with questions for reflection. Written to be accessible for a lay audience while maintaining scholarly integrity. Simultaneously functioned as the book artifact component of Good's DMin dissertation ("Between Breaths: The Dialogic Shape of Christian Authority") at Portland Seminary.

**Foreword note**: Gregory Boyd wrote the foreword, calling the book "enjoyable to read—without sacrificing anything by way of biblical scholarship."

**Key endorsers**: Scot McKnight (Northern Seminary) — "accessible, witty and theologically sound"; Leonard Sweet (Evangelical Seminary) — "a rare treasure"; Gregory Boyd — commends the balance of storytelling and scholarship.

**Description**: A movement manifesto for a more hopeful, Jesus-centered Christianity. Good argues that Western Christianity is entering a period of reformation analogous to the Protestant Reformation — driven by new technologies, ecclesial scandals, and a crisis of biblical authority. The book presents eight central theses addressing Christian belief and practice, organized in three parts: a dilemma, a rediscovery, and a proposal for transformation. She argues "the Christian story the church has been telling has become too small" and invites readers into a movement already emerging across denominational lines that rediscovers authentic, Christ-focused narrative — "God is breathing life among us, restoring creation, and reconciling all divided things." The final chapter explores unity through a "gravitational approach" where "Jesus is Lord" is the centered story around which the Jesus movement revolves, drawing on Anabaptist theologian Mark Baker's centered-set ecclesiology. David C. Cramer (Anabaptist World, Jan 19, 2024) describes it as "both an apologetic for the Christian faith and a manifesto for reforming it."

**Key endorsers**:
