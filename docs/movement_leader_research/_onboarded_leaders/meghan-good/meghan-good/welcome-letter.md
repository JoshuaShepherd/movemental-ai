Meghan

You have built one of the most coherent teaching bodies of work in
Anabaptist Christianity, around a single idea pressed hard for sixteen
years. Two books carry it. *The Bible Unwrapped: Making
Sense of Scripture Today* came out from Herald Press in 2019 with a foreword
from Greg Boyd and a 2019 Outreach Magazine resource-of-the-year nod.
*Divine Gravity: Sparking a Movement to Recover a Better Christian Story*
followed from the same house in 2023, with a foreword from Dennis R.
Edwards. Behind the books sit a BA from Gordon College, an MDiv from Duke
Divinity School, and a DMin from Portland Seminary, where you held the first
Frederick Buechner Award for preaching. After sixteen years pastoring in
Oregon and Arizona, you now serve as Resource Development Manager at Jesus
Collective and chair its Theology Circle.

All of it is somewhere. None of it is in one place. The books sell on
Amazon, on Bookshop.org, and on Christianbook.com, each a separate island
with no path back to you. Your articles sit on partner mastheads — Outreach
Magazine, Anabaptist World, Mennonite Church USA — under their archives, not
yours. The interviews are scattered across other people's
feeds: *Divine Gravity* over two parts on *Greg Boyd: Apologies &
Explanations*, again with Joshua Johnson on *Shifting Culture*, and earlier
with Teena Dare on *Faith, Work & Rest*. You host *Reading Jesus* yourself,
walking through Luke, but it lives on its own channel with no thread tying
it to the books it grows out of. The relationships are split the same way.
The leaders you convene live inside the Jesus Collective membership rolls
and the Theology Circle roster. The students you guide through Teaching
Leadership live on AMBS cohort lists. Sixteen years of congregants at
Trinity Mennonite and your Oregon churches sit in directories you no longer
keep. The forewords alone put you one degree from Boyd at Woodland Hills and
from Edwards at Northern Seminary, but that adjacency lives in publisher
metadata. And the rest — the pastors and hosts who write you — runs through
a contact form and your own memory. What is
missing is the layer above all of this: the one surface that lets a reader
