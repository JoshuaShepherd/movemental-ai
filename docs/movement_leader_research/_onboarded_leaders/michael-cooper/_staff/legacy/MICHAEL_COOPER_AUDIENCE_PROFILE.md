## Executive Summary

Michael T. Cooper's audience is a **high-credibility, academy-plus-agency-plus-movement** set rather than a mass-media one: field missionaries and church planters who are movement-invested but wary of the CPM/DMM "industry," seminarians funneled through accredited programs, evangelical missiologists, free-church (notably EFCA-leaning) pastors, and a substantial Global South student population. The defining audience fact is structural and inverse to a typical solo author: Cooper has **large lent institutional reach** (East-West's ~$33M missionary/donor base, the Kairos degree funnel, the Mission India MA) but a **paper-thin owned-audience layer** (~120–175 brand-social followers, 12 podcast ratings, no list of scale). The 227-episode podcast and the William Carey bestseller prove sustained engagement; the missing layer is the owned audience that should have accrued from them and did not. This profile maps the segments, content consumption, unmet needs, and distribution strategy to close that gap.

## TAM Analysis

### Market framing

Cooper's addressable market is the **global evangelical-missiology and movement-leadership world** — narrower and more academic/practitioner-serious than a trade-devotional market, but with real institutional depth and a strong Global South tail.

- **Movement-invested mission practitioners** (Missio Nexus / EMS orbit): tens of thousands in aggregate; Cooper's engaged subset is far smaller.
- **Seminarians / degree-seekers** in his partner institutions (Kairos, Mission India, Knox, Nepal Ebenezer): the cohort populations of those programs.
- **Evangelical missiologists / academics**: small, high-signal (the EMS / *Missiology* / *Global Missiology* readership).
- **Global South students and planters** (India, Nepal, South Korea, Romania, Africa, S/SE Asia): substantial institutional reach.

## Primary Audience Segments

### Segment 1: CPM-skeptical, movement-invested practitioners (~35% — core audience)

**Who**: field missionaries and church planters invested in *movements* but wary of the CPM/DMM/T4T industry — its metrics, jargon, and method-first posture.
**Where they find him**: The Ephesiology Podcast; *Ephesiology*; Missio Nexus; the Motus Dei CPM scholar-practitioner network (Warrick Farah engages him).
**Why they engage**: a theologically serious, exegetically grounded account of movement dynamics *without* the technique-driven vocabulary they distrust — "disciples multiply while volunteers keep busy."
**What they want more of**: biblical/archaeological grounding for movement claims; a non-pragmatic theology of multiplication; coaching that is not CPM-by-another-name.

### Segment 2: Seminarians & degree-seekers (~25% — pipeline audience)

**Who**: students entering accredited, ministry-embedded programs via Kairos University, Mission India (Nagpur), Knox, and Nepal Ebenezer — often non-residential cohorts.
**Where they find him**: the Master Classes LMS; Kairos; Mission India; church-hosted Seminary@YourChurch cohorts.
**Why they engage**: Cooper directs the MA in Missiology of Movements and is an affiliate professor; his courses are the on-ramp to accredited credentials. Netland (TEDS) expects *Ephesiology* to be "used as a text in Bible colleges."
**What they want more of**: structured laddered curriculum, oral-culture pedagogy, and credentialed pathways.

### Segment 3: Academics & missiologists (~15% — peer / validation audience)

**Who**: missiologists, intercultural-studies faculty, and NT-mission scholars in the evangelical academy.
**Where they find him**: EMS volumes; *Missiology* / *Global Missiology* reviews (Priebe 2021 positive; Keene); Academia.edu; guest lectures at LSE, Bordeaux, Loyola, Baylor.
**Why they engage**: Ephesiology is treated as a serious contribution (2020 Reader's Choice Award); the 2026 Asia Minor archaeology volume raises his scholarly footprint.
**What they want more of**: peer-reviewed grounding, the archaeological evidence base, engagement with the Roland Allen lineage his endorsers repeatedly invoke.

### Segment 4: EFCA-leaning free-church evangelical pastors (~15% — values-aligned audience)

**Who**: free-church, conservative-evangelical pastors — with notably heavy EFCA presence among his endorsers (Rick Burke, ReachGlobal-EFCA; Kerry Doyal, EFCA district superintendent).
**Where they find him**: endorser word-of-mouth; ReachGlobal/EFCA equipping channels; church-hosted Master Classes.
**Why they engage**: a movement theology compatible with free-church polity and a high view of disciple-formation, without progressive or charismatic edges.
**What they want more of**: church-deployable curriculum (Seminary@YourChurch is built for this) and leadership material grounded in the Apostolic Fathers.

### Segment 5: Global South students & church planters (~10% — geographic-reach audience)

**Who**: students and planters in India, Nepal, South Korea, Romania, Africa, and S/SE Asia.
**Where they find him**: the Mission India MA; partner seminaries (AGST Nepal, Torch Trinity Seoul); oral-culture teaching modules.
**Why they engage**: contextualization is a core theme; he has equipped planters across five continents and teaches explicitly for oral cultures.
**What they want more of**: translated and oral-format material — **none catalogued yet** (the clearest format gap for this segment).

## Unmet Needs & Content Gaps

1. **An owned relationship** — the single biggest unmet need across all segments. The podcast and bestseller built trust the audience can't be re-contacted through (no newsletter of scale).
2. **A discoverable corpus** — readers of one book can't find the rest; a 17-title corpus has no canonical home.
3. **Translated / oral-format material** — the Global South segment is institutionally large but format-starved.
4. **A free / low-cost course on-ramp** — nothing routes podcast listeners and *Ephesiology* readers into the paid Master Classes pipeline.
5. **Re-surfaced academic record** — three of four journal placements are effectively invisible (titles/years UNKNOWN, not on his own properties).

## Distribution Channel Strategy

### Primary (high priority)
1. **Unified Ephesiology corpus hub** — the canonical front door for all segments; the six imprints become source links.
2. **A real newsletter** — convert lent institutional reach into an owned list (target 1,500 in 6 months, 4,000 by 12). Segmentable by interest (practitioner / seminarian / academic).
3. **The Ephesiology Podcast** — already the strongest owned channel; index it, drive ratings, cut clips.
4. **Master Classes / Seminary@YourChurch** — the LMS for the seminarian and EFCA-pastor segments (complement, never compete).

### Secondary
5. **Guest podcast circuit** — Theology in the Raw, Shifting Culture, Exponential-adjacent and DMM/CPM shows; the "church planting is not the mandate" thesis is a natural booking hook back to the hosted show.
6. **EMS / Missio Nexus / academic channels** — for the peer/validation segment.
7. **Mission India / partner-seminary funnels** — for the Global South segment (with translated/oral modules).

### Emerging
8. **Short-form Asia-Minor archaeology video** — the visual companion is unusually suited to it (Global South + practitioner reach).
9. **Narrated / audiobook formats** — fill the *Ephesiology* audio gap.

## Content Strategy Recommendations

- **Lead with consolidation, not creation.** Movemental's job is to surface and concentrate a dispersed corpus and to capture the audience the existing content already earns — not to generate new content.
- **Segment the newsletter** — practitioner (movement theology, disciple-making), seminarian (curriculum pathways), academic (the archaeology/journal record).
- **Build the free intro course** — "Introduction to Ephesiology" as top-of-funnel to the Master Classes.
- **Invest in the Global South format gap** — translated/oral modules where the institutional reach is largest and the owned content is thinnest.
- **Protect the register and the institutions** — keep the scholarly depth; aggregate-and-link East-West / Kairos / Mission India audiences, never reroute them.
