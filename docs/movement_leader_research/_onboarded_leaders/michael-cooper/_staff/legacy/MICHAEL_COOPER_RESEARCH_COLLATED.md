## Editorial bio

Michael T. Cooper is an American missiologist and missiological theologian best known for coining and developing "Ephesiology" — the study of the first-century church-planting movement centered in Ephesus — as a framework for understanding and catalyzing contemporary movements. His formation is unusually interdisciplinary: an undergraduate degree in Environmental Design from Texas A&M, an MA in Missions from Columbia International University, and a PhD in Intercultural Studies from Trinity Evangelical Divinity School focused on religious movements and early Christian history. His early scholarship was ethnographic and comparative-religion oriented — most notably field research on contemporary Druidry (book 2010; a *Missiology* journal article 2008) — before his work pivoted decisively toward New Testament movement studies and the archaeology of Asia Minor.[^kairos][^sage]

His field experience anchors his academic voice: more than thirty years in missions, including roughly ten years as a pioneer church planter in Romania after the fall of communism and several years in international development. He has equipped church planters across Africa, Europe, the Americas, South Asia, and Southeast Asia, and has lectured at the London School of Economics, the University of Bordeaux, Loyola University, and Baylor University. Earlier he directed the School of Biblical and Religious Studies at Trinity International University and served as president/CEO of an international NGO.[^laymen][^firstvoices]

What he built is a small ecosystem around the Ephesiology brand: the 2020 book *Ephesiology* (William Carey Publishing; 2020 Catalyst Services Reader's Choice Award for best book in Missiology), The Ephesiology Podcast (220+ episodes since 2019), the Ephesiology Master Classes / Seminary@YourChurch cohort-based theological-education program (in partnership with Kairos University), and the TELOS. ministry organized around three pillars — plant churches, care for the marginalized, defend the faith. His current institutional footprint centers on East West (as missiologist-in-residence), Kairos University (affiliate faculty), and Mission India Theological Seminary (program director, MA in Missiology of Movements).[^telos][^masterclasses]

| Field | Value |

| Introduced | 2020 (*Ephesiology: A Study of the Ephesian Movement*) |

"Ephesiology" reframes the Ephesian church-planting movement as a discrete object of study — explicitly *not* a commentary on Ephesians and *not* a biblical theology of missions, but "a missiological theology of the Bible" that treats Paul and John as missiological theologians who connected Jesus to the cultural narrative of Ephesus, producing a movement Cooper claims relates to roughly 40 percent of the New Testament.

### Antithesis prohibition (non-negotiable)

Do not frame mission in pragmatic, method-driven, church-planting-as-metric terms — the language of "planting churches," counting sites, or mobilizing volunteers as the goal. Cooper's most-corrected reader misreading is the assumption that church planting is the biblical mandate; his consistent correction is that "making disciples is the mandate while church planting is a result," and that "disciples multiply while volunteers keep busy." In his voice, missiology is grounded first in theology (a "missiologically theocentric" passion), never in technique divorced from disciple-formation. Derived from his quoted positions on the Patheos / Jackson Wu article ([patheos.com](https://www.patheos.com/blogs/jacksonwu/2020/10/28/is-church-planting-a-biblical-mandate/)) and *Make Disciples*.

### Distinctive claims

- Church planting is **nowhere commanded** in Scripture; it is a *result* of disciple-making, not the mandate.
- The Ephesian movement should be treated as a discrete object of study — "Ephesiology" — and as a normative model for contemporary movements.
- Roughly **40 percent** of the New Testament relates to the Ephesian movement.
- The Bible is best read as "a missiological theology" with mission at its center, rather than mission as one topic within systematic theology.
