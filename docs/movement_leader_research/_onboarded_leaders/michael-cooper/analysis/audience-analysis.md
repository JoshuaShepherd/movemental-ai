## Primary audience segments

### 1. Missionaries and church-planting practitioners — CPM-skeptical but movement-invested (core audience)

**Who**: field missionaries and church-planting practitioners who are invested in *movements* but wary of the CPM/DMM/T4T "industry" — its metrics, its jargon, its method-first posture.
**Size estimate**: UNKNOWN as a discrete number; reached substantially through East-West's missionary base (~50 countries / 10 regions) and the broader Missio Nexus / EMS practitioner world (tens of thousands of evangelical mission practitioners in aggregate; Cooper's engaged subset is far smaller).
**Where they find him**: The Ephesiology Podcast; *Ephesiology* (William Carey bestseller); Missio Nexus; the Motus Dei CPM scholar-practitioner network (Warrick Farah engages him).
**Why they engage**: he gives them a theologically serious, exegetically grounded account of movement dynamics *without* the technique-driven vocabulary they distrust — "disciples multiply while volunteers keep busy."
**What they want more of**: the biblical/archaeological grounding for movement claims; a non-pragmatic theology of multiplication; coaching that is not CPM-by-another-name.

### 2. Seminarians and degree-seekers (pipeline audience)

**Who**: students entering accredited, ministry-embedded degree programs — funneled through Kairos University, Mission India Theological Seminary (Nagpur), Knox Theological Seminary, and Nepal Ebenezer Bible College, often via non-residential cohorts.
**Size estimate**: UNKNOWN; Master Classes / Seminary@YourChurch enrollment numbers are not catalogued. The four certificate tracks ladder into five Kairos degrees, so the addressable pool is the cohort population of these partner institutions.
**Where they find him**: the Ephesiology Master Classes LMS (masterclasses.ephesiology.com); Kairos University; Mission India; Seminary@YourChurch cohorts hosted in local churches.
**Why they engage**: Cooper is the program director of an MA in Missiology of Movements and an affiliate professor; his courses are the on-ramp to accredited credentials. Netland (TEDS) expects *Ephesiology* to be "used as a text in Bible colleges."
**What they want more of**: structured laddered curriculum, oral-culture pedagogy (he teaches *Teaching in Oral Cultures*), and credentialed pathways.

### 3. Academics and missiologists (peer / validation audience)

**Who**: missiologists, intercultural-studies faculty, and NT-mission scholars in the evangelical academy.
**Size estimate**: small, high-signal; the EMS / *Missiology* / *Global Missiology* readership.
**Where they find him**: the EMS volumes; *Missiology* and *Global Missiology* book reviews (Priebe 2021 positive; Keene); Academia.edu (sfseminary.academia.edu); guest lectures at LSE, Bordeaux, Loyola, Baylor.
**Why they engage**: Ephesiology is treated as a serious contribution (2020 Catalyst Reader's Choice Award for best Missiology book); the 2026 Asia Minor archaeology volume raises his scholarly footprint.
**What they want more of**: peer-reviewed grounding, the archaeological evidence base, and engagement with the Roland Allen lineage that endorsers (Newell, Stamoolis) repeatedly invoke.

### 4. EFCA-leaning free-church evangelical pastors (values-aligned audience)

**Who**: free-church, conservative-evangelical pastors — with a notably heavy EFCA presence among his endorsers (Rick Burke, ReachGlobal-EFCA; Kerry Doyal, EFCA district superintendent).
**Size estimate**: UNKNOWN; reached through endorser networks and church-hosted Seminary@YourChurch cohorts.
**Where they find him**: endorser word-of-mouth; ReachGlobal/EFCA equipping channels; church-hosted Master Classes.
**Why they engage**: he offers a movement theology compatible with free-church polity and a high view of disciple-formation, without progressive or charismatic edges.
**What they want more of**: church-deployable curriculum (Seminary@YourChurch is built precisely for this) and leadership material grounded in the Apostolic Fathers.

### 5. Global South students and church planters (geographic-reach audience)

**Who**: students and planters in India, Nepal, South Korea, Romania, Africa, and South/Southeast Asia.
**Size estimate**: UNKNOWN; substantial institutional reach via Mission India (Nagpur), Asia Graduate School of Theology Nepal, Torch Trinity (Seoul), and Cooper's decade of Romanian church planting.
**Where they find him**: Mission India MA in Missiology of Movements; partner seminaries; oral-culture teaching modules.
**Why they engage**: contextualization is a core theme; he has equipped planters across five continents and teaches explicitly for oral cultures.
**What they want more of**: translated and oral-format material (none catalogued yet — see gap-analysis.md).

## East-West $33M base as audience substrate

A distinctive feature of Cooper's reach is that his primary institutional anchor is not his own brand. **East-West Ministries International** (Plano TX; founded 1993; ECFA member since 1999; ~50 countries / 10 regions; FY2025 revenue ~$32.97M, expenses ~$30.47M) provides a large missionary and donor substrate that his work piggybacks. As missiologist-in-residence, Cooper's teaching reaches East-West's field force and supporter base — but **he does not own that audience relationship**; East-West does. The same is true of the Kairos and Mission India student funnels. This is the central audience fact of the dossier (see gap-analysis.md): large lent institutional reach, thin owned reach.
