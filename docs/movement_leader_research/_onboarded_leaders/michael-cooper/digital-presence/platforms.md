## The dispersion inventory

Cooper's body of work lives across **a wide spread of publishing imprints, brand domains, profile sites, and distribution channels**. Crucially, his problem is **not too little content** (the failure mode of a thin-surface practitioner) — it is **dispersion**: high output scattered across many self-publishing imprints + brand sites + podcast platforms, sitting on top of a thin owned-audience layer (~120–175 social followers, 12 podcast ratings) despite ~17 books and 227 podcast episodes. The audience effect is sub-additive because no single surface concentrates either the catalog or the audience.

## What this inventory means

Three things.

1. **His problem is dispersion, not absence.** The contrast with a thin-surface practitioner is total: Cooper has the hub, the LMS, the 227-episode podcast, the accredited degree ladder, and ~17 books. But those assets are spread across **6+ publisher imprints, two brand domains, a misspelled-slug hosted blog, three retail profiles, and several podcast platforms** — so no single surface carries the whole catalog or the whole audience. Onboarding consolidates; it does not have to build from zero.

2. **The owned-audience layer is thin relative to the output.** ~17 books + 227 episodes have produced roughly **120–175 brand-social followers, 12 podcast ratings, 23 Goodreads ratings, and 0 Goodreads followers**. That is the asymmetry to name: enormous content production, minimal owned distribution. The bottleneck is audience capture (email + social + a personal creator identity), not content creation.

3. **The framework is ownable but not yet diffused.** "Ephesiology" is a category of one — Cooper coined it and uniquely holds the Ephesus/Acts 19 movement-theology niche — and it is vertically integrated (own seminary → Kairos degrees → 227-ep podcast → East-West donor anchor). That vertical integration is rare and valuable; the unrealized leverage is turning a deep-but-scattered estate into a concentrated, audience-capturing platform.
