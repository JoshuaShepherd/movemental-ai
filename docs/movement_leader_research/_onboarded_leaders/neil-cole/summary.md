# Neil Cole

Neil Cole is a church-multiplication practitioner and author whose calling has been, from the beginning, to recover the church's smallest reproducible unit — the disciple in relationship to another disciple — and to release it to multiply. Born and raised in Los Angeles in 1960, trained as a fine artist and a New Testament student, and shaped by a season as an ocean lifeguard, he left a conventional Grace Brethren pastorate in Alta Loma, California in 1999 to start a different kind of church in the smoking section of a Long Beach coffeehouse. That community, Awakening Chapel, and the organization it seeded — Church Multiplication Associates — became the seedbed for two operational technologies that have shaped a generation of planters: the Life Transformation Group, a two-or-three-person unit of Scripture, confession, and prayer, and the Organic Church Planters' Greenhouse training cycle. His conviction, repeated across his corpus, is that "the highest potential of the church is in its smallest unit." Across a remarkable nine-year publishing run (2005–2014) he became one of the most-cited North American voices in the missional, simple-church, and organic-church conversation — *Organic Church* (2005) named the category; *Church 3.0* (2010), *Search & Rescue* (2008), *Church Transfusion* (2012), and *Primal Fire* (2014) deepened it. He co-founded 100 Movements in 2015 with Alan Hirsch, Dave Rhodes, Will Mancini, and Jessie Cruickshank. Then, by his own account, God told him to stop; he kept a thirteen-year public silence and re-emerged in 2024 as an elder voice received by the next generation of microchurch leaders. His significance is that he built rapid, decentralized, low-bar/high-cost discipleship multiplication into a repeatable, exported practice — and then spent more than a decade living the anti-platform argument his books had always made.

## Calling

Cole's calling is to **make disciples who make disciples**, and to design the simplest possible structures that let that multiplication run. He is not a builder of institutions or a celebrity-pastor brand — both *Organic Leadership* (2009) and *Primal Fire* (2014) are structurally arguments against hierarchy and the concentration of leadership. His instinct is agricultural rather than industrial: church grows from a living seed that already contains its whole DNA, rather than being assembled from a kit. His irreducible operating principle, carried into Starling Initiatives, is "Listen to Jesus and do what He says."

## Body of Work

- **Discipleship technology** — the Life Transformation Group (*Cultivating a Life for God*, 1999) and the Organic Church Planters' Greenhouse (with Paul Kaak): the practical machinery of reproducible discipleship.
- **The organic-church canon** — *Organic Church* (2005), *Church 3.0* (2010), *Church Transfusion* (with Phil Helfer, 2012): naming and operationalizing decentralized, household-scale church.
- **Discipleship and leadership** — *Search & Rescue* (2008), *Ordinary Hero* (2011), *Organic Leadership* (2009), *Journeys to Significance* (2011).
- **Fivefold ministry** — *Primal Fire* (2014), his APEST book, written with four co-thinkers each carrying one of the five gifts.
- **Late, interior writing** — *Rising Tides* (2018), *Pray* (2019), *VIRAL* (2021), *Journeys* (2024), self-published under Starling Initiatives, narrating a theology of obscurity and intimacy with Christ.

## Significance

Cole is consistently cited as a bridge between the simple/organic-church stream and the global Disciple-Making Movement (DMM) / Church Planting Movement (CPM) stream; his LTG was adapted by the International Mission Board into a tool called CHAT, and practitioners like Curtis Sergeant and Steve Addison treat his work as foundational. His frameworks run parallel to and are mutually endorsed with Alan Hirsch's mDNA and APEST work, which is part of why 100 Movements could draw on both. His late-career witness — thirteen years of chosen obscurity by a high-output author — gives unusual weight to his core claim that intimacy with Christ produces fruit that platform-building cannot.
