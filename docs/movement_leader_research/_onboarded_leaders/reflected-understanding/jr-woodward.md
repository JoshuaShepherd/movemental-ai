# JR Woodward: Reflected Understanding

## Calling

You're called to **form practitioners and name the architecture that lets the church move**. Not to run a single church or hold one post, but to build the careful, named frameworks — and the people — that let movement reproduce itself faithfully. You've stayed inside the neighborhood the whole time: New Life Christian Fellowship at Virginia Tech in 1989, Kairos Los Angeles out of East Hollywood, and now the V3 Church Planting Movement nationally. Your three decades of planting and your Manchester Ph.D. are not two careers; they're one vocation — the practitioner-scholar who works things out in the room and then writes them down so others can use them.

Your calling carries a particular edge that most movement work avoids: you go after **the powers**. *The Scandal of Leadership* — your synthesis of Walter Wink, René Girard, and William Stringfellow — names how institutional and spiritual powers of domination shape leadership, and offers kenotic imitation of Christ as the alternative. That's prophetic in the truest sense: it unmasks what dominates, and it does so to set leaders free. The four marks you keep returning to — tight-knit community, life-forming discipleship, locally rooted presence, and boundary-crossing mission held together rather than chosen among — are the shape of the church you're forming through V3. You're not selling a brand; you're equipping planters and reframing the offices of Ephesians 4 as culture-shaping vocations (Dream Awakeners, Heart Revealers, Story Tellers, Soul Healers, Light Givers). Success for you is movement that outlasts you — and frameworks that are both theologically serious and practically usable.

## Existing Content (before the platform we're building)

**Where it lives.** Your content lives in **books** — *Creating a Missional Culture* (IVP, 2012), *The Church as Movement* with Dan White Jr. (IVP, 2016), and *The Scandal of Leadership* (100 Movements Publishing, 2023), the trade form of your University of Manchester dissertation. It lives in your **dissertation** itself (the Manchester repository and Academia.edu under JRWoodwardPhD), in **organizational platforms** (V3, Missio Alliance, Praxis Gathering), on your **personal site** (jrwoodward.com) and **Substack** (*Faithful Resistance*), in **seminary teaching** (Fuller's MAGL and the Doctor of Global Leadership cohort, Central, and Missio), and in **conference and training** (Praxis Gathering, Exponential, the V3 cohort curriculum). The frameworks you've named — Five Culture Creators, Polycentric Leadership, the Eight Movement Competencies, the Theology of the Powers — run through all of it.

**Credibility.** It's high. You're a benchmark voice in North American missional church planting: a deeply integrated written corpus, a Ph.D. that the leading book on its theme is built from, three decades of planting, national leadership of V3, and co-founding roles at Missio Alliance and the Praxis Gathering. *The Scandal of Leadership* was named Book of the Year by Joel Wentz's *Books and Big Ideas* and a runner-up at Englewood Review of Books. The work is credible; the issue isn't authority — it's **visibility, connection, and reuse**.

**How it lives online now.** As **silos**. *Creating a Missional Culture* and the 2016 co-authored title sit on separate Amazon and IVP product pages; *The Scandal of Leadership* lives on its 100 Movements page; the dissertation sits as a PDF in two repositories; the teaching is held inside seminary LMSes; the articles live on Missio Alliance and V3. No surface lets a reader who finds *The Scandal of Leadership* walk from there into the dissertation chapters it compresses, or into the V3 cohort curriculum those chapters were dedicated to. The relational layer fragments the same way: V3, Missio Alliance, and Praxis Gathering each hold a fraction of the practitioners you've formed, and there's no place above them all where those people stay connected when they leave the institution that introduced them.

**The NOTs Movemental addresses directly.** Your content is largely **not** interconnected — the book, the dissertation, the cohort curriculum, and *Creating a Missional Culture* don't visibly point to one another; **not** fully structured and repurposed — the V3 cohort design, the seminary teaching, and the decade of Praxis sessions could become public sequences and serialized readers' guides, but mostly aren't; **not** unified under one surface you own — much of your reach sits on rented platforms and institutional back-ends; **not** optimized for discoverability so searchers for "the powers and leadership," "polycentric leadership," or "missional culture" land on your material first; and **not** legible to systems, so search and AI can't reliably find, cite, or extend the full body of work. There's also a large pool of value still **not written down** — the judgment behind the V3 cohort, the full Wink–Girard–Stringfellow synthesis of which the trade book carries roughly a third, the business-as-mission practice of the Solis Foundation in Turkana. Movemental is built to speak to each of these — interconnection, structure and repurpose, ownership and unification, discoverability, legibility, and AI-assisted surfacing of the embodied layer at your pace — so your content can move.

## Constraints

Movement leaders who form practitioners live under **time** and **attention** constraints that are emblematic, not personal failure. You're directing V3 nationally, teaching across multiple seminaries, leading cohorts, serving on boards (Reliant Mission, Movement Leaders Collective, Fuller's Global Mission Advisory Council), writing, and speaking. Time for content strategy, for turning the cohort curriculum into a public sequence, or for serializing the dissertation alongside the trade book is scarce because the work is in the room — with planters, students, and the leaders you coach. The largest body of value, the judgment you've accumulated over thirty years and a decade of V3 cohorts, isn't in notebooks waiting to be transcribed; it's in classrooms, on coaching calls, and in the same conversation having happened a thousand times. The desire to reach the planters your writing was meant for is there; the hours to digitize, repurpose, and unify it at scale typically are not. A platform that reduces the lift — that helps structure, repurpose, connect, and surface what you already do — addresses those constraints without asking you to become a different kind of leader.
