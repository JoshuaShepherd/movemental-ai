# Liz Rios: Reflected Understanding

## Calling

You're called to **prepare a pulpit for those the field was not built for**. Not to start one more church planting network for its own sake, but to retrain the discipline itself from the margins — so that Latina, Black, and bivocational women of color don't have to plant churches inside curricula written around assumptions that were never theirs. When you say it plainly, the language is *disrupt and cultivate*: disrupt the oppressive systems and the attractional, church-growth defaults you were trained inside, and cultivate faith spaces of transformation in their place. That has been the through-line for thirty-five years — from the **Center for Emerging Female Leadership** you founded in New York (1996–2001) to mentor emerging women ministers, through your pastoring and consulting, into **Passion2Plant**, the only national BIPOC-woman-led church planting network in the United States, and into **Púlpito Fellows**, the Lilly Endowment–funded bilingual preaching fellowship you direct. Your theology gives this calling its weight: a *mujerista* and barrio theology that reasons from the lived experience of Latinas, a justice-oriented ecclesiology that treats civic engagement as constitutive of church planting rather than an add-on, and a conviction that women's leadership is an ecclesiological and eschatological question, not an HR one. You name the exclusions explicitly because you lived them — planting in a community marked by poverty, systemic neglect, and food insecurity, with no network that had prepared you for it. The calling is to make sure the next woman is prepared. You're not building a brand; you're authoring the curriculum the field forgot to write.

## Existing Content (before the platform we're building)

**Where it lives.** Your content lives in **books and chapters** — *Don't Buy The Lie* (2012), your contribution to *Need to Know* with 100 Movements Publishing (2023) alongside Alan Hirsch, Scot McKnight, Jo Saxton, and Lisa Rodriguez-Watson, the co-curated *Rhythms of Rest* with Our Daily Bread, and "The Women are Warriors" in Oxford University Press's volume on Latino religion and civic activism. It lives in **dissertations and academic work** (an EdD from Nova Southeastern, a DMin from Southeastern, a 2025 MA in Social Justice from Union). It lives in **columns** across Sojourners, Christianity Today, Influence, PCPJ, Red Letter Christians, and Religion News Service; in **long-form writing** split between your *Mujerista in Motion* Substack and your Medium archive; in **video and audio** across The Passion Center channel, the separate Púlpito Voices channel, *Cafecito Conversations*, and *Latinas of Legacy*; and in **high guest velocity** — repeat appearances on other people's podcasts and networks. Your owned teaching is distributed across three sites: elizabethrios.com, thepassioncenter.org, and passion2plant.com.

**Credibility.** It's high and well-attested. Adjunct faculty at Fuller; board member at Sojourners; executive team of the Latino Leadership Circle; senior consultant with Freedom Road; consulting editor at *Outreach Magazine*; the only woman inducted into Palm Beach Atlantic's inaugural Florida College of Preachers. The authority is not in question. The issue is **visibility, connection, and reuse**.

**How it lives online now.** As **silos**. The book sits on its Amazon page; the *Need to Know* chapter on the publisher's site; the Oxford chapter inside Oxford's catalog; the columns each on their host platform's surface; the writing split across two newsletters with no cross-post tissue; the video across two uncoordinated channels. There is no single place where "everything Liz has written and said about mujerista theology, justice-focused church planting, and the digital pulpit" is structured, linked, and findable. Púlpito Fellows artifacts — each fellow's "three digital products," the session decks, the cohort debriefs — live inside program and reporting systems rather than in any public, searchable archive. So the work doesn't **move**: it stays where it was first placed.

**The NOTs the platform addresses directly.** Your content is largely **not** consolidated under one surface you control; **not** structured and repurposed so a fellowship becomes a public sequenced course and a chapter becomes a pathway; **not** interconnected so the DMin thesis, the Sojourners column, the *Need to Know* essay, and the Púlpito curriculum visibly reference each other; **not** served by an AI that reflects your voice and corpus; **not** optimized so searchers for "Latina church planting," "mujerista theology," or "justice-focused church" find your material first; and **not** yet doing this work **bilingually**, in the English-and-Spanish register your audience actually reads in. The platform is built to speak to each of these — consolidation, structure and repurpose, interconnection, an AI that serves your voice, bilingual discoverability, and ownership — so your content can move.

## Constraints

Leaders carrying mDNA priorities live under **time** and **money** constraints that are emblematic, not personal failure. You're directing a three-year fellowship, founding and running a national planting network, teaching at Fuller, consulting, sitting on boards, and writing across multiple outlets — co-vocationally. Time for content strategy, for repurposing talks and cohort sessions into articles and courses, or for building a unified bilingual digital home is scarce because the work is in the room: in cohort calls, at Cafecito tables, in classrooms, with the Latina planters your role exists to serve. There is also the particular care your *mujerista* commitment to authenticity demands — content that represents you cannot be flattened or generic; voice and register matter. The desire to reach every leader God has given you is there; the hours to do it at scale, in both languages and every format, typically are not. A platform that reduces the lift — that helps structure, repurpose, connect, and amplify what you already do, in your own voice and your own languages — addresses those constraints without asking you to become a different kind of leader.
