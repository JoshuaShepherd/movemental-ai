## Calling

You're called to **recover a better Christian story by teaching people to read everything through the lens of Jesus**. The whole of your work expands one compressed sentence — "God looks like Jesus. And that changes everything." — into a hermeneutic, an ecclesiology, and a theology of power. Where much of the field works in the register of strategy and metrics, you work in the register of *narrative and text*: you read Scripture as a unified story climaxing in Christ, you put on "Jesus glasses" before you read a hard passage, and you trust that a scene, a confession, or a concrete image will carry a theological claim further than a proposition ever could. That is a preacher's instinct and a theologian's discipline at once. You didn't arrive at this from a desk — you pastored for more than sixteen years, at Albany Mennonite and then over a decade as Lead Pastor of Trinity Mennonite, and you went and earned the doctorate to give an account of what you had been living. In 2018 you became the first-ever recipient of the Frederick Buechner Award for Excellence in Preaching, and the lineage shows: literary, honest about doubt, quietly funny, willing to name what is genuinely hard before pressing through to something real.

Your most-repeated correction carries the whole calling: belonging is a **trajectory of motion toward Jesus**, not a position inside a boundary. The church recovered on that center is a *centered set* — "individuals and groups who would naturally repel each other will be held together when they find a center of gravity in something bigger and better." You name categories and then teach them — the Jesus lens, dialogic authority, divine gravity, cross-shaped power, the eight theses of a better story — and you ground every one of them in the conviction that the kingdom advances by self-giving love rather than coercive force, the "devil's bargain" Jesus refused in the wilderness. The prophetic content is there; the rhetorical mode stays pastoral. And in May 2025 you reorganized your public life around exactly this: you resigned a twelve-year pastorate to take a movement-level role as Resource Development Manager at Jesus Collective, where you also chair the international Theology Circle. *Divine Gravity* is literally subtitled *Sparking a Movement* — and you have now stepped into building one.

## Existing Content (before the platform we're building)

**Where it lives.** Your content lives in **books** — two commercially published Herald Press titles, *The Bible Unwrapped* (2018, foreword by Gregory Boyd, Outreach's 2019 Resource of the Year) and *Divine Gravity* (2023, foreword by Dennis R. Edwards) — plus a foreword to Boyd and Boren's *God Looks Like Jesus* (2025), the DMin dissertation *Between Breaths* in George Fox Digital Commons, and a free subscriber eBook, *Reading Scripture with Jesus*. It lives in a growing **daily video engine** — the "Reading Jesus" YouTube channel, 54+ Luke devotionals in the first months of 2026 alone. It lives in **articles** — eight *Anabaptist World* Bible columns (2020–2022), pieces in *The Other Journal* and *Missio Alliance*, and 20+ essays on your own journal at meghanlarissagood.com. It lives in **audio**, as a guest across Shifting Culture, ReKnew/Greg Boyd, *Discover the Word*'s ten-episode "Story of Scripture" panel, and more. And it lives across **institutional homes** — Jesus Collective, your AMBS Doctor of Ministry faculty role in Teaching Leadership, MennoCon addresses, and a sixteen-year preaching ministry.

**Credibility.** It's high and earned in two currencies at once — pastoral and scholarly. *Divine Gravity* carries an endorser slate — Richard Mouw, Brian McLaren, Greg Boyd, David Gushee, with Gushee naming you "a rising star of the next generation" — that punches well above the weight of a small denominational press, and the Buechner Award marks the preaching itself. You write and preach as a narrative theologian: from the biblical text, from honest human experience, and from a coherent Christ-centered frame. The issue isn't authority. It's **dispersion and discovery**.

**How it lives online now.** As **disconnected islands**. The daily "Reading Jesus" engine isn't surfaced on your own website; the website's newsletter is site-native with no public archive; the two books, the columns, the podcast appearances, the speaking, and the Jesus Collective work each sit where they were first put, with no single hub that cross-links them. A reader who finds *Divine Gravity* has no obvious path to the YouTube devotionals, the *Bible Unwrapped* study guide, or the next column. And the largest reservoir of all — sixteen-plus years of award-caliber narrative preaching — is almost entirely un-archived and undiscoverable.

**The NOTs Movemental addresses directly.** Your corpus is largely **not** consolidated into one canonical home where books, devotionals, columns, talks, and frameworks are structured, linked, and findable; **not** re-indexed so the growing "Reading Jesus" library is searchable by theme and passage; **not** connected to an AI that reads your corpus and reflects your voice; **not** optimized for discovery so searchers for "reading the Bible through Jesus," "divine gravity," or "centered-set church" land on your material first; **not** interconnected so the daily videos visibly feed the books and the newsletter; and **not** captured into an owned relationship layer above borrowed institutional reach. Movemental is built to speak to each of these — consolidation, re-indexing, a voice-true AI, discoverability, interconnection, and an owned layer — so the work can actually *move*.

## Constraints

The defining constraint here is **not production** — you are already running a daily content engine on top of two books, a column history, and a full slate of speaking and teaching. The constraint is **time, attention, and dispersion** at the exact moment your role changed. You stepped out of a settled pastorate into a movement-builder role at Jesus Collective and a faculty load at AMBS, and the hours to consolidate a scattered body of work, build a canonical index of the "Reading Jesus" library, recover a sermon archive, stand up an owned-audience layer, and interconnect every channel simply aren't there when the work itself is in the writing, the teaching, and the network. That's emblematic of a movement-level calling, not a personal failure.

Two more constraints shape every content decision. First, **register**: your voice is accessible-literary — theological depth that refuses to flatten into thin devotional content, and refuses to harden into academic stiffness. The work loses its substance if it's pushed either direction, and the humor and honest-doubt-to-conviction movement are load-bearing, not decorative. Second, **complement, never compete**: Jesus Collective, AMBS, Herald Press, and your own website are yours to keep; what's missing is the connective public-facing front door and the owned relationship layer above them, not a replacement for any of them. A platform that reduces the lift — that consolidates, surfaces, interconnects, and captures what you already produce daily, while preserving your register and respecting your institutions — addresses these constraints without asking you to become a different kind of leader.
