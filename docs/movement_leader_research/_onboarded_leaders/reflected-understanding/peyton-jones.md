# Peyton Jones: Reflected Understanding

## Calling

You're called to **recover the first-century church for a post-Christian world** — and to do it by putting disciple-making back in front of church-planting. Not to start a new method, but to retrieve an old one: the explosive, Spirit-dependent, every-member apostolic pattern of Acts that the modern church quietly tamed. Your whole body of work circles one conviction you state plainly — that "planting a church should never be our focus," because "if you plant churches, discipleship may or may not happen. Yet if you devote yourself to making disciples, churches will inevitably be planted." That's the through-line from *Church Zero* to *Reaching the Unreached* to *Church Plantology* to *Discipology*: planting is the result, never the goal.

You name the moment honestly: the post-Christian West now looks like the apostles' pre-Christian world, so "if the challenges are the same, then so are the solutions." That reframing is the heart of your calling — to send out a "new breed" of planters who read their cities the way Paul read his, who treat planting as both an **art** (contextual, culture-exegeting, creative) and a **science** (reproducible, biblical, Acts-derived). Twelve years in Wales — "the most unchurched part of the UK" — earned you the right to say it; the curriculum you wrote for NAMB, the training centers you built across North America, and the thousands of leaders you've catalyzed on three continents are how you keep saying it. You're not selling a platform persona; you're forming planters, and the language you already use — *plantology*, *discipology*, *raiders of the lost art*, *gospel penetration, saturation, and maturation* — is the right language. It stays tethered to Scripture, to the book of Acts, and to the actual cost of disciple-making before institution.

## Existing Content (before the platform we're building)

**Where it lives.** Your content lives in **books** (*Church Zero* with David C. Cook; *Reaching the Unreached* with Alan Hirsch, *Church Plantology*, and now *Discipology* with Zondervan's Exponential Series), in **NewBreed Training** (the Jump School cohort and the CityWide regional-team model), in **podcasts** (the long-running Church Planter Podcast with Pete Mitchell, and the newer Church Plantology Podcast), in **interviews and columns** (Outreach, New Churches / Send Network, Through the Word, Exponential), and in **teaching** at the graduate level, currently at Calvary University. Your personal and organizational anchor is newbreedtraining.com; the rest is distributed across publishers, podcast feeds, and the sites of organizations you've served.

**Credibility.** It's high and field-earned. *Church Plantology* is a best-selling church-planting textbook, reviewed in the *Great Commission Research Journal*; you carry an MA in Theology from W.E.S.T. in the Lloyd-Jones lineage at Sandfields; you served as Content Director for Exponential and Through the Word; and you've planted on three continents. The work is credible — the issue isn't authority, it's **visibility, connection, and reuse**.

**How it lives online now.** As **silos**. Books sit on retailer and publisher pages; podcast episodes sit in their feeds, with episode titles and dates largely uncatalogued; your video footprint is a scatter of interviews and book-release conversations rather than a structured library; your columns are spread across several outlets. There is no single place where "everything Peyton has taught about disciple-making, apostolic planting, and reaching the unreached" is gathered, linked, and easy to find. So the corpus doesn't **move** — it stays where it was first put.

**The NOTs the platform addresses directly.** Your content is largely **not** structured and repurposed to live as findable digital content (talks → articles, book themes → courses, the Jump School curriculum → a navigable path); **not** connected to a personalized AI that reflects your voice and corpus; **not** optimized for discoverability so searchers for "church planting," "disciple-making," "reaching the unreached," or "first-century church" land on your material first; **not** interconnected so the books, the podcasts, the NAMB curriculum, and the training programs visibly reference each other; and **not** owned and unified under one surface you control. The platform is built to speak to each of these — structure and repurpose, an AI that serves your voice, discoverability, interconnection, ownership, and legibility — so your content can move the way you've always wanted disciples to multiply.

## Constraints

Leaders carrying an apostolic, disciple-making priority live under **time** and **money** constraints that are emblematic, not personal failure. You're running a training organization, hosting podcasts, writing books on a steady cadence, teaching at the graduate level, and still doing the on-the-ground work of forming planters in rooms and cities. Time for content strategy — for turning a podcast back-catalog into searchable teaching, for repurposing Jump School into structured courses, for building a unified digital home — is scarce precisely because the work is with the people. The desire to reach every planter God might send you is there; the hours to make that happen at scale, across every format, typically are not. Those constraints shape every content decision: what gets recorded, what gets written, what becomes a course. A platform that reduces the lift — that helps structure, repurpose, connect, and amplify what you already do — meets those constraints without asking you to become a different kind of leader. It just helps the disciple-making multiply.
