## Calling

You're called to **form everyday missionaries by sending them out and reflecting with them** — to put mission back at the center of how the church learns, rather than treating it as a program bolted onto a settled congregation. Where much of the field trains people by front-loading information, you work in the register of *practice first*: disciples are formed by doing mission and then reflecting on it, not by acquiring propositional knowledge and hoping action follows. That conviction has a name in your work — **action-reflection discipleship** — and it runs from your Fuller dissertation *Missional Emergence* straight through the Forge America cohort curriculum and your Denver Seminary teaching weeks. It's a scholar's theory and a practitioner's habit at once. You didn't arrive at it from a desk; you planted, pastored worship, co-founded a missional coffee shop with your wife, and have been a national director of a training network, and then you went and codified what you'd been living.

Your most-repeated invitation carries the whole calling: **"joining Jesus outside the walls of the church."** That's the subtitle of *Life Out Loud* and it's also your job description — your Pulpit Rock title is literally Pastor of Missional Culture. The thesis is a recentering, not an attack on the gathered church: ordinary believers are commissioned as everyday missionaries in the places they live, work, and play, and the local congregation becomes a sending body rather than a spectator stand. You hold this with a curatorial generosity that is distinctive — when you wanted to map the church's future, you didn't write a single-author prescription; you convened fourteen voices into *Red Skies* and hosted the conversation. And you carry it into a region through **the Pando Collective**, a micro-church network nested inside a local church that connects, activates, and launches Kingdom expressions across the Front Range. Forge lineage, Hirsch and Frost upstream, Tampa Underground as a cited inspiration — you sit firmly inside the missional movement as a practitioner-scholar translating its ecclesiology into training systems and living experiments.

It lives in an **action-reflection essay** on Missio Alliance and additional bylines across V3 Church Planting and the Forge America blog. It lives in **podcast appearances** — Forge America's own Episode 73 on *Red Skies*, the Curious Friends interview, the Koinesúnē Pando conversation. It lives in **frameworks** you teach but have never trademarked — action-reflection discipleship, joining Jesus outside the walls, the Red Skies conversations, the Pando network model, business-as-mission. And it lives across **institutional roles** — National Director of Forge America, Founder/Director of Pando, Pastor of Missional Culture at Pulpit Rock, and adjunct faculty at Fuller, Denver Seminary, and Grand Canyon University.

**Credibility.** It's high and earned in the field. You hold an MA in Global Leadership and a DMiss from Fuller; *Life Out Loud* carries Michael Frost; *Red Skies* convenes Alan Hirsch, Debra Hirsch, Brian Sanders, Mark DeYmaz, Leonard Sweet and more under your editorial hand; and you teach on the national conference circuit — Exponential, Mosaix, Awakenings. You write and lead as a scholar-practitioner: from the dissertation, from the cohort, from the coffee shop and the Front-Range network. The issue isn't authority. It's **dispersion and discovery**.

**How it lives online now.** As **scattered, mostly borrowed surfaces**. Your personal site, rowlandsmith.net, is thin — a bio and a speaking page, no essay archive and no aggregated bibliography. Everything else lives on platforms you don't own: Forge America and Pando (both Wix), Pulpit Rock's sermon archive, Missio Alliance, 100 Movements, the redskiesfuture.com companion site. There's no personal podcast, no YouTube or Vimeo channel, no newsletter or email list, and no single "where to start" page that connects the two books, the essay, the talks, and the frameworks. So a genuinely movement-shaping body of work doesn't **circulate** as one corpus — it stays wherever each piece was first published.

**The NOTs Movemental addresses directly.** Your corpus is largely **not** consolidated into one canonical home where the books, the essay, the talks, and the frameworks are structured, linked, and findable; **not** re-indexed so the action-reflection material and the Red Skies conversations are searchable by theme; **not** connected to an AI that reads your corpus and reflects your voice; **not** optimized for discovery so searchers for "joining Jesus outside the walls," "action-reflection discipleship," or "micro-church network" land on your material first; **not** interconnected so books, essays, podcast appearances, and Pando experiments visibly reference each other; and **not** captured into an owned relationship layer above the institutional reach you carry. Movemental is built to speak to each of these — consolidation, re-indexing, a voice-true AI, discoverability, interconnection, and an owned layer — so your work can move.

## Constraints

The defining constraint here is **bandwidth and dispersion**, not capability. You're carrying a national director role, founding and running a regional micro-church network, pastoring missional culture at a local church, and teaching as adjunct across three institutions — all at once. The hours to consolidate a two-publisher catalogue, build a canonical bibliography and "where to start" page, inventory and index your scattered bylines and podcast appearances, stand up an owned-audience layer, and interconnect Forge, Pando, Pulpit Rock, and your own thin site simply aren't there when the work itself is in the cohort, the network, and the classroom. That's emblematic of a practitioner-scholar calling, not a personal failure.

Two more constraints shape every content decision. First, **register**: your voice is invitational and practitioner-academic — field-tested observation framed in missional-theological language, generous enough to host other voices rather than dominate them. The work loses its character if it's flattened into thin promotional content or hardened into jargon. Second, **complement, never compete**: Forge America, Pando, Pulpit Rock, 100 Movements, and your seminary homes are yours to keep; what's missing is the connective public-facing front door and the owned relationship layer above them, not a replacement for any of them. A platform that reduces the lift — that consolidates, surfaces, interconnects, and captures what you already produce, while preserving your register and respecting your institutions — addresses these constraints without asking you to become a different kind of leader.
