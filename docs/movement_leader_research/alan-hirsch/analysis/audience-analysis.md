## Audience Profile

### Who Is the Audience?

**Primary audience**: Church leaders (senior pastors, lead pastors, executive pastors) of mid-to-large Protestant churches in the US, Australia, UK, and Canada who are questioning the attractional/institutional model and seeking frameworks for missional transformation. Typically ages 35-55, seminary-educated, leading churches of 200-2,000+ members.

**Secondary audience**: Church planters, missional community leaders, and denominational strategists actively building new expressions of church. Younger (28-45), often bi-vocational, connected through networks like Exponential, Forge, Verge, and Fresh Expressions. They use Hirsch's frameworks (mDNA, APEST) as practical tools for team-building and organizational design.

**Tertiary audience**: Seminary students and academic missiologists studying missional ecclesiology, movement theory, and church leadership. They encounter Hirsch through required reading at Wheaton, Fuller, Asbury, and George Fox seminaries, and through academic citations. Also includes lay leaders and "missionally curious" Christians who discovered Hirsch through Goodreads, podcast appearances, or recommendations from their pastor.

## Audience Personas

### Persona 1: Marcus -- Senior Pastor Seeking Transformation

| Field | Value |
|-------|-------|
| Role | Senior pastor of a 600-member evangelical church (suburban US) |
| Age | 44 |
| Education | MDiv from a major evangelical seminary |
| Tradition | Non-denominational evangelical, formerly Southern Baptist |
| Discovery path | Recommended *The Forgotten Ways* by a church planting friend; later encountered Hirsch at Exponential conference |
| Primary format | Books first, then conference talks, then podcasts |
| Use case | Seeking frameworks to transition his attractional church toward missional engagement without losing the congregation |
| Pain point | Church is "successful" by traditional metrics (attendance, budget) but feels spiritually stagnant; senses that programs and Sunday services alone are not producing disciples |

**What Marcus values in Hirsch**: The mDNA and APEST frameworks give him a theological vocabulary and diagnostic tool for what feels wrong. The concept that the church is fundamentally a movement, not an institution, validates his instinct that something needs to change. He appreciates that Hirsch is not anti-church but pro-transformation.

**What Marcus struggles with**: Hirsch's books can feel academic and repetitive. Marcus wants more practical "how-to" steps. He appreciates the Forgotten Ways Handbook and Activating 5Q more than the theoretical works. He worries about how to implement these ideas without alienating his elder board.

**What James struggles with**: Some theological overreach (e.g., equating APEST with divine attributes in 5Q). The repetition across books. The lack of engagement with critical scholarly responses. He recommends Hirsch to students with caveats about exegetical rigor.

## Movemental Audience Implications

- **Content format priority**: Books and structured courses should be the anchor content on the platform. Hirsch's audience is accustomed to deep, framework-driven content and will pay for it. The 150,000+ APEST assessment users represent a built-in audience for digital training products. Video content is a major gap -- centralizing his dispersed video footprint (38+ videos across 20+ channels) onto Movemental would provide immediate differentiation.

- **Topic focus**: APEST/5Q content should be the lead offering -- it has the broadest appeal, the most engaged audience (150K+ assessments), and the most practical application. The Forgotten Ways / mDNA framework is the prestige content that establishes credibility. Discipleship and organizational transformation (Metanoia) content should follow. Communitas/liminality is a growing topic that resonates with post-COVID church leaders.

- **Cross-pollination**: Michael Frost shares the largest audience overlap and would be the highest-value companion author on the platform. Brad Brisco (if already on Movemental) would cross-link naturally through Forge and Exponential networks. Neil Cole, Hugh Halter, and Alan Roxburgh share significant audience segments. Dave Ferguson bridges into the megachurch/multisite audience that Hirsch alone does not fully reach.

- **Growth opportunity**: The biggest growth opportunity is digital -- Hirsch's audience is underserved online. No personal YouTube channel, no major free online course, no centralized video library. The 150,000+ APEST assessment users are a warm audience that could be converted to platform subscribers. International growth (UK, Australia, New Zealand, South Africa) is also significant given his origins and existing conference relationships in those markets.

- **Monetization signal**: Strong. Hirsch's audience already pays for content: books across 7 publishers, paid masterclasses on Ephesiology, paid Forge residencies, paid APEST assessments, conference registration fees ($200-500+), and consulting engagements with megachurches (Future Travelers). Seminary students pay tuition for programs he teaches in. The audience is comprised of church leaders with organizational budgets who regularly invest in leadership development. The 150,000+ APEST assessment completions at paid or registration-gated access is the strongest monetization signal -- this audience is accustomed to paying for frameworks and tools, not just consuming free content.
