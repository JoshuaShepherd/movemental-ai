# Competitive Landscape Analysis: Brad Brisco

**Research Date**: 2026-04-12
**Subject**: Brad Brisco — Director of Multiplication Strategies, NAMB/Send Network
**Primary Niche**: Covocational/bivocational church planting, missional ecclesiology
**Confidence Level**: High

---

## 1. Direct Competitors

These authors cover substantially identical territory — missional ecclesiology and church planting — with overlapping audiences.

### Lance Ford

- **Overlap**: Highest of any peer. Co-authored 4 of Brisco's 5 books. Co-founded Sentralized Conference and KC Sentral with Brisco. Shared theological framework, shared audience, shared publisher relationships.
- **Distinction**: Ford tilts more toward leadership deconstruction ("Unleader") and neighborhood-level missional practice. Has a co-authorship relationship with Alan Hirsch (Unleader, Right Here Right Now) that gives him a separate orbit.
- **Threat Level**: Low direct threat — they are collaborators, not competitors. The risk is audience confusion: readers may not distinguish their individual voices.

### Hugh Halter

- **Overlap**: Very high. Halter's "BIVO: A Modern-Day Guide for Bi-Vocational Saints" (2013) occupies nearly identical territory to Brisco's "Covocational Church Planting" (2021). Both write about incarnational community, missional living, and marketplace-integrated ministry.
- **Distinction**: Halter is a practitioner-storyteller (house painter turned church planter). His Lantern Network and Brave Cities network give him a tangible movement infrastructure. More embodied, less institutional than Brisco.
- **Books**: The Tangible Kingdom (2008), Tangible Kingdom Primer (2009), AND: The Gathered and Scattered Church (2010), BIVO (2013), Flesh (2014), Righteous Brood, Happy Hour (Life as Mission Series).
- **Threat Level**: Moderate — Halter is the closest topical competitor on bivocational ministry specifically.

### Alan Briggs

- **Overlap**: High on bivocational church planting. Briggs serves as Director of Frontline Church Planting in Colorado and Multiplying Pastor at Vanguard Church. His "Staying is the New Going" (2015) covers similar "bloom where you're planted" missional territory.
- **Distinction**: Briggs has pivoted toward leadership coaching and burnout prevention (Stay Forth Coaching, "AntiBurnout"). His audience skews younger and more entrepreneurial. His platform is more polished and coaching-oriented.
- **Books**: Staying is the New Going (2015), Everyone's a Genius (2016), Guardrails (2016), AntiBurnout (2023).
- **Threat Level**: Moderate — competing for the same church planter audience, but Briggs is moving toward coaching/wellness.

### JR Woodward

- **Overlap**: High. National Director for V3 Church Planting Movement. Co-authored "The Church as Movement" (IVP, 2016). Shares IVP's Forge line with Brisco and Ford. Both operate within missional-incarnational church planting networks.
- **Distinction**: Woodward is more academically oriented (PhD, University of Manchester). His "Creating a Missional Culture" (2012) focuses on organizational culture and APEST framework. V3 is a standalone movement infrastructure, not housed within a denomination.
- **Books**: Creating a Missional Culture (2012), The Church as Movement (2016, with Dan White Jr.), The Scandal of Leadership (2023).
- **Threat Level**: Moderate — overlapping audience, but Woodward occupies a more academic/culture-design niche.

## 2. Adjacent Voices

These authors cover related territory but with a different angle, audience, or institutional context.

### Alan Hirsch

- **Relationship**: Mentor-tier figure. Hirsch's frameworks (APEST, movements, missional-incarnational) are the theological scaffolding Brisco builds upon. Hirsch wrote the foreword for several Forge Partnership books including those by Ford/Brisco.
- **Distinction**: Hirsch operates at the meta-framework level (movements theory, fivefold ministry). Brisco applies those frameworks to the specific context of covocational church planting within the SBC. Hirsch is international; Brisco is North American/denominational.
- **Books**: 9+ books including The Forgotten Ways (2006), 5Q (2017), Reframation (2019).
- **Digital Presence**: alanhirsch.org, 100Movements, Forge, 5Q Collective. Multiple organizational platforms. Moderate social media presence.
- **Threat Level**: None — Hirsch is upstream, not a competitor.

### Michael Frost

- **Relationship**: Adjacent thought leader. Frost co-wrote "The Shaping of Things to Come" with Hirsch, which is foundational to the entire missional-incarnational stream Brisco operates within. Frost's "Surprise the World" (2016) is the practical missional primer Brisco's audience also reads.
- **Distinction**: Frost is Australian, operates globally from Morling College's Tinsley Institute. He is more prolific (20+ books), more provocative ("Exiles"), and has broader name recognition. He is a missiologist-agitator; Brisco is a missiologist-strategist.
- **Books**: 20+ including The Shaping of Things to Come (2003), Exiles (2006), Surprise the World (2016).
- **Digital Presence**: mikefrost.net. Moving from X to Bluesky. International conference circuit. Required reading in seminaries worldwide.
- **Threat Level**: Low — Frost is a tier above in visibility and breadth.

### Ed Stetzer

- **Relationship**: Complex. Stetzer wrote the foreword for "The Missional Quest" (Ford/Brisco) and has been a champion of the missional movement from within evangelical institutional structures. Both serve the church planting practitioner.
- **Distinction**: Stetzer is a research-driven public intellectual with massive institutional reach (Dean at Talbot/Biola, Editor-in-Chief of Outreach Magazine, Moody Radio host, Lausanne regional director). He covers church planting but also culture, politics, and evangelicalism broadly. He is a generalist where Brisco is a specialist.
- **Books**: 12+ including Planting Missional Churches (2006, 2016 2nd ed.), Breaking the Missional Code (2006), Christians in the Age of Outrage (2018).
- **Digital Presence**: Strongest of any peer. @edstetzer on X (100K+ followers est.), Outreach Magazine platform, Moody Radio, ChurchLeaders.com. Institutional megaphone.
- **Threat Level**: Low as direct competitor (different scale) but high as attention competitor — Stetzer can absorb the oxygen on any church planting topic.

### Dave Ferguson

- **Relationship**: Adjacent. Ferguson operates the church multiplication infrastructure (Exponential, formerly NewThing) that Brisco's church planters plug into. Ferguson's lens is multiplication systems and leadership development.
- **Distinction**: Ferguson is a mega-church pastor (Community Christian Church) turned movement CEO. His 2025 merger of NewThing into Exponential makes him the gatekeeper of the largest church planting conference ecosystem (~3,000 churches in network). More organizational/systems, less theological/missional.
- **Books**: 8+ including Exponential (2010), Hero Maker (2018), B.L.E.S.S. (2021).
- **Digital Presence**: daveferguson.org, Exponential.org platform. Strong conference circuit presence.
- **Threat Level**: Low direct, but Ferguson's platform can elevate or sideline any voice in the church planting space.

### Neil Cole

- **Relationship**: Parallel track. Cole's "Organic Church" movement pioneered the house church / simple church model that overlaps with Brisco's covocational emphasis on smaller, less-resourced church forms.
- **Distinction**: Cole is more radical — his model is fully decentralized, house-church focused, with no paid clergy as a starting assumption. Brisco works within denominational structures; Cole works outside them. Cole's Church Multiplication Associates claims 700+ churches in 32 states and 23 nations.
- **Books**: 10+ including Organic Church (2005), Church 3.0 (2010), Organic Leadership (2009), Primal Fire (2014).
- **Digital Presence**: Modest. Blog-based. Lower institutional profile than peak influence years (2005-2012).
- **Threat Level**: Low — Cole's audience has shifted to a more radical fringe.

### Mike Breen

- **Relationship**: Tangential. Breen's discipleship-first approach (Building a Discipling Culture) feeds the same pipeline — you need disciples before you can plant churches. But his framework is more structured (LifeShapes, Huddles, 3DM system).
- **Distinction**: Breen built a proprietary discipleship system (3DM, now rebranded "Discipling Culture") with its own publishing arm, training pipeline, and certification. More systemized and franchise-like than the organic missional stream.
- **Books**: 6+ including Building a Discipling Culture (3 editions), Multiplying Missional Leaders, Family on Mission.
- **Digital Presence**: 3dmovements.com, 3dmpublishing.com. Has a PhD from Trinity International University. Platform is training-system focused.
- **Threat Level**: Low — different system, different audience segment (more megachurch-adjacent).

### Jeff Vanderstelt

- **Relationship**: Parallel. Vanderstelt's "Saturate" framework (missional communities, gospel fluency, everyday discipleship) overlaps substantially with Brisco's missional ecclesiology.
- **Distinction**: Vanderstelt is a church pastor (Doxa Church, Bellevue WA) with a tighter theological framework (gospel-centered, more Reformed). His Soma Family of Churches is a network of missional communities. More gospel-identity focused; Brisco is more missiology/strategy focused.
- **Books**: Saturate (2015), Gospel Fluency (2017), Making Space (2019).
- **Digital Presence**: saturatetheworld.com. The Saturate platform includes training resources, podcast, and field guides.
- **Threat Level**: Low-moderate — overlapping audience, but Vanderstelt's Reformed theological home is different from Brisco's SBC/post-Christendom positioning.

### Reggie McNeal

- **Relationship**: Senior statesman. McNeal's "Missional Renaissance" (2009) and "The Present Future" (2003) were early prophetic voices in the missional shift. His "Kingdom Collaborators" (2018) covers territory adjacent to Brisco's covocational emphasis.
- **Distinction**: McNeal is a consultant and speaker, not a church planter. He diagnoses the problem; Brisco prescribes the treatment. McNeal is more Kingdom-theology oriented; Brisco is more practice-and-strategy oriented.
- **Books**: 8+ including The Present Future (2003), Missional Renaissance (2009), Kingdom Come (2011), Kingdom Collaborators (2018).
- **Digital Presence**: reggiemcneal.org. Consulting-oriented. Lower social media profile.
- **Threat Level**: None — McNeal is a complementary upstream voice.

## 4. Differentiation: What Makes Brisco Unique

### The Institutional-Missional Bridge

Brisco occupies a position no other peer holds: he is the **only voice who is simultaneously embedded in a major denominational institution (NAMB/SBC) and fluent in post-Christendom missional theology (Newbigin, Frost, Hirsch)**. This is rare and strategically valuable.

- **Hirsch, Frost, Cole, Halter** operate outside denominational structures
- **Stetzer** has moved toward broader evangelical public intellectualism
- **Ferguson** runs a para-church network
- **Breen** built a proprietary system

Brisco translates radical missional theology into language and strategy that denominational church planters can actually implement within their institutional context.

### The Covocational Specialist

"Covocational Church Planting" (2021) is his solo breakout work and his sharpest differentiator. While Halter's "BIVO" (2013) told the story, Brisco's work provides the **strategic framework and theological rationale** for marketplace-integrated church planting within a denominational system. Key distinctions:

- **Halter** = bivocational as personal testimony and lifestyle
- **Brisco** = covocational as institutional strategy and missiological conviction
- The reframing from "bi-vocational" (two jobs) to "co-vocational" (integrated calling) is a linguistic and theological innovation Brisco owns

### The Practitioner-Scholar Balance

Brisco holds a doctorate in missional ecclesiology focused on congregational transition. He has 18+ years of college teaching and 20+ years of church planting. This gives him credibility in both academic and practitioner settings that most peers lack in combination:

- **Woodward** has the PhD but less denominational infrastructure
- **Stetzer** has institutional reach but has moved away from hands-on planting
- **Halter** has practice credibility but less academic weight
- **Ford** has the practice but relies on Brisco for the theological scaffolding

### Strategic Sequencing Recommendation

**Phase 1 (Foundation)**: Brisco + Hirsch + Ford on the platform together. This creates the strongest content ecosystem — the theorist, the strategist, and the co-author/practitioner. Natural cross-linking, shared vocabulary, established trust.

**Phase 2 (Expansion)**: Add Woodward, Frost, McNeal. These voices expand the theological range without competing for Brisco's specific niche. They create learning pathways and attract adjacent audiences.

**Phase 3 (Careful Addition)**: Consider Halter if the platform can curate content to prevent audience confusion. Position Halter as "the story" and Brisco as "the strategy" for the same movement.

**Avoid**: Bringing Stetzer or Ferguson onto the platform primarily to amplify Brisco — their audience scale would dwarf Brisco's niche. Better to leverage their platforms for outbound promotion of Brisco's Movemental content.

## 7. Key Takeaway

Brad Brisco's competitive advantage is not breadth or platform size — on those metrics, he trails most peers. His advantage is **specificity and positioning**: he is the only voice translating post-Christendom missional theology into actionable strategy for denominational church planters, with the institutional credibility of NAMB behind him and the scholarly depth of a doctorate in missional ecclesiology. 

Movemental's opportunity is to give Brisco the digital infrastructure he currently lacks — unified content hub, course delivery, audience building tools, and network amplification — so that his unique positioning can reach the audience that already needs his message but cannot find it through his fragmented current presence.
