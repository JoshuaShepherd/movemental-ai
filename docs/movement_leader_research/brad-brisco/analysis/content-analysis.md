## Executive Summary

Brad Brisco is a practitioner-theologian whose work centers on a single animating conviction: the church is missionary by nature, not merely by activity, because it participates in the mission of a missionary God (missio Dei). His distinctive contribution is the **covocational framework** -- a theological and practical reframing of marketplace work as a primary vehicle for mission rather than a compromise or stepping stone to fully-funded ministry. Operating at the intersection of Southern Baptist institutional structures and the broader missional-incarnational movement, Brisco serves as a bridge figure who translates post-Christendom missiology into accessible, actionable paradigms for evangelical church planters. His body of work progressively moves from theoretical foundations (missional ecclesiology) to embodied practice (neighboring, covocational planting), always structured around named paradigm shifts that challenge inherited assumptions.

## Named Frameworks & Models

### Covocational vs. Bivocational Distinction

| Field | Value |
|-------|-------|
| Introduced in | *Covocational Church Planting* (2021); earlier articles at NAMB/Send Network |
| What it describes | A reframing of marketplace-engaged ministry from compromise to calling |
| Components | Bivocational = second job to supplement church salary, with intent to go full-time; Covocational = marketplace calling IS the primary vocation, church planting is integrated, no intent to leave the marketplace |
| Visual | No (conceptual distinction) |
| Adoption | Growing -- adopted by NAMB/Send Network, Missio Alliance, multiple podcast hosts frame conversations using this term |

**How it works**: Brisco argues that the traditional "bivocational" label carries an implicit hierarchy: pastoral ministry is the real calling, marketplace work is a necessary compromise. The "covocational" reframing eliminates this hierarchy. A covocational planter sees their marketplace role as equally sacred and strategically essential -- it provides incarnational access to people and places that a fully-funded pastor cannot reach. The framework is both theological (all vocation is sacred) and strategic (marketplace presence is a missionary advantage, not a limitation).

### Three Theological Foundations of Missional

| Field | Value |
|-------|-------|
| Introduced in | Exponential East 2016 talk, *ReThink* eBook |
| What it describes | The theological bedrock of missional ecclesiology |
| Components | (1) The missionary nature of God and the church; (2) Incarnational mission; (3) Participation in the missio Dei |
| Visual | No |
| Adoption | Used within NAMB/Send Network training contexts |

**How it works**: This framework provides the theological scaffolding for all of Brisco's practical work. Foundation 1 establishes that mission is ontological (the church IS missionary), not functional (the church DOES mission). Foundation 2 grounds mission in the incarnation -- God entered a specific place and culture, so must the church. Foundation 3 reorders agency -- the church joins God's existing mission rather than asking God to bless the church's plans. These three foundations cascade into every practical framework Brisco offers.

### 9 Key Paradigm Shifts (later expanded to 12 Missiological Principles)

| Field | Value |
|-------|-------|
| Introduced in | *ReThink* eBook (2018, Send Institute/NAMB) |
| What it describes | The mental model changes required to activate a church for mission |
| Components | (1) The Need to ReThink; (2) ReThink the Nature of the Church: From Church Served to Church Sent; (3) ReThink Mission: From Excarnate Mission to Incarnate Mission; (4) ReThink the Missio Dei: From Church with a Mission to Mission with a Church; (5) ReThink Culture: From Place of Privilege to Posture of a Servant; (6) ReThink Vocation: From Secular Vocations to Sacred Callings; plus 3 additional shifts (7-9) |
| Visual | No (list/framework) |

**How it works**: Each paradigm shift follows a consistent structure: "From [inherited assumption] to [missional alternative]." This rhetorical pattern makes the shifts memorable and actionable. The framework is designed for use in leadership retreats, church planting cohorts, and denominational training contexts. The expansion from 9 to 12 principles suggests ongoing refinement as Brisco applies the framework to new contexts.

| Field | Value |

Together they form a diagnostic: the church must change both because its assumptions are wrong (paradigm shifts) and because the world around it has changed (cultural shifts). This dual framework gives Brisco a comprehensive change narrative.

### Missional Community Model

| Field | Value |
|-------|-------|
| Introduced in | "What is a Missional Community?" blog post; NAMB article on missional communities and church planting |
| What it describes | A committed group of 12-25 Jesus followers participating in God's mission in a specific neighborhood or network |
| Components | Extended family size (12-25), Spirit-empowered, place-based, mission-oriented |
| Visual | No |
| Adoption | Moderate -- part of broader missional community movement (Soma, 3DM, etc.); Brisco's version is less programmatic |

**How it works**: Brisco positions the missional community as the basic unit of incarnational mission. It is larger than a small group (which tends to be inward-focused) but smaller than a congregation (which tends to be program-driven). The 12-25 size mirrors the "extended family" social unit, enabling genuine relational depth while maintaining enough diversity for outward engagement. The community is organized around a shared place (neighborhood) or network (workplace, school), not around a shared program.

### Missional Engagement Strategy

| Field | Value |
|-------|-------|
| Introduced in | "Developing a Missional Engagement Strategy" (New Churches article) |
| What it describes | A sequenced approach to helping churches become missional, starting with identity before programs |
| Components | Begins with helping people see themselves as sent, missionary people before launching programs |
| Visual | No |
| Adoption | NAMB/Send Network training contexts |

**How it works**: This framework addresses a common implementation failure: churches that adopt "missional" programs without undergoing the identity shift that makes those programs authentic. Brisco's strategy starts with theology (who are we?) before moving to strategy (what do we do?), ensuring that missional activities flow from missionary identity rather than becoming another church program bolted onto an attractional framework.

### Centered-Set vs. Bounded-Set Ecclesiology

| Field | Value |
|-------|-------|
| Introduced in | "Centered-Set Approach" blog post; Into the Harvest podcast #234 |
| What it describes | A model for understanding belonging and discipleship based on orientation (moving toward Jesus) rather than boundary markers (in/out categories) |
| Components | Bounded-set = clear boundary, you're in or out; Centered-set = defined center (Jesus), people are moving toward or away |
| Visual | Yes (classic set theory diagrams) |
| Adoption | Widely used concept (originated with Paul Hiebert); Brisco applies it specifically to missional ecclesiology and discipleship |

**How it works**: Brisco applies Hiebert's anthropological set theory to ecclesiology. A bounded-set church defines itself by who is in and who is out (membership, doctrinal boundaries). A centered-set community defines itself by its center (Jesus) and asks whether people are moving toward that center. This framework supports Brisco's broader project of reducing the clergy-laity divide and creating more porous, mission-oriented communities.

### Has their thinking shifted?

Brisco's thinking has not so much shifted as deepened and synthesized. His core conviction -- that the church is missionary by nature -- has remained constant since his 2003 encounter with Frost and Hirsch. What has evolved is the scope and specificity of application. Early work (2012-2013) focused on making missional theology accessible to existing churches. The middle period (2016) applied it to neighborhoods and place. The mature period (2018-2021) systematized it into paradigm shift frameworks and extended it into the covocational space, which represents the fullest integration of his pre-ministry experience with his theological convictions.

The most notable evolution is from observer to advocate on the covocational question. Early NAMB writings used "bivocational" language; by 2021 he had developed a fully articulated theological and practical case for "covocational" as a distinct and preferred category. This terminological shift reflects a deeper theological conviction: that marketplace presence is not a concession to economic reality but a strategic and sacred advantage for mission.

His most recent work (2024-2025) shows emerging interest in centered-set discipleship models and engagement with digital ecclesiology questions, suggesting the next phase of his thinking may involve applying missional-incarnational frameworks to post-institutional and digitally-mediated church forms.

## Unique Contribution

### What only this author says

- **The covocational distinction**: While many discuss bivocational ministry, Brisco is the primary voice arguing that marketplace calling and church planting calling are co-equal vocations (not primary and secondary). The terminological reframing from "bi-" to "co-" carries the entire theological argument.

- **Missional theology inside SBC structures**: Brisco is distinctive in operating as a missional-incarnational voice from within the Southern Baptist Convention's institutional apparatus (NAMB/Send Network). Most missional theologians operate outside or alongside denominational structures; Brisco works within one of the largest, translating post-Christendom missiology into the language and systems of mainstream evangelical church planting.

- **The three-move argument pattern applied systematically**: While others make individual missional arguments, Brisco has created a comprehensive system of named paradigm shifts (9/12) that can be taught sequentially in training contexts. This pedagogical structuring makes his work uniquely deployable for denominational and network training.

### Where they disagree with peers

| Topic | Their Position | Mainstream Position |
|-------|---------------|-------------------|
| Bivocational ministry | Covocational is preferred -- marketplace work is a primary calling, not a compromise | Bivocational is a pragmatic necessity for smaller or under-resourced churches; fully-funded is the ideal |
| Church planting launch strategy | Start with missional community life and incarnational presence, not with a Sunday worship launch | Launch Sunday is the primary milestone; gather a core team and launch public worship as quickly as possible |
| Clergy-laity distinction | All believers are missionaries; professional pastoral ministry can hinder the church's missionary nature | Trained, ordained pastoral leadership is essential for healthy churches |
| North America as mission context | Post-Christendom reality -- North America is a mission field requiring missionary posture | North America is a Christian nation experiencing cultural decline that can be reversed through church revitalization |
| Church as institution vs. movement | The church is a missionary community first; institutional expressions should serve mission, not the reverse | The local church institution is the primary vehicle for God's work; strengthen the institution |

### What would be lost without their work

Without Brisco's work, the missional-incarnational conversation would remain largely outside mainstream evangelical church planting structures. His role as a bridge figure -- translating Newbigin, Bosch, Frost, and Hirsch into NAMB training materials and denominational strategy -- is irreplaceable. The covocational concept would likely remain unnamed and untheologized, leaving marketplace-engaged church planters without a positive theological identity for their calling.

## Content Interconnections

### How themes connect across works

Brisco's body of work follows a clear logical progression: **identity** (who is God? who is the church?) leads to **theology** (what is mission?) leads to **place** (where does mission happen?) leads to **practice** (how do we do it?).

- *Missional Essentials* (2012) provides the foundational vocabulary and concepts -- a 12-week on-ramp to missional thinking.
- *The Missional Quest* (2013) addresses sustainability -- how to be a missional church "for the long run" without burning out on idealism.
- *Next Door as It Is in Heaven* (2016) localizes the theology -- mission happens in your neighborhood, through hospitality, third places, and incarnational presence.
- *ReThink* (2018) systematizes the change framework -- nine specific paradigm shifts that churches and planters must undergo.
- *Covocational Church Planting* (2021) integrates everything -- missional theology + incarnational presence + marketplace calling = a complete model for church planting that starts with who you are and where you already are.

Each book assumes and builds on the previous work. Articles and podcast appearances reinforce and extend specific themes from the books. The CGGC Triennial keynotes (2022) appear to be a comprehensive oral presentation of the *ReThink* framework.

### Recommended reading order

| Order | Work | Why this sequence |
|-------|------|------------------|
| 1 | *Missional Essentials* (2012) | Foundational vocabulary and concepts; designed for group study; low barrier to entry |
| 2 | *ReThink* (2018) | Free eBook; systematizes the paradigm shifts; provides the intellectual framework for everything else |
| 3 | *The Missional Quest* (2013) | Addresses sustainability and long-term commitment; prevents the burnout that often follows missional enthusiasm |
| 4 | *Next Door as It Is in Heaven* (2016) | Applies theology to place; practical neighborhood engagement; the "how" of incarnational presence |
| 5 | *Covocational Church Planting* (2021) | The synthesis; best read after the theological foundations are in place; the most mature and distinctive work |

## Movemental Content Strategy Implications

- **Pillar mapping**: Brisco serves the **Missional Ecclesiology**, **Church Planting**, and **Vocation/Marketplace Ministry** content pillars. His work also feeds into **Discipleship** and **Neighborhood Engagement** categories.

- **Cross-author connections**: Strong alignment with **Alan Hirsch** (APEST, apostolic movement, missional-incarnational framework -- Hirsch is a direct upstream influence). Natural pairing with **Lance Ford** (co-author on 3 books). Conceptual overlap with **Michael Frost** (incarnational theology) and **Ed Stetzer** (church planting research). Potential contrast/complement with authors who represent more institutional or attractional ecclesiology.

- **Content gaps**: Brisco has not yet produced content on: (1) covocational ministry for **women** church planters specifically; (2) covocational models in **non-Western/global** contexts; (3) the intersection of covocational planting with **bi-racial/multi-ethnic** church contexts; (4) a systematic **theology of work** beyond the church planting application; (5) **digital/hybrid** expressions of missional community (his Church Digital podcast appearance was exploratory, not definitive).

- **Series potential**: (1) "9 Paradigm Shifts" -- each shift as a standalone lesson/article/video; (2) "Covocational Foundations" -- theology of vocation + practical marketplace ministry; (3) "Neighboring" -- practical series drawn from *Next Door as It Is in Heaven* chapters; (4) "From Bivocational to Covocational" -- identity shift series for marketplace ministers; (5) "Post-Christendom Church" -- cultural analysis and strategic response.

- **Searchable concepts**: missional church, missional ecclesiology, covocational, bivocational church planting, incarnational presence, missio Dei, missional community, neighborhood engagement, theology of place, paradigm shifts, post-Christendom, centered-set vs. bounded-set, APEST, church multiplication, marketplace ministry, sacred vocation, third places, missional discipleship, church as missionary.
