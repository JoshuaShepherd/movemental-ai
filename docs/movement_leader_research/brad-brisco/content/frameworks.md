### Covocational Church Planting

| Field | Value |
|-------|-------|
| Introduced | 2021 (*Covocational Church Planting*); earlier articles at NAMB/Send Network |
| Components | Bivocational = a second job to supplement a church salary, with intent to go full-time; Covocational = the marketplace calling IS the primary vocation, church planting is integrated, with no intent to leave the marketplace |
| Visual / model | — (conceptual distinction; no published diagram located) |
| Adoption evidence | Adopted by NAMB/Send Network (free bilingual e-book), Missio Alliance, multiple podcast hosts who frame conversations using the term; on the Wheaton Billy Graham Center church-planting reading list |
| Source | [Amazon — Covocational Church Planting](https://www.amazon.com/Covocational-Church-Planting-Aligning-Marketplace/dp/1736282115), [missioalliance.org](https://www.missioalliance.org/the-welcome-rise-of-co-vocational-church-planters/) |

The traditional "bivocational" label carries an implicit hierarchy — pastoral ministry is the real calling, marketplace work a necessary compromise. Brisco's "covocational" reframing eliminates that hierarchy: marketplace presence is a strategic and sacred missionary advantage, not a limitation. The reframing from "bi-" to "co-" carries the entire theological argument.[^contentanalysis]

| Field | Value |
|-------|-------|
| Introduced | Exponential East 2016 talk; *ReThink* e-book |
| Components | (1) The missionary nature of God and the church; (2) Incarnational mission; (3) Participation in the missio Dei |
| Visual / model | — |
| Adoption evidence | Used within NAMB/Send Network training contexts |
| Source | [exponential.org/missional-ecclesiology](https://exponential.org/missional-ecclesiology/) |

The theological scaffolding under all of Brisco's practical work: mission is ontological (the church IS missionary), grounded in the incarnation, and the church joins God's existing mission rather than asking God to bless the church's plans.[^contentanalysis]

### Missional Community Model

| Field | Value |
|-------|-------|
| Introduced | "What is a Missional Community?" blog post; NAMB article on missional communities and church planting |
| Components | A committed group of 12–25 Jesus followers, Spirit-empowered, place-based, mission-oriented |
| Visual / model | — |
| Adoption evidence | Part of the broader missional-community movement (Soma, 3DM); Brisco's version is less programmatic |
| Source | [missionalchurchnetwork.com](https://www.missionalchurchnetwork.com/blog/what-is-a-missional-community) |

The basic unit of incarnational mission — larger than an inward-focused small group, smaller than a program-driven congregation — sized to the "extended family" social unit and organized around a shared place or network rather than a shared program.[^contentanalysis]

### Centered-Set vs. Bounded-Set Ecclesiology

| Field | Value |
|-------|-------|
| Introduced | "Centered-Set Approach" blog post; Into the Harvest podcast #234 (2025) |
| Components | Bounded-set = clear boundary, you're in or out; Centered-set = defined center (Jesus), people moving toward or away |
| Visual / model | Yes — classic set-theory diagrams |
| Adoption evidence | Widely-used concept originating with Paul Hiebert; Brisco applies it to missional ecclesiology and discipleship |
| Source | [missionalchurchnetwork.com](https://www.missionalchurchnetwork.com/blog/bounded-set-vs-centered-set-1), [intotheharvest.org/pod234](https://www.intotheharvest.org/pod234/) |

Brisco applies Hiebert's anthropological set theory to ecclesiology: a centered-set community defines itself by its center (Jesus) and asks whether people are moving toward it, supporting his broader project of reducing the clergy-laity divide.[^contentanalysis]

(Two additional named diagnostics — the **10 Crucial Cultural Shifts** and the **Missional Engagement Strategy** — appear in his blog and New Churches articles; both are training-context frameworks without published adoption metrics.[^contentanalysis])
