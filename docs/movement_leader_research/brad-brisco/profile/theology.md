## Core Thesis

The church is not an institution that sends missionaries — the church IS missionary by nature, because it is rooted in the nature of a missionary God. Therefore, every believer is a sent person, every neighborhood is a mission field, and every vocation is a context for incarnational mission.

## Theological Tradition

**Primary**: Missional-Incarnational ecclesiology, rooted in the missio Dei tradition
**Secondary influences**: Anabaptist-influenced praxis (through Frost/Hirsch), evangelical church planting tradition (SBC/NAMB context), post-Christendom missiology

Brisco operates within the Southern Baptist Convention's institutional structure (NAMB/Send Network) while drawing heavily from the broader missional movement that crosses denominational lines. This positions him as a bridge figure — bringing missional theology into mainstream evangelical church planting practice.

## Distinctive Positions

- **The church IS missionary, not just a sending body**: The church's nature — not just its activity — is fundamentally missionary. This is rooted in the missionary nature of God himself (missio Dei).
- **Covocational > Bivocational**: Brisco elevates the covocational model over the traditional bivocational framing. The marketplace calling is not a compromise or stepping stone — it is a primary context for mission.
- **Post-Christendom awareness**: North America is a missionary context, not a Christian nation. Church strategy must shift accordingly.
- **Incarnational presence over attractional models**: The heart of mission is staying and embedding in a place, not attracting people to a location.
- **Start with missionary behaviors, not Sunday services**: Church plants should begin with missional community life and incarnational activity, not with launching a worship gathering.
- **Diminish the clergy-laity divide**: All believers are missionaries; the professional pastor model can actually hinder the church's missionary nature.
- **Theology of Place**: Where you live matters theologically. Neighborhood engagement is not optional outreach — it is participation in the missio Dei.
- **Robust Christology as foundation**: A "robust understanding of Jesus" is essential for church life and missional engagement.

## Theological Evolution

Brisco's theological journey tracks from conventional church planting within evangelical structures to a missional-incarnational paradigm. The pivotal moment was his 2003 encounter with Frost and Hirsch's "The Shaping of Things to Come," which he describes as fundamentally changing his thinking about church, mission, and church planting.

His career trajectory mirrors this evolution: from restaurant industry, to seminary, to conventional church planting strategy, to missional ecclesiology (doctoral work), to national missional strategy (NAMB/Send Network). His role titles at NAMB shifted from "Director of Bivocational Church Planting" to "Director of Multiplication Strategies" — reflecting a broadening focus.

His most recent theological development is the covocational framework, which represents the synthesis of his pre-ministry marketplace experience with his missional ecclesiology. The publication of "Covocational Church Planting" (2021) marks the maturation of an idea that had been gestating since his restaurant-industry days: that the marketplace is not a lesser calling but a primary mission field.
