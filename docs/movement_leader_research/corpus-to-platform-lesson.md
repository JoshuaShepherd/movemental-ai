# When Your Work Goes Live: A Lesson from Alan Hirsch

**For movement leaders, co-laborers, and anyone preparing to launch a unified platform**

*Based on the Alan Hirsch research dossier and baseline analysis (movemental-ai, April 2026). Alan is the teaching example; the lesson applies to any leader whose influence has outrun their digital home.*

---

## The question this lesson answers

You have spent decades writing, speaking, founding organizations, and shaping a field. A platform is about to go live — articles, books, courses, assessments, perhaps tools that help people find and follow your work in one place.

The question is not whether your work matters. It already does.

The question is: **What actually changes when the corpus becomes retrievable?** How should you think about reach, growth, and the size of the audience you can serve — without confusing fame in the room with findability on the web, or mistaking a launch for instant multiplication?

This lesson uses Alan Hirsch as the case study because his dossier makes the pattern unusually visible. Brad Brisco, Dave Ferguson, Michael Frost, and others in the same ecosystem will recognize the same shape in their own work, at different scales.

---

## Part 1: Two kinds of influence — and why both matter

Most movement leaders live with a split they feel but rarely name.

**Embodied influence** is what happens in rooms: conferences, cohorts, consulting engagements, seminary classes, the book someone passes to a planter, the conversation after a talk. It is relational, deep, and hard to scale. It is also where trust is built. People do not give their lives to a framework because of a Google result. They give their lives because someone they trust embodied it.

**Retrievable influence** is what happens when your work can be *found*: searched, linked, read in sequence, cited by another leader, surfaced when someone asks an AI assistant a question, discovered by a pastor in Manila who never met you at Exponential. It is shallower at first contact — but it is how movements multiply beyond your calendar.

Alan Hirsch has spent thirty-plus years accumulating embodied influence at an exceptional level. Twenty books across seven publishers. Seven organizations founded or co-founded. Adjunct faculty at four seminaries. More than 150,000 people have taken his APEST assessment. He is, by any honest measure, a gravitational center of the missional movement.

And yet the research describes something startling: **almost none of that embodied weight has been converted into a retrievable system.**

His books live on Amazon, Goodreads, and publisher sites — indexed as products, not as an interconnected body of thought. His articles sit on Saturate, Verge, Christianity Today, and a Squarespace blog — useful pieces, but not a connected library. His videos are scattered across more than twenty YouTube channels; he has no channel of his own. His assessment lives on a separate domain; when someone finishes, they get a score and, for most, no clear next step. His email list is roughly 1,500 subscribers on Substack — about 4% of his Twitter following, when a well-run list often converts 15–25% of social reach into owned contact.

The fragmentation story is not that Alan lacks content. **The fragmentation story is that the content does not talk to itself — and the people searching for what he has already written often find someone else's summary instead.**

That gap — between embodied authority and retrievable authority — is the subject of this lesson.

## Part 2: Alan as the teaching example — what the dossier actually shows

Before launch, the Alan Hirsch research paints a precise picture.

### What is already proven

The dossier does not ask us to guess whether there is demand for missional thinking, APEST, or movement DNA. The demand is documented:

- **150,000+ APEST completions** — people who voluntarily spent fifteen to thirty minutes engaging with his framework. That is behavioral proof, not theoretical interest.
- **16,762 Goodreads ratings across 92 editions** — which, by the standard industry heuristic that only 1–5% of readers leave ratings, implies somewhere between roughly 335,000 and 1.6 million people have read his books over twenty-one years.
- **A co-authorship and endorsement network** that touches Michael Frost, Dave Ferguson, Tim Catchim, Lance Ford, Rob Wegner, and dozens of others — the organic scenius of the field.
- **Seminary adoption, conference circuits, organizational training** — Forge, 5Q, 100 Movements, Future Travelers — all generating embodied outcomes.

Triangulating those signals, a careful estimate puts Alan's **currently realized audience** — people meaningfully formed by his work, not just aware of his name — in the range of **50,000 to 80,000**. That is already a substantial movement-sized community.

### What is latent but unreachable

The same dossier identifies a much larger **latent audience**: people who would engage deeply if they could find the work, follow a path through it, or encounter it in their language and format.

The clearest example is the APEST dead end. If 150,000 people took the assessment and only a fraction continued into deeper engagement, **well over 100,000 demonstrated interest and received no onward journey.** That is not a marketing failure in the cynical sense. It is an architecture failure: the bridge was never built.

Other latent pools include book readers who never discovered courses or pathways; Spanish- and Portuguese-speaking leaders for whom translations exist as files but not as a live platform; seminary students assigned *The Forgotten Ways* with no structured digital companion; practitioners doing movement work in the Global South who align with his frameworks but have never heard his name because his ideas are locked in English print.

A speculative but reasonable estimate for latent reach — people who would meaningfully engage if accessibility improved — runs **500,000 to 1.5 million**. The upper bound is uncertain. The APEST pool is not.

### What is already built but not yet live

Here is the unusual finding in Alan's case, and it matters for how leaders should interpret launch:

The problem is **not** primarily "we still need to create the content." In the alan-hirsch platform build, the research documents **165 digitized book chapters, dozens of articles, four full course structures, seven learning pathways, assessment integration, and a production-ready site** — built but not yet deployed as the unified home.

For Alan, launch is closer to **turning on the lights** than to **building the house.**

That distinction changes everything about expectations.

## Part 3: What changes at launch — in plain language

When a leader like Alan moves from fragmented presence to a unified, structured platform, several things shift. None of them require you to become a "content marketer." They are consequences of making embodied work **legible** to the systems people use to find answers.

### 1. Your authority becomes visible to search — and to AI

Google and AI assistants do not reward hidden expertise. They reward **recognizable, connected, citable expertise.**

Alan already has the first three letters of what Google calls E-E-A-T — Experience, Expertise, Authoritativeness — in the real world. He lacks the fourth leg in digital form: **Trustworthiness that machines can read.** That means a clear author home, consistent biographical signals, pages that explain his frameworks in his voice, links from peers and publishers back to one canonical place, and content structured so a chapter on mDNA is not trapped inside a PDF on a retailer site.

When the platform goes live with intentional structure — pillar pages for APEST, mDNA, missional church, and the like; articles that answer specific questions; pathways that connect assessment to formation — **Alan stops losing definitional queries to Wikipedia summaries and second-hand explainers.**

This is not vanity SEO. It is stewardship: if you coined the language of a movement, you should not be absent when someone asks what that language means.

### 2. The assessment stops being a dead end

For Alan, the single highest-leverage change is connecting **demonstrated intent** to **a journey.**

150,000 people raised their hand. A platform launch that wires assessment → recommended pathway → course or article sequence → email capture → community is not a growth hack. It is **honoring interest you already earned in rooms and on other people's websites.**

Even conservative math is instructive. If 10% of historically stranded assessors re-engage through a new journey, that is 15,000 people — roughly a **20–30% expansion of realized reach** without acquiring a single stranger from cold traffic. If email capture improves from 4% to even 12% of social reach, thousands of leaders move from "I follow you on Twitter" to "I receive your work in my inbox" — a channel you own.

Movement leaders should expect **step one of growth to be reactivation**, not celebrity.

### 3. The corpus starts working while you sleep

Alan does not need to write twenty new books to multiply reach. He needs his **existing corpus** to work in smaller, searchable units: articles drawn from chapters, glossary pages for framework terms, FAQ pages pastors actually query, course modules already drafted, video clips curated from years of talks.

Every major thought leader repurpose work this way — not because they are diluting their message, but because **a 300-page book serves a different moment than a 1,500-word answer to a specific question.** The book is formation. The article is discovery. Both are faithful if the article truly represents the book.

Professionally, sites that connect related pages into clear topic clusters often see **meaningfully higher organic discovery over 6–18 months** in specialized fields — not because tricks beat substance, but because structure helps systems understand "this person is the source on this topic."

For Alan's niche — missional church, APEST, apostolic movements — competition for **framework-named queries** is weaker than for generic terms like "church growth." That means **disproportionate early gains on the language he invented**, if the platform names and organizes that language clearly.

### 4. The network becomes a graph, not just relationships

Alan’s relational network is already strong. Frost, Ferguson, Brisco, Cole, Halter — the dossier maps a dense ecosystem of co-authorship and mutual endorsement.

What is missing is **structural legibility**: links from their sites to his canonical pages, shared pathways, a scenius where movement leaders visibly reference one another’s work on topics they genuinely share.

That layer compounds slowly — often **year two and three**, not month one — but it raises the ceiling. Movements multiply through trusted voices pointing to trusted voices. The web simply makes those pointers persistent and searchable.

### 5. Formats you skipped become doors you closed

The research is blunt about format gaps: **three of twenty books on Audible**; no personal YouTube channel; irregular newsletter cadence; translations sitting offline. Each gap is a population you never meet: commuters, visual learners, Spanish-speaking leaders, younger practitioners on YouTube.

Launch does not require fixing every gap on day one. It does require seeing format not as "marketing extras" but as **mission access** — different doors into the same room.

## Part 4: How to think about TAM without fooling yourself

**TAM** — total addressable market — is a phrase from business strategy. For movement leaders, translate it as: **how many people could this work serve if it were fully accessible?**

Use three nested circles, not one big number.

**The market circle** is everyone in the world who might care about missional church, movement multiplication, fivefold ministry, and adjacent questions. For this field, credible estimates run roughly **1.4 to 2.9 million** people globally — pastors, planters, lay leaders, seminary students, institutional innovators. That is the outer boundary. No individual leader captures it.

**The active circle** is people already seeking content like yours — reading books, taking assessments, attending Exponential, enrolling in Forge-like training. Perhaps **200,000 to 500,000** globally for this movemental lane. Alan is already a significant node here.

**The realized circle** is people actually formed through your work today. For Alan, **50,000 to 80,000** — supported by books, assessments, conferences, and organizations.

**The capturable circle** is who launch can realistically reach in the first 12–24 months **without assuming viral fame**: historical assessors, current book readers you can invite, email subscribers you can grow, searchers who find framework pages, peers who link to your hub. For Alan, a plausible **year-one engaged audience** might move toward **80,000 to 120,000** if assessment pathways, email capture, and corpus publishing execute well — still a range, not a promise.

The lesson for Brad, Alan, and every leader in the room: **launch primarily converts capturable reach before it expands the outer market.** That is good news. You are not starting from zero. You are **recovering interest you already earned** and **making decades of work findable.**

Do not measure launch success only by "how big is the missional church market." Measure it by **how much of your existing body of work becomes a pathway someone can actually walk.**

## Part 5: What we can say with confidence — and what we cannot

Honest forecasting separates **evidence** from **hope**.

### High confidence

- Alan’s embodied authority and demand signals (books, assessments, network) are real and documented.
- The digital gap — fragmentation, dead-end assessment, weak email capture, absent owned video hub — is real and suppresses reach.
- Connecting assessment to pathways is the largest short-term lever because behavior already proved interest.
- Launch with structured pages for his frameworks improves discoverability for **named concepts** (APEST, mDNA, apostolic genius) more than for generic religious keywords.
- Much of the content and infrastructure for Alan already exists; **activation matters as much as creation.**

### Medium confidence

- Email list growth into the **5,000–10,000** range within 6–12 months, with clear lead magnets and assessment capture.
- Meaningful organic discovery from corpus-derived articles over the first year, if published with steady cadence and internal linking.
- Becoming a primary source AI systems cite for **framework-specific questions** in his niche, within months of a well-structured launch.
- Year-one engaged audience moving toward the **80,000–120,000** band if funnels work — conversion rates are untested.

### Lower confidence — treat as directional, not promises

- Latent TAM of **500,000 to 1.5 million** includes unmeasured search demand and Global South potential.
- Incremental revenue estimates in the research (**roughly $140K–$380K/year** in additional digital revenue) depend on pricing, completion rates, and cohort uptake nobody has live data for yet.
- Dominance on broad terms like "how to grow a church" — unlikely quickly, and probably the wrong goal.

Movement leaders should hold plans loosely where conversion is untested and hold convictions firmly where decades of embodied work have already spoken.

## Part 6: Conclusions — what this lesson asks of you

### 1. You are probably not starting from zero

If your dossier looks anything like Alan's — books, talks, organizations, assessments, a peer network — your launch is likely a **retrieval and journey event**, not a reputation-building event. The question is whether your embodied archive becomes **a system people can enter**, not whether you are credible. You already are.

### 2. The first win is honoring people who already raised their hand

Before you chase new audiences, **wire the bridges**: assessment to pathway, talk to article, book to course, social follower to email, scattered video to one library. Alan's 150,000 assessors are the parable. Every leader has an equivalent — the mailing list you never built, the conference talks never transcribed, the cohort curriculum locked in a folder.

### 3. Structure is stewardship, not salesmanship

Pillar pages, linked articles, glossaries, and clear author homes are not "gaming Google." They are how you **keep your frameworks from being misquoted by strangers** and how you serve the pastor searching at 11 p.m. for language you spent decades refining. Structure makes trust **visible**.

### 4. Growth will come in steps, not a single spike

Expect: a launch moment → reactivation of warm audiences → steady discovery from searchable corpus → slow compounding as peers link and the scenius graph forms. Movements already know this pattern. It is how multiplication works in rooms. The platform simply extends the same patience to the web.

### 5. Format gaps are mission gaps

Audiobooks, video, translation, newsletter rhythm — these are not vanity metrics. They are **who you exclude without intending to.** Alan's dossier shows strong English print, weak audio, scattered video, offline Spanish. Your gaps will differ. Name them honestly.

### 6. Measure what formation requires

Reach metrics — traffic, subscribers, rankings — matter as **leading indicators**, not as the mission. The dossier's deepest critique of Alan's current system is not low Twitter engagement; it is **150,000 people who touched a diagnostic and had nowhere to go.** For movement leaders, the ultimate measure is still **depth of formation and multiplication in communities**, not pageviews.

Build the informational layer so more people can find the door. Then measure whether they walk through it into embodied practice — the same measure you have always used.

## A closing word to the room

Alan Hirsch spent a lifetime helping the church remember how movements multiply. The irony in his dossier is familiar to many of you: **the movement architect whose own digital movement infrastructure lagged far behind his embodied influence.**

That is not a moral failure. It is the predictable result of leaders who do the work in rooms first and treat the web as an afterthought — publisher pages, org sites, conference hosts, each holding a fragment.

Launch, done well, does not turn you into a content marketer. It turns your corpus into **a coherent gift** — findable, linkable, path-shaped — for the leader you will never meet in person but who needs exactly what you already wrote.

For Alan, the case is unusually clear because the research, the corpus, and the platform build are all visible in one place. For Brad Brisco and others preparing the same transition, the shape repeats at your scale: **authority you already have; reach you have not yet organized.**

The work ahead is not to become someone else online. It is to **stop leaving your own movement stranded at the score screen** — and to let the body of work you have already given the church finally work as a system.
