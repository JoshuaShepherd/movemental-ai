Danielle

You have built one of the most distributed bodies of work in Christian justice and communication, and you built most of it twice — once as practice, once as text. Nine books carry your name: *Just: Imagine*, *The Liberating Truth*, *Boundless: Living Life in Overflow* with Stephen Court, *A Beautiful Mess*, *The Zombie Gospel*, *The Ultimate Exodus*, *Better Together*, *The Other Side of Hope*, and the curated *Need to Know*. Behind them sit roughly twenty-two years as a commissioned officer in The Salvation Army, where you planted churches, founded training schools, and stood up Justice Departments and anti-trafficking work. Today you lead Boundless Communications Inc., the umbrella over Brave Global, Amplify Peace, the Women Speakers Collective, Infinitum, Hagar's Voice, and IMBY.

All of it is somewhere. None of it is in one place. The books are split across five publishers — InterVarsity Press carries *The Zombie Gospel* and *The Ultimate Exodus*, Monarch carries *Boundless* and *A Beautiful Mess*, W Publishing carries *Better Together* — and a reader buying any of them lands on Amazon, Audible, Christianbook, or Waterstones rather than on a page you control. The podcast has been renamed twice: what began as the *DJ Strickland Podcast* became the *Boundless Podcast* and is now *Right Side Up* with James Sholl, scattered across Apple Podcasts, Spotify, YouTube, Buzzsprout, and a Substack. Your articles sit on other people's mastheads — Global Leadership Network, DTS Voice, Premier Christianity, Outreach Magazine — and your talks live on conference platforms, the GLS18 "Better Together" main session and the "Leading Through the Dip" conversation with Craig Groeschel held on globalleadership.org rather than under your name. The relational layer is fragmented the same way. Brave Global holds its own list of young women and partner churches; Amplify Peace holds the rosters of everyone who traveled to Rwanda or the Middle East; the Stop The Traffik and World Vision partnerships hold theirs. The rolodex that connects those worlds lives in your phone. Through *Need to Know* you are one co-author degree from Scot McKnight, Jo Saxton, and Mandy Smith — inside the broader missional network whether anyone sees it or not. What is missing is the layer above all of it — the one that lets a reader move from any single piece to every other, and lets the people you met through Brave Global or an Amplify immersion stay connected after the institution that introduced them moves on.

The largest pool of your work is the part that was never written down, and it is the part most at risk of staying that way. Your officership taught you how to plant a church and run an anti-trafficking initiative long before you wrote a sentence about freedom in *The Ultimate Exodus*. The curation logic behind *Need to Know* — why those particular women, in that order — exists mostly in your head. So do the judgment calls behind an Amplify Peace immersion: which leaders to visit, how to hold grief and hope in the same week, why the domestic-violence rate in that Rwandan community fell. So do the decision rules behind Brave Global — how you speak to a teenage girl about trafficking without re-traumatizing her. That wisdom lives in trip debriefs, coaching calls, and the same talk given from a thousand stages.

This is where Movemental's AI does work that was not possible before. The tools are finally good enough that the embodied layer can surface on your schedule rather than a publisher's calendar. None of this replaces your voice. Every draft is yours to edit or refuse. The AI does the inventorying and the connective tissue. The authorship stays yours.
