## Editorial bio

David Docusen grew up in Detroit, Michigan, and built his life's work as a pastor in Charlotte, North Carolina, where he and his wife Dara planted Center City Church in 2010.[^multiply] The church was deliberately positioned on a dividing line between affluent Uptown Charlotte and historically high-poverty, majority-Black West Charlotte — a vantage point that, by his own account, exposed how blind he had been to the inequities in his own community and reshaped his understanding of the gospel.[^bold] That conviction drove him into doctoral study at Southeastern University, where he completed a Doctor of Ministry in 2019 with a dissertation on the church's response to racial and socioeconomic inequity in Charlotte.[^diss]

His magnum opus is **Neighborliness**, which grew directly out of that dissertation. First published through Fedd Books in 2020 (*Finding the Beauty of God Across Dividing Lines*) and then expanded and re-released by **Thomas Nelson** in 2022 (*Love Like Jesus. Cross Dividing Lines. Transform Your Community.*), the book argues that Christians are called to cross the dividing lines of race, economics, and politics to "recognize and amplify the beauty of God" in their communities.[^amazon] It carries a foreword by Mark Batterson, was featured on Good Morning America, and (per the author) reached #1 in four Amazon categories.[^about] He followed it in 2023 with *Tomorrow & the Days to Come*, a discipleship guide for new believers.[^rns]

Docusen is a credentialed Assemblies of God minister whose institutional footprint now centers on **The Neighborliness Center**, which he and Dara launched in 2021 to build strategic plans for churches, nonprofits, and businesses pursuing equitable access to education, economic development, housing, and healthcare.[^about] He also serves as **President of the International Ministry Network**, was a founding board member of Freedom Communities in Charlotte, sat on the board of RELEVANT Media Group, and serves on the Southeastern University board of trustees.[^linkedin][^freedom] His signature contribution to the wider conversation is the concept of **"neighborliness"** itself.

### Antithesis prohibition (non-negotiable)

Do not frame neighbor-love as **charity or outreach without relationship and proximity** — the well-meaning, top-down volunteerism that helps *to* the poor rather than *with* them and treats neighbors as projects. Docusen's most-repeated correction is "We need less outreach and more relationships," and his sharpest warning is that "volunteerism that is not rooted in relationship and understanding can be toxic." A paired prohibition: never split gospel from justice — he rejects both gospel-without-justice and justice-without-gospel ("you can't talk about the Gospel without talking about reconciliation"; "social justice is embedded into the larger narrative of biblical justice"). Derived from his Goodreads-catalogued book quotations, the 2017 Movement blog profile, and *The Baptist Paper* review. ## Theological positioning

| Domain | Position |
|--------|----------|
| Tradition | Pentecostal — credentialed Assemblies of God minister; B.A./M.A./D.Min. from Southeastern University (AG flagship) |
| Ecclesiology | The local church as a **bridge between rich and poor**, called to look like its city; "our beautiful church looks nothing like our beautiful city" names the failure he resists |
| Missiology | Holistic / community-development over event-based outreach (housing, education, economic development, healthcare); asset-based and resident-led; "less outreach and more relationships" |

| Culture | Engages race / economics / politics directly but anti-polemical — seeks understanding over winning, nuance over reflex; curiosity and relationship as the cultural posture |

### Distinctive claims

- Coined and **operationalized "neighborliness" as a full framework** — book, sermon series, nonprofit consultancy, and conference — turning the Good-Samaritan ethic into a holistic community-development methodology rather than a sentiment.
- A **Pentecostal / Assemblies of God voice on racial and economic justice** — a tradition not typically associated with this conversation — carried into AG's flagship outlets (Influence Magazine, AG News).
- "**Less outreach, more relationships.**" He explicitly downgrades programmatic outreach and volunteerism ("can be toxic") in favor of proximate, long-term relationship between rich and poor.
- **Gospel and justice are one thing, not two** — refusing both the justice-only and gospel-only camps.
- **Recovers lament** as a discipline he argues much of white evangelicalism has lost.
- **Reframes gentrification with nuance** rather than blanket condemnation — the test is whether longtime residents are treated as image-bearing partners and decision-makers.
