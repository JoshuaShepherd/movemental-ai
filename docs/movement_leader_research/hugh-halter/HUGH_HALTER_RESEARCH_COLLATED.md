## Editorial bio

Hugh Halter (b. 1966) is an American author, speaker, and practitioner who has spent more than thirty years as a missionary in North America, pioneering innovative church expressions first in Portland, Oregon, then in Denver, Colorado — where he co-founded the Adullam congregational network of incarnational communities — and now in Alton, Illinois.[^goodreads][^outreach] His writing partnership with **Matt Smay** produced his magnum opus, *The Tangible Kingdom* (Jossey-Bass, 2008, foreword by Alan Hirsch), along with its eight-week *Primer* and *AND: The Gathered and Scattered Church*. The "Tangible Kingdom" — incarnational community embodying the life of Jesus in an actual neighborhood — is the through-line of his entire corpus.[^9marks][^forge]

His books run from *The Tangible Kingdom* and *AND* through *Sacrilege*, *BiVO*, *Flesh*, and *Brimstone* to the more recent Life as Mission Series (*Happy Hour*, *Righteous Brood*) and *Brave Cities* (with Taylor McCall) — a body of work arguing plainly for a church less churchy and more tangible, with belonging preceding believing.[^goodreadslist] He sits within the Forge / Movement Leaders Collective lineage seeded by Alan Hirsch, serves as national director of **Missio** and co-director (with Smay) of the **Missional Church Apprenticeship Practicum (MCAP)**, and co-directs the consulting firm **Brave Cities**.[^forge][^lifeandleadership]

In Alton, Halter founded the **Lantern Network** (2016) and opened **Post Commons** (2018) — a 12,000-square-foot former federal post office turned coffee house, brunch kitchen, events center, whiskey society, and co-working hub — and, alongside his wife Cheryl, co-directs **Rí Beag Refuge**, an 80-acre equine therapy farm. The thread across all of it is kingdom work that takes up physical space in a specific city, with a door anyone can walk through.[^idverify]

### Antithesis prohibition (non-negotiable)

Do not render Halter's voice as doctrinal-systematic argument, altar-call formula, or attractional-church program language. His most-corrected reader misreading is treating the gospel as a *concept to be argued* or a *prayer to be prayed* rather than an embodied, relational life among people. His correction is explicit: "What makes the gospel good news isn't the concept, but the real-life person who has been changed by it," and he inverts the conventional "believe → belong" into "belonging enables believing." Avoid program/metric language ("attend," "convert," "close the sale"); favor presence, table, neighborhood, and belonging. Derived from *The Tangible Kingdom* and *Flesh* (profile/voice-analysis.md).

## Theological positioning

| Domain | Position |
|--------|----------|
| Tradition | Evangelical Protestant; missional / incarnational stream (Forge lineage) |
| Ecclesiology | Incarnational, gathered-and-scattered; church as embodied community in a neighborhood, not an attractional program |

### Distinctive claims

- "Belonging enables believing" — the order of conversion is relational before propositional.
- The gospel's persuasive power is the changed *person*, not the *concept*.
- Bi-vocational ministry is a normative, strategic missional posture, not a deficit.
- Kingdom ecosystems — benevolent businesses and place-based ventures (Post Commons) — are a legitimate "new way of being church."
- "Holy nonjudgment": discernment can be retained while condemnation is refused.
