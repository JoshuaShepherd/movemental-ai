# Theology: Hugh Halter

Hugh Halter's theology is incarnational and missional at its core. He works from the conviction that the church does not exist primarily to gather a crowd on Sunday but to embody the kingdom of God among the people and places it is sent to — that the gospel is most credible when it is lived in flesh, in homes, at tables, and in ordinary neighborhoods.

## Incarnational community

The center of Halter's thought is the incarnation as a model for the church, not only a doctrine about Jesus. In *Flesh: Bringing the Incarnation Down to Earth* he presses the claim that following Jesus means becoming present, local, and embodied in the same way God became present in Christ. *The Tangible Kingdom* frames this as creating communities that make the reign of God touchable and visible rather than abstract.

## The gathered-and-scattered church

In *AND: The Gathered and Scattered Church* he refuses the either/or between attractional and missional models, arguing the church must be both — gathered for worship and formation, scattered as sent people into the everyday world. The "AND" is itself a theological claim: the kingdom is held in tension, not collapsed into one mode.

## Bi-vocational ministry as calling

*BiVo* reframes bi-vocational ministry not as a financial fallback but as a theological posture — a way of staying woven into the economic and relational fabric of a place, closer to the pattern of the apostle Paul and of Jesus' own embeddedness in ordinary work.

## Holy nonjudgment and the unorthodox ways of Jesus

*Brimstone* and *Sacrilege* press into how Jesus actually moved toward outsiders, sinners, and the religiously dismissed. Halter names "holy nonjudgment" as a discipline: a posture that refuses condemnation without softening truth, modeled on the way Jesus made room for the people his religious context excluded.

## Family and place as mission

In *Righteous Brood* (with Dave and Sue Ferguson) the family becomes a unit of mission rather than a private retreat from it. In his most recent work, *Brave Cities*, the theological frame widens to "kingdom ecosystems" — the conviction that whole cities and economies, not only individuals or congregations, are the canvas on which the kingdom is meant to become visible, where worship, enterprise, justice, and reconciliation belong together.
