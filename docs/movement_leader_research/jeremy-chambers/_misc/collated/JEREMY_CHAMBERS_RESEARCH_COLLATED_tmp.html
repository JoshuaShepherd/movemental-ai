Editorial bio

Jeremy "Jemo" Chambers is an American church planter, spiritual director, and missional practitioner who occupies a distinctive intersection in the contemporary missional church — the seam between Renovaré-style contemplative formation (the Foster / Willard / Hudson lineage) and the Hirsch-Frost / Forge / 100 Movements missional-movement stream. Born around 1986, he reports praying about a future move to Denver since age thirteen — a vocational specificity that anticipated nearly two decades of geographic and ministerial trajectory.

After a B.A. in Ministry Leadership at Johnson University and two master's degrees from Trinity Evangelical Divinity School (Intercultural Studies; Christian Studies / Church History), Chambers and his Costa-Rican-born wife Monica Paredes Chambers founded Arise RVA Church Network in Richmond, Virginia in 2013 — a network of microchurches that grew to roughly seven communities and affiliated with the V3 Movement under the Baptist General Association of Virginia. In late 2019 the family relocated to Denver to help Rowland Smith launch the Denver expression of The Pando Collective, a Front Range network seeking to network, resource, and launch missional micro-expressions of the church. He has since become Training Director of Forge America — the U.S. arm of Alan Hirsch's globally-networked missional training movement — making him a curricular gatekeeper for the next generation of Forge-formed practitioners.

His two books — Kingdom Contours (2020; revised 2025) and The Art of Missional Spirituality (2023) — are both co-authored with Monica and both published in the 100 Movements Publishing orbit, with forewords from Alan Hirsch, Debra Hirsch, and Michael Frost. The Art of Missional Spirituality (31 sacred practices, foreword by Alan & Debra Hirsch) is his magnum opus to date: a calendar-structured fusion of contemplative discipline and neighborhood mission designed to refuse the modern dichotomy between "intimacy with God" and "engagement with neighbors." Across Renovaré articles, the V3 blog archive, Missio Alliance, and a 2024 podcast circuit (Renovaré, Everyday Disciple, Pivotal People, Dandelion Resourcing, Shifting Culture), his work consistently presents missional life as a rhythm, not a strategy — formation and sentness folded into a single way.

Six Foundational Paradigms (Kingdom Contours)

Components (1) Christocentric and Trinitarian; (2) Discipleship — being and making Jesus followers; (3) Incarnational Living — adopting a missional strategic mindset; (4) Movement Wisdom — being movemental; (5) Organic Systems — structuring for multiplication; (6) Deep Community — taking on risks by faith
Visual / model Six-paradigm enumeration; no diagram surfaced — book chapters function as the model
Adoption evidence Curricular spine of Arise RVA, Arise Church Denver, and Pando Collective microchurch coaching; co-published by 100 Movements Publishing (Hirsch's imprint) with Hirsch + Frost as named contributors on revised edition

Chambers' six paradigms extend and reframe Alan Hirsch's mDNA from The Forgotten Ways into a planter-facing toolkit, paired with an appendix titled "Baby Steps: How to Start a Micro-Church." The framework's distinctive contribution is foregrounding Deep Community as a discrete paradigm — the relational substrate that the other five depend on.

Antithesis prohibition (non-negotiable)

Do not separate intimacy from mission, or contemplation from sentness. Chambers' load-bearing rhetorical move is to refuse the dichotomy — "spirituality that doesn't draw you away from your neighbors but propels you into the lives of others and the needs of your city" (Art of Missional Spirituality back cover, jemochambers.com). Avoid writing as if formation and mission are sequential (first one, then the other), compartmentalized (mission for God and time with God as separate items on a to-do list), or hierarchical (the contemplative life as more "advanced" than the missional life). In the same posture, avoid method absolutism — Chambers explicitly distances himself from voices who treat microchurch as the only valid ecclesial form: "Sometimes you'll find in one segment...people who are like all about micro church and they're like it's the only way" (Pivotal People 2024). And avoid credit-seeking framing of service — "Goodness can be beautiful in its own right. I can serve someone and never have to receive credit" (Pivotal People 2024).

Distinctive claims

  • Mission and contemplation are one rhythm, not two stages. This is the load-bearing claim that distinguishes him from purely missional voices (Hirsch, Frost, Brisco — for whom contemplative formation is supportive but not constitutive) and from purely contemplative voices (Foster, Willard, Hudson — for whom mission is implication but not structure).
  • Microchurch is a form, not the form. He distances himself from movement absolutists in his own ecosystem. This is unusual within microchurch advocacy literature.
  • Distraction, not opposition, is the dominant spiritual threat. Sets him apart from cultural-warrior framings of the missional church.
  • Calling vs. assignment as a distinct pastoral diagnostic. A practical contribution to compassion-fatigue / sustainable-mission discourse not present in upstream Renovaré or Forge teaching at this level of specificity.
  • Co-authorship with spouse as norm. Almost all published work signed and written with Monica Paredes Chambers — a marriage-as-writing-dyad model rare in the missional church publishing space (Alan and Debra Hirsch are the closest parallel).
