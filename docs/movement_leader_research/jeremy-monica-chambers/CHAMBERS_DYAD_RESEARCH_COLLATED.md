## 1. Executive summary (90-second take)

Jeremy and Monica Paredes Chambers are a married missional author-practitioner dyad whose published work sits at a **rare seam in contemporary movement theology** — the seam between Richard Foster's **Renovaré** spiritual-formation tradition (Foster / Willard / Hudson / Boa lineage) and Alan Hirsch's **Forge / 100 Movements / V3** missional-movement stream. Both are **Renovaré Institute alumni** and ongoing Renovaré Ministry Team members; Jeremy is the national **Training Director of Forge America**; together they co-lead the **Arise Network** (seven microchurches in the U.S. and abroad, originated as Arise RVA in Richmond, Virginia in 2013) and **Pando Denver** (20+ leaders) within Rowland Smith's Pando Collective. They report participation in launching disciple-making movements in **40+ countries**.

Their **magnum opus** is *The Art of Missional Spirituality: 31 Sacred Practices for Jesus-Followers* (100 Movements Publishing, November 2023; foreword by Alan & Debra Hirsch) — a calendar-month curriculum of daily practices that **refuses the binary between interior formation and exterior mission**. Their earlier book *Kingdom Contours* (2020; revised edition 2024–25 with foreword by Alan Hirsch and Michael Frost) is a six-paradigm extension of Hirsch's mDNA into a microchurch planter's toolkit. Both books are **100% co-authored**; Renovaré articles, Missio Alliance essays, and podcast appearances are also joint-bylined. The dyad's signature rhetorical move is **respiratory metaphor**: contemplation is "breathing in," mission is "breathing out," and either alone is fatal — *"if you're always breathing out, you're gonna hyperventilate; if you're always breathing in, you're gonna suffocate."*

Their **distinctive contributions** to the missional church discourse: (1) **practice-level integration** of contemplative discipline with neighborhood mission, pairing each interior practice with an outward send; (2) **microchurch as form, not the form** — explicit disavowal of microchurch absolutism, which is unusual within microchurch literature; (3) **distraction (not opposition or heresy) named as the dominant spiritual threat** in their 2025 Missio Alliance essay; (4) **calling vs. assignment** as a pastoral diagnostic for compassion fatigue; (5) **marriage as published missional form** — co-authorship is the public norm, with the closest parallel being Alan & Debra Hirsch.

## 5. Editorial bio (3 paragraphs)

Jeremy and **Monica Paredes Chambers** are a married author-practitioner team operating at the seam between **Renovaré-style contemplative formation** (Foster / Willard / Hudson / Boa lineage) and the **Hirsch–Frost / Forge / 100 Movements missional-movement stream** — a juncture few writers in the contemporary missional church meaningfully inhabit. Monica's formation arc moves from Costa Rica through a missionary family's relocation to **Lancaster, Pennsylvania** — where her parents planted a Latin American congregation for the U.S. Hispanic context — into a decade of microchurch planting in Richmond, Virginia and, since approximately 2022, into the **Denver metro (Littleton)**, where they practice incarnational neighborhood mission and report roughly thirty acts of kindness per week across their micro-parish. Jeremy (born ~1986) reports praying about a future move to Denver since age thirteen — a vocational specificity that anticipated nearly two decades of geographic and ministerial trajectory.

After Jeremy's **B.A. in Bible at Lancaster Bible College** and two master's degrees from **Trinity Evangelical Divinity School** (M.A. Intercultural Studies; M.A. History of Christian Thought / Church History) and the couple's joint completion of the **Renovaré Institute**, they founded **Arise RVA Church Network** in Richmond in 2013 — a network of microchurches that grew to roughly seven communities and affiliated with the **V3 Movement** under the **Baptist General Association of Virginia**. In late 2019 the family relocated to Denver to help **Rowland Smith** launch the Denver expression of **The Pando Collective**, a Front Range network for missional micro-expressions of the church. Jeremy serves as **Training Director of Forge America** (the U.S. arm of Alan Hirsch's globally-networked missional training movement), making him a curricular gatekeeper for the next generation of Forge-formed practitioners. Both are ongoing **Renovaré Ministry Team** participants (attended a 2024 ministry team retreat) and **Pastors Retreat 2022** featured speakers.

Their two books — *Kingdom Contours* (2020; revised 2024–25) and ***The Art of Missional Spirituality*** (November 2023) — are both co-authored with Monica and both published in the **100 Movements Publishing** orbit, with forewords by **Alan Hirsch**, **Debra Hirsch**, and **Michael Frost**. *The Art of Missional Spirituality* (31 sacred practices, foreword by Alan & Debra Hirsch) is their magnum opus: a calendar-structured fusion of contemplative discipline and neighborhood mission designed to refuse the modern dichotomy between "intimacy with God" and "engagement with neighbors." Across Renovaré articles (2022, 2023), the V3 Movement blog (2018–2025), a 2025 Missio Alliance essay on compassion fatigue, and a 2024 launch podcast circuit (Renovaré *Life With God*, Pivotal People, Dandelion Resourcing, Everyday Disciple, Shifting Culture #197), their work consistently presents missional life as a **respiratory rhythm**, not a strategy — formation and sentness folded into a single way, carried in public almost exclusively as **we**.

## 6. Core thesis & distinctive contribution

The Chambers' load-bearing intellectual claim — the proposition that distinguishes them from peers in either upstream tradition — is **respiratory missional spirituality**: contemplation and mission are not sequential stages, hierarchical levels, or competing items on a to-do list; they are **two phases of one breath**, and either alone is fatal.

This thesis is sharper than it sounds in summary because it does double duty:

- **Against pure-contemplative voices** (Foster, Willard, Hudson, Renovaré-as-such), the Chambers refuse the "retreat-and-return" pattern in which formation precedes mission. They argue mission *is* part of formation; the neighbor is not the destination but the locus where the practice itself completes.
- **Against pure-missional voices** (Hirsch, Frost, Brisco, Forge-as-such), the Chambers refuse the substitution of activism for interior life. Compassion fatigue, in their 2025 Missio Alliance diagnosis, is **self-inflicted by overexposure to crisis without the corresponding inward breath**.

The proposition is operationalized in *The Art of Missional Spirituality*'s **31-practice calendar architecture**, where each interior discipline is paired with an outward send — solitude pairs with secret acts of kindness; praying the hours pairs with neighborhood prayer rhythms; centering pairs with hospitality. This pairing is **unusual** in the spiritual formation literature, which more typically arranges practices on a single inward axis. It is also unusual in the missional church literature, which more typically arranges practices on a single outward axis. The pairing — and the calendar that forces it daily — is the Chambers' proprietary architectural move.

A second distinctive contribution: **non-absolutism within microchurch advocacy**. Most microchurch literature (Hirsch on house church, Sanders on Underground, Cole on organic church) implicitly or explicitly elevates the form. The Chambers explicitly disavow this — *"Sometimes you'll find in one segment...people who are all about micro church and they're like it's the only way"* (Pivotal People 2024) — placing them in the unusual posture of microchurch practitioners who model the form without absolutizing it.

A third distinctive contribution: **distraction (not heresy, not opposition) as the dominant spiritual threat**. Their 2025 Missio Alliance essay names *"Distraction may be the single most damaging danger to the inner life with Jesus"* — a diagnosis that sets them apart from the cultural-warrior framings dominant in evangelical missional church discourse and aligns them more with the contemplative diagnosis of accidie / acedia.

A fourth distinctive contribution: **calling vs. assignment** as a discrete pastoral diagnostic for sustainable mission. Calling = overall trajectory oriented by God (companion tool: Hirsch's APEST). Assignment = temporary Spirit-highlighted action (may pause calling for, e.g., a family crisis). The distinction is not present at this level of specificity in either the upstream Renovaré or upstream Forge teaching, and it serves as the Chambers' practical contribution to the compassion-fatigue / sustainable-mission discourse.

A fifth distinctive contribution: **co-authorship with spouse as the published norm** — paralleling Alan & Debra Hirsch, but at a level of completeness (every book; every Renovaré article; almost every podcast appearance) that is rare across the missional publishing space. The Chambers model marriage itself as a public missional unit.

### 10.3 Antithesis prohibition (non-negotiable)

1. **Do not separate intimacy from mission, or contemplation from sentness.** Their load-bearing rhetorical move is to refuse the dichotomy. Avoid writing as if formation and mission are sequential (first one, then the other), compartmentalized (mission for God *and* time with God as separate items on a to-do list), or hierarchical (the contemplative life as more "advanced" than the missional life). Source: [TAoMS back-cover thesis](https://www.jemochambers.com/the-art-of-missional-spirituality).
2. **Do not frame practices as transactional leverage or outcome guarantees.** Monica explicitly disavows this on Pivotal People 2024 — *"These practices are just you making yourself available to God, to connect with him and to be able to receive his goodness and his love."* The substrate voice must keep practices in the register of **availability**, **positioning**, **faithfulness**, and **receiving**, never in the register of leverage or guarantee.
3. **Do not absolutize microchurch as the only valid ecclesial form.** They explicitly distance themselves from voices who treat microchurch as the only valid form — *"Sometimes you'll find in one segment...people who are all about micro church and they're like it's the only way"* (Pivotal People 2024).
4. **Do not write solo-authoritative voice for either spouse on books or Renovaré pieces without explicit exception.** Co-authorship is the public norm and the voice should reflect "we."
5. **Do not seek credit-receiving framings of service.** *"I can serve someone and never have to receive credit. Goodness can be beautiful in its own right."* (Pivotal People 2024).
6. **Do not attribute the Tampa marketing Monica, the AI-operator Monica, or Army Capt. Jeremy to this corpus.**

### 11.1 Distinctive claims (dyad)

- **Mission and contemplation are one rhythm, not two stages.** This is the load-bearing claim that distinguishes them from purely missional voices (Hirsch, Frost, Brisco — for whom contemplative formation is supportive but not constitutive) and from purely contemplative voices (Foster, Willard, Hudson — for whom mission is implication but not structure).
- **Microchurch is *a* form, not *the* form.** They distance themselves from movement absolutists in their own ecosystem. Unusual within microchurch advocacy literature.
- **Distraction, not opposition, is the dominant spiritual threat.** Sets them apart from cultural-warrior framings of the missional church.
- **Calling vs. assignment as a distinct pastoral diagnostic.** A practical contribution to compassion-fatigue / sustainable-mission discourse not present in upstream Renovaré or Forge teaching at this level of specificity.
- **Co-authorship with spouse as norm.** Almost all published work signed and written with the spouse — a marriage-as-writing-dyad model rare in the missional church publishing space (Alan and Debra Hirsch are the closest parallel).
- **31-practice architecture** pairs each inward discipline with outward send (unusual vs. retreat-then-return curricula).
- **Deep Community** named as a discrete sixth Kingdom Contours paradigm (relational substrate for the other five).
- **Secrecy discipline** elevated against approval-seeking (Sermon-on-the-Mount ethic — Secret Acts of Kindness as a primary discipline).
