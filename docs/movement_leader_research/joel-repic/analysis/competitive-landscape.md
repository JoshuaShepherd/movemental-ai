## The positioning question

If someone online wanted to read a voice writing on the exact intersection of:

1. **Microchurch ecclesiology**
2. **Indigenous leadership in post-industrial / marginal communities**
3. **Hope as defiant spiritual practice**
4. **Twenty-year embedded neighborhood ministry**

there is essentially **no dominant voice currently occupying that intersection**. Each of those four axes has strong individual voices. None of them sit at the center.

This gives Joel an unusually clean positioning opportunity if Movemental helps him occupy it.

## Nobody sits at the center

Of the ~25 voices named above, **none occupies all four axes simultaneously**. Consider the closest analogues:

- **Brian Sanders** occupies axes 1 + (partially) 2. His Tampa context is urban but not post-industrial; his framing is ecclesial more than community-development.
- **Gregory Boyle** occupies axes 3 + (partially) 2. His frame is Catholic, Los Angeles gang ministry, not microchurch or church planting.
- **John Perkins** occupies axes 2 + (partially) 3. His frame is civil-rights-era African-American community development, not microchurch.
- **Dan White Jr.** occupies axes 1 + 4. Strong neighborhood-church voice but not specifically post-industrial or community-development-leaning.
- **Hugh Halter** occupies axes 1 + 4. More missional-incarnational breadth than post-industrial depth.

**Joel's unique center**: all four axes together, specifically shaped by Aliquippa's post-industrial Ohio River Valley context, spoken with a Christian-and-Missionary-Alliance pastoral register.

His "competitive disadvantage" (thin digital) and his "competitive advantage" (institutional inheritance) both point at the same solution: **consolidate a platform under his name now, before his ED role generates enough demand that the lack of capture infrastructure becomes a bottleneck.**

## Risks and counter-positioning

1. **Brian Sanders' gravitational pull.** Sanders is 6-books-deep into microchurch authorship, is a talented communicator, and is still active on the Underground board. Joel's voice could be read as "Sanders' successor" rather than as its own voice. **Mitigation**: emphasize Aliquippa / post-industrial context, hope-as-protest language, and indigenous-leadership framing that are distinct from Sanders' primary emphases.
2. **Lucas Pulley's proximity.** Pulley has his own domain, is actively writing, and has been the public Underground voice for five years. Clear editorial division of lanes between Pulley and Repic is necessary. **Mitigation**: Pulley = Movements Director voice / coaching / multiplication systems; Repic = ED / neighborhood / formation / hope-as-protest voice.
3. **The "too practitioner to publish" trap.** Joel's voice is so deeply relational and place-based that some of his strongest material could feel too context-bound for a national audience. **Mitigation**: editorial partnership that helps him scale specificity (name the street, name the person, name the theology) rather than flattening into general principles.

## Net positioning statement (proposed)

> Joel Repic is the voice of twenty years of faithful presence in a post-industrial American neighborhood — a pastor who learned that hope, consistently practiced, is a form of protest; that indigenous leaders carry more wisdom than visiting experts; and that a hundred small, decentralized expressions of church can do more in a hurting city than any single large one. He now leads one of North America's most distinctive microchurch networks, and his voice is shaped as much by Aliquippa as by Tampa.

If that sentence is roughly accurate, the competitive landscape has no other author currently standing in it.
