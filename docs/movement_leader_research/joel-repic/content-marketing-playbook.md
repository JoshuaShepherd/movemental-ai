# Your Next Season: A Content Marketing Playbook for Joel Repic

Joel, I've been listening. Not just to your words, but to what sits underneath them — and what I hear is a person who has spent twenty years in one hard zip code learning how to show up, and who has now been asked to carry what you learned to a hundred-plus microchurches across the Tampa Bay Area and thirty-something sister movements around the world. That is a staggering handoff. And if I'm honest, what concerns me is not whether you can do the work — you've done it. What concerns me is whether anyone outside Aliquippa will ever know how you did it.

Because here's the thing I see, and I want to be direct with you. Your public body of work, as of this writing, is almost invisible. Two posts on hopeisprotest.com. Six sermons preserved on a small-church podcast feed that could vanish with a domain change. One long Exponential interview from October 2022. A bonus episode of the Microchurches Podcast where you delivered your first teaching as the new Underground Executive Director. One episode with Chelsea about raising kids in microchurch life. A redirected byline archive at legacy.cmalliance.org that we can't even get to anymore. A Leadership Network article. A CMA feature quote. Regional press from Beaver County. An Instagram account with nine hundred and some followers, a dormant Twitter, a stale LinkedIn.

And yet what you've actually built over two decades is the opposite of thin. You founded Aliquippa Impact in 2005. You and Chelsea moved onto a drug-and-prostitution corner and had people living with you. You pastored The Gospel Tabernacle. You helped organic multiplication over a decade coalesce into The Greenhouse Network and its Greenhouse Lab incubator. You served as Church Planting Director for the Christian and Missionary Alliance's Western Pennsylvania District. You formed dozens of leaders whose names are in your phone and nowhere else. You said, once, in a regional paper: "Every time we show up in the life of a kid, we are protesting the hopelessness they feel." That sentence alone is a book that hasn't been written yet.

So here is what I want you to hear first. **You are not behind.** You are where most leaders wish they were — a deep practitioner with a coherent voice, at the beginning of a national role, with a brand-new audience of a hundred microchurches actively wanting to listen to you. What you don't have is infrastructure. The stories are in you; they just haven't been captured. The frameworks are in you; they just haven't been named. The theology is in you; it just hasn't been written. That's not a deficiency. That's a season.

Let me tell you what I see in your voice, because your voice is extraordinarily consistent for someone who has barely externalized it. You believe hope is protest — not as a slogan but as a working theology of consistent presence. You believe local neighborhood leaders must drive their own development — not as a value but as a posture that has shaped how you've spent two decades. You believe Jesus works best in weakness, so why not embrace it. You believe everyone gets to play — anyone hearing a call from God should find a radical yes when they come to you. You believe in diverse, decentralized expressions of church — not because it's fashionable but because it's what actually held together in Aliquippa. You believe in prayer and Spirit-led simplicity, and you've structured actual organizations around that conviction. That is a body of theology. It just doesn't have a body of work attached to it yet.

Your twenty years in Aliquippa produced content every single day. The sermons you preached that aren't in the archive. The cohort meetings you led at Aliquippa Impact. The neighbors who sat at your dinner table. The kids whose goals you helped shape. The other pastors in the CMA Western PA district you coached through church plants. The Greenhouse Network leaders you convened. The Exponential panel you sat on. The denominational meetings where you argued for "everyone gets to play." Every one of those was content. None of it was captured. Most of it is gone. But the rest of your life is ahead of you, and the Tampa chapter is brand new, and the window for externalizing the Aliquippa chapter before it fades behind the Tampa chapter is open right now — and not for long.

Here's what I want you to stop worrying about: growth. You don't need to go viral. You don't need to compete with Brian Sanders. You don't need to out-teach any of the authors who have spent the last decade publishing books about microchurch ecclesiology. That is not your calling, and it wouldn't serve you even if it were. What you need is a modest, durable, owned surface where people who already love the Underground, already trust you through CMA, already know Aliquippa Impact, already heard you on Exponential — can find you in one place, read what you're thinking this week, and stay close over years. You need an audience you own rather than rent. You need a record of what you have lived. You need an archive that won't disappear when a podcast feed gets deleted. You need a hub that lets someone encounter the full Joel Repic instead of a fragment.

And here's what I want you to start leaning into. **Oral history before anything else.** You are a story-first teacher. Your sermons prove it. Your Hope Is Protest posts prove it. Your Exponential interview proves it. The single most valuable content asset you have is not a book you could sit down and write — it's a series of conversations you could sit down and have. Ten long-form interviews. Fifteen. Someone on our team, a microphone, a quiet room, and a list of questions designed to draw out the twenty years. That becomes audio. That becomes written essays. That becomes frameworks. That eventually becomes a book. But the book is downstream of the conversations.

Let me sketch the shape. **Month one, relaunch hopeisprotest.com as your Movemental author hub** — same name, same masthead, same voice — but now with a newsletter underneath it, an archive structured chronologically, and a landing page that says who you are now and what you're doing. We migrate the two existing posts. We import the six Gospel Tabernacle sermons before they disappear. We pull whatever is still reachable from the CMA legacy archive. We link the Exponential podcast and the Leadership Network article. That's week one. Your whole scattered footprint is suddenly in one place. That alone will be the single most impactful digital move of your career to date.

**Month one to three, we capture the oral history.** Not a podcast you have to host. Not a book you have to draft. A series of recorded conversations. Aliquippa origins — how did you and Chelsea end up on that corner. Aliquippa Impact — what actually happened in year one, year five, year fifteen. The Greenhouse genesis — how organic multiplication over a decade became a formalized network. CMA Western PA — what did you learn coaching planters in a denominational structure. The Underground transition — what was the discernment, what's the posture in year one. Hope as protest — what do you actually mean by it, what does it cost, what does it produce. Indigenous leadership — what's the difference between "no savior" as a slogan and "no savior" as a practice. Prayer and Spirit-led simplicity — what has your rule of life actually been. These become the raw material for everything else.

**Month three to six, we turn the raw material into frameworks.** "The Greenhouse Method" as a course — indigenous leadership for post-industrial communities, drawn from the interviews plus the Exponential appearance. "Hope Is Protest" as a written series — five to eight essays under your byline on your own site that give you durable written artifacts for the first time. "Microchurch Life With Kids" as a short module with Chelsea, drawing on your Microchurches Podcast Ep. 101 appearance. Each one of these is a modest, focused asset. None of them requires you to become a different kind of writer than you already are. All of them take what you've already said or lived and make it possible for someone else to learn it.

**Month six and beyond, we think about the bigger ones.** "The Aliquippa Chapter," serialized — essays released on a cadence, eventually compiled into the book that doesn't exist yet but should. The Sanders + Pulley + Repic three-voice pathway on microchurch movements — you and Brian and Lucas, each bringing a different lens to the same ecclesiology, hosted together on Movemental, linking out to your three author hubs. Your own hosted podcast — you, interviewing microchurch leaders across the Underground's thirty-plus global sister movements, becoming the journalist of your own ecosystem. An ED Letters archive — your ongoing teaching to the Underground family, preserved as both audio and text, so year three of your Tampa ED role is something future leaders can read and not just remember.

Here's what I don't want you to change. Don't change how you talk. You sound like Joel Repic. Your Exponential interview, your Hope Is Protest posts, your sermons, your quotes in the Aliquippa Impact feature — they are all recognizably the same person. That voice is worth more than any framework we could build around it. Don't change your theology. Don't change your relationships. Don't change your rhythms of prayer and presence. Don't change the posture of arriving in Tampa without predetermined answers. Keep all of that. What we're adding is not a different Joel — it's a Joel that can be found, read, listened to, and learned from by people who aren't physically at an Underground gathering.

And don't worry about monetization, not yet. Your audience is small enough and new enough that any paid product right now would feel premature. Build the list. Build the archive. Build the trust. Give things away generously, the way the Greenhouse Lab gives away coaching and financial services and marketing support to indigenous leaders. In a year, once we know who's reading, we can think about whether there's a subscription or a cohort or a course offering that serves real demand. But the first year is stewardship, not monetization. You have never had an owned audience before. Let's build it right.

Here's the frame to hold onto when it feels like too much. **You are not building a brand. You are honoring twenty years of faithful work so it can keep serving people after you.** The content isn't for your resume. It's for the young church planter in a post-industrial Ohio River Valley town who needs to know someone else has figured out how to stay for twenty years. It's for the Underground microchurch leader in Manila or Cape Town or Dublin who needs a new Executive Director's voice and a framework for what indigenous leadership actually means. It's for Chelsea's therapist peers at Christian Counselors Collaborative who want to understand what church planting among the poor actually requires. It's for the next generation of leaders whose names aren't in anyone's phone yet.

You have said — more than once — that 90 percent of what you do is show up. This is exactly that. Showing up on the page. Showing up on the audio. Showing up in an inbox twice a month. The practice is not different from what you've done for twenty years. It's just translated into a new medium. And the medium, finally, will let what you learned in Aliquippa travel past the zip code where you learned it.

That's the next season. Not more work — just work that doesn't disappear.

And that, I think, is something worth protesting hopelessness for.
