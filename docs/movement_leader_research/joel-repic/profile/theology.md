## Core thesis

**The Kingdom of Heaven shows up in unlikely places, among unlikely people, through relentless faithful presence in a particular neighborhood — and that presence is itself a form of protest against structural despair.** The church is not an event attended but a family of diverse, decentralized expressions arising wherever ordinary people are empowered — in weakness, through prayer, in the Spirit — to show up consistently among the poor and marginal.

## Theological tradition

**Primary**: Missional-incarnational ecclesiology, shaped inside the **Christian and Missionary Alliance** ordination tradition (evangelical, historically Alliance-oriented, strong on Holy Spirit ministry, distinct from mainline or Reformed traditions), but operating in the **microchurch / decentralized movement** ethos most associated with the Underground Network he now leads.

**Secondary influences**:
- Neighborhood / asset-based community development
- Post-industrial community theology
- Urban Kingdom theology (Aliquippa as specific context)
- Underground Network manifesto (Jesus, the poor, the lost, microchurches, biblical justice, prayer, etc.)

He occupies a **hybrid position**: evangelical-C&MA by ordination and heritage, microchurch by ecclesial conviction, community-development by practice. The combination is uncommon — C&MA leaders rarely anchor in microchurch decentralization, and microchurch voices rarely carry C&MA institutional standing. Joel's distinctive position is bridging those two worlds.

## Distinctive positions

- **Hope is an active verb, not a feeling.** Consistent presence among the hopeless is itself an act of defiance — the theological weight of "showing up" is doing more work in his frame than most American evangelical vocabulary allows.
- **Indigenous leadership is theological, not pragmatic.** The refusal of savior models isn't a tactic; it's a conviction about how the Kingdom actually spreads.
- **Post-industrial communities are a primary mission context**, not an exception. Aliquippa-shaped theology applies more widely than Aliquippa.
- **Decentralization is ecclesially normative**, not novel. Multiple small simultaneous expressions are closer to the grain of the New Testament church than centralized congregational forms.
- **Biblical justice matters to God**, and must matter to the church — preached as a series at Gospel Tabernacle, not treated as optional.
- **Reconciliation is preachable and practicable** in racially diverse economically distressed communities (the May 2021 sermon co-preached with Kiarra Thorne is the clearest artifact of this conviction).
- **Everyone is called to play.** Full participation of all believers, not just credentialed leaders.
- **Collaboration > branding.** Named preference for setting aside names and agendas to accomplish more together.
- **Prayer and Spirit-led life are non-negotiable** — not add-ons to strategic work, but the condition of it.

## Theological evolution

Joel's theological voice is remarkably consistent from the earliest available material (the Aliquippa Impact quote set circa 2016) through the Exponential podcast (2022) through the Tampa Underground announcement (2025). The through-line is **hope as protest via consistent presence** — whether the specific vocabulary of the moment is "showing up," "indigenous leadership," "everyone gets to play," or "decentralized expressions." There is no evidence of a paradigm shift in his thinking; his work reads as a 20-year deepening of a stable set of convictions rather than a sequence of intellectual reinventions.

The most significant *expansion* visible across the record is the move from **single-neighborhood practice** (Aliquippa Impact) → **regional network** (Greenhouse Network) → **denominational leadership** (CMA Western PA) → **national / global movement** (Underground Network). The theology does not change; the scope does. His Movemental value is less about presenting novel frameworks and more about helping a network the size of the Underground hear the same settled convictions in a fresh voice.

## Where his theology overlaps with Movemental / Alan Hirsch's corpus

- **Missional-incarnational instinct** (shared with Hirsch, Frost, Halter, Brisco)
- **Decentralization / multiplication** (shared with the Underground family and broader movement ecosystem)
- **Apostolic-movement logic** (compatible with Hirsch's APEST framing, though not explicitly cited)
- **Communitas, shared weakness, liminality** (aligned posture; not framed in Hirsch's vocabulary but consonant with it)

## Where his voice is distinctive within that ecosystem

- **Post-industrial community context** (Aliquippa specificity) — most microchurch voices come from cities with different economic profiles
- **Hope as protest** framing — linguistically uncommon in the microchurch ecosystem
- **C&MA institutional standing** — unusual among microchurch practitioners
- **Youth development-first entry point** — he came to ecclesiology through children's ministry, which shapes his frame
- **Community-development vocabulary** (asset-based, indigenous leadership) runs through his work more explicitly than through most microchurch peers
