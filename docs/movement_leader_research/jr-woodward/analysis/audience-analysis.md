## Audience Profile

### Who Is the Audience?

**Primary audience**: Church planters and missional community leaders actively starting or sustaining new faith communities in North America. Typically ages 28-45, seminary-educated or in training, often bi-vocational, connected through V3, Forge, Missio Alliance, or Exponential networks. They use Woodward's polycentric leadership framework and "four spaces of belonging" model as practical tools for community design.

**Secondary audience**: Senior pastors and denominational leaders questioning hierarchical leadership models and seeking alternatives after witnessing leadership scandals and burnout. Ages 35-55, established in ministry, drawn to Woodward through *The Scandal of Leadership* and podcast appearances on shows like Theology in the Raw and The Roys Report. They are looking for theological frameworks to diagnose power dysfunction in their organizations.

**Tertiary audience**: Seminary students and academic missiologists studying missional ecclesiology, powers and principalities theology, and movement theory. They encounter Woodward through assigned reading at Fuller, Central Seminary, Missio Seminary, or America Evangelical University, and through his Academia.edu profile and Google Scholar citations (~274). Also includes justice-oriented Christians who discovered his Substack "Faithful Resistance" through its intersection of theology and political analysis.

### Positioning Summary

JR Woodward occupies a distinctive niche in the missional ecclesiology space:

- **Smaller than Hirsch** in raw reach (~25-40K vs. ~150-200K) but with a more cohesive, practitioner-dense audience
- **Stronger academic credentials** than most peers (PhD Manchester, 4 adjunct roles, 274 citations, active Academia.edu profile)
- **Highest-rated recent book** in this peer group (*Scandal of Leadership* at 4.77/5 -- exceptional for any non-fiction title)
- **Most politically engaged voice** through "Faithful Resistance" Substack -- differentiates from Hirsch's more framework-focused content
- **Training pipeline is the anchor**: V3's 1,000+ trained planters represent the most deeply engaged audience segment
- **Digital presence is the gap**: No hosted podcast, no YouTube channel, no Wikipedia page, modest social following relative to institutional influence

## Movemental Audience Implications

- **Content format priority**: Training programs and structured courses should be the anchor offering. Woodward's audience is formed through multi-month cohorts (V3 discernment, V3 cohort, V3 immersions), not weekend conferences or viral social content. A Movemental-hosted digital training pathway could extend V3's reach beyond the current cohort model. Books are the secondary anchor -- his titles have excellent ratings (4.25 avg; 4.77 for *Scandal*) and serve as entry points to deeper engagement.

- **Topic focus**: *The Scandal of Leadership* content should be the lead -- it addresses the most urgent felt need in his audience (power abuse in the church), has the highest engagement signal (4.77/5, podcast concentration), and intersects with a post-2024 cultural moment around institutional accountability. Polycentric leadership / Ephesians 4 equipping content follows as the constructive alternative. "Faithful Resistance" political theology is a growth area that differentiates Woodward from every other author in the Movemental stable.

- **Cross-pollination with Alan Hirsch**: This is the highest-value author pairing on the platform. Hirsch wrote forewords for two of Woodward's three books, published him through 100 Movements, and shares the APEST/Ephesians 4 framework. Hirsch provides the macro-framework (mDNA, APEST, movement theory); Woodward provides the diagnostic (what goes wrong with leadership) and the political-prophetic edge (Faithful Resistance). A "Hirsch + Woodward" content track on polycentric leadership could be a flagship offering. Dan White Jr. (co-author of *The Church as Movement*) is the natural third voice.

- **Growth opportunity**: The biggest growth opportunity is digital infrastructure. Woodward has no hosted podcast, no personal YouTube channel, no free online course, no centralized video library, and a modest Substack (~hundreds of subscribers on a ~1-year-old publication). His content is scattered across Seminary Now, V3, conference recordings, and 10+ third-party podcast feeds. Movemental could centralize this dispersed content and provide the digital platform his institutional influence warrants. A Movemental-produced podcast or interview series featuring Woodward would fill a major gap.

- **Academic audience is unique**: Woodward's 477 Academia.edu followers, 274 Google Scholar citations, and 4 seminary adjunct roles give him a credentialed academic audience that most Movemental authors cannot match (Hirsch excepted). This audience can be reached through academic content tracks, syllabus adoption programs, and institutional partnerships.

- **Monetization signal**: Moderate-Strong. Woodward's audience already pays for content: V3 cohort training fees (multi-month programs), seminary tuition at 4 institutions, book purchases, Substack paid tier ($10/month or $100/year), and Praxis Gathering conference registration. The audience is comprised of church planters and denominational leaders who invest in leadership development. However, the audience is smaller than Hirsch's and skews toward bi-vocational planters with tighter budgets -- pricing should reflect this. The V3 training model (cohort-based, relationship-intensive) suggests the audience values depth over breadth and would pay premium for high-touch digital formation, not just self-paced video content.
