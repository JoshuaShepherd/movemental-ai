## 2. PhD thesis (metadata + abstract + structure)

**Full title**: *Missional Leadership, Mimetic Desire, and a Theology of the Powers*  
**Author (thesis title page)**: Gailand A Woodward Jr.  
**Award**: Doctor of Philosophy, University of Manchester, Faculty of Humanities, **2020**  
**School**: School of Arts, Languages and Cultures  
**Research site**: Nazarene Theological College, Manchester  
**Final word count (thesis)**: 80,385 words  
**Dedication**: To the V3 Church Planting Movement  

### Research question (from abstract)

> How might a theology of the Powers help missional leaders to practice a spirituality which reshapes their desires for the sake of discipleship, community, and mission?

### Abstract (condensed from thesis; see PDF for full wording)

All ecclesial leadership exercises power — legitimately through Christ-like holy love, or illegitimately through worldly selfish ambition. The thesis addresses the “fall” of leaders into **domineering** leadership (pride, manipulation, status-seeking, kingdom-building), which hurts people, misrepresents Christ, and damages witness — especially acute for **missional** leadership that claims visible kingdom witness.

Missional literature has raised **imitation** of leaders and human **desire**, and has begun to retrieve **Powers** language, but has not adequately linked these threads. **Walter Wink** (Powers as “domination system”; leaders who mimic the Powers open the church to the demonic) and **René Girard** (imitation and desire tied to the Powers) are combined to build an **imitation-based conceptual framework**: missional leadership ↔ mimetic desire ↔ theology of the Powers. Leaders “fall” when they mimic the Powers through malformed desire; redeemed leadership is **imitating Christ**, reshaping desire toward discipleship, community, and mission. **There is no neutral ground: not to imitate Christ is to imitate the Powers.** Missional leadership is ultimately about being an example worth imitating.

### Chapter outline (from thesis table of contents)

1. **The Challenge of Missional Leadership** — nature of missional leadership; misshaping/reshaping; themes (spirituality, culture/world, imitation); Van Gelder, Roxburgh, etc.  
2. **The Subversion of Leadership** — 1 Peter / Houston; imitation-based framework; three directions of imitation; Powers rediscovery.  
3. **Leadership and the Powers** — Walter Wink: naming, unmasking, engaging; Pauline principalities; domination system; evaluation.  
4. **Leadership and Imitation** — René Girard; mimetic cycle; Powers in James; positive mimesis; evaluation.  
5. **Missional Leadership Worthy of Imitation** — William Stringfellow (image, institution, ideology) mapped to **identity, praxis, telos**; “scandal of leadership”; Christlike imitation; suggestions toward a **catechism** for leader formation.

**Conclusion** — summary, significance (missiological contribution; deeper diagnosis than psychology-only accounts), directions for further study (e.g., ideology in collective life, James K.A. Smith on liturgy and desire, positive mimesis in community), closing prayer for leaders.
