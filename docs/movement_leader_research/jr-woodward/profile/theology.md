# Theological Profile: JR Woodward

**Status**: Complete
**Last updated**: 2026-04-12

---

## Core Thesis

The Western church has adopted worldly patterns of power and domination that damage its witness, cripple its mission, and fuel the epidemic of fallen leaders. Recovery requires two interrelated moves: (1) cultivating a polycentric, shared leadership culture rooted in the full fivefold ministry of Ephesians 4, and (2) practicing a self-emptying spirituality grounded in the imitation of Christ rather than the imitation of the Powers. The church is fundamentally a movement -- neighborhood-rooted, discipleship-fueled, boundary-crossing -- and its leadership must reflect the kenotic, communal character of the triune God.

## Theological Tradition

**Primary**: Missional-incarnational theology, rooted in post-Christendom missiology and practical theology

**Secondary influences**: Anabaptist-adjacent ecclesiology (emphasis on community, peace, incarnational presence), Wesleyan-Methodist tradition (PhD at University of Manchester through Nazarene Theological College / Manchester Wesley Research Centre), liberation theology motifs (Oscar Romero as exemplar, Solis Foundation work with the poor in Kenya), and charismatic movement dynamics (studied rather than personally claimed)

**Denominational context**: Has operated across denominational lines throughout his career. Founded NLCF as part of Great Commission Ministries; V3 has ties to Virginia Baptists. Not bound to a single tradition; works with church planters from multiple backgrounds.

|-----------|-------------|----------------|
| **Polycentric Leadership** | Leadership structured around "many centers that interrelate" rather than hierarchical (top-down) or flat (undifferentiated) models. Reflects the communal nature of the triune God. Activates the priesthood of all believers more effectively than CEO-pastor models. | *Creating a Missional Culture* (2012) |
| **Five Culture Creators** | Woodward's practical application of Ephesians 4:11 APEST gifts, each renamed to emphasize their culture-shaping function: Apostle = **Dream Awakener**, Prophet = **Heart Revealer**, Evangelist = **Story Teller**, Pastor = **Soul Healer**, Teacher = **Light Giver**. Each creates a distinct environment within missional culture. | *Creating a Missional Culture* (2012) |
| **Five Environments** | Five cultural environments that a healthy missional community cultivates: (1) Learning, (2) Healing, (3) Welcoming, (4) Liberating, (5) Thriving. Each environment corresponds to one of the five culture creators. | *Creating a Missional Culture* (2012) |
| **The Cultural Web** | Six interconnected dimensions that shape congregational and neighborhood culture: Language, Artifacts, Rituals, Ethics, Institutions, Guiding Narratives. Leaders must understand all six to cultivate missional culture. | *Creating a Missional Culture* (2012) |
| **Five Cultural Megashifts** | Five macro-level transitions that missional leaders must navigate: (1) Media: print/broadcast to digital, (2) Philosophy: modernity to postmodernity, (3) Science: classical to emergent, (4) Geography: rural to urban, (5) Religion: Christendom to post-Christendom. | *Creating a Missional Culture* (2012) |
| **Eight Movement Competencies** | Eight necessary competencies for creating movemental, neighborhood-rooted churches. Framework for church planter training in V3. | *The Church as Movement* (2016, with Dan White Jr.) |
| **Four Spaces of Belonging** | A framework for understanding how discipleship and mission happen across four relational spaces of varying intimacy and size. | Articles and teaching; [V3 Movement](https://thev3movement.org/2014/01/14/discipleship-and-the-four-spaces-of-belonging/) |
| **Theology of the Powers** | A robust synthesis drawing on Walter Wink (spiritual powers embodied in systems), Rene Girard (mimetic desire and scapegoating), and William Stringfellow (Image, Institution, Ideology as categories of how powers operate). Argues that principalities and powers are created good but fallen and redeemable, while Satan/demonic forces are emergent realities without redemptive possibility. | PhD thesis (2020), *The Scandal of Leadership* (2023) |
| **Imitation-Based Leadership Framework** | Leaders are inescapably imitative beings (per Girard). When they imitate worldly power models, they become domineering; when they imitate Christ's kenotic self-emptying, they become life-giving servants. "Not to imitate Christ is to imitate the Powers." | PhD thesis (2020), *The Scandal of Leadership* (2023) |
| **Three Contours of Leadership** | Framework for diagnosing leadership health: (1) Identity -- who leaders are, (2) Praxis -- how they lead and relate, (3) Telos -- what they value and pursue. Domineering leaders malform across all three. | *The Scandal of Leadership* (2023) |

## Distinctive Positions

- **Polycentric over hierarchical or flat**: Woodward rejects both top-down hierarchy (CEO-pastor) and undifferentiated flat structures, advocating instead for multiple interrelated centers of leadership. This reflects Trinitarian theology -- God's own communal nature.
- **APEST as culture creators, not just roles**: The fivefold gifts are not merely leadership offices but culture-shaping vocations. Each creates a distinct environment (learning, healing, welcoming, liberating, thriving) necessary for a fully missional community.
- **Domineering leadership is a theological problem, not just a psychological one**: The epidemic of fallen church leaders cannot be adequately diagnosed through psychology or management theory alone. It requires a theology of the Powers that understands how spiritual forces operate through institutional systems.
- **Mimetic desire shapes leaders unconsciously**: Drawing on Girard, Woodward argues that desires are not self-generated but are formed through models. Leaders who unconsciously absorb worldly models of power will inevitably reproduce domination, regardless of their stated theology.
- **Church as neighborhood-rooted movement**: The church should be locally embedded, not institutionally detached. Woodward himself served on a neighborhood council; V3 trains planters to root churches in specific neighborhoods.
- **Kenotic leadership as the alternative**: The solution to domineering leadership is not better management but self-emptying (kenotic) spirituality that reshapes desires through the imitation of Christ. Oscar Romero serves as Woodward's primary exemplar.
- **Christendom's corporate adoption distorts ecclesial practice**: Woodward traces how Christianity moved from "a fellowship in Palestine, a philosophy in Greece, an institution in Rome, a government in Europe, and an enterprise in America" -- and argues that the corporate-enterprise model must be dismantled in favor of movement ecclesiology.
- **"Being" precedes "doing" in mission**: Missional identity must be established before missional action. God changes people by changing their identity, their sense of self-definition, before calling them to particular tasks.

## Intellectual Genealogy

**Shaped by**:
- **Walter Wink** -- Theology of the Powers; naming, unmasking, and engaging principalities embodied in systems; the "Domination System" as a spiritual-structural reality
- **Rene Girard** -- Mimetic desire theory; triangular desire (we desire what our models desire); scapegoat mechanism; Jesus as the one who reveals and disrupts the scapegoating pattern
- **William Stringfellow** -- Prophetic resistance to the Powers; analysis of how powers manifest through Image, Institution, and Ideology; the "power of resurrection" against the "power of death"
- **Oscar Romero** -- Exemplar of kenotic, Christlike leadership under persecution; positive model of resisting the Powers through self-emptying service
- **Lesslie Newbigin** -- Post-Christendom missiology (shared influence with Alan Hirsch)
- **James K.A. Smith** -- Cultural liturgies and the formation of desire (referenced in reviews of *Creating a Missional Culture*)
- **Henri Nouwen** -- Spiritual formation and pastoral identity (cited in *Creating a Missional Culture*)
- **Miroslav Volf** -- Theology of embrace and reconciliation (cited in *Creating a Missional Culture*)

**In conversation with**:
- Alan Hirsch (closest senior collaborator; wrote forewords for two of Woodward's books; shared APEST/missional movement framework)
- Dan White Jr. (co-author, co-leader of V3)
- David Fitch (fellow missional theologian; contributor to *The Scandal of Leadership*)
- Amos Yong (Pentecostal theologian; contributor to *The Scandal of Leadership*)
- Preston Sprinkle (extended podcast conversation on leadership and power)
- Jesse Eubanks (V3 co-leader)

**Pushes back against**:
- **CEO-pastor / hierarchical leadership**: The concentration of power in a single senior leader, which Woodward sees as a corporate import that distorts biblical ecclesiology and creates conditions for abuse.
- **Attractional church model**: The "come and see" paradigm that builds institutions rather than embedding in neighborhoods. Woodward advocates incarnational, "go and be" presence.
- **Surface-level diagnosis of leadership failure**: Approaches that treat fallen leaders as individual moral failures without examining the systemic, spiritual, and mimetic forces that shape desire and behavior.
- **Christendom assumptions**: The default assumption that the church occupies a privileged cultural position. Woodward works from a post-Christendom, missionary posture.
- **Shepherd-Teacher dominance**: Like Hirsch, Woodward argues that the Western church has marginalized apostolic, prophetic, and evangelistic functions in favor of pastoral care and teaching -- creating an imbalanced, maintenance-oriented church.

## Theological Evolution

Woodward's theology has developed through three distinct phases, each building on the previous:

1. **Missional culture phase (2003-2012)**: Rooted in his Virginia Tech and Los Angeles church planting experience, Woodward developed his practical theology of polycentric leadership, the five culture creators, and the cultural web. *Creating a Missional Culture* (2012) is the primary output. The focus is on how to build missional communities from the inside out -- culture as the unseen force that shapes a church's effectiveness more than vision or strategy.

2. **Movement ecclesiology phase (2012-2016)**: Working with Dan White Jr. and deepening his involvement with V3, Woodward expanded from individual church culture to the dynamics of church-planting movements. *The Church as Movement* (2016) outlines the competencies needed for movement multiplication. The shift is from "how do we make one church missional?" to "how do we catalyze a movement of missional churches?"

3. **Power, desire, and imitation phase (2016-2023)**: Woodward's PhD research marked a significant deepening into the theological roots of leadership failure. Drawing on Wink, Girard, and Stringfellow, he moved from the practical-constructive (what healthy leadership looks like) to the diagnostic-prophetic (why leaders fall and what spiritual forces are at work). *The Scandal of Leadership* (2023) represents his most theologically sophisticated work. The turn toward desire formation and mimetic theory adds a dimension absent from his earlier work.

Throughout all three phases, Woodward's commitment to neighborhood-rooted, incarnational, movement-oriented ecclesiology has remained constant. The progression is from practical culture-building to movement-catalyzing to deep diagnostic theology -- each layer adding depth to the same fundamental vision.
