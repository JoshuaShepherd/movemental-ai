# Calling Profile

Kate Coleman's vocational arc has been a sustained call to form leaders — and especially to clear the obstacles, internal and structural, that keep gifted people from leading well. The call surfaced early and against resistance. She came to faith as a science student at Southampton, was drawn into leadership soon after, and took her first significant role as an elder in a church that did not initially welcome women in leadership. She has named that season a "baptism of fire," and it set the question she has carried ever since: what is it that holds leaders back, and what would it take to set them free?

That question moved from the pew to the pulpit to the page. Her first pastorate at Chalk Farm Baptist Church in London made her the first Black woman accredited as a Baptist minister in the UK — not a goal she set out to claim, but a threshold she crossed because the call would not let her stop short of it. In 2000 she planted The Regeneration Centre in Birmingham, learning the costly, on-the-ground work of building a church from nothing. The "firsts" accumulated: President of the Baptist Union of Great Britain, Chair of the Evangelical Alliance Council, preacher at the opening of national party conferences, contributor to United Nations leadership curriculum for women. In each, the through-line is the same — she went first so that others could follow.

The founding of Next Leadership gathered the calling into a vocation she could give the rest of her life to. There, the pastoral instinct became a discipline: strategic advising, mentoring, and coaching for leaders across the public, private, voluntary, and church sectors. *7 Deadly Sins of Women in Leadership* was the calling written down — the seven self-defeating behaviours named so plainly the book outgrew its self-published run and was taken up by Zondervan. *Metamorph* extended it from diagnosis to transformation, reading godly self-discovery, healthy relationships, and Spirit-led mission through the ordinary, extraordinary people of Scripture.

Her calling now is curatorial as much as instructional. After thirty-five years she holds an enormous store of judgment — who is ready for which room, why a particular leader keeps stalling, how a transformation actually sequences — and the task of this season is to translate that embodied wisdom into forms others can reach. The four questions she returns to in talks and coaching — "Why me? Why here? Why this? Why now?" — are as much her own ongoing examination as a tool she hands to others. The vocation is not finished; it is being handed on.
