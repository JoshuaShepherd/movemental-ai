# Theology & Leadership Emphases

## Transformation as the heart of leadership

Coleman's central conviction is that leadership change is inseparable from personal transformation. The title *Metamorph* names it directly: leaders are not merely trained but transformed — a metamorphosis of life that issues in a transformation of leadership. She frames this transformation along three lines: **godly self-discovery, healthy relationships, and Spirit-led mission.**

## Scripture read through ordinary, extraordinary people

Her theological method is biographical and narrative. In *Metamorph* she reads leadership formation through the lives of biblical figures — Moses, Esther, the Woman at the Well — treating them as "extraordinary, ordinary people" whose stories disclose how God forms leaders through real, unfinished lives rather than ideal ones.

## Naming the self-defeating

A distinctive theological-pastoral emphasis is her willingness to name what holds leaders back. The "seven deadly sins" framing turns inward: limiting self-perceptions, failure to set boundaries, inadequate personal vision, an unhealthy work-life rhythm, the "disease to please," colluding instead of confronting, and neglecting family and inner circle. These are treated not as moral failings to be shamed but as patterns to be surfaced, owned, and transformed.

## Women, calling, and the Spirit

Coleman holds a clear biblical-equality conviction: where the Spirit is given room, women rise to lead. Her own historic "firsts" — first Black woman accredited Baptist minister, first Black woman President of the Baptist Union, first Black woman Chair of the Evangelical Alliance Council — are read by her not as personal achievement but as evidence of what the Spirit does when calling is honoured rather than constrained. Her association with CBE International situates her in the biblical-equality tradition.

## Formation over performance

Throughout, the emphasis falls on inner formation as the engine of durable, public leadership. Rhythm, boundaries, self-perception, and relationships are treated as theological matters — the soul-work without which leadership becomes self-defeating. The recurring diagnostic "Why me? Why here? Why this? Why now?" is a tool for re-anchoring leaders in calling rather than in role or achievement.
