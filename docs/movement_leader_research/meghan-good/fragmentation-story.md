## The Story

Meghan Larissa Good has spent **more than sixteen years** as a pastor and is, by one specific measure, among the most decorated preachers of her generation: in **2018 she became the first-ever recipient of the Frederick Buechner Award for Excellence in Preaching**. She studied at **Gordon College** (BA, Biblical & Theological Studies and Philosophy, 2006), **Duke Divinity School** (MDiv, 2009 — followed by work at Duke's Center for Reconciliation and teaching in Uganda), and **Portland Seminary** (DMin, 2018), where her dissertation, *Between Breaths: The Dialogic Shape of Christian Authority*, became the engine for her first book. She pastored **Albany Mennonite Church** in Oregon (~2008–2013) and then served more than a decade as **Lead Pastor of Trinity Mennonite Church** in Glendale, Arizona (~2013–May 2025). She teaches in the **Doctor of Ministry in Leadership** program at **Anabaptist Mennonite Biblical Seminary** and has served on its Board of Directors.

Her two books form a single argument in two movements. *The Bible Unwrapped: Making Sense of Scripture Today* (Herald Press, 2018, foreword by **Gregory Boyd**) taught ordinary readers to interpret all of Scripture through the lens of Jesus and won **Outreach Magazine's 2019 Resource of the Year** in Theology/Biblical Studies. *Divine Gravity: Sparking a Movement to Recover a Better Christian Story* (Herald Press, 2023, foreword by **Dennis R. Edwards**) moved from *how to read* to *what kind of church to become* — a call for a Spirit-led, grassroots new reformation. Its endorser list — **Richard Mouw, Brian McLaren, Greg Boyd, David Gushee** — punches far above the weight of a small denominational press; Gushee called her "a rising star of the next generation."

Then, in **May 2025**, she did the thing her second book argues for. She **resigned her twelve-year pastorate at Trinity Mennonite** to take a movement-level role as **Resource Development Manager at Jesus Collective**, the Jesus-centered network associated with Greg Boyd, where she also **chairs the international Theology Circle**. She moved to Dayton, Ohio for the transition and began producing **daily Jesus-centered devotionals on her "Reading Jesus" YouTube channel** — 54-plus episodes walking through Luke in the first months of 2026 alone. She has, deliberately, stopped being a single-congregation pastor and become a builder of the very movement *Divine Gravity* describes.

And here is where the fragmentation becomes visible. For a voice this good — an award-winning preacher with two well-reviewed books, an Outreach award, an A-list endorser network, and a daily content engine — almost **nothing is connected to anything else, and the audience is small relative to the quality**. Her "Reading Jesus" YouTube channel, the single most valuable asset she has, is **essentially invisible from her own website** and social feeds: no embeds, no cross-posting, no funnel. Her website (meghanlarissagood.com) has a thoughtful newsletter lead magnet — a free eBook, *Reading Scripture with Jesus* — but the journal posts don't reinforce it and the YouTube series doesn't point to it. Her **social follower counts are not even publicly visible**, which is itself a signal of scale. There is **no online course**, no centralized hub, **no Wikipedia page**, and — most strikingly — **sixteen-plus years of award-caliber preaching that sits almost entirely un-archived and unreachable**. The gap here is not the gap of a megachurch leader whose reach is owned by an institution; it is the gap of an exceptional teacher whose body of work has never been assembled into a place a stranger could find and follow.

## Synthesis

Meghan Good's fragmentation signature is the inverse of a megachurch leader's. Dave Ferguson's reach is enormous but institutionally owned; **Good's reach is modest, personally held, and almost entirely un-assembled.** Her best asset — a daily "Reading Jesus" YouTube engine producing dozens of episodes — is invisible from her own website. Her best credential — the first-ever Buechner Award for preaching — is unused as a platform. Her two books make one continuous argument but live on separate product pages with no connective tissue. Sixteen years of award-caliber sermons are essentially unreachable. And all of this lands at a once-in-a-career moment: in May 2025 she left a settled pastorate specifically to build the movement *Divine Gravity* describes, which means the value of an owned, connected platform has never been higher for her than it is right now. The trapped value here is not buried inside an institution; it is scattered across her own channels, waiting to be gathered into a single place a stranger could find on a Tuesday and follow for years.
