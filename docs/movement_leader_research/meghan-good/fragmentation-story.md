# Meghan Larissa Good — Fragmentation Story

## The Story

Meghan Larissa Good has spent close to two decades building a coherent body
of work around a single conviction — "God looks like Jesus, and that changes
everything" — and yet that body of work has no single place to live. The
durable artifacts are real and findable: two books from Herald Press, a
2019 Outreach Magazine award, a DMin from Portland Seminary, sixteen years
of pastoring in Oregon and Arizona, and now a network and teaching role at
Jesus Collective and AMBS. But the work has accumulated faster than any one
surface could hold it. A reader who finds *Divine Gravity* on Amazon has no
path to her *Reading Jesus* podcast; a listener to that podcast has no path
to her DMin teaching at AMBS; a Jesus Collective conference attendee has no
path to the sixteen years of sermons that taught her how to say any of it.
Each piece points somewhere, but nothing points to everything. The most
recent move — a weekly devotional newsletter — is the first owned channel
she has built, and it sits alone, unconnected to the corpus behind it.

## Relational Fragmentation

- **Jesus Collective** holds her network relationships — she is Resource
  Development Manager and chairs the Theology Circle, which means the network
  of leaders she mentors and convenes lives inside Jesus Collective's
  membership and conference systems (jesuscollective.com), not her own.
- **AMBS** holds her teaching relationships — the DMin in Leadership cohort
  she serves as Faculty of Record for Teaching Leadership exists on the
  seminary's student rosters and learning-plan records.
- **Trinity Mennonite Church** (Glendale, AZ) and her earlier Oregon
  congregations hold sixteen years of pastoral relationships in church
  directories and member rolls she no longer administers.
- **Co-author / collaborator adjacency to the wider missional network:** her
  two forewords place her one degree from significant figures — Greg Boyd
  (Woodland Hills Church, ReKnew) wrote the foreword to *The Bible
  Unwrapped*, and Dennis R. Edwards (Northern Seminary, Missio Alliance,
  Story of God commentary series) wrote the foreword to *Divine Gravity*.
  Through Jesus Collective she sits in the same circle as Bruxy Cavey and
  others. These ties are real but live in publisher metadata and event
  programs, not in any relationship surface she controls.
- **The personal rolodex** — the pastors, conference hosts, podcast hosts,
  and DMin students who reach her — runs through a contact form,
  connect@meghanlarissagood.com, and her own phone and memory.

## Embodied / In-Their-Head layer

This is the largest pool of value and the one most at risk of going
unwritten. Sixteen years of pastoring at Trinity Mennonite and in Oregon
produced thousands of sermons, almost none of them published under her name.
The judgment calls behind the *Introduction to Jesus-centered Preaching*
course — how she decides what makes preaching actually Jesus-centered rather
than merely Bible-quoting — exist in course notes and in the room. The
curation decisions behind the Theology Circle at Jesus Collective, and
behind conference sessions like *Reimagining Hope*, live in planning
conversations. The pragmatic wisdom of *The Bible Unwrapped* — how to teach
a congregation to read Scripture as encounter with the risen Jesus rather
than as a rulebook — was lived in adult-formation classrooms for years
before it became a book, and most of that practice never made the page. The
DMin Teaching Leadership work, where she helps students "integrate
knowledge into practice," is precisely the transfer of in-the-room wisdom
that currently happens one cohort at a time. It exists in notebooks,
coaching conversations, classrooms, and the same sermon-prep instinct
exercised a thousand times.

## Synthesis

Meghan Larissa Good's fragmentation pattern is the **teacher-pastor turned
network-builder whose published artifacts under-represent her actual body of
work.** The visible layer — two award-noted Herald Press books and a growing
podcast — is genuinely strong, but it is dispersed across retailers, partner
publications, and other people's podcast feeds with no hub connecting them.
The relational layer is held entirely inside institutions she serves but does
not own: Jesus Collective's network, AMBS's cohorts, and former
congregations' directories. And the embodied layer — sixteen years of
preaching, the curation behind a Jesus-centered preaching course, the
formation logic of *The Bible Unwrapped* lived before it was written — is
the deepest reservoir and the least captured. She has just built her first
owned channel, a newsletter, but it stands alone. The work is not to author
more; it is to gather what already exists into one place and surface the
unwritten teaching at her own pace.
