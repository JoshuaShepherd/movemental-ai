# Theological Profile: Meghan Larissa Good

**Status**: Complete
**Last updated**: 2026-05-27

---

## Core Thesis

The church's most fundamental crisis is a distorted image of God — one shaped by power, fear, violence, and cultural accommodation rather than by the person of Jesus Christ. The remedy is not merely doctrinal correction but a movement-level recovery of a "better Christian story": a story in which Jesus is the definitive revelation of who God is, the lens through which all of Scripture must be read, and the gravitational center around which a fractured church can reconvene. When the church recovers the Jesus-centered God — the God who looks like Jesus — it gains a new resource for biblical interpretation, a new vision of salvation as cosmic restoration, a new politics of self-sacrificial love over coercive power, and a new basis for unity that transcends theological and cultural fractures.

## Theological Tradition

**Primary**: Anabaptist/Mennonite — Jesus-centered, peace-church, Scripture-through-Christ, community-embodied, Spirit-led Christianity. Good consistently names the Anabaptist heritage as the tradition she is drawing on and commending, describing it as "the transformative power of the Jesus-centered approach to faith that Anabaptism represents" ([AMBS](https://ambs.edu/employees/meghan-good/)). However, she reaches well beyond Mennonite institutional boundaries, speaking to evangelical, mainline, and post-evangelical audiences.

**Secondary influences**:
- Post-evangelical and progressive evangelical convergence (Greg Boyd, Brian McLaren, David Gushee)
- Ecumenical theological retrieval (Barbara Brown Taylor's literary-preaching tradition; Marva Dawn's theology of divine weakness)
- Reconciliation theology (shaped by her post-MDiv work at Duke's Center for Reconciliation and time in Uganda)
- Narrative theology and hermeneutics (reading the Bible as a unified narrative arc culminating in Christ)

**Denominational context**: Credentialed within Mennonite Church USA (Mennonite ordination/credentialing assumed from lead pastoral role at MC USA congregations — specific ordination details UNKNOWN — needs research). Ecumenically engaged: she has spoken at Church of the Brethren events, evangelical conferences, and non-Mennonite Christian venues. Her theology is denominationally rooted but not denominationally bounded.

## Key Frameworks & Models

|-----------|-------------|----------------|
| **The Jesus Lens / "Jesus Glasses"** | Reading all of Scripture through Jesus as the supreme interpretive authority. Jesus is not "the softer side of God that gets averaged" with divine judgment; he is the fullness of God and the logic of Scripture's narrative arc. Every Old Testament text must be read toward its fulfillment in Christ. "The goal of Scripture, according to the early Christians, is to lead people to an encounter with the living Christ." | *The Bible Unwrapped* (2018); Anabaptism at 500 conference (2021); throughout her teaching |
| **Dialogic Biblical Authority** | Authority is not located in a single source (Scripture alone, tradition alone, Spirit alone) but emerges from dynamic conversation among Scripture, tradition, Spirit, reason, and community experience. Articulated formally in her DMin dissertation *Between Breaths* (2018) and embodied practically in *The Bible Unwrapped*. | Dissertation (2018); *The Bible Unwrapped* (2018) |
| **Divine Gravity / Centered-Set Ecclesiology** | Jesus himself is the gravitational center that holds together a diverse, fractured church. Borrowing from centered-set theory, Good argues that Christian community is defined not by boundary positions (who is in/out) but by trajectory of motion toward Jesus. Those drawn toward the divine grace embodied in Jesus are the church, regardless of their position on peripheral questions. | *Divine Gravity* (2023); [Anabaptist World review](https://anabaptistworld.org/starting-point-for-a-new-reformation/) |
| **The Better Christian Story (Eight Theses)** | *Divine Gravity* proposes eight interlocking convictions that constitute a recovered Christian narrative: (1) Jesus as authoritative interpretive lens for God and Scripture; (2) salvation as setting all things right on earth; (3) discipleship as acknowledging and following Jesus; (4) Christian community as both means and goal of God's transformation; (5) God's people as reconciliation ambassadors; (6) the Spirit guiding and resourcing mission; (7) sacrificial love overcoming evil; (8) church unity centering on Jesus as gravitational focal point. | *Divine Gravity* (2023) |
| **Cross-Shaped Power** | The Jesus way of exercising power is categorically different from worldly (coercive, sword-shaped) power. God's kingdom advances through self-sacrifice, vulnerability, and suffering love — not military, political, or institutional force. Jesus's temptations in Matthew 4 were genuine precisely because the use of power for righteous ends (liberation of the oppressed) was available to him; he refused. This "cross-shaped power" appears "slower, more painful, seemingly like losing before winning" but alone genuinely defeats evil. | *Divine Gravity* (2023); website journal essay "Jesus and the Devil's Bargain" (2024) |
| **Wounded Image of God** | Many contemporary Christians suffer from a "profound wound in their image of God" — shaped by distorted theologies of divine violence, wrath, and power that contradict what is revealed in Jesus. Healing requires direct encounter with the Jesus-centered God. "As our image of God is transformed by Jesus, we are set free to experience deeper transformation in our own humanity." | [meghanlarissagood.com/about](https://meghanlarissagood.com/about/); throughout her work |
| **The New Reformation** | The church is living through another reformation-scale recalibration, analogous to the sixteenth-century Radical Reformation. Good defines reformation as "a repentance movement, a regularly required period of communal reflection and reorientation." The signs include post-pandemic deconstruction, ecclesial scandals, and a crisis of biblical authority — but also a spontaneous Spirit-led movement rediscovering Jesus-centered faith. | *Divine Gravity* (2023); [Anabaptist World](https://anabaptistworld.org/starting-point-for-a-new-reformation/) |
| **Salvation as Cosmic Restoration** | Good's soteriology is expansive: salvation is not primarily the rescue of individual souls from hell but God's project of "setting all things right" — restoring all of creation, reconciling divided humanity, and renewing the cosmos. This follows the Anabaptist peace-church tradition's integration of redemption and shalom. | *Divine Gravity* (2023); MennoCon21 (2021) |

## Distinctive Positions

- **Jesus as the definitive revelation of God**: Good's central commitment is that Jesus does not reveal one aspect of God alongside other competing divine attributes. Jesus is the fullness of God (Colossians 1:19), God's exact imprint (Hebrews 1:1-3), the original Word (John 1:1). This means that divine violence, wrath toward enemies, and coercive power as displayed in some Old Testament texts must be interpreted through — not simply averaged with — what Jesus reveals. "Jesus is not the softer side of God that gets averaged" with anything. — [Anabaptist World, planning-an-anabaptist-bible](https://anabaptistworld.org/planning-an-anabaptist-bible/)

- **Christocentric over bibliocentric hermeneutics**: Good does not relativize Scripture — she insists on reading it faithfully and in its entirety — but she insists that Christ, not a flat reading of the whole text, is the interpretive authority. This is the Anabaptist way and, she argues, the theology of the early church: "The goal of Scripture, according to the early Christians, is to lead people to an encounter with the living Christ." — [Anabaptist World](https://anabaptistworld.org/planning-an-anabaptist-bible/)

- **Nonviolence is not optional but is intrinsic to the gospel**: The cross-shaped power framework means that coercive force — even for righteous ends — is a "devil's bargain" rejected by Jesus. Good is explicit that the kingdom does not advance by the sword. This integrates her Anabaptist peace-church heritage with her Christocentric theology: nonviolence is not simply a political stance but flows from the very character of God revealed in Jesus.

- **Jesus and peacemaking are inseparable**: At MennoCon21, Good challenged Mennonites who pursue peace work without explicit Christ-proclamation. She grounded this in Ephesians 1:10 (Christ as the culmination of God's purposes) and Ephesians 2:14 (Jesus as our peace). Reconciliation without a center of gravity in something "bigger and better" lacks the power to hold together those who would naturally repel each other. "The best thing in the world is being with Jesus and participating in his work." — [Anabaptist World](https://anabaptistworld.org/mennocon-question-do-peacemakers-need-jesus/)

- **Centered-set over bounded-set ecclesiology**: The church is not defined by its doctrinal fences but by its gravitational orientation toward Jesus. This allows Good to affirm a broad, Spirit-led movement of Jesus-centered Christians across denominational lines while maintaining theological content: the center (Jesus) is non-negotiable; the trajectory toward him is the defining criterion.

- **History as divine-human partnership, not divine determinism**: Good resists both naive providentialism and fatalism. Drawing on 1 Samuel 8 (God granting Israel's request for a king "while noting this 'answer to prayer' does not reflect God's own judgment"), she argues that God permits human choice and its consequences as a teaching mechanism. God is "managing hundreds, thousands, millions of storylines all at once." — "Four Forgotten Truths," [meghanlarissagood.com](https://meghanlarissagood.com/2024/11/09/four-forgotten-truths/)

- **God's kingdom advances from the margins, not from power centers**: Good consistently pushes against Christians' emotional investment in worldly power structures, arguing that God works from "Bethlehem and Capernaum" rather than Rome and Jerusalem. This is a political-theological position with Anabaptist roots: the church is a contrast community, not a power broker.

- **Biblical authority is dialogic, not monolithic**: Her DMin dissertation's central argument — that the Bible's authority functions through conversation among multiple sources and communities rather than as a self-interpreting, univocal text — undergirds her entire approach to hermeneutics. This positions her against both fundamentalist biblicism and liberal dismissal, toward an Anabaptist communal reading practice.

- **True prophetic leadership is marked by suffering, not success**: Drawing on Paul's "boasting in weakness" (2 Corinthians 11) and Jesus's warnings about false messiahs (Mark 13), Good argues that spiritual authority flows from costly discipleship and sacrificial love, not charisma or rhetorical skill. Authentic Christian leaders "bear the cross marks. Not just from their enemies, but for their enemies, for love of them." — "The Test of a Prophet," [meghanlarissagood.com](https://meghanlarissagood.com/2024/08/03/the-test-of-a-prophet/)

## View of Scripture

Good holds a high view of Scripture as the witness that leads people to Christ, while rejecting both fundamentalist inerrancy and skeptical dismissal. Her position:

- **Scripture as sacred narrative arc**: The Bible tells a unified story from creation to new creation, with all threads converging in and being redefined by Jesus. Her 2026 lecture series, "All the Stories are Jesus," exemplifies this: she walks through creation, exodus, temple, kingdom, wisdom, and exile, showing how each thread culminates in Christ.

- **Genre-sensitive reading**: *The Bible Unwrapped* emphasizes that readers must attend to literary genre — what kind of text is this? — before applying it. Confusing law, poetry, prophecy, and apocalyptic leads to misreading.

- **Christ as the canon within the canon**: Not all Scripture speaks with equal directness to the character of God. Jesus is the supreme disclosure; other texts are read in his light. This does not mean dismissing difficult texts but reading them through, not alongside, Christ.

- **Authority as community practice**: Derived from her DMin work, authority emerges in the lived conversation of Scripture, Spirit, tradition, reason, and community. No single locus suffices alone.

## Eschatology and Hope

Good's eschatology is integrative and hope-oriented rather than escapist. Salvation encompasses "setting all things right on earth" — a present-and-future, cosmic-and-particular restoration project. She speaks of the church as channels of divine power capable of transformation that exceeds what we have yet attempted: "We can be channels of power beyond ourselves, but we've tried it so little, we don't know the difference." — [Anabaptist World](https://anabaptistworld.org/mennocon-question-do-peacemakers-need-jesus/)

Her commencement theology frames this eschatologically: the community's "primary work is to proclaim to the world its glorious destiny and to rejoice ahead of time in the victory that is overcoming everything" — a proleptic participation in Christ's triumph already won. The "broken world has been saved and it will be saved by Jesus Christ" — [AMBS](https://ambs.edu/graduates-urged-to-pursue-ministry-of-celebration). This grounds her activist agenda in eschatological confidence rather than anxious striving.

## Intellectual Genealogy

**Shaped by**:
- **Anabaptist tradition** (especially the Radical Reformation's Christocentric hermeneutics and peace theology) — foundational
- **Gregory Boyd** (open/cruciform theism; Christocentric hermeneutic; "cross-shaped" power) — closest collaborative theological influence; their work is mutually reinforcing
- **Marva Dawn** (*Powers, Weakness, and the Tabernacling of God*) — theology of divine weakness and kenotic power; cited as recommended resource
- **Frederick Buechner** (literary preaching tradition; narrative theology of grace) — the award named in his honor acknowledges a deep affinity with his approach; Good's narrative, accessible theological writing bears Buechner's influence
- **Barbara Brown Taylor** (embodied, literary preaching; interviewed by Good in 2013) — aesthetic and homiletical influence
- **Nathan Hatch** (*The Democratization of American Christianity*) — cited as recommended reading on reformations and grassroots religious movements; Good applies this historical lens to the present "new reformation"
- **Duke Divinity tradition** (Stanley Hauerwas and the church-as-contrast-community strand, though Good applies it with more irenic and less adversarial tone)

**In conversation with**:
- Brian McLaren (progressive evangelical reform; endorsed *Divine Gravity*)
- David P. Gushee (Christian ethics; endorsed *Divine Gravity*)
- Scot McKnight (accessible New Testament scholarship; endorsed *The Bible Unwrapped*)
- Dennis R. Edwards (Black Anabaptist theology; contributed to *Divine Gravity*)
- Richard J. Mouw (Reformed evangelical irenicism; endorsed *Divine Gravity* — a cross-tradition endorsement)

**Pushes back against**:
- **"Knee-jerk biblicism"**: Flat, literalistic readings that ignore genre, narrative arc, and the Christ-center — *The Bible Unwrapped* is the sustained rejoinder
- **Skeptical dismissal**: Academic or cultural dismissal of Scripture's authority and coherence — Good holds both together
- **Distorted theologies of divine violence and coercion**: Any theology that places God's violence on the same level as God's self-revelation in Jesus
- **Peace without Jesus**: Mennonite peace work that loses its explicitly Christ-centered anchor — her MennoCon21 address directly challenged this tendency
- **Worldly definitions of Christian success and authority**: Charisma, rhetorical power, and institutional prestige as markers of spiritual authority — replaced by cross-bearing
- **Christian celebrity culture**: "The Search for Heroes" (2024 essay) distinguishes authentic spiritual character from celebrity appeal
- **Church as power center**: Any vision of the church as a political/cultural power broker rather than a contrast community advancing from the margins

## Theological Evolution

Good's theology has developed through three recognizable phases, each building on the previous:

1. **Reconciliation and incarnational phase (2006–2013)**: Shaped by Duke Divinity, the Center for Reconciliation, Uganda, and Albany Mennonite. The central questions were: What does it mean for the church to embody reconciliation? What is the relationship between Jesus and shalom? This phase produced a pastor committed to the social-political dimensions of the gospel and the peace tradition of Anabaptism, but as yet without the crystallized "Jesus lens" framework.

2. **Hermeneutics and Scripture phase (2013–2018)**: The DMin at Portland Seminary catalyzed the articulation of what had been an intuition into a systematic hermeneutical framework. *The Bible Unwrapped* is the primary output. The focus is on how to read Scripture faithfully in a post-biblical-authority-crisis world — neither biblicism nor dismissal, but Christ-centered, genre-sensitive, dialogic engagement.

3. **Movement and story phase (2018–present)**: *Divine Gravity* represents Good's most expansive theological vision — from how to read the Bible (phase 2) to what the whole Christian story means and how to catalyze a movement around it. The "better Christian story," the new reformation, Jesus Collective, and her post-pastoral network work all belong to this phase. The frame shifts from congregation to movement; from hermeneutics to missiology; from the local to the global.

Throughout all three phases, the unchanging center is Jesus: who he is, what he reveals about God, and what it means to follow him. The progression is from incarnational ethics to hermeneutical framework to movement theology — each layer adding scope to the same fundamental Christological conviction.
