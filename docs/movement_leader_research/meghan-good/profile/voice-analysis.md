## Voice Summary

Meghan Larissa Good writes and preaches as a literary-narrative theologian: someone who moves between careful exegesis, vivid story, and cultural diagnosis with apparent ease, and who consistently brings theological weight down into the register of accessible, direct prose. She is an award-winning preacher in the tradition of Frederick Buechner — her prose has Buechner's quality of theological honesty, the willingness to name doubt alongside conviction, and the use of the specific concrete detail to carry abstract truth. But her register is distinctly her own: warmer, more activist in orientation, and more explicitly Anabaptist than Buechner's Calvinist irony.

Where JR Woodward is a diagnostic framework-builder and Alan Hirsch is a prophetic provocateur, Good occupies a third position: the **narrative theologian-preacher**. She is at her most distinctive when anchoring a large theological claim in a specific human scene, when moving from personal confession of uncertainty to theological resolution, and when helping readers feel why the Jesus-centered God is not just correct but beautiful. Her tagline — "God looks like Jesus. And that changes everything." — is characteristic: short, declarative, quietly radical, and opening immediately onto the next practical and theological question.

## Signature Patterns

- **Core tagline**: "God looks like Jesus. And that changes everything." — appears on the homepage, in speaker bios, across organizational affiliations. Not merely a slogan: it is her epistemological and hermeneutical claim in compressed form. Every piece of her work is an expansion of this sentence.

- **Recurring phrases and formulations**:
  - "Through the lens of Jesus" / "putting on our Jesus glasses" — her standard formulation for Christocentric hermeneutics
  - "A better Christian story" — the *Divine Gravity* frame for the recovery project
  - "A center of gravity in something bigger and better" — her image for the Jesus-centered ecclesial vision
  - "Cross-shaped power" — her term for the kenotic alternative to coercive force
  - "The broken world has been saved and it will be saved by Jesus Christ" — her theological confidence about eschatology and mission
  - "The Spirit of God is stirring something, possibly even on the scale of some of the great reform movements" — her movement-discernment frame
  - "Trajectory of motion toward Jesus" — her centered-set description of belonging

- **Favorite movement**: Skepticism to conviction. Good frequently begins by voicing the doubt or difficulty that the reader (or she herself) feels, names it honestly, then works through it toward a theologically grounded resolution. This is the Buechner move: the resurrection of grace from the site of admitted doubt.

- **Opening style**: Concrete, specific, often personal — she opens with a scene or a confession rather than a thesis. "The article explores Jesus's temptations in Matthew 4 through a reframed theological lens" — but the actual opening is: Good admits she initially dismissed these temptations as too obvious to take seriously, then a friend's insight shifted everything. She earns her theology by showing the work of getting there.

- **Argument architecture**: Good's typical essay or address moves through:
  1. **Concrete opening** — a scene, an admission, a puzzling text, a cultural observation
  2. **Naming the real question** — what is actually at stake (not the surface question but the deeper one)
  3. **Honest acknowledgment of difficulty** — what makes this genuinely hard to believe or live
  4. **Theological ground** — what Scripture (through the Jesus lens) actually says; what the tradition offers
  5. **The turn** — a reframe that changes how the question looks
  6. **Practical implication or invitation** — what this means for the reader's life, church, or practice

- **Preaching voice (from MennoCon19 and MennoCon21 reports)**:
  - Rooted in the specific biblical text; attends to narrative context and imagery
  - Willing to name what the church has gotten wrong or neglected without being adversarial
  - Calls toward action and hope from a place of theological confidence, not anxious urgency
  - Uses pneumatological and creation imagery (breath, wind, ruach) in a way that feels physically and spiritually alive

- **Prophetic register without prophetic posture**: Good makes challenging, even confrontational theological claims (the church is running on the wrong breath; peace without Jesus lacks a center; Christians have a wounded image of God) — but she delivers them with invitation rather than denunciation. The prophetic content is present; the rhetorical mode is pastoral.

- **Literary allusions and cultural references**: The Buechner award is diagnostic — she reads widely. Recommends Nathan Hatch on religious democratization; interviews Barbara Brown Taylor on embodied faith; engages Marva Dawn on divine weakness. She brings cultural, historical, and literary references into theological essays without footnoting them into academic stiffness.

## Speaking vs. Writing

Based on speaking bios, podcast episode notes, and conference reports:

- **Preaching and writing are closely integrated**: Good's essays read like sermon introductions, and her sermons apparently read like theological essays. The award-winning preacher and the accessible author are the same voice. This distinguishes her from theologians who write abstractly and preach concretely, or vice versa.

- **Speaking topics match written themes precisely**: Her speaking topics — Jesus-centered Christianity, biblical interpretation through Jesus, depolarizing the church, Holy Spirit discernment, contemporary preaching — are exactly the themes of her books and journal essays. No disconnect between platform and publication.

- **Workshops on preaching craft**: Good's speaking portfolio includes "contemporary preaching" workshops — how to construct sermons for audiences with short attention spans, use of story and metaphor, introductory and advanced preaching technique. This makes her unusual: a theologian who also teaches homiletics, and whose voice reflects that self-awareness about the craft of communication.

- **Ecumenical range**: She has spoken to Mennonite Church USA conventions, Church of the Brethren clergy, Jesus Collective gatherings, Mennonite educational institutions, Eastern Mennonite University, Canadian Mennonite University (as instructor), evangelical conferences, and non-Mennonite Christian gatherings. Her voice adapts to these contexts without losing its Anabaptist theological roots.

- **Tone in person**: Described as a "passionate preacher and storyteller" (Herald Press); "flair for storytelling and quirky sense of humor" (speaker bio). In conference contexts, she delivers serious theological content with energy and warmth. The Clergywomen's Retreat (70+ women, 16 church districts) used her for four days of morning sessions — suggesting sustained authority and engagement over time, not just a single keynote.

## The Buechner Inheritance

The Frederick Buechner Award for Excellence in Preaching is the most precise external indicator of Good's voice. Buechner (1926-2022) was known for: theological honesty about doubt and grace; the use of vivid, literary prose in theological writing; the conviction that grace hides in ordinary life and must be excavated by careful attention; and a refusal of religious cliché in favor of lived reality. Good's writing exhibits all of these: the willingness to name what is genuinely puzzling or painful about the Christian faith, the use of specific, surprising imagery (rabid badgers; dinosaur bones; volcanoes), and the consistent movement from doubt toward grace without bypassing the doubt. She received this award as the inaugural recipient — not as an established figure inheriting a tradition but as the one who inaugurated it, suggesting Portland Seminary recognized in her something that defined what the award should honor.

## Overall Assessment

Good's voice is that of a **narrative theologian with pastoral depth and prophetic content** — someone who can move across the full spectrum from devotional warmth to theological rigor to prophetic challenge without losing the thread of accessibility. She is the preacher who helps the deconstruction survivor realize that what they were trying to escape was not Jesus but a distorted image of God; the theologian who helps the peace-church activist see that their peacemaking needs a center of gravity; the writer who helps the ordinary Christian reader understand that every biblical story is, at its root, a Jesus story. The "better Christian story" she is after is not an abstraction — it is a felt sense of the beauty and coherence of Jesus-centered faith, communicated through a voice that has been honed in the pulpit, sharpened in the academy, and tested in sixteen-plus years of local congregation life.
