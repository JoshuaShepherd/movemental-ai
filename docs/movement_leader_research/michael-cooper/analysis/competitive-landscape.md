## Framing

Cooper does not have *competitors* in the commercial sense — evangelical missiology is a collaborative field, and several figures below are his collaborators, endorsers, or shared-institution peers. The right question is: **in the space of "voices readers and practitioners turn to for movement theology, Pauline mission, and the Ephesian/early-church model," who else occupies that attention? And what is Cooper's distinct position?**

The single most important competitive fact: **"Ephesiology" is a category of one.** Cooper coined it and is the only figure occupying the Ephesus / Acts 19 movemental-theology niche as a named discipline.

## Cooper's distinct position in this landscape

**1. He owns a category of one.**
"Ephesiology — the study of the Ephesian movement" is a coined, named discipline. No one else frames the Ephesus / Acts 19 movement as a discrete object of study and a normative model. Schnabel writes commentary; Garrison and Addison write movement dynamics; Cooper alone owns the named field.

**2. He has exegetical-theological depth that the CPM practitioners lack.**
Garrison, Addison, and Payne write from field data and method. Cooper writes from the New Testament text, the Apostolic Fathers, and — increasingly — the archaeological record of Asia Minor. He is the theology-and-text end of the movement conversation, where most of the practitioner peers sit at the method-and-data end.

**3. He refuses CPM/DMM jargon — deliberately.**
This is strategic positioning, not omission. No "people of peace," "T4T," "generational mapping," or site-counting. His thesis ("making disciples is the mandate while church planting is a result") is a theological corrective *to* the CPM mainstream. That makes him the natural home for the large practitioner audience that is movement-invested but CPM-skeptical.

**4. He is vertically integrated.**
Schnabel has commentaries; Hirsch has a trade platform; Garrison has field credibility. Only Cooper pairs a coined framework with his own accredited LMS (→ Kairos degrees), his own seminary MA program (Mission India), a 227-episode podcast, and a ~$33M institutional anchor (East-West). The pipeline from podcast → book → certificate → accredited degree is his alone in this set.

**5. He is academically anchored but practitioner-credible.**
TEDS PhD, EMS, Missio Nexus, guest lectures at LSE/Bordeaux/Loyola/Baylor — *plus* 30+ years in missions and a decade of Romanian church planting. He bridges the academy and the field in a way most direct peers sit on one side of.

## Competitive positioning for Movemental

Movemental should position Cooper as:

- **The originator of "Ephesiology"** — the missiologist who turned the Ephesian movement into a named field.
- **The exegetical-theological voice inside the movements conversation** — text, patristics, and Asia-Minor archaeology, not method and metrics.
- **The scholar-practitioner** — TEDS-credentialed and EMS-anchored, but 30+ years in the field across five continents.
- **The vertically integrated movement educator** — podcast to book to certificate to accredited degree.
- **A trusted voice in the ecosystem** — endorsed by Hirsch, carried by Missio Nexus and EMS, engaged by Motus Dei (per the canonical ecosystem-layer doctrine).

Movemental should **not** position Cooper as:

- A CPM/DMM method advocate (he is its theological corrective; do not adopt its jargon for him).
- The next Alan Hirsch (different altitude — Hirsch is paradigm/systems; Cooper is exegetical/material-culture).
- A pure academic NT scholar (that is Schnabel's lane; Cooper is the practitioner-theology counterpart).
- A trade-devotional popularizer (his register is scholarly; flattening it destroys his differentiation).
- A church-planting how-to author (that is Payne's lane; Cooper's thesis is that church planting is a *result*, not the mandate).
