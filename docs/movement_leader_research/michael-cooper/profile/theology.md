## Core Thesis

Mission is not a subset of theology — **mission is the center of theology**. The Bible is best read not as a systematic theology with a missions chapter, but as **"a missiological theology"**: a sustained account of God's movement to the nations, in which figures like Paul and John function as *missiological theologians* who connected the teaching of Jesus to the cultural narrative of their world. Cooper's signature instantiation of this thesis is **Ephesiology** — the study of the first-century Ephesian church-planting movement as a discrete object and a normative model for contemporary movements.

From this follows his most-repeated correction: **church planting is nowhere commanded in Scripture; making disciples is the mandate, and church planting is a _result_.** Cooper is a conservative-evangelical missiological theologian who reframes the entire movement conversation around disciple-multiplication and a theocentric passion for the glory of God among the nations.

## Theological Tradition

**Primary**: Conservative-evangelical missiology — the Trinity Evangelical Divinity School / Evangelical Missiological Society / William Carey Publishing / Missio Nexus stream. Cooper is an academically credentialed missiologist (PhD, Intercultural Studies, TEDS) working squarely inside the evangelical missions academy, not at its experimental edge.

**Secondary**: New-religious-movements and comparative-religion method (his early ethnographic work on Druidry; publications in *Nova Religio* and *Pomegranate*); patristic / early-church retrieval (the Apostolic Fathers); and an emerging material-culture / archaeology-of-Asia-Minor turn.

**Register**: Unlike a pure practitioner-articulator, Cooper writes as a **scholar-practitioner**. He argues from the biblical text, from first-century history and the archaeological record, *and* from contemporary church-planting-movement field data — interleaving the two. He is comfortable with academic apparatus (journals, edited volumes, EMS) while writing trade-accessible books and hosting a weekly podcast. He is conservative on the authority and centrality of Scripture, evangelistically driven, and mildly contrarian toward American evangelical pragmatism.

## Distinctive Positions

- **Church planting is nowhere commanded.** Cooper's most-corrected reader misreading is the assumption that church planting is the biblical mandate. His consistent reply: "Nowhere in the Bible is the church commanded to plant churches… making disciples is the mandate while church planting is a result."
- **Roughly 40% of the New Testament relates to the Ephesian movement.** A signature, frequently-repeated claim that justifies treating "Ephesiology" as a discrete and weighty field.
- **The Bible is "a missiological theology."** Mission at the center, not as a chapter — a deliberate inversion of how systematic theology typically files missions.
- **Disciples must identify with the life of Christ.** "Disciples are not made unless they identify with the life of Christ." Christ-formation is the substance of discipleship; activity is not discipleship. "Disciples multiply while volunteers keep busy."
- **Movement health is a DNA property.** Multiplication and sustainability depend on healthy, replicable spiritual DNA — and movements fail when that DNA degrades into false teaching (the fifth analytical question).
- **Leadership should be Christoform, not hierarchical or novelty-driven.** Recovered from the disciples of the apostles, against the celebrity/innovation models of contemporary leadership culture.
- **Strong contextualization, guarded against syncretism.** Paul and John connected the gospel to Ephesian culture; faithful contextualization is mandatory, but the contextualization/syncretism boundary must be policed (his EMS 13 contribution).
- **A mild evangelical-identity polemic.** *When Evangelicals Sneeze* frames American evangelicalism as suffering "identity loss," to be cured via the Great Commandment, Compassion, and Commission.

## Theological Evolution

Cooper's theological arc tracks his career arc with unusual tightness: **(1) new-religious-movements scholar** — the doctoral and early-career ethnographer of contemporary Druidry, publishing in *Missiology*, *Nova Religio*, and *Pomegranate*, and directing the School of Biblical and Religious Studies at Trinity International University; **(2) leadership primer** — *Lead Like Paul* (2017), an early, compact statement of the Pauline-leadership themes (the five C's) he would later expand; **(3) missiological theologian** — the 2020 publication of *Ephesiology*, which reframed the whole project: mission at the center of theology, the Ephesian movement as a discrete field, the disciple-making-over-church-planting correction; **(4) ecosystem and patristic deepening** — *Make Disciples* (2023), *First Christian Voices* (2023), and *Letters to Churches* (2025), operationalizing disciple-multiplication and recovering Christoform leadership from the Apostolic Fathers; and **(5) archaeology of Asia Minor** — the 2026 *Gods, Emperors, Philosophers, and a New Movement* and its visual companion, grounding the movement claims in inscriptions, architecture, and material culture.

The remarkable continuity is method. The questions an NRM ethnographer asks of a living movement — how did it start, what do its members believe, who leads, how does it multiply, why does it fracture — are exactly the five analytical questions Ephesiology asks of the first-century Ephesian movement. The Druidry researcher and the missiological theologian are the same analyst, turned from the present to the first century, and now to the excavated record beneath it. The through-line: *mission is the center of theology; the measure of mission is multiplying disciples who identify with the life of Christ; and the model for both is the Ephesian movement, read with a scholar's questions and a planter's stake.*
