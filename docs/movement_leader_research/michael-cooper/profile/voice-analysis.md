## Voice Summary

Michael Cooper writes and speaks like **a missions-academy scholar who never stopped being a field practitioner**. His default move is *definitional*: he coins or sharpens a term, fences off what it is *not*, and then builds an argument on the cleaned-up definition. His most characteristic rhetorical gesture is the **provocative inversion** — taking a near-universal evangelical assumption ("our job is to plant churches") and flipping it ("nowhere is the church commanded to plant churches; disciple-making is the mandate, church planting is a result"). He is mildly contrarian toward American evangelical pragmatism, but his contrarianism is *constructive*: he corrects in order to recenter, not to demolish.

The register is **scholar-practitioner**, and the interleaving is the signature. A typical Cooper passage moves fluidly between first-century textual or archaeological analysis (Acts 19, Ephesian inscriptions, the Apostolic Fathers) and contemporary church-planting-movement examples — and then lands a compressed aphorism ("disciples multiply while volunteers keep busy") that a practitioner can carry out the door. He is academically credentialed but trade-accessible; he assumes a reader who wants both the footnote and the takeaway.

## Signature Patterns

- **Recurring phrases / hallmark lexicon**:
  - "Ephesiology" / "the Ephesian movement"
  - "missiological theology" / "missiological theologian"
  - "a missiological theology of the Bible"
  - "missiologically theocentric"
  - "make disciples" / "disciple-making" / "disciples multiply"
  - "church-planting movement (CPM)"
  - "multiply" / "multiplication"
  - "healthy DNA"
  - "Christoform" (leadership)
  - "contextualization" (guarded against "syncretism")

- **Favorite rhetorical moves**:
  - **Coining a term + mock-dictionary pronunciation.** His defining gesture: he doesn't just name "Ephesiology," he renders it as a dictionary headword — *Ephesiology [n. i-fē-zē-ä-lə-jē]* — performing the act of establishing a discipline. The pronunciation gloss is a branding device as much as a clarification.
  - **The disciple-making-vs-church-planting inversion.** The central provocative reversal of his corpus: church planting is *demoted from mandate to result*, disciple-making *promoted from method to mandate*. He stages it as a discovery the reader has been missing ("Nowhere in the Bible…").
  - **The "not-this-but-that" definitional fence.** "Ephesiology is *not* a commentary on Ephesians, *not* a biblical theology of missions — it is a missiological theology of the Bible." He defines by exclusion before he defines by content.
  - **The quantified claim as hook.** "Roughly 40% of the New Testament relates to the Ephesian movement" — a memorable, repeatable statistic that licenses the whole project.
  - **The aphoristic contrast pair.** "Disciples multiply while volunteers keep busy." Two clauses, one antithesis, fully portable.

- **Argument structure** — Cooper builds in a consistent five-move pattern:
  1. *Coin or sharpen a term* (often with the dictionary-pronunciation flourish).
  2. *Fence it* — say what it is not.
  3. *Apply the five analytical questions* — start, belief, leadership, multiplication, defection.
  4. *Interleave first-century evidence with contemporary CPM examples.*
  5. *Land a compressed aphorism* the practitioner can reuse.

- **Opening style**: Often opens by *establishing a category* — naming the field, the term, or the claim — rather than with a hook or anecdote. The reader is oriented to a discipline before they are told a story.

- **Closing style**: Returns to the theocentric center — mission as the declaration of "the glory of God to the nations" — keeping the practical argument tethered to a doxological frame.

## Sample Passages

> *"Nowhere in the Bible is the church commanded to plant churches."*
> — [Is Church Planting a Biblical Mandate? (Patheos / Jackson Wu), 2020](https://www.patheos.com/blogs/jacksonwu/2020/10/28/is-church-planting-a-biblical-mandate/)

The signature inversion in nine words. It does not argue *yet*; it simply asserts the absence the rest of the piece will exploit. Declarative, slightly startling, and engineered to make a CPM audience stop and re-examine its own foundational assumption.

> *"Making disciples is the mandate while church planting is a result."*
> — [Patheos / Jackson Wu, 2020](https://www.patheos.com/blogs/jacksonwu/2020/10/28/is-church-planting-a-biblical-mandate/)

The constructive half of the inversion. Note the balanced clause structure — *mandate* vs. *result* — which is how Cooper habitually resolves a provocation: not into vagueness but into a clean reordering of categories.

> *"Pure and simple, disciples multiply while volunteers keep busy."*
> — [*Make Disciples* (book description)](https://www.amazon.com/Make-Disciples-Spiritual-Christ-Follower-Ephesiology/dp/B0CPNTVDXB)

The aphoristic contrast pair at its purest. "Pure and simple" is the practitioner's tell — a man who has planted churches signaling that the academic point has a field edge. *Multiply* vs. *keep busy* is the whole discipleship theology compressed to a slogan.

> *"[The Ephesian movement] is best characterized by faithful disciples committed to a missiologically theocentric passion to declare the glory of God to the nations."*
> — [Patheos / Jackson Wu, 2020](https://www.patheos.com/blogs/jacksonwu/2020/10/28/is-church-planting-a-biblical-mandate/)

The scholarly register at full extension — "missiologically theocentric" is dense, coined, and unmistakably his. This is the doxological close: even the most analytic sentence ends pointed at "the glory of God to the nations."

> Ephesiology is *"the study of the Ephesian movement"* — *"a missiological theology of the Bible."*
> — [*Ephesiology* front matter](https://www.everand.com/book/547780170/Ephesiology-A-Study-of-the-Ephesian-Movement)

The founding definitional act. Two glosses, one establishing the *object* (the Ephesian movement) and one the *method* (missiological theology of the Bible). This is the sentence the entire ecosystem — book, podcast, master classes, MA — radiates from.

> *"Disciples are not made unless they identify with the life of Christ."*
> — [Patheos / Jackson Wu, 2020](https://www.patheos.com/blogs/jacksonwu/2020/10/28/is-church-planting-a-biblical-mandate/)

A definitional fence applied to discipleship: it tells you what does *not* count before it tells you what does. Christ-formation as the non-negotiable criterion, stated as a boundary condition.

## Speaking vs. Writing

Cooper has a **strong, sustained spoken presence**: he hosts **The Ephesiology Podcast** — **227 episodes since 2019**, weekly cadence, 4.8/5 on Apple — co-hosted with **Andrew Johnson**, published to Apple, Spotify, and YouTube. This is not an incidental channel; it is a primary one, and it is dialogical. Where his books perform the solo definitional work, the podcast is conversational — a co-hosted format that lets him test, restate, and apply the framework against a partner's questions and against guests' field experience.

His **spoken voice is more discursive and pedagogical** than his terse written aphorisms: in long-form audio he develops the five analytical questions at length, walks through first-century context, and connects to contemporary movement examples in a teacherly cadence. The written corpus distills; the podcast expands. He also lectures in formal academic settings (LSE, Bordeaux, Loyola, Baylor) and teaches in cohort programs (Master Classes / Seminary@YourChurch; the Mission India MA), so his spoken register flexes from popular-podcast to graduate-seminar depending on audience — the academy gets the apparatus, the cohort gets the application.

The volume and consistency of the spoken corpus mean his framework is **heavily oral-distributed**, not book-bound — a key asset for any downstream voice-fidelity or retrieval work.

## Vocabulary and Terminology Preferences

Deliberate, consistent terminology — the lexicon *is* the brand:

- **"Ephesiology"** — his coined headword; always treated as a discipline/field, often with the dictionary-pronunciation gloss.
- **"The Ephesian movement"** — preferred over "the Ephesian church"; *movement* is load-bearing.
- **"Missiological theology" / "missiological theologian"** — mission at the *center* of theology; Paul and John classed as missiological theologians.
- **"Missiologically theocentric"** — his densest coinage; the theological grounding test for any mission claim.
- **"Make disciples" / "disciple-making" / "disciples multiply"** — the mandate vocabulary, always set against "church planting."
- **"Multiply" / "multiplication"** — the success metric; he prefers it to "growth" or "reach."
- **"Healthy DNA"** — borrowed movement-biology metaphor for replicable, sustainable spiritual character.
- **"Christoform"** — his leadership coinage from the Apostolic Fathers.
- **"Contextualization"** — paired with, and policed against, **"syncretism."**
- **"Church-planting movement (CPM)"** — the conversation he enters, even as he corrects its framing.

He pointedly **demotes**: "church planting" (recast from mandate to mere *result*), "volunteers" (the foil for real disciples), and metric/pragmatic language ("counting sites," mobilizing for activity's sake). He does **not** lean on attractional, growth-hacking, or platform-building vocabulary. The avoidance is itself a theological statement: technique divorced from disciple-formation is, in his frame, the error to be corrected.
