## Who he is

Michael T. Cooper is an American evangelical missiologist and missiological theologian best known for coining **"Ephesiology"** — "the study of the Ephesian movement" — as a framework for understanding and catalyzing contemporary movements. He is a scholar *and* a field practitioner: more than thirty years in missions, roughly ten of them as a pioneer church planter in post-communist Romania, alongside a PhD in Intercultural Studies from Trinity Evangelical Divinity School and guest lectures at the London School of Economics, the University of Bordeaux, Loyola University, and Baylor University.

His formation is unusually interdisciplinary — a **BED in Environmental Design from Texas A&M**, an **MA in Missions from Columbia International University**, and the **TEDS doctorate** (focus on religious movements; minor in early Christian history). His early scholarship was ethnographic: field research on contemporary Druidry, published in *Missiology*, *Nova Religio*, and *Pomegranate*. That new-religious-movements method — how does a movement start, what do its adherents believe, who leads it, how does it multiply or fracture — is precisely the lens he later turns on the *first-century* Ephesian movement.

His current institutional footprint centers on three simultaneous roles: **missiologist-in-residence at East-West Ministries International**, **affiliate professor at Kairos University**, and **program director of the MA in Missiology of Movements at Mission India Theological Seminary** — plus his own **TELOS. / Ephesiology** ministry, the **Ephesiology Master Classes / Seminary@YourChurch** cohort program, and the 227-episode **Ephesiology Podcast** (co-hosted with Andrew Johnson). His personal biography, by contrast, is almost entirely undocumented — no public birth date, birthplace, or family information. He presents himself vocationally, not personally, and this dossier honors that rather than inventing detail.

## What he contributes

Cooper's signature move is **reframing missiology AS theology** rather than as a subset of it. Where most evangelical writers treat "missions" as an applied department downstream of systematic theology, Cooper inverts the order: the Bible is read as "a missiological theology" with mission at the center; Paul and John are read as missiological theologians who connected Jesus to the cultural narrative of Ephesus. Three things make this distinctive — he **coins a category and treats it as a discipline** ("Ephesiology," a category of one), he **refuses CPM/DMM operational jargon** as a deliberate theological position, and he **grounds movement claims in material culture** (the 2026 Asia Minor archaeology volume and its photographic companion).

His most-cited corrective — "**making disciples is the mandate while church planting is a result**" — anchors a coherent ten-framework corpus: Ephesiology, missiological theology, disciple-making-over-church-planting, healthy DNA, Christoform leadership, *Lead Like Paul*'s Five C's, the contextualization/syncretism boundary, and the TELOS. three pillars. The flagship *Ephesiology* (William Carey, 2020) won the 2020 Catalyst Services Reader's Choice Award for best book in Missiology and carries 13 named endorsers, including **Alan Hirsch** — situating Ephesiology beside *The Forgotten Ways* / APEST in the broader movemental conversation.

## The gap

Cooper's gap is the **inverse** of the typical pre-platform movement leader (and the precise inverse of Lucas Pulley, who had book-length thinking and no books). Cooper has the opposite problem: he is extraordinarily prolific yet sits on a paper-thin owned-audience layer. The asymmetry:

- **Dispersion, not absence.** ~17 books are split across at least six imprints (William Carey, Wipf & Stock, Sacred Roots / Samuel Morris, Sacred Tribes Press, and self/Ephesiology lines), with no single discoverable hub. A reader who finds *Ephesiology* on Amazon has no easy path to *Make Disciples*, *Letters to Churches*, or the 2026 archaeology volume.
- **Owned-audience near-zero.** ~17 books + 227 podcast episodes have produced ~120–175 brand-account followers, ~40 Goodreads ratings, 12 Apple podcast ratings, and no newsletter of scale.
- **Lent-to-owned never captured.** East-West's ~$33M missionary/donor base, the Kairos degree funnel, and the Mission India MA route audiences through institutions Cooper does not own; the 227-episode podcast converts almost none of its reach into an owned relationship.
- **Undiscoverable.** No Wikipedia article, no verified Google Scholar profile, a misspelled Patheos slug (`/blogs/ephesilogy/`).

The problem is not production. The problem is that a serious, award-winning, vertically integrated body of work is scattered and undiscoverable, and the institutional reach it should have converted into an owned relationship was never captured.
