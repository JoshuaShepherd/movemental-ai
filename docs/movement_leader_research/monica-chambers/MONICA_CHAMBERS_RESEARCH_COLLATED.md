## 3. Editorial bio

Monica Paredes Chambers is a Costa Rica-born spiritual director, church planter, and urban missionary who functions as one half of a married author-practitioner team with her husband Jeremy Chambers. Her formation arc moves from Costa Rica through a missionary-family relocation to Lancaster, Pennsylvania — where her family planted a Latin American church serving the U.S. context — into a decade of microchurch planting in Richmond, Virginia, and her current ministry base in Littleton, Colorado (Denver metro), where she has lived since approximately 2022.

She is best known as co-author of *The Art of Missional Spirituality: 31 Sacred Practices for Jesus-Followers* (100 Movements Publishing, 2023), which carries a foreword by Alan and Debra Hirsch and is the central artifact of her published voice — an attempt to fuse contemplative spiritual formation (Foster–Willard–Renovaré tradition) with movemental missional ecclesiology (Hirsch / Forge / V3 tradition). Her earlier co-authored work, *Kingdom Contours*, is structured around Alan Hirsch's *The Forgotten Ways* / mDNA as a blueprint and reads as a practitioner's curriculum for movement leaders.

Her current institutional footprint spans three nodes: **Forge America** (where she and Jeremy serve as movement coaches), **The Pando Collective / Pando Denver** (a network of 20+ leaders), and **The Arise Network** (a network of seven microchurches in the U.S. and abroad, originating as Arise RVA in Richmond). She also serves Renovaré as a contributing author and podcast guest. Together, she and Jeremy report participation in launching disciple-making movements across 35+ countries.

## 5. Frameworks

### 31 Sacred Practices for Jesus-Followers

| Field | Value |
|-------|-------|
| Introduced | 2023, *The Art of Missional Spirituality* |
| Components | 31 daily (or weekly-for-a-year) practices grouped across: (1) prayer-focused (intercessory, soaking, centering, praying the hours), (2) scripture engagement (creative memorization alternatives), (3) relational/missional (spiritual friendship, secret acts of kindness, solitude/silence, communion) |
| Visual / model | One-month linear calendar (31 practices); also configurable as weekly rhythm across a year |
| Adoption evidence | Used as curriculum in Pando Denver (20+ leaders), Arise Network (7 microchurches), and global Forge cohorts; foreword by Alan & Debra Hirsch; Goodreads 4.47 / 17 ratings |
| Source | [The Art of Missional Spirituality (Amazon)](https://us.amazon.com/Art-Missional-Spirituality-Practices-Jesus-Followers/dp/1955142440) |

The framework argues that personal spirituality and kingdom mission collapse into a single rhythm: practices are not retreat *from* the neighbor but propellant *into* the neighbor, and a daily 31-practice arc trains both interior intimacy with God and exterior incarnational presence in one move.

The frame functions as Monica's signature critique of both burnout-prone missional activism and disengaged contemplation: each is fatal in isolation; only respiratory rhythm sustains.

#### Antithesis prohibition (non-negotiable)

Do not frame spiritual practices as transactional means of earning God's favor or producing guaranteed outcomes. Monica explicitly disavows this in her *Pivotal People* podcast teaching ("These practices are just you making yourself available to God, to connect with him and to be able to receive his goodness and his love"). The substrate voice must reject both works-righteousness phrasing ("if you do X, God will do Y") and outcome-control phrasing ("this practice will produce these results"); it must keep practices in the register of *availability*, *positioning*, *faithfulness*, and *receiving*, never in the register of leverage or guarantee. Derived from: Pivotal People Podcast S2E70 transcript notes; Renovaré "Praying the Hours" excerpt.

| Domain | Position |
|--------|----------|
| Tradition | Evangelical–missional with strong contemplative/spiritual-formation overlay; bridges Hirsch/Forge/V3 missional ecclesiology with Foster/Willard/Renovaré spiritual formation tradition |
| Ecclesiology | Microchurch / organic / movemental — small, nimble, multiplying communities; anti-attractional; explicitly aligned with Alan Hirsch's *Forgotten Ways* |
| Missiology | Incarnational; neighborhood-embedded; "sent one" identity; mission as outflow of formation rather than substitute for it |

| Christology | Christocentric and Trinitarian (first element of *Kingdom Contours*); Jesus-is-Lord as movemental confession |
| Anthropology / formation | Whole-person formation across "being, knowing, and doing" (Ken Boa framework adopted in *Finishing Well*); practices as positioning the self to receive grace |
| Prayer / spirituality | Rooted in classical disciplines (Foster lineage): praying the hours, the Examen, centering prayer, solitude/silence, communion; uses Celtic prayer sources (Northumbria Community), Book of Common Prayer, Psalms 27/63/90/143 |
| Culture | Latin American–Anglo bridge background; urban North American missional focus; globally networked (35+ countries) |

#### Distinctive claims

- Spiritual formation and missional engagement are not sequential or hierarchical but *respiratory*: contemplation and mission are two phases of one rhythm, and either alone is fatal.
- The 31 Sacred Practices framework is unusual in pairing each contemplative discipline with an explicitly missional outflow (e.g., solitude paired with neighborhood acts of kindness), refusing the "retreat then return" pattern of most spiritual formation curricula.
- Microchurch is positioned not as one ecclesial option among many but as the form most native to early-church movement dynamics, with explicit citation of Hirsch's *The Forgotten Ways* as architectural source.
- Secrecy as discipline — deliberately withholding acts of kindness from social recognition — is elevated to a primary anti-formation practice against approval-seeking, more rigorous than most evangelical formation traditions.
- Co-authorship as missional form: every public artifact (book, podcast, neighborhood ministry) is jointly held with spouse Jeremy, framing marriage itself as a missional unit rather than a backdrop to individual ministry.
