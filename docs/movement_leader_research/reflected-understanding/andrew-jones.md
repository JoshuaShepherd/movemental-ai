# Andrew Jones: Reflected Understanding

## Calling

You're called to **chronicle and connect a movement** — to watch what God is doing at the grass roots and narrate it generously for everyone else trying to find their footing. Where many of your peers wrote the books and built the frameworks, you did something rarer: you kept a living archive. For more than two decades, as Tall Skinny Kiwi, you wrote a movement into legibility in real time — essays, field reports, recommendations, candid retrospectives — so that emerging and missional church wasn't only theorized but *witnessed*. That's a prophetic vocation in the truest sense: you name what is actually happening, you point people toward each other rather than toward yourself, and you tell the whole story, including the parts about decline, without flinching.

Your calling has always been bivalent, and the two halves hold each other up. On one side, thirty years of embodied, third-sector work across more than seventy countries — aid worker, church developer, pastor, mission consultant — discovering and empowering "impact entrepreneurs" doing social transformation where the institutions weren't. On the other, an unusually early and prolific digital presence that began in 1997 and never stopped. You're a practitioner who writes and a writer who has actually planted, traveled, and built. The convictions that recur across your corpus are the right ones, and they're yours: that the church is a **rhizome** — spreading wide rather than building high; that mission begins in the local and the ordinary, so that "home" is itself a mission field; that emerging church was never a style but "a radically different mindset, value system and worldview"; and that digital and now metaverse space is native terrain for the gospel, to be inhabited rather than feared. Your 2022 Greenbelt talk, *In The Metaverse As In Heaven*, is simply the latest expression of a conviction you've carried since the early days of the web. You are, by temperament, a centrist and a bridge — curious, mischievous, allergic to dogmatism and tidy definitions — and your authority comes not from a credential or an institution but from having been present, online and in person, for the whole arc.

## Existing Content (before the platform we're building)

**Where it lives.** Your real book is your **blog** — *tallskinnykiwi.com*, with its earlier life on TypePad and Blogger — a two-decade, serialized history of the emerging and missional church. Landmark entries anchor it: the multi-part "Emerging Church Definition" essays of 2004, the retrospective "Emerging Church Movement (1989–2009)?", the 2010 "Goodbyes to Emergent Village," and the recent metaverse pieces. Around that hub sit your **podcast** (*Tall Skinny Kiwi* — "the latest innovations in church, mission and religion," on Apple Podcasts and Spotify), your **speaking** (Greenbelt 2022, Passionfest, the Forge Grassroots Festival, the Menlo Church conversation), and the long trail of **organizational and field work** (BOAZ, NZCMS, Church Mission Society, Dawn Ministries) that gave the writing its ballast.

**Credibility.** It's high, and it's a particular kind. You're not a benchmark voice because you published a flagship trade book — you're one because you were *there*, reading and writing the movement as it formed, trusted enough that Brian McLaren recommended bookmarking your site and Christianity Today called you "an irrepressible New Zealander who chronicles innovative efforts to proclaim the gospel." That trust was earned in public, over twenty years, by pointing at other people's good work and telling the truth about your own movement. The issue was never authority. The issue is **findability, structure, and reuse**.

**How it lives online now.** As a **chronological archive on rented ground**. Twenty-plus years of writing sit in reverse-date order on a blog platform you don't fully own, scattered across an old TypePad domain and the current site, with the podcast and the talks living somewhere else again. The themes you've returned to for two decades — rhizome church planting, emerging church, mission at home, faith in a networked culture — are *in there*, but they aren't gathered into series, they aren't linked to each other, and a newcomer searching for "rhizome church planting" or "emerging church definition" has no obvious way in. The work doesn't **move** in the sense of circulating and compounding; it stays where the date stamp filed it.

**The NOTs this platform addresses directly.** Your content is largely **not** organized into the thematic series your archive is begging for (church planting, emerging church, mission, social transformation, faith in a digital culture); **not** structured and repurposed so that talks become articles and podcast episodes become written pieces; **not** interconnected, so the blog, podcast, and speaking don't visibly reference one another; **not** optimized for discovery, so the two decades of writing that *should* be the first result for emerging-church searches often aren't; and **not** owned and unified under one surface you control rather than a rented blog platform. None of this asks you to change your voice or migrate away from anything — your existing content stays yours, intact, importable. It simply makes the archive you already built navigable, connected, and durable.

## Constraints

The constraints you live under are emblematic of grass-roots movement leaders, not a personal shortfall. The same instincts that make you valuable — being mobile, being present, pointing at others rather than building a monument to yourself — are the instincts least compatible with the slow, sit-still work of curating an archive. You've spent thirty years on the move, from Haight-Ashbury to a campervan in Europe to an island in Orkney, and the work that mattered was always in the room or on the road, not in the content-management system. Time to gather two decades of posts into series, to turn talks into articles, to build a single owned home, is exactly the time a working practitioner-chronicler does not have. There's also a quieter constraint: you are temperamentally allergic to self-promotion, which is a virtue in a connector and a liability for someone whose own best work is buried under twenty years of date stamps. A platform earns its place here only if it reduces the lift — if it helps structure, connect, and surface what you've already written without asking you to become a different kind of leader, or a louder one, than you are.
