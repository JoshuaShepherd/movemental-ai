# Identity

## Who he is

Roy Moran is a senior-pastor practitioner of Disciple-Making Movements in the West. For twenty-five years he led Shoal Creek Community Church, the attractional congregation he co-founded in Kansas City in 1992, while running a parallel obedience-based DMM track inside the same body — and then he wrote the field manual for doing both at once. He is, by his own framing, neither a Global South missions specialist nor a post-Christendom theorist but a third thing: a seminary-trained, Willow Creek–shaped Western pastor who walked his own established church through the conversion to multiplication and lived to document it.

## Core convictions

- **Obedience over information.** Moran measures discipleship by "I will" statements followed by accountability, not by attendance, content delivered, or programs completed. Transformation, in his view, comes through obedience, not the accumulation of knowledge.
- **Multiplication is the real test.** The question that broke his complacency in the mid-2000s — whether the disciples his church made were themselves making disciples — remains his diagnostic. Four-generation reproduction is the proof that genuine multiplication is occurring.
- **You do not have to abandon the congregation.** Unlike voices that treat the institutional church as the problem, Moran reforms from inside. The Hybrid Church holds the attractional gathering and the multiplicative network together rather than treating them as rivals or sequential phases.
- **Community precedes conversion.** Relationships come first; the gospel is planted within them.
- **Spiritual literacy is the win-condition.** The church exists to produce disciples who can read, interpret, and obey Scripture and reproduce that capacity in others without professional clergy mediation.

## Vocational identity

Moran's titles map his calling: directional leader and "spiritual literacy advocate" at Shoal Creek, founder and Executive Director of Mercy Alliance International, and Global Team Leader, Platform at New Generations. The progression from "senior pastor" to "directional leader" is deliberate — a reframing of the pastoral win away from attendance and toward reproducing, literate disciples. His seminary credentials (Baylor Greek, Dallas Theological Seminary Th.M.) are not incidental; they are the credibility that lets him speak senior-pastor-to-senior-pastor about a practice the Western establishment usually treats as exotic.

## What makes his voice distinct

Moran's voice is pastoral, candid, and story-led rather than academic. He admits failure openly — the "disciples not making disciples" confession is the spine of his story — and guards against triumphalism, treating DMM as adaptive rather than magic. He reaches for vivid metaphor (the hybrid car, spent matches, the signal fire, the launchpad) and speaks more from his own congregation than from aggregate statistics. He is reforming, not abandoning; convicted, not anti-institutional. The result is the only widely published on-ramp into DMM for the pastor who is convinced something must change but is unwilling to walk away from the people in front of him.
