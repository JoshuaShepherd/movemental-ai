## Framing

Harris occupies a **well-populated** category — "how to hear God's voice / discern the Spirit" is one of the most crowded niches in popular evangelical and charismatic publishing, anchored by bestsellers from Dallas Willard, Priscilla Shirer, and the Virklers. Unlike a category-of-one figure, Harris is **not the originator of her topic**; she is a comparatively recent entrant into a space with established mega-sellers. The right question is therefore: **among the voices readers and churches turn to for "hearing God," where does Harris sit, and what makes her distinct?**

Her answer is a specific combination almost no competitor holds at once: **academic rigour + lived experience + a democratising, ecclesial frame + a credible egalitarian female ACC voice**. She is not the biggest name in the space; she is arguably the most **theologically responsible and productized** voice within the classical-Pentecostal slice of it.

## Harris's distinct position in this landscape

**1. Rigour *and* experience — held together.**
Most of the space splits into two camps: the academically serious (Willard, Grudem) and the experientially popular (Virkler, Shirer, Deere). Harris's differentiator is that she is **both** — a PhD and a Brill monograph ([2023](https://brill.com/display/title/68992)) *and* a story-driven, "show don't tell" teacher who insists hearing God is "a normal part of every Christian's life." Her own line: "There are a lot of good books that will tell you how to hear God's voice. I wanted to write a book that will *show* you" ([Eden](https://www.eden.co.uk/blog/tania-harris-talks-god-conversations-and-cake-p129058)).

**2. Democratising the prophetic — not for "the wise and the weird."**
Her signature corrective is that hearing God is **ordinary, accessible to every believer, and reliably testable** — not the property of elite prophets or strange mystics. This positions her against both the hype-prophetic performers on one side and the cessationist dismissal on the other.

**3. Ecclesial, not individualistic.**
Where much of the genre is intensely personal ("how *you* can hear God"), Harris uniquely frames discernment in **community** — *The Church Who Hears God's Voice* ([Paternoster 2022](https://www.amazon.com/Church-Who-Hears-Gods-Voice/dp/1788932463)) makes the **local church the God-ordained container** for testing revelation ("God speaks twice" — communal confirmation). This ecclesial turn is rare in the category.

**4. A credible egalitarian female ACC voice.**
She is an **ordained female Pentecostal pastor and former church planter** writing serious theology in a space whose biggest charismatic names are predominantly male, and whose biggest female name (Shirer) sits in a complementarian-leaning, devotional lane. Harris's egalitarian witness (CBE, The Junia Project) plus academic credibility is a distinctive combination.

**5. A coherent named framework set.**
She owns a small, ownable framework vocabulary — the **Three Tests** (Would Jesus say this? / Is someone else saying this? / Are spiritual signs following?), the **Three Questions**, **phenomenological equivalency**, and the **four characteristics of the revelatory experience** — giving her teaching more structural identity than most popular "hearing God" authors carry.

## Where she is *not* the leader

Honesty about position matters for calibration:

- **Reach**: Willard, Shirer, the Virklers, and Greig all dwarf her in audience size and book-review volume. Her Goodreads footprint is <100 ratings across all titles ([Goodreads](https://www.goodreads.com/author/show/7790026.Tania_Harris)).
- **Category ownership**: she did not coin "hearing God's voice"; she entered an established category and differentiated within it (contrast Michael Cooper, who owns "Ephesiology" outright).
- **Topic breadth**: she is a **single-topic specialist** (hearing God / Spirit-led discipleship). All three trade books, the monograph, the courses, and the podcast orbit one question. This is a strength for clarity and a risk for breadth (see [gap-analysis.md](gap-analysis.md)).

## Competitive positioning for Movemental

Movemental should position Harris as:

- **The rigorous-and-experiential voice on hearing God** — PhD-grade depth delivered in warm, story-driven pastoral prose; the responsible alternative to method-first or hype-prophetic teaching.
- **The democratiser** — hearing God as "a normal part of every Christian's life," not for "the wise and the weird."
- **The ecclesial discernment voice** — the local church as the safe container; "God speaks twice."
- **A credible egalitarian female Pentecostal theologian** — diversifying a trusted-voices roster that skews male and Western.
- **A trusted voice on Spirit-led discipleship** in the ecosystem (per canonical doctrine) — adjacent to, not centered on, movement ecclesiology.

Movemental should **not** position Harris as:

- A movement-ecclesiology theorist (that is Hirsch / Cooper territory; her doctrine is **adjacent** — Spirit-led discipleship, not movement DNA).
- A hype-prophetic "words-of-knowledge" performer (an anti-pattern she actively dismantles).
- A Scripture-bypassing, individualistic charismatic (the other anti-pattern she dismantles — Scripture anchors and the church governs).
- A mass-market trade-devotional popularizer in Shirer's mold (her register is rigour-plus-pastoral; flattening it to pure devotion loses her differentiation).
- The biggest name in "hearing God" (she is the most theologically responsible and productized voice in the classical-Pentecostal slice — not the reach leader).
