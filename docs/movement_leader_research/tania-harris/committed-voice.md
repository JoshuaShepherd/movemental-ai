## Editorial bio

Tania Harris is an Australian Pentecostal pastor, practical theologian, author, and broadcaster, ordained with the Australian Christian Churches and based in Sydney. Her vocation grew out of a single turning point: as a young woman, around age 21, she prayed what she calls a radical prayer — "Lord, speak to me. And if you make it really clear, I'll do whatever you say" — and subsequently experienced what she describes as God beginning to speak to her. At 26 she sensed a call to full-time ministry, an unexpected direction given a conservative church background "where women weren't allowed to carry the offering bag, much less be pastors." She retrained with a Masters in Ministry, and in her early 30s planted and led a church in a beachside suburb of Melbourne — as a single woman.

From the pastorate she moved into theological education: lecturing at Harvest Bible College in Melbourne, where she pioneered online training and was named Alumna of the Year in 2013, then relocating to Sydney as Academic Dean of Hillsong College. Her own experiences of hearing God became the seed of a wider ministry. She founded **God Conversations** (publicly active from around 2013–2014) to address three questions — *How does God speak? How do you know it's God? And what difference does it make to the church?* The ministry now spans a monthly podcast, four books, online courses, a church-licensing campaign resource, media appearances across Australia, New Zealand, and the UK, and training for denominations and ministers in what she calls "Spirit-led discipleship."

Harris is unusual in pairing a popular-ministry footprint with serious academic work. She earned a **PhD from Alphacrucis College in 2020** for empirical practical-theology research on Pentecostal revelatory experience across three Australian churches, published as the scholarly monograph *Hearing God's Voice* (Brill, *Global Pentecostal and Charismatic Studies* vol. 47, 2023). Her trade books — *God Conversations* (Authentic Media, 2017), *The Church Who Hears God's Voice* (Paternoster, 2022), and *God Dreams* (Authentic Media, 2024) — translate that research for a general Christian readership. Today she leads God Conversations, holds a visiting lectureship in Pentecostal Spirituality at Regents Theological College (UK), and continues to speak, broadcast, and write — all oriented around equipping ordinary believers to discern the Spirit within the accountability of the local church.

## Why she is a trusted voice

Per `docs/build/strategy/movement-leaders-as-ecosystem-layer.md`, Movemental treats movement leaders as a **trusted-voice and ecosystem layer**, not as an implementation audience or a parallel funnel segment. Tania Harris belongs in that layer for reasons that are specific and verifiable.

First, **she owns a clearly defined space.** Where many leaders are generalists, Harris is the practitioner-scholar of one thing: *how God speaks today and how ordinary believers can recognise and respond.* "God Conversations" is both her ministry brand and her reframing of hearing God as a relational, two-way conversation. That focus makes her a credible reference voice for the hearing-God / Spirit-led-discipleship conversation across Pentecostal, charismatic, and evangelical audiences alike.

Second, **she carries rare dual credibility.** She is at once a pastor and church planter (the lived authority of having done it) and a published academic (a Brill monograph, peer-reviewed articles in the *Journal of Pentecostal Theology* and *JEPTA*, a PhD, a visiting lectureship). Her endorsers span both worlds — Hillsong pastoral staff and Alpha Australia on the trade side; Wolfgang Vondey, Frank Macchia, Jacqueline Grey, and Kenneth Archer on the academic side. She delivers PhD-grade rigour in warm pastoral prose: "biblical depth and practical wisdom" in the same breath.

Third, **her doctrine guards the very things Movemental cares about.** She is not a hype-prophetic performer, and she is not an individualist. Her signature correction is that hearing God is *not* for "the wise and the weird" — not rare, not elite, not strange — but a normal part of every Christian's life. Yet she equally refuses the ungoverned charismatic claim: revelation is always tested ("Would Jesus say this?"), always confirmed in community ("God speaks twice"), and always held within the local church. "We need the confidence to know that we can hear from God as well as the humility to know we can get it wrong." That dialectical balance is exactly the posture a trusted-voice layer should model.
