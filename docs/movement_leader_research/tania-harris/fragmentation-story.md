## The Story

Tania Harris has done something most movement leaders in this research set have not: she has already **productized her ministry**. Over roughly a decade she has built a real, working funnel out of a single coherent body of teaching. There is a free 7-day "God Conversations" devotional and a free fortnightly email at the top. There are four books — three trade titles and a Brill monograph — across the middle. There is a paid course catalogue on Teachable ("How To Hear God's Voice," "God Dreams: the Course," even a German-language edition). And at the deep end there is a church-licensing campaign, "50 Days of God Conversations," plus a pastors-and-leaders Zoom cohort and pay-what-you-can leadership coaching. A monthly podcast, now at 106 episodes, runs alongside it all. This is not a content-poor practitioner. It is a leader who has already built the ladder most leaders never get around to building.

And that is exactly what makes Harris's fragmentation different. The usual story is a leader with deep influence and almost no infrastructure. Harris's is closer to the opposite: **a genuinely well-built funnel whose pieces are scattered across enough platforms, imprints, and gated surfaces that the whole is far harder to find — and far smaller in measured reach — than the quality of the work deserves.** Her problem is not that the pieces don't exist. It is that they live in too many places, and that the front door is hard for a stranger to find at all.

Consider the dispersion. Four books across **three publishers** — Authentic Media (*God Conversations*, *God Dreams*), Paternoster (*The Church Who Hears God's Voice*), and Brill (*Hearing God's Voice*). The courses live on a separate **Teachable** subdomain, not the main site. The podcast is spread across Apple, Spotify, and Amazon Music. Teaching video sits on YouTube and a site `/watch` hub. Her writing is split between her own blog, Premier Christianity's UK column, CBE *Mutuality*, The Junia Project, and The Pneuma Review. Her academic work is in Brill, the *Journal of Pentecostal Theology*, *JEPTA*, and a thesis PDF that circulates on a cloud bucket. Her broadcast presence runs through Hope 103.2 and TBN UK. That is a wide, high-quality footprint — and it is scattered across at least a dozen surfaces, none of which gathers the others.

Then there is the honest discoverability problem layered on top. **There is no Wikipedia article** (verified absent). **There is no Google Scholar profile.** Her primary site, godconversations.com, **returns HTTP 403 to automated crawlers** — meaning the very surface she controls is partly invisible to the machines that increasingly mediate discovery. The measured reach is modest and niche: roughly **122 total Goodreads ratings** across all four titles (averages 3.8–4.0), a **5.0-star podcast rating from 29 raters**, an Instagram following near **2,800**, a ministry Facebook page near **3,900**. The headline social and video numbers that matter most — YouTube subscribers, X followers, newsletter list size, website traffic — are **login-gated or undisclosed, and remain unknown.** The work is strong; the discoverability is thin.

In short: Tania Harris has built one of the more complete owned funnels of any leader in this set — free magnet, email, books, courses, church licence, podcast — and **the discoverability and consolidated reach of that funnel are a fraction of what its quality should command.** The asymmetry here is not between influence and infrastructure (she has both). It is between *a well-made funnel* and *a unified, findable front door* that gathers it and lets the machines — and the strangers — see it whole.

## Relational Fragmentation

Harris's relational layer is **credible but un-consolidated.** Her audiences and her credibility are gathered by other institutions, not channelled into a list she owns:

- The **Hope 103.2** broadcast audience hears "Everyday God Conversations" on the radio — owned by the station.
- The **Regents Theological College** (UK) students and the **Academia.edu** readers know her academic work — owned by those institutions.
- The **denominations and pastors** she trains in Spirit-led discipleship are reached through their own structures.
- Her **podcast** audience lives on Apple, Spotify, and Amazon feeds.
- Her **academic standing** — endorsements from Wolfgang Vondey, Frank Macchia, Jacqueline Grey, and Kenneth Archer, the Brill imprint, the peer-reviewed articles — is an endorsement-jacket fact, not an owned-audience asset.

Even her strongest relational equity (a clear, defensible niche; respected supervisors and endorsers across both the academy and the popular church) does not currently flow back into a single place she controls and can speak to directly.

## Why This Matters for Movemental

Per `docs/build/strategy/movement-leaders-as-ecosystem-layer.md`, movement leaders are a **trusted-voice and ecosystem layer**, not an implementation audience. Harris fits that framing as a credible voice in a space *adjacent* to Movemental's movement-DNA core — her Spirit-led-discipleship focus sits beside, not inside, it (Movemental fit **7.4 / 10**). Her fragmentation is a distinctive, almost inverted variant of the problem Movemental exists to solve:

1. She is not a leader who needs help building a funnel. **She already has one** — a genuinely good free→email→course→church-licence ladder. The work is **consolidation and discoverability**, not productization. That makes her one of the lower-effort, higher-readiness onboards in the set.
2. Her pieces are scattered across three publishers, a separate Teachable subdomain, four external article platforms, a 403-gated primary site, and split personal/ministry social identities — exactly the kind of dispersion a unified, AI-legible ecosystem layer is built to gather.
3. Her measured reach is modest and niche, and her discoverability is actively impaired: no Wikipedia, no Scholar profile, a site that blocks crawlers, gated social counts. A unified, machine-legible front door is precisely the lever that grows reach without diluting her doctrine.
4. **Movemental complements, never cannibalizes.** Her courses and her church licence are hers and stay hers; the platform's job is to make them findable and interconnected, not to replace them.

**The fragmentation story here is not a deficiency report. It is the inventory of a well-built ministry funnel that has never been consolidated into one findable, AI-legible front door — and that consolidation is exactly the platform-layer work Movemental is positioned to do.**
