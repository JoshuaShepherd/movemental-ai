## Core Thesis

**God still speaks today — to every believer, not just to an elite — and the experience can be recognised, tested, and responded to within the local church.** Harris is a classical-Pentecostal practical theologian whose entire project is the democratisation of hearing God's voice. Her signature correction is against the misconception that hearing God is *rare, weird, or reserved for special leaders and prophets*; her signature constructive claim is that contemporary revelatory experience is **phenomenologically equivalent** to the biblical experience — the same *kind* of encounter — while remaining distinct from canon-forming Scripture and always tested *by* Scripture and *in* community.

From this follow her two governing instincts, held in deliberate tension: **confidence** ("we can hear from God") and **humility** ("we can get it wrong"). She refuses to collapse either pole — she will not let cessationist caution silence the Spirit, and she will not let ungoverned individualism bypass Scripture and the church.

## Theological Tradition

**Primary**: Classical Pentecostal / charismatic — ordained Australian Christian Churches (the Australian Assemblies of God), worshipping at Hillsong. She bridges charismatic *practice* with academic *practical theology*, a relatively rare combination. Her own roots are in "a conservative background where women weren't allowed to carry the offering bag," which she has consciously moved beyond.

**Secondary**: Empirical practical theology and "ordinary theology" — her doctoral method draws on Mark Cartledge's "Testimony in the Spirit" and Jeff Astley's "ordinary theology," studying how *ordinary* believers actually hear and discern God. She is also conversant with theology-of-revelation literature (Niels Hvidt), Lukan pneumatology (Menzies/Turner, Keener), and the sociology of religious experience (Glock & Stark, Berger).

**Register**: **Accessible scholar-practitioner.** She delivers PhD-grade rigour in warm, pastoral, story-driven prose — "biblical depth + practical wisdom." She is comfortable with academic apparatus (Brill, *JPT*, *JEPTA*, conference papers) while writing trade books and hosting a popular podcast. She is conservative on the authority of Scripture, experientially confident on the Spirit, and egalitarian on women in ministry.

## Distinctive Positions

- **Hearing God is "a normal part of every Christian's life."** The democratising thesis is the heartbeat of her theology: Pentecost democratised access to the Spirit (Acts 2:17, 39), so hearing God is not for "the wise and the weird" but for "every believer" — "we can all hear him for ourselves."
- **Phenomenological equivalency, not canonical equivalency.** Contemporary revelation is the *same kind* of experience as the biblical one, yet it is **not** Scripture and never forms canon. This is the careful hinge that lets her affirm experience without undermining the Bible.
- **Scripture is the test, never bypassed.** Personal revelation is "Extra-Biblical" (additional to, never contradicting) and must align with Jesus as revealed in Scripture; she tests by 1 John 4:1 and by the first of the Three Tests, "Would Jesus say this?"
- **"God speaks twice."** Genuine revelation seeks communal confirmation; the lone, unaccountable prophet is an anti-pattern. Discernment belongs to the body, not the isolated individual.
- **The revelatory experience is distinct from the gift of prophecy.** She carves out a technical category (the four characteristics above) precisely to distinguish ordinary, universally-accessible hearing-God from the specialist charismatic gift — engaging and differentiating from Wayne Grudem's *The Gift of Prophecy*.
- **Egalitarian on women in ministry.** An ordained female pastor and church planter, she writes egalitarian exegesis (e.g. on 1 Tim 2:11) for The Junia Project and CBE, and notes the ACC's egalitarian position. ([juniaproject.com](https://juniaproject.com/author/tania-harris/), [cbeinternational.org](https://www.cbeinternational.org/person/tania-harris/))
- **Christocentric discernment.** Jesus is the benchmark of all discernment — "Would Jesus say this?" is the first and weightiest test.
- **Most God conversations are ordinary.** "Most God conversations are about everyday matters of the heart that lead us in the way of Jesus." She deliberately deflates the sensational; the daily formation matters more than the spectacular word.
- **Dreams and visions speak in symbol.** In *God Dreams*: "Nearly all dream-visions are symbolic. They speak the language of pictures and imagery… A picture truly can tell a thousand words." Interpretation is a learnable, theologically-governed skill.

## The Two-Front Correction (positioning)

Harris's theology is best understood as a careful path between two errors she actively dismantles — a **dialectical balance** that defines her:

**Against the cessationist / "isn't Scripture enough?" front:** She insists God *still* speaks "via his Holy Spirit just as he did in Bible times." Her Pneuma Review piece, "The Theological Problem of Spirit versus Scripture" (2022), and her Premier Unbelievable? debate with cessationist Jim Osman (2023, "Does God speak today through dreams, visions & prophecy?") stake out the continuationist case directly.

**Against the ungoverned, individualistic charismatic front:** She refuses the lone-prophet, Scripture-bypassing, "I have a word" mode. Her Three Tests, her "God speaks twice" communal principle, and her whole ecclesial model (*The Church Who Hears God's Voice*) exist to *govern* revelation, not merely to celebrate it. Discernment is testable, communal, and Christ-centred.

The signature contrast phrase that holds both fronts together: hearing God is **not for "the wise and the weird"** — neither the cautious intellectualism that explains the Spirit away nor the strange mysticism that bypasses Scripture and community.

## Theology of Revelation ↔ Scripture (the careful hinge)

This is the most theologically precise part of her work and the reason she earns academic respect. She holds, simultaneously:

1. Contemporary revelatory experience is **real** and **phenomenologically equivalent** to the biblical experience (same *kind*).
2. It is **not Scripture** and does **not** add to canon — it is "Extra-Biblical" in the sense of *additional to*, not *contrary to*.
3. It is therefore **subordinate to and tested by** Scripture: the first and weightiest test is "Would Jesus say this?", measured against Christ as revealed in the Bible.

The error she names in academic Pentecostalism is fitting one's own genuine experience into a "phenomenologically inferior framework" — treating contemporary hearing-God as a lesser, second-class echo of the biblical accounts. Her corrective dignifies the experience *without* compromising the unique authority of the canon.

## Spiritual Formation

For Harris, hearing God is not an end in itself but the engine of **Spirit-led discipleship**. The point of conversation with God is formation: "most God conversations are about everyday matters of the heart that lead us in the way of Jesus." This keeps her project pastoral rather than sensational — the goal is Christlikeness and obedience ("I'll do whatever you say"), not spiritual experiences collected for their own sake. The local church is the formational container, and learning to recognise and respond to the Spirit is, in her hands, a core discipleship competency to be taught and practised — not a rare charism to be admired.

## Theological Evolution

Harris's theology tracks her career arc tightly: **(1) practitioner** — a young believer and then pastor who simply *experienced* hearing God ("tears, questions and plenty of arguments") and learned to discern it in the field; **(2) popular teacher** — *God Conversations* (2017), distilling that experience into the Three Questions and the Three Tests via the "show, don't tell" story method; **(3) ecclesiologist** — *The Church Who Hears God's Voice* (2022), moving the locus of discernment from the individual to the community; **(4) scholar** — the Brill monograph (2023) and the peer-reviewed articles, building the empirical and theological case for phenomenological equivalency and the revelatory-experience category; and **(5) the dreams turn** — *God Dreams* (2024), extending the framework into the symbolic language of dreams and visions.

The remarkable continuity is the *thesis*: from the first prayer at 21 to the Brill monograph, the claim never changes — **God still speaks, to everyone, and you can learn to recognise and respond.** What changes is the rigour and the container: lived experience becomes a teachable method, then a communal ecclesiology, then a peer-reviewed theology. The practitioner and the scholar are the same person, turned from living the experience to explaining it.
