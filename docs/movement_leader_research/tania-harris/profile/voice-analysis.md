## Voice Summary

Tania Harris writes and speaks like **a teacher-pastor who earned a doctorate to defend what she already practised**. Her default move is *narrative-then-principle*: she opens with a real God-encounter — biblical or personal — and lets the discernment principle emerge from the story rather than stating the rule first. This is her own stated method: "There are a lot of good books out there that will tell you how to hear God's voice. I wanted to write a book that will *show* you." Her most characteristic posture is **dialectical balance** — she habitually holds two poles together (confidence *and* humility, experience *and* Scripture, the supernatural *and* the everyday) and refuses to let either collapse.

The register is **accessible scholarship**: PhD-grade rigour delivered in warm, pastoral, conversational prose. She assumes a reader who is hungry to hear God but nervous about getting it wrong, and she pastors that reader on both fronts at once — giving permission ("you can hear him") and guardrails ("here's how to test it"). She organises teaching around **questions** ("How does God speak? How do you know it's God? Would Jesus say this?"), which keeps the tone exploratory and invitational rather than declarative.

## Signature Patterns

- **Hallmark lexicon** (the vocabulary *is* the brand):
  - "God conversations"
  - "hearing God's voice"
  - "recognise and respond" (to the Spirit)
  - "revelatory experience"
  - "how God speaks and what happens when we listen"
  - "the church who hears God's voice"
  - "a normal part of every Christian's life" / "every believer"
  - "Would Jesus say this?"
  - "God speaks twice"
  - "the wise and the weird"
  - "Spirit-led discipleship"
  - "God-stories"
  - "God dreams" / "dream-visions"

- **Favorite rhetorical moves**:
  - **Show, don't tell.** Her defining gesture: lead with a real encounter, let the principle surface from it. She explicitly contrasts her books with those that merely "tell you how."
  - **The held-together pair.** "We need the *confidence* to know that we can hear from God as well as the *humility* to know we can get it wrong." She resolves tension not into one pole but into a *both/and* — the dialectical signature.
  - **The democratising deflation.** She takes a thing assumed to be elite or strange and makes it ordinary: hearing God is "a normal part of every Christian's life," not for "the wise and the weird"; "most God conversations are about everyday matters of the heart."
  - **Question-framing.** She organises whole books and talks around questions ("How does God speak? How do you know it's God? What difference does it make to the church?"), keeping the posture exploratory and the reader included.
  - **The test as reassurance.** She offers the Three Tests not as gatekeeping but as permission-with-safety — "God speaks twice," "Would Jesus say this?" — so the nervous reader can step out without fear.

- **Argument structure** — a consistent five-move pattern:
  1. *Pose a question* the reader is already asking ("How do you know it's God?").
  2. *Tell a story* — a biblical encounter and/or her own (show, don't tell).
  3. *Name the principle/test* that the story reveals (one of the Three Tests).
  4. *Hold the balance* — affirm confidence and humility, experience and Scripture.
  5. *Land it as ordinary and accessible* — this is for every believer, in the everyday, within the church.

- **Opening style**: Often opens with a *story or a question*, not a thesis — drawing the reader into an encounter before naming the lesson.

- **Closing style**: Returns to the everyday and the communal — formation "in the way of Jesus" and the church as the place where hearing God is practised — keeping the supernatural tethered to ordinary discipleship.

## Antithesis / Prohibition

Harris repeatedly corrects the misconception that hearing God is **rare, elite, or "weird"** — the property of special leaders, prophets, or strange mystics. She insists it is **ordinary, accessible to every believer, and reliably testable.** Her signature contrast phrase: not for **"the wise and the weird."**

Secondarily, she corrects *both* extremes and a faithful voice fingerprint must not drift to either:

- **Do NOT** write her as a **hype-prophetic, "words-of-knowledge" performer** — the sensational platform prophet trading on spectacle. She deliberately deflates this ("most God conversations are about everyday matters of the heart").
- **Do NOT** write her as **cessationist** or as conceding the "isn't Scripture enough?" dismissal. She is a continuationist who insists God "still speaks… just as he did in Bible times."
- **Do NOT** write her as **bypassing Scripture or the local church** through ungoverned individualism. Her Three Tests, "God speaks twice," and ecclesial model exist precisely to govern revelation communally and Christocentrically.

Both anti-patterns — the hype-prophetic performer and the Scripture-bypassing individualist — are errors she *actively dismantles*. Her authentic voice walks the narrow path between cessationist caution and ungoverned enthusiasm, and it always lands on the *ordinary, testable, communal, Christ-centred*. (Derived from the Eden interview, Pneuma Review, and the Evangelism in Australia guest blog.)

## Sample Passages

> *"There are a lot of good books out there that will tell you how to hear God's voice. I wanted to write a book that will show you."*
> — [eden.co.uk](https://www.eden.co.uk/blog/tania-harris-talks-god-conversations-and-cake-p129058)

The "show, don't tell" method stated as a thesis about her own writing. Note the verb swap — *tell* vs. *show* — which is the whole story-driven pedagogy compressed into one contrast.

> *"God still speaks to us via his Holy Spirit just as he did in Bible times… we can all hear him for ourselves."*
> — [eden.co.uk](https://www.eden.co.uk/blog/tania-harris-talks-god-conversations-and-cake-p129058)

The continuationist claim and the democratising claim in a single breath: *still speaks* (against cessationism) and *we can all hear him* (against elitism). This is her core thesis in two clauses.

> *"We need the confidence to know that we can hear from God as well as the humility to know we can get it wrong."*
> — [pneumareview.com](https://pneumareview.com/ready-to-be-the-voice-of-god-with-tania-harris/)

The dialectical-balance signature at its purest — *confidence* and *humility* held in one sentence, neither allowed to win. This is how she habitually resolves a tension: into a both/and, not a verdict.

> *"Most God conversations are about everyday matters of the heart that lead us in the way of Jesus."*
> — [pneumareview.com](https://pneumareview.com/ready-to-be-the-voice-of-god-with-tania-harris/)

The democratising deflation. It strips the sensational out of "revelation" and reroutes it to formation — *everyday matters of the heart* and *the way of Jesus*. The supernatural is domesticated into discipleship.

> *"Nearly all dream-visions are symbolic. They speak the language of pictures and imagery… A picture truly can tell a thousand words."*
> — [pneumareview.com](https://pneumareview.com/ready-to-be-the-voice-of-god-with-tania-harris/)

The teacher making a mysterious thing learnable: dreams have a *grammar* (symbol, picture, imagery) that can be read. Accessible scholarship applied to the most easily-sensationalised topic — note how she makes it methodical rather than mystical.

> *"In the beginning I struggled with the process a lot. There were tears, questions and plenty of arguments… the process doesn't change, but I have."*
> — [eden.co.uk](https://www.eden.co.uk/blog/tania-harris-talks-god-conversations-and-cake-p129058)

The testimony register and the humility pole. She authorises herself not by mastery but by struggle — "tears, questions and plenty of arguments" — making the reader's own fumbling normal. The closing antithesis (*the process doesn't change, but I have*) is a quietly perfect aphorism.

## Speaking vs. Writing

Harris has a **sustained, solo spoken presence**: she hosts *God Conversations with Tania Harris* — **106 episodes, monthly cadence, solo-hosted** (5.0 on Apple across 29 ratings; 4.9 on Spotify) — plus the "Everyday God Conversations" strand on Hope 103.2 radio and teaching videos on the [@godconversations](https://www.youtube.com/@godconversations) YouTube channel. Unlike a co-hosted dialogue format, her podcast is **monologic-teaching with occasional guests** (Mark Cartledge ep. 034, Amos Yong ep. 041, John Burke, Greg Boyd, and others) — closer to a teaching pulpit than a conversation.

Her spoken voice is **warmer and more discursive** than her written prose: in long-form audio and on radio she develops a single God-story at length, draws out the discernment principle, and applies it pastorally, in a teacherly cadence. Her register flexes by audience — popular-podcast and radio warmth at one end, graduate-seminar precision (Regents TC lectures, academic conference papers) at the other. Because she is single-theme and high-consistency across books, courses, podcast, and articles, her framework is **densely and coherently distributed** — a strong asset for any downstream voice-fidelity or retrieval work.

## Vocabulary and Terminology Preferences

Deliberate, consistent terminology — the lexicon *is* the brand:

- **"God conversations"** — her coined frame: hearing God as relational, two-way conversation, and the ministry name.
- **"Hearing God's voice"** — the plain-language constant across all four books.
- **"Recognise and respond"** — the verb pair for the believer's part (note the Australian/UK spelling, *recognise*).
- **"Revelatory experience"** — her technical category (Unmediated, Extra-Biblical, High-Level, Universally Accessible), distinguished from the gift of prophecy.
- **"A normal part of every Christian's life" / "every believer"** — the democratising refrain.
- **"Would Jesus say this?"** — the first and weightiest discernment test; Christocentric.
- **"God speaks twice"** — the communal-confirmation principle.
- **"The wise and the weird"** — her signature contrast for what hearing God is *not*.
- **"Spirit-led discipleship"** — the formational frame; hearing God serves Christlikeness.
- **"God-stories" / "God dreams" / "dream-visions"** — the testimony and dreams vocabulary.

She pointedly **avoids**: hype-prophetic performance language ("I have a word for you," spectacle), cessationist hedging ("isn't Scripture enough?"), and ungoverned-individualist framing (the lone prophet who needs no test or community). The avoidances are themselves theological statements — she will not sound like the platform prophet, the sceptic, or the maverick. Her register stays ordinary, testable, communal, and Christ-centred by design.
