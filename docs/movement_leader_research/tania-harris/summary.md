## Who she is

Rev. Dr. Tania Harris is an Australian Pentecostal pastor, practical theologian, author, and broadcaster, ordained with the **Australian Christian Churches** and founding director of **God Conversations** — the ministry and brand she built around a single relational reframe: hearing God as a two-way *conversation* available to every believer. Her vocation grew from a personal turning point: as a young woman (around age 21) she prayed what she calls a radical prayer — *"Lord, speak to me. And if you make it really clear, I'll do whatever you say"* — and began to experience God speaking. At 26 she sensed a call to full-time ministry, unexpected given a conservative background "where women weren't allowed to carry the offering bag, much less be pastors." In her early 30s she planted and led a church in a beachside Melbourne suburb, as a single woman.

She is unusual in pairing a popular-ministry footprint with serious academic work. She earned a **PhD from Alphacrucis College in 2020** for empirical practical-theology research on contemporary Pentecostal revelatory experience, published as the scholarly monograph *Hearing God's Voice* (Brill, *Global Pentecostal and Charismatic Studies* vol. 47, 2023). Her three trade books — *God Conversations* (Authentic Media, 2017), *The Church Who Hears God's Voice* (Paternoster, 2022), and *God Dreams* (Authentic Media, 2024) — translate that research for a general readership. Her current footprint: leading God Conversations from Sydney, a visiting lectureship in Pentecostal Spirituality at Regents Theological College (UK), a monthly podcast, and ongoing speaking and broadcasting across Australia, New Zealand and the UK. Her personal life is largely undocumented (no public birth date; single, never married) — this dossier presents her vocationally and theologically rather than inventing detail.

## What she contributes

Harris's project answers three questions — **How does God speak? How do you know it's God? What difference does it make to the church?** — and her signature move is to teach the answers through *story* rather than rule: "There are a lot of good books that will *tell* you how to hear God's voice. I wanted to write a book that will *show* you." Three things make her distinctive. She **democratises the prophetic** — hearing God is "a normal part of every Christian's life," not for "the wise and the weird." She **makes discernment testable** through the **Three Tests** — *Would Jesus say this?* (Christ-alignment in Scripture, first and most important), *Is someone else saying this?* ("God speaks twice"; communal confirmation, not the lone prophet), and *Are spiritual signs following this?* (supernatural confirmation), modeled on Peter's Joppa vision. And she **holds the dialectic** — "the confidence to know we can hear from God as well as the humility to know we can get it wrong."

Beneath the warm pastoral prose sits real scholarly apparatus: her central claim of **phenomenological equivalency** (today's hearing-God experience is the *same kind* as the biblical one, while remaining distinct from canon) and her technical category of the **revelatory experience** (Unmediated, Extra-Biblical, "High-Level," Universally Accessible — distinct from the specialist gift of prophecy). Her ecclesiology grounds all of it: revelation belongs inside Scripture (the test) and the local church (the container). The *Hearing God's Voice* monograph carries academic endorsers including Wolfgang Vondey, Frank Macchia, Jacqueline Grey, and Kenneth Archer.
