# Calling Profile

Tiffany Smith's calling was forged in the field before it was ever written on a page. Two years as an International Mission Board missionary in Japan turned a Texas psychology graduate into a missiologist with a lifelong conviction: that the work of God moves through multiplied disciples and multiplied churches, not through programs that stay put. That overseas season is the origin she keeps returning to, and it set the trajectory for everything that followed.

From Japan, her calling matured inside Southern Baptist mission infrastructure rather than outside it. She mobilized missionaries with the Southern Baptists of Texas Convention, served as a National Mobilizer with the Send Institute, built leadership-pipeline training as a NAMB Pipeline Consultant, and since August 2021 has coordinated church-planting strategy for the entire continent as Send Network Strategies Coordinator at the North American Mission Board. Across more than two decades, the through-line has been mobilization — finding leaders, developing them, sequencing their growth, and sending them. She frames her own work as "catalytic, movement-focused," language that reveals how she sees her vocation: not to run a ministry but to catalyze movements that outlast her involvement.

Her academic formation matches the calling. A Doctorate in Missional Leadership from New Orleans Baptist Theological Seminary and a Master of Divinity from Southwestern Baptist Theological Seminary gave theological and missiological structure to instincts she had already lived. But her deepest expertise is practitioner expertise — the judgment accumulated across thousands of coaching conversations, cohort calls, and equipping sessions with church planters and emerging leaders.

What is newer in her arc is the decision to put her own voice into public under her own name. For most of her career her best thinking has lived inside institutions and other people's platforms. Late in the arc she opened "Missional Mayhem" — a blog and, beginning in 2025, a podcast — to say in her own words what she has spent two decades practicing: how to multiply disciples, how to develop leaders, how to keep the ministry of presence while engaging powerful new tools like AI, and what it costs a woman to lead in church planting. Her calling now has two layers: the institutional mobilizer who equips planters across North America, and the emerging public voice trying to make that embodied practice reachable by the planters who will never sit in a NAMB cohort.
