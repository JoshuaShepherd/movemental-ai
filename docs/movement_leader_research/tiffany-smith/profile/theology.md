# Theology & Missiological Emphases

Tiffany Smith writes and works as a missiologist first. Her theological emphases are practical and movemental rather than systematic — oriented toward how the church multiplies and how leaders are formed and sent.

## Multiplication and "movemental" growth

Her central conviction is that disciples and churches are meant to multiply, not merely add. She uses "movemental" as her own descriptor for the kind of catalytic, reproducing growth she is after, and frames mobilization (finding, developing, and sending leaders) as the engine of it. Her IMB years in Japan ground this in a missionary's instinct for engaging the nations and reaching people far from the church.

## Relational discipleship modeled on Jesus' circles (the 3, 12, & 72)

She reads Jesus' relational pattern as a deliberate model for discipleship and community. In her essay on church community she names three concentric circles drawn from the Gospels — the **3** (the inner circle of Peter, James, and John), the **12** (the disciples Jesus walked with daily and shaped for ministry), and the **72** (the broader community trained and sent). Her theological claim is that Jesus "modeled intentional, limited, and scalable relationships," that "every circle has its purpose, and confusing them sets us up for relational hurt," and that pursuing deep, sacred friendship "in a culture that is distracted, exhausted, and isolated… is an act of rebellion."

## "The sent life" in a post-Christian world

She treats mission as a way of life ("living the sent life") to be carried wherever God places a believer, and she reads the present North American moment as post-Christian and culturally chaotic — a context that calls for grace and truth held together rather than retreat.

## Technology, AI, and the ministry of presence

A distinctive and recurring theological-ethical theme: she presses the question of how to use powerful tools, including AI, "from disruption to missional opportunity" without losing the irreplaceable ministry of presence. She frames this as a discipleship and stewardship question, not merely a productivity one.

## Women in missional and church-planting leadership

She speaks and writes directly about the calling, singleness, and leadership development of women in church planting and missions — treating it as a theological and ecclesial question, not only a practical one.

## Rest, abiding, and soul care

Alongside the activism of multiplication, her writing returns to abiding, anchoring rhythms, and not growing weary — a theology of sustainable, dependent ministry that resists burnout.
