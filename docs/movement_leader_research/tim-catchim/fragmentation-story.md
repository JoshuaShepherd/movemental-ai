## The Story

Tim Catchim is one of the two names on the cover of *The Permanent Revolution: Apostolic Imagination and Practice for the 21st Century Church* — the foundational APEST text he co-authored with Alan Hirsch for InterVarsity Press — and again on *The Permanent Revolution Playbook: APEST for the People of God*, the 2014 six-week group format that put the framework into the hands of working teams. He earned a B.A. in Biblical Studies from Faulkner University, planted in urban and semi-urban contexts, started a curbside recycling service and the Go Green Initiative, worked with at-risk youth, and now lives and works in Nashville, Tennessee. In 2021 he founded **Trimtab** — a coaching and consulting agency at trimtab.guide built around APEST application, team dynamics, and organizational design, anchored by the **Co-Lab** community of practitioners. Simultaneously he serves as **Team Leader at OneLife Nashville**, a movemental church plant currently in its foundation-laying phase, with its public site sitting on a Wix subdomain at onelifenashville.wixsite.com.

That is a remarkably coherent body of work — two books with one of the most-cited missional theologians alive, a coaching agency, a Co-Lab cohort, a church plant, and a leadership philosophy that integrates APEST with atonement theology, MBTI, Enneagram, group dynamics, and organizational design — and almost none of it is searchable in any structured form. The two books are indexed at IVP, Amazon, Apple Books, and AbeBooks, with a long-form summary at lifeandleadership.com and a Clay.earth profile at clay.earth/profile/tim-catchim. He is an active enough contributor to Hermeneutics Stack Exchange to have a user page at hermeneutics.stackexchange.com/users/16471/tim-catchim. Beyond that, the digital archive thins out fast. The dossier flags social media presence (Twitter/X, LinkedIn, Facebook, Instagram, YouTube) as **not confirmed**. No personal podcast, no newsletter, no video channel, no article inventory, no conference recordings, no course platform.

What is missing is not the work — the work is happening every week in coaching conversations, in Co-Lab cohort sessions, in OneLife team huddles, in writing partnerships, in client engagements. What is missing is the **bridge between Tim's embodied practice and a public archive**. The Permanent Revolution is fifteen-plus years old; the playbook is from 2014; Trimtab is from 2021. The decade-plus of pragmatic APEST application that sits between those publication dates — the case studies, the framework refinements, the team-design patterns, the sermons, the OneLife stories — lives in his head, his coaching notes, and the rooms he facilitates. The downstream effect: a practitioner whose foundational text is canonical in the movemental conversation but whose ongoing voice is barely audible online.
