# Sandbox Season Playbook

> Runnable version of the nine-piece sandbox canon. Templates, session plans, and facilitator notes for one 12-week season with one organization. Covers 5–6 staff, 3–5 candidate experiments, one portfolio outcome.

## What this playbook is

The nine sandbox articles are the argument. This playbook is what running them looks like on a Tuesday. A senior team, a 12-week calendar, four templates, one shared document, and a set of facilitator notes that get the work done without converting the work into theater.

It is written to be run by an internal Portfolio Owner with Movemental as the outside facilitator. An organization that wants to run it without Movemental can — the templates and notes are sufficient — but the facilitation role has to be held by someone willing to resist the team's own momentum.

## The core decisions (made, not enumerated)

**Season length: 12 weeks.** Short enough to stay inside a quarterly rhythm. Long enough for three full experiment cycles with real revision between them. Six weeks is a pilot; twenty weeks is a program; twelve is a season.

**Team size: 5–6 participants.** Below four, you do not get enough experiments. Above seven, the shared document loses signal and the weekly session stops being a working meeting. Mixed seniority on purpose: one senior sponsor who attends three of twelve sessions, one Portfolio Owner in every session, three to four experiment operators from two or three departments.

**Cadence: weekly 90-minute facilitated session + ~3 hours async work per participant.** Total commitment: 4.5 hours/week × 12 weeks = 54 hours per participant over a season. This sits inside what a stretched nonprofit can actually absorb without breaking the rest of their work.

**Artifact discipline: one shared document.** Not a tool, not a platform, not a dashboard. A single living document (Notion page, Google Doc, or markdown file) that every participant can read and write. Private experiments are not sandbox experiments.

**Safety Owner is named on day one.** Usually the senior leader who already owns governance or legal. Their flag, once written, is load-bearing — not a vote, not negotiable.

**What's out-of-scope by default:** Any use case involving minors, vulnerable populations, trauma-adjacent programming, pastoral care content, or regulated financial workflows. These require explicit expansion of scope before they can enter a candidate list. Season one stays clear of them.

## The season at a glance

| Week | Phase | Session focus | Artifact produced |
|------|-------|---------------|-------------------|
| 1 | Orient | Kickoff, roles, boundaries | Season charter |
| 2 | Discover | First 8-pattern scan | Candidate list (12–30) |
| 3 | Discover | Filter to 3–5; write experiment briefs | Experiment briefs |
| 4 | Test | First experiments run | Run 1 results |
| 5 | Test | First reviews + scoring | First scoring sheet |
| 6 | Test | Second experiments run | Run 2 results |
| 7 | Test | Second reviews + scoring | Cumulative scoring |
| 8 | Judge | Ethical & relational flag round 1 | Flag paragraphs |
| 9 | Judge | Third experiments (if time) + scoring | Final scoring |
| 10 | Judge | Flag round 2 + reroute decisions | Reroute notes |
| 11 | Assemble | Portfolio draft | Portfolio page v1 |
| 12 | Assemble | Portfolio review + handoff | Portfolio v1 + one-pagers |

Three experiment cycles, staggered. Each cycle runs ~2 weeks from brief to score. Three cycles means the team feels the cadence by week six, which is when the formation begins to stick.

## Template A — Season Charter (Week 1 artifact)

```
SEASON CHARTER — [Organization] — [Start date] to [End date]

Senior Sponsor: [Name, title] — attends Weeks 1, 6, 12
Portfolio Owner: [Name, title] — attends all weeks
Experiment Team: [3–4 names and titles]
Safety Owner: [Name, title] — writes ethical & relational flags
Facilitator: [Name]

Boundaries inherited from Safety (list explicitly):
- [Data tier rules]
- [No-fly workflows — list specifically]
- [Approval thresholds]

Data classes in-scope this season:
- [List specific data classes that can be touched]

Data classes out-of-scope this season:
- [List — e.g., minor data, pastoral care notes, donor PII above tier X]

Success criteria for the season:
- A portfolio of 5–10 validated use cases
- 2–3 high-value, low-risk use cases ready for Solutions consideration
- One board-ready governance one-pager
- Five to six staff with trained pattern recognition and working judgment

Explicit non-goals:
- No tool procurement decisions this season
- No deployments to live donor-facing or youth-facing work
- No scaling of any experiment beyond the sandbox
```

Facilitator notes for Week 1: Do not skip the out-of-scope list. The team will want to list only in-scope items. The out-of-scope list is where the season's integrity lives. Spend 20 minutes on it. If the Safety Owner cannot articulate it on the spot, the season is not ready to start.

## Template B — The 8-Pattern Scan (Week 2 artifact)

One page per pattern. Three minutes per pattern in session; 20 minutes async beforehand.

```
PATTERN: [Name — e.g., Repetition]

Quick definition: [One sentence from the canon]

Where in our work, in the last 90 days, has something like this shown up?

Candidate 1:
- The work: [What it is, 1–2 sentences]
- Who does it now: [Role]
- Rough frequency / volume: [How often]
- Why this might be an AI value case: [1 sentence]
- Obvious trap: [What could go wrong specific to us]

Candidate 2: [same fields]

Candidate 3: [same fields]
```

Facilitator notes: If a pattern produces zero candidates in five minutes, move on — the pattern is not live in this org right now, and that is useful information. If a pattern produces eight candidates, take the four most specific and kill the rest. Vague candidates waste experiment cycles.

Personalization (pattern 8) gets a separate page and a reminder note: *no personalization candidate enters the filter list without Safety Owner pre-read.*

## Template C — Experiment Brief (Week 3 artifact, one per experiment)

```
EXPERIMENT BRIEF

Candidate name: [Short, specific — "Donor thank-you draft assist" not "fundraising AI"]
Pattern: [One of the eight]
Hypothesis: [One sentence — what this experiment might show]

INPUT
- What the assistant receives: [Specific artifacts, named]
- Source: [Where this material comes from]
- Data tier: [Per Safety — confirm this is in-scope]
- Redactions/substitutions: [Any applied, listed]

OUTPUT
- Artifact produced: [Specific form]
- Length / shape: [Approximate]
- Elements required: [Must contain X, Y]
- Register: [Voice/tone]

TIME COMPARISON
- Human-only baseline time: [Honest realistic, not worst case]
- Assisted total time (including review): [To be measured]

QUALITY JUDGMENT
- Named reviewer: [Not the experiment owner]
- Criteria (written in advance): [3–5 specific criteria]
- Verdict form: Paragraph, not score

RISK FLAGS (to be filled after run)
- Confidentiality: [Observed?]
- Accuracy: [Observed?]
- Authorship: [Observed?]
- Voice: [Observed?]
- Relational: [Observed?]

OWNER: [Name]
REVIEWER: [Name]
RUN WINDOW: [Dates]
```

Facilitator notes for Week 3: Reject any brief where the input is *some of our recent X*. Reject any brief where the reviewer is the same person as the owner. Reject any brief where the quality criteria read like *the output should be good*. Five rejections in week three is normal and correct.

## Template D — Scoring Sheet (Weeks 5, 7, 9)

One row per experiment, one column per dimension. Scores: green, yellow, red. Yellow is the default for uncertain; scoring up to green requires a written reason.

```
EXPERIMENT: [Name]

TIME SAVED
Score: [G/Y/R]
Measured minutes saved (net of review): [Specific number]
Reason: [1–2 sentences]

QUALITY IMPROVED
Score: [G/Y/R]
Reviewer verdict paragraph: [~100 words]

RISK INTRODUCED
Score: [G/Y/R]
Categories observed: [List from the five categories]
Incident register entry needed: [Y/N]

REPEATABILITY
Score: [G/Y/R]
Second-person run: [Did another staff member reproduce the result?]
Reason: [1–2 sentences]

OUT-LOUD-FOR-ONE QUESTION
For each green above: "What would have to be true for this score to be wrong?"
Answer: [Written by someone other than the scorer]
```

Facilitator notes for Weeks 5, 7, 9: The out-loud-for-one question is the single most important moment of the season. It is a thirty-second discipline that prevents six months of incidents. Do not skip it. If nobody can answer it, the score drops to yellow automatically.

## Template E — Ethical & Relational Flag Paragraph (Weeks 8 and 10)

Written by the Safety Owner for every experiment that scored green on at least three of four dimensions. One paragraph per experiment. Short. Specific. Sitting in the shared document alongside the scoring sheet.

```
FLAG FOR: [Experiment name]
WRITTEN BY: [Safety Owner name]
DATE: [Date]

AUTHORSHIP: [Could the person whose name is on this defend it as theirs? 1–2 sentences.]

VOICE: [Does this sound like us, or like everyone else doing this work? 1–2 sentences.]

TRUST: [If the recipient knew how this was made, would the relationship hold? 1–2 sentences.]

FORMATION: [What are we becoming by doing this at scale? 1–2 sentences.]

VERDICT: [Clear, one of four]
- NO FLAG RAISED — graduates to portfolio
- YELLOW FLAG — graduates with stated conditions [list]
- RED FLAG (REROUTE) — returns to sandbox with revised scope [describe]
- RED FLAG (PARK) — does not move forward this season; condition for reopening [describe]
```

Facilitator notes for Weeks 8 and 10: The Safety Owner's flag is not negotiable. Push back on any attempt to argue a red flag into a yellow. If the Safety Owner waffles, the flag is not working. The flag is supposed to create short-term friction for long-term coherence; that friction is the work.

## Template F — Portfolio Page (Week 11–12 artifact)

One-page format, one entry per validated use case.

```
USE CASE: [Name]
Pattern: [From the eight]
Scoring verdict: [G/G/G/G or combination]
Flag verdict: [From Template E]
Safety boundaries: [Which boundaries this use case respects]
Owner: [Name — who's accountable for what happens next]
Description (one paragraph): [What this use case is, specific enough for a cold reader]
Status: [Graduated / Held with caution / Parked]
```

At the top of the page:

```
SEASON PORTFOLIO — [Organization] — [End date]
Total use cases: [5–10]
- High value, low risk (candidates for Solutions): [2–3]
- High value, high risk (held with caution, Skills-stage): [1–2]
- Parked (waiting on specific conditions): [remainder]
Rejected (documented separately): [count + pointer to rejection log]
```

Facilitator notes for Week 12: The portfolio review is ten minutes per use case, maximum. If a use case takes longer than ten minutes to discuss, it does not belong in the portfolio yet — it belongs in another sandbox cycle. Size discipline is a forcing function. Resist the urge to be generous.

## Weekly session structure (reused across the season)

Every weekly session: 90 minutes, same shape, different content.

- **0–10 min — Anchor.** What moved since last week. Not narratives; specific artifacts written. Anyone without an artifact ready gets five minutes at the end, not the start.
- **10–60 min — Working block.** The week's primary work. Scan (Week 2), brief writing (Week 3), review (Weeks 5, 7, 9), flag writing (Weeks 8, 10), portfolio assembly (Weeks 11, 12).
- **60–75 min — Honest audit.** One question: *what did we almost miss this week?* Ten minutes on it. This is where formation happens.
- **75–90 min — Next-week setup.** Who owns what, by when, with what artifact deliverable.

Facilitator notes: The honest audit (minutes 60–75) is the session's load-bearing segment. Skip it under time pressure and the season becomes execution-only. Formation requires reflection-in-action; the audit is the reflection. Protect it.

## Adult-learning commitments embedded in the design

This is a formation curriculum dressed as a process. Five adult-learning principles are load-bearing in the design:

**Authentic tasks.** Every experiment uses the team's real work, not simulations. Relevance is the precondition for transfer; simulations do not produce the judgment formation requires.

**Spaced practice.** The three experiment cycles are intentionally spaced over 12 weeks rather than compressed. Spacing is what lets the eye develop between cycles.

**Social learning.** The shared document, the peer-reviewer pairing, and the Safety Owner role all externalize what would otherwise be private learning. Judgment is reinforced through public artifact-making, not private reflection.

**Reflection-in-action.** The honest audit and the flag paragraph are the reflection moments. Both happen while the work is still live, not afterward. Reflection *after* a season is too late to shape the work; reflection *during* a session is how the season ripens.

**Progressive disclosure.** The eight-pattern catalog is introduced once, in Week 2, and then referenced across the season. Participants do not need to master all eight in Week 2 — they need to encounter them and return to them as the cycles surface more examples. Repetition without repetition.

## Handoff

A season produces three artifacts that outlive the season: the portfolio (Template F), a governance one-pager (derived from Safety Owner's accumulated flags and boundaries), and a cohort of 5–6 staff with formed pattern recognition and working experiment discipline. That cohort becomes the basis for the Skills-stage engagement, the subject of the next playbook.

**Appendix: What this playbook deliberately does not include**

- Tool recommendations (the season runs on whatever assistant the organization has already approved).
- Prompt templates (those belong to the team, not the playbook — the patterns are transferable across prompts).
- Specific metrics targets (the scoring is qualitative for a reason; a target would bias the scoring).
- Board presentation templates (the portfolio page is the board artifact; additional layers compress out the specificity that makes it trustworthy).
- Cross-organization benchmarking (averages destroy specificity; see Article 9, *Discovery Engine, Not Generator*).
