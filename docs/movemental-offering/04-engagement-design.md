# Sandbox Season — Engagement Design

> How Movemental runs the sandbox-season playbook with a client. Shape, cadence, roles, deliverables, pricing structure, and what the engagement deliberately does not promise.

## The one-sentence shape

Movemental runs a 12-week facilitated sandbox season for one organization, with a cohort of 5–6 of their staff, producing a portfolio of validated AI use cases, a governance one-pager, and a trained internal team capable of running the next season without us.

## Why this shape and not others

Several shapes were considered and rejected. Each rejection is load-bearing.

**Not a workshop series.** A two- or three-day workshop produces energy and no formation. The learning does not survive the return to normal work. Workshops are a training artifact; this engagement is a formation artifact.

**Not a cross-organization cohort (yet).** A multi-org cohort is attractive for Movemental's economics but weak for the client. Cross-org cohorts cannot share real work in detail, which collapses the authentic-task principle. A single-org season keeps the experiments specific. Cross-org cohorts become viable in a later product (Skills-stage curriculum, covered in a separate engagement design).

**Not open-ended consulting.** Hourly engagements bias both parties toward more hours. A fixed-fee season forces scope discipline and makes the client's budget predictable. Open-ended engagements also dilute the accountability — with a 12-week endpoint, the portfolio *has* to exist by Week 12.

**Not outcome-based pricing.** Paying on "ROI realized" sounds attractive and creates perverse incentives. The outcomes that matter (formed judgment, coherent governance, honest portfolio) are deliberately not measurable in the timeframe of the engagement. Movemental is paid for the work done; the organization owns whether they carry it forward.

## Team roles (Movemental side)

**Lead Facilitator.** Runs the twelve weekly sessions. Present every session. Holds the honest-audit discipline. Writes the governance one-pager draft in Week 8 for Safety Owner review.

**Senior Advisor.** Josh or another senior member. Attends Weeks 1, 6, and 12. The Senior Sponsor's counterpart — the person the client CEO/ED can push back on. Signs off on the portfolio at handoff.

**Producer.** Manages scheduling, artifact templates, async communication, and the shared document hygiene. Not a facilitator role; an infrastructure role. One producer can carry 3–4 simultaneous seasons.

For a single season: 1 Lead Facilitator (12 × 2 hrs session + prep = ~60 hours), 1 Senior Advisor (3 sessions + 6 hours consult = ~12 hours), 1 Producer (1 hour/week × 12 = ~12 hours). Total Movemental delivery hours: ~84 hours per season.

## Team roles (client side)

Reprinted from the playbook for clarity in the sales conversation.

**Senior Sponsor.** CEO or ED. Attends Weeks 1, 6, 12. Signs the season charter. Defends the portfolio to the board.

**Portfolio Owner.** Senior leader who will own the portfolio after handoff. Attends every session. Usually a strategic operations role or a chief-of-staff equivalent.

**Safety Owner.** Senior leader responsible for governance or ethics. Writes the ethical and relational flag paragraphs in Weeks 8 and 10. Non-negotiable role.

**Experiment Team.** 3–4 staff from 2–3 departments. Mixed seniority. Do the experiment work.

## Deliverables (fixed list)

At Week 12, the client receives six things. Every season, same six. No bespoke additions; scope discipline is the product.

1. **Validated portfolio** (Template F from the playbook). 5–10 use cases with scoring verdicts, flag verdicts, and status.
2. **Governance one-pager** drafted by Lead Facilitator, reviewed by Safety Owner, signed by Senior Sponsor. Board-ready.
3. **Portfolio one-pager.** Board-ready summary of the portfolio for the next Board agenda.
4. **Shared document archive.** The full working record of the season — experiment briefs, scoring sheets, flag paragraphs. Client-owned.
5. **Rejection log.** Candidates that were screened out, with reasons. Used in future seasons.
6. **Season-two scope memo.** 2-page assessment of what should be in the next sandbox season (typically 2–3 quarters later) and what should instead move into Skills-stage work.

## Pricing structure

Fixed fee by engagement zone, priced to the client's revenue tier and scope breadth.

| Zone | Org revenue | Scope | Fixed fee | Payment structure |
|------|-------------|-------|-----------|-------------------|
| Small | Under $2M | Single department, 4 participants | $18,000 | 50% start / 50% Week 12 |
| Mid | $2M–$10M | Multi-department, 5–6 participants | $32,000 | $10K start / $11K Week 6 / $11K Week 12 |
| Large | $10M–$50M | Org-wide, 6–8 participants + 2 observers | $55,000 | $18K start / $18K Week 6 / $19K Week 12 |
| Enterprise | $50M+ | Org-wide, multiple cohorts | Custom | Milestone structure |

Why milestone-weighted instead of monthly: monthly payments incentivize drag. Milestone-weighted payments reward completion. If the season goes sideways at Week 4, both parties should feel that sideways in the structure.

Why a published range instead of negotiated: published pricing compresses the sales cycle and sets the expectation that Movemental is selling a product, not custom consulting. Pricing that bends to the client ends up bending on scope too.

## What's explicitly out of scope (named on page one of every proposal)

- Tool procurement (client buys their own assistants, independent of Movemental).
- Implementation engineering (deployments into production systems).
- Ongoing facilitation after Week 12 (quarterly portfolio refreshes are a separate $4–6K engagement).
- Cross-departmental work beyond the designated cohort.
- Any experiment touching minors, vulnerable populations, pastoral care content, or regulated financial workflows in season one. (These can be brought in in season two, with explicit expansion of Safety boundaries.)
- Board presentations, donor meetings, or external communications about the season.
- Recruitment, hiring, or HR implications of the season's findings.

A proposal that does not include this out-of-scope list fails. It is the list that keeps the season's integrity intact; removing it is the most common way an engagement slides into scope creep.

## Follow-on products (priced separately, named upfront)

**Quarterly portfolio refresh.** $4,000–$6,000 per quarter. 3 facilitated sessions, updated portfolio, updated governance. Designed to run every quarter after the initial season.

**Skills-stage engagement.** 16 weeks, $45,000–$75,000, dedicated to deepening the judgment of the cohort on the high-risk use cases that were held with caution. Runs after at least one portfolio refresh.

**Solutions-stage advisory.** Project-based, scoped to a specific deployment the client chooses to move into production. Not sold until the portfolio has existed for at least two quarters.

The full arc (Safety read + Sandbox + Skills + Solutions) is named so the client can see where they are in the staircase. Movemental does not require the client to take every step. Many organizations will do the Sandbox season and then run everything else internally. That is the design.

## Criteria for accepting an engagement

Movemental declines seasons where:

- The CEO or ED will not commit to attending Weeks 1, 6, 12. (Senior Sponsor cannot be delegated downward below the role that will defend the portfolio to the board.)
- A Safety Owner cannot be named on day one. (Without a named Safety Owner, flag discipline collapses.)
- The organization is under active acute crisis (layoffs, leadership transition in motion, legal action). (A sandbox needs stable enough conditions that a 12-week horizon is credible.)
- The organization wants a season shorter than 10 weeks. (Compression kills the spaced-practice principle.)
- The organization wants to use the season to build a productized AI tool rather than form internal judgment. (See Article 9, *Discovery Engine, Not Generator*.)

Declining is a product decision, not a personality decision. Movemental's product is seasons that actually produce formation. Seasons run in the wrong conditions produce artifacts that look correct and form no one. Those artifacts eventually hurt both the client and Movemental's reputation; declining is the cheaper move for both parties.

## How a season actually gets sold

The sales sequence, in order:

1. **Research-and-brief.** Before any proposal, Movemental produces a situation brief on the prospective client (their strategic moment, their leadership, their likely pain points). Evidence of understanding beats sales copy at this stage.
2. **Capability brief** (Artifact 6 in the chain). A 5–10 page document circulated before the first real conversation. Compressed methodology, shape of the engagement, pricing structure, out-of-scope list.
3. **Exemplar review.** A composite case showing what a completed season looks like for a comparable organization. Usually the point where a serious buyer either leans in or steps back.
4. **Tailored proposal** (Artifact 7). Customized to the specific client's cohort composition, data classes, and scope. 2–5 pages. Signable.
5. **One-page proposal** (Artifact 8). A ninety-second compression for the executive who needs to approve the line item.

The entire sales motion is designed to be short. A serious buyer should move from first conversation to signed contract in three to five weeks. Deals that require longer than eight weeks of sales cycle are usually deals that will also require ten weeks of scope negotiation once they start — a pattern worth walking away from.

## What success looks like from inside a season

For the client:
- Week 1–3: Mild discomfort at the rigor. Some team members push back on scoring discipline.
- Week 4–6: The first portfolio candidates start looking real. The team's pattern-recognition vocabulary begins to normalize.
- Week 7–9: The first flag returns with a red. The team learns what the flag actually does.
- Week 10–12: The portfolio takes shape. The senior team begins describing the work in canon language without being prompted.

For Movemental:
- The Lead Facilitator is redundant by Week 10. The Portfolio Owner is running most of the session. This is the goal.
- The Senior Advisor's Week 12 session is a handoff conversation, not a delivery conversation.
- The client asks about the next season before Week 12 ends. If they do not, something went wrong in the formation that is not visible yet.

## Closing design note

The engagement is priced and scoped to be a real product, not a bespoke consultation. Productization is what lets Movemental do this work at scale without losing the specificity the work requires. The trade-off is real: a client who wants custom everything will go elsewhere. That is acceptable. The client who wants a proven season that produces actual formation is the client this engagement is designed for.
