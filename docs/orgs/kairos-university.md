## Executive Summary

**Kairos University** is a private, ATS- and HLC-accredited theological university headquartered in **Sioux Falls, South Dakota**. It is simultaneously a degree-granting institution and the **institutional hub of a global competency-based theological education (CBTE) network** connecting ~100 partner organizations across 63+ countries.

What distinguishes Kairos is not a single product but a **people-and-process platform**: a subscription-priced, mentor-team-driven, outcome-based model that treats the student's **vocational context as the primary classroom**. The seminary does not primarily deliver fixed courses; it **stewards connections** between students, mentor teams, learning-experience creators, partners, and local faith communities.

Kairos launched the **Kairos Project** in 2014 (14 pilot students at Sioux Falls Seminary), renamed the accredited entity **Kairos University** in October 2021, and claims status as **one of the largest and most diverse accredited CBTE systems in the world**. Independent observers (Paul LeBlanc, *Students First*; ATS *Colloquy Online*) cite Kairos as a pioneering CBTE case study.

**Do not confuse with:**
- **Kairos Pacific University** ([kairos.aeu.edu](https://kairos.aeu.edu)) — separate Korean-American evangelical institution in California
- **Kairos University (Florida)** — unrelated religious training school with lower accreditation
- **kairos.computer** — unrelated AI automation product

## 1. Identity & Mission

### Official calling (public-facing)

> *"Kairos University cultivates followers of Jesus who flourish in their vocations for the life of the world."*

Alternate phrasing used in catalog and about pages:

> *"We nurture / steward followers of Jesus who flourish in their vocations for the sake of the world."*

### Four brand pillars

Kairos repeatedly frames itself through four adjectives:

| Pillar | Meaning in practice |
|--------|---------------------|
| **Affordable** | Flat monthly subscription; no fee stack; debt reduction as design goal |
| **Accessible** | Global participation; context-local learning; no residential requirement |
| **Relevant** | Vocational-context proficiency; customized pathways; mentor-adapted work |
| **Faithful** | Discipleship-first framing; NAB Statement of Beliefs as doctrinal anchor |

### Kingdom calling (institutional mission statement)

From the 2023–2024 academic catalog:

> Kairos serves in the kingdom by providing systems of theological education and integrative counseling that are affordable, accessible, relevant, and faithful for the purpose of empowering people where they are, thereby offering a journey toward God's hope.

### Theological positioning

- **Interdenominational** in practice: 70+ Christian denominations represented in the network
- **Doctrinal anchor:** Affirms the **North American Baptist Conference Statement of Beliefs** (1982)
- **Historical Baptist roots** via Sioux Falls Seminary / German Baptist lineage — but explicitly multi-denominational today
- **Great Commission** framing: collaborative mission is not institutional preservation but participation in reconciliation through Christ

### What Kairos is *not*

- Not a traditional residential seminary model (though it maintains a Sioux Falls campus)
- Not a MOOC or content-delivery-only platform
- Not a software company — though software is central to operations
- Not solely an accrediting body — it is the **sole accredited degree-granting entity** for the network

## 2. History & Institutional Lineage

### Timeline

| Year | Event |
|------|-------|
| **1858** | Rochester Theological Seminary (NY) establishes German Department — origin of Sioux Falls Seminary lineage |
| **1931** | Becomes German Baptist Seminary |
| **1945** | Renamed North American Baptist Seminary |
| **1949** | Relocates to Sioux Falls, SD |
| **2007** | Renamed Sioux Falls Seminary (May 18) |
| **2009** | Moves to current environmentally focused campus adjacent to Augustana University |
| **2014** | **Kairos Project launches** — 14 students, fall cohort; Greg Henson becomes 12th president |
| **2020** | Joins Digital Theological Library as co-owner |
| **2021 (Oct)** | Sioux Falls Seminary legally renamed **Kairos University**; global network formalized |
| **2021–2025** | Legacy partner integrations continue; curriculum evolves through numbered versions (7.0 → 11.0) |
| **2025** | Curriculum transition away from numbered version labels toward unified rolling updates |

### Legacy partner consolidation narrative

Kairos University emerged when multiple historically distinct seminaries **ceded governance** to a shared platform while preserving brand identity as "schools within a university." Legacy partnerships use teach-out agreements: partner schools phase out independent accredited programs, transfer students and core faculty into Kairos, and dissolve as separate legal entities while retaining dba brands.

The 2024 **10-Year Stewardship Report** frames this as Spirit-led convergence of parallel movements:
- German Baptists in Rochester, NY
- Wesleyan farmers in Pennsylvania
- Quakers in Texas
- Baptist leaders in Edmonton church basements
- And others

### Name etymology

"Kairos" (καιρός) — the opportune, God-appointed moment — signals **contextual, vocational, kairos-time discipleship** as opposed to chronos-time classroom scheduling. The name also distinguishes the project from the parent seminary brand before the 2021 rename.

## 5. The Kairos Project & CBTE Model

### Definition

The **Kairos Project** is Kairos University's flagship educational mode: a **journey of discipleship** that also yields accredited degrees. It blends:

- Competency-based education (CBE) outcome orientation
- Traditional learning experiences where appropriate
- Fully customized educational pathways
- Context-rooted ministry and vocation integration

### CBTE — Competency-Based Theological Education

Kairos did not invent CBE, but claims leadership in **CBTE** — applying competency logic specifically to theological formation. Key shift:

> Traditional model: credit hours × seat time × fixed syllabus  
> CBTE model: integrated outcomes × demonstrated proficiency × mentor-team assessment × contextual adaptation

Paul LeBlanc (*Students First*, Harvard Education Press) profiles Kairos as a leading CBTE pioneer. ATS *Colloquy Online* (Graham, Jan 2022) cites Sioux Falls Seminary/Kairos for the **"most institutionally comprehensive redevelopment"** among ATS member schools exploring CBTE.

### Platform philosophy (critical distinction)

Greg Henson's **Platform Thinking** white paper and blog series insist:

> Kairos is a platform — **not primarily a software platform**, though software is involved — but a **people-and-process-based platform**.

The seminary's role shifts from **sole content provider** to **steward of interactions** between:
- Students ↔ vocational contexts
- Learning experience creators ↔ sojourners
- Partners ↔ accredited degree pathways
- Mentors ↔ proficiency assessment

Software metaphors map to Kairos constructs:

| Software concept | Kairos equivalent |
|------------------|-------------------|
| API (Application Programming Interface) | **Program Interface** — non-negotiable partnership requirements |
| SDK (Software Development Kit) | **Program Development Kit** — tools for contextualized program building |
| Knowledge Base | **Story Center** — blog, white papers, narrative documentation |

### Core requirements (all partners/programs)

Every Kairos Project partnership must include:

1. **Mentor Team** structure
2. **Development Path** architecture for accreditation tracking
3. **Master Assessment Review** process and rubric
4. **Aligned Program Outcomes** (shared degree outcomes)
5. **Partner Liaison** designation
6. **Subscription Pricing** for tuition
7. **Gatherings** participation while enrolled
8. **Educational Pathways** (standard or partner-customized)

## 6. Educational Frameworks & Vocabulary

### Six principles of CBTE

Co-developed with **Northwest Seminary** (Kenton Anderson, Immerse program) as a conversation partner:

| Principle | Summary |
|-----------|---------|
| **Collaborative Mission** | Great Commission work involves voices outside institutional walls — churches, businesses, parachurch, practitioners |
| **Mentored Teamwork** | Discipleship in community; relationships carry more authority than roles; co-learning |
| **Contextualized Discipleship** | Formation always happens *somewhere*; context shapes the journey |
| **Customized Proficiency** | Standards of excellence are contextually defined, not institutionally imposed |
| **Integrated Outcomes** | Nothing is fully discrete; outcomes are telos, not isolated competencies |
| **Holistic Assessment** | Proficiency assessed across content, character, and craft as a whole |

### Six practices of CBTE

| Practice | Summary |
|----------|---------|
| **Affordable Programs** | Low operating cost by design; scholarships shift burden rather than solving cost structure |
| **Unified Systems** | Transcripts, email, billing, assessment — integrated, not siloed |
| **Flexible Technology** | Tech must flex as much as the educational journey |
| **Collective Governance** | Power distributed; previously excluded voices at the governance table |
| **Continuous Improvement** | Spirit-led change as normal; expect ongoing iteration |
| **Quality Framework** | Explicit articulation of how quality is defined and maintained |

### Standards of Excellence trilogy

Blog/white paper series framing contextual quality:

- **Context** — proficiency definitions emerge from local vocational reality
- **Community** — formation is communal, not individualistic
- **Change** — contingency and adaptation are features, not bugs

Related concept: **"Knowing is Integrative"** — knowledge requires content + character + craft integration.

### Kairos Quality Framework (four cascading commitments)

1. Standards of excellence are **contextually defined**
2. Shared **principles and practices** shape everything
3. Quality governed through **shared processes**, not shared content
4. Power of program development, assessment, and engagement **distributed** across the learning community

### Content · Character · Craft

The **Development Path** for each outcome organizes learning around three focus areas:

- **Content** — knowledge domains (Bible, theology, history, etc.)
- **Character** — dispositions, spiritual maturity, way-of-Jesus formation
- **Craft** — skills, practices, vocational competencies

Assessment rubrics are **generalized and holistic** — not task-specific or analytic — enabling mentor teams to particularize expectations per student context.

### Ethnohermeneutics

Dean **Larry Caldwell**'s framework (cited in Kairos blog): biblical interpretation must engage the **local community of believers**. Featured prominently in Gatherings and case study discussions as a method for context-rooted exegesis.

### Network practices (1 Corinthians 3:9 collaboration ethos)

Partners commit to: Partnership · Transparency · Shared Resources · Outcome-based learning · Local Church integration · Organic Discipleship · Innovation · Technology · Ongoing Improvement · Hospitality

## 7. Curriculum Architecture

### Outcome-based structure

Programs are organized around **program outcomes**, not course lists. Each outcome has:

- **Development Path** — outcome description, integrated statement of Christian maturity, content/character/craft focus areas, development process steps
- **Standard Path** — optional faculty-authored target sequence (adaptable)
- **Outcome Path** (Kairos 10.0+) — unified path combining development + standard path features
- **Indicators of proficiency** — observable behaviors, attitudes, skills, dispositions
- **Starting projects** — prior-learning assessment and proficiency gap identification
- **Suggested inputs and interactions** — non-prescriptive resources and activities

### Bookends: Starting Well · Continuing Well

Every program begins with **Starting Well** (mentor team formation, pathway design) and ends with **Continuing Well** (integration, sending, completion). These are outcomes, not orientations.

### Master Assessment Review (MAR)

The shared assessment mechanism by which mentor teams document and confirm outcome proficiency. Uses **generalized holistic rubrics** — same rubric architecture across programs, particularized in conversation with student context.

### Curriculum versioning history

Kairos iterated numbered curriculum versions (1.0 through 11.0) with different structure/flexibility balances:

| Version era | Character |
|-------------|-----------|
| 1.0, 7.0, 8.0, 9.0 | More flexibility, less prescriptive structure |
| 3.0, 4.0, 5.0 | Exhaustive progress maps, rigid gathering requirements, 72 pages of targets |
| **10.0** | **"Supported flexibility"** — Outcome Paths with indicators + starting projects; single path per outcome |
| **11.0** | Doctoral (ThD, PhD) updates |
| **2025 transition** | Deprecating numbered version labels; unified rolling updates for all students |

### Prior learning & proficiency credit

- **BA level:** Up to 60 credits prior learning credit (case-by-case)
- **Graduate level:** Demonstrated proficiency for prior coursework / experience
- **Transfer credit:** Up to 2/3 of credits in select programs

### Blended degree paths

Kairos offers blended integrations (e.g., BA/MA, BA/MDiv, BAL/MAL) where master's-level work integrates with bachelor's outcomes.

## 9. Learning Experience Modalities

Kairos uses multiple experience types (especially visible in BA program documentation):

| Modality | Description |
|----------|-------------|
| **Gatherings** | Week-long large-group seminars (in-person Sioux Falls/Edmonton or virtual via Zoom); case study presentations; teaching sessions; breakout groups |
| **Huddles** | Smaller cohort learning experiences |
| **Self-Paced Learning Experiences** | Async Pathwright courses |
| **Scheduled Learning Experiences** | Cohort-based with registration windows (~4 weeks before start) |
| **Projects** | Contextual vocational projects adapted by mentor team |
| **Prior Learning Assessment** | Credit/proficiency for demonstrated prior formation |
| **Case Studies** | Vocational-context scenarios prepared pre-Gathering, discussed in small groups — frequently cited as students' favorite component |

### Gatherings evolution

- Pre-2020: Primarily in Sioux Falls, SD and Edmonton, Alberta
- COVID pivot (2020): Virtual Gatherings on Zoom — ~300 participants from 10 countries (nearly 2× some in-person events)
- Requirement evolution: MDiv once required 6 in-person Sioux Falls Gatherings → shifted to 4 case studies (online or in-person)
- Partner-led plenary/workshop integrations (e.g., Global Trust Partners, VantagePoint3)

### Learning Experience Library

Searchable catalog on **Pathwright** — all network learning experiences. Partners contribute courses; MOUs enable cross-institutional sharing beyond the shared LMS.

## 10. Mentor Team Model

Every Kairos student works with a **mentor team** of three roles:

| Role | Description |
|------|-------------|
| **Faculty Mentor** | Kairos Affiliate Faculty; integrationist; assigned by Kairos; academic standards + scholarly research capability |
| **Vocational Mentor** | Supervisor, leader, or experienced colleague in student's ministry/work context; student-invited |
| **Personal Mentor** | Confidant, spiritual companion; rooted in faith; student-invited |

Mentors are **co-learners and sojourners**, not merely evaluators. Faculty mentors complete a formal **Faculty Mentor Pathway** (vetting + onboarding on Pathwright).

### Faculty affiliate network

The catalog lists **Kairos Affiliate Professors** — distributed global faculty supporting partner and legacy brands. Legacy partner faculty retain brand affiliation (e.g., "Evangelical Legacy Faculty," "Taylor Legacy Faculty").

## 13. Tuition, Financial Model & Aid Posture

### Subscription tiers (current public pricing)

| Monthly rate | Programs |
|--------------|----------|
| **$150/mo** | Spiritual direction certificates; learning experience audit |
| **$300/mo** | Graduate Certificate; BA/AA Christian Thought; MDiv; MA; MAL; DMin |
| **$400/mo** | MA Counseling; MA Marriage & Family Therapy; DPC |

### Financial model principles

- **No fee stack** — subscription covers tuition + most fees + DTL access
- **No tuition increases** — price locked for duration of program
- **Pace = total cost** — finish faster → pay less total; no per-credit billing for subscription programs
- **75–90% below national average** claimed
- **96% reduction in student borrowing** claimed since Kairos Project launch
- **Debt-free education** as explicit design goal

### Title IV exit

Kairos **voluntarily exited Title IV** federal aid (per current tuition page). Rationale: subscription model + debt avoidance. VA benefits pursuit ongoing. Catalog-era Stafford loan documentation may be stale.

### Other funding pathways

- Church/denomination support (explicitly encouraged)
- Denominational scholarships, foundation grants, employer tuition reimbursement
- Canada Student Grants; Alberta full-time grants
- Military/veterans benefits (in transition)
- Tuition Exchange (faculty/staff dependents)
- Jerusalem University College scholarships

### Cost of Attendance (federal aid era documentation)

When Title IV was active, COA examples used subscription rate × 6 months per semester + housing estimate. Books cost $0 in COA because DTL included in subscription.

## 21. Strategic Observations for Platform Builders

Patterns Kairos embodies that are relevant to Movemental's infrastructure thesis:

### 1. Platform > product

Kairos's deepest innovation is **governance and partnership architecture**, not Pathwright configuration. The Program Interface/SDK/Story Center metaphor is an explicit invitation to think about **stewardship of networks**, not content ownership.

### 2. Context as LMS

The student's vocation, church, and community **are** the learning environment. Technology (Pathwright) tracks outcomes; formation happens elsewhere. This inverts the LMS-centric model most EdTech assumes.

### 3. Subscription as strategy

Flat monthly pricing is not merely billing — it is a **theological statement** about debt, access, and kingdom collaboration. Exiting Title IV doubles down on the model.

### 4. Distributed assessment authority

Holistic rubrics + mentor teams + contextualized proficiency definitions = **assessment at the edge**. Accreditation is maintained through shared processes (MAR, Development Paths), not centralized grading.

### 5. Library as cooperative infrastructure

DTL co-ownership shows how seminaries can **share born-digital infrastructure** to collapse costs — analogous to shared content/research infrastructure in movemental leader networks.

### 6. Narrative documentation as moat

The Story Center (years of blog + white papers) is both **doctrine** and **onboarding**. New partners don't just get software — they get a documented philosophy of education.

### 7. Continuous curriculum migration

Kairos 7.0 → 10.0 → unified 2025 updates demonstrates **living curriculum** as operational norm. Pathwright path versioning enables non-destructive migration — a pattern worth studying for any competency-based platform.

### 8. Fragmentation risks Kairos solves

| Problem in traditional seminary | Kairos response |
|--------------------------------|-----------------|
| Residential bottleneck | Context-local learning |
| Credit-hour seat time | Outcome proficiency |
| Siloed institutions | Legacy + contextualization network |
| Library costs | DTL cooperative |
| Fixed syllabus | Suggested inputs + mentor adaptation |
| Student debt | Subscription + Title IV exit |
| One-size-fits-all assessment | Contextualized standards of excellence |
