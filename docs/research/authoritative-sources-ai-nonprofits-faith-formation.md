# Authoritative sources: AI, nonprofits, churches, and human formation

This document is a **return-to** list for grounding articles, talks, and product education in **credible, citable evidence** about how artificial intelligence intersects with civil-society organizations (including churches), donors, staff, volunteers, and the people they serve—especially youth and families in the United States.

It is scoped for the **Movemental** organizational site context: mission-driven institutions that care about **trust, stewardship, formation, and long-horizon outcomes**, not only efficiency.

## How to use this list

- **Start with the tier** that matches your claim: opinion → start with Pew/Gallup/Ipsos; macro labor/costs → BLS/OECD/Stanford AI Index; governance → NIST + your jurisdiction’s regulator; nonprofit operations → NTEN/TechSoup/Candid-class sources; scholarship → Semantic Scholar/OpenAlex/PMC.
- **Prefer primary documents** (the report PDF, the agency page, the dataset) over secondary blog summaries.
- **Date-stamp everything**: AI moves quickly; cite the **report year**, **field dates**, and **revision** where applicable.
- **Distinguish evidence from advocacy**: trade associations, think tanks, and campaigns can be useful for *arguments and framing* but should not be presented as neutral statistics without corroboration.
- **Use the router first for drafts**: [Movemental content angles (writer’s router)](#movemental-content-router) maps common questions to sections and lists typical mis-use patterns.

## Source-quality rubric (quick)

| Signal | Strong | Weak |
| --- | --- | --- |
| Method | Published methodology, sample frame, limitations | “Survey says” with no methods |
| Independence | Nonprofit research institute, government statistical agency, peer review | Vendor whitepaper as sole source for market size |
| Fit | Sector-specific (nonprofit) + general AI trends triangulated | Single viral headline |
| Humility | Confidence intervals, replication, dissenting studies | Certainty language on contested social effects |

## Movemental content angles (writer’s router)

This section does **not** add new authorities. It maps **recurring questions** from nonprofits, churches, and movement leaders to the **numbered source sections** below, with **misuse warnings** so drafts stay honest under Movemental’s mix of **trust, stewardship, formation, and outcomes**.

### Evidence vs. formation language (use both labels)

| Layer | What it can prove | Where it usually lives here |
| --- | --- | --- |
| **Empirical** | Prevalence, attitudes, reported behavior, institutional context, economics (with methods limits) | Sections 1–8, 11–12 |
| **Ethical / pastoral / theological** | Obligations, virtues, wise response, congregational discernment | Section 9 (plus your tradition’s primary texts and credentialed interpreters—outside this doc) |
| **Debate / advocacy** | Competing values and policy stakes | Section 10 (always label incentives) |

In public pieces, **do not** present the second layer as if it were representative survey data—or vice versa.

| “What does AI change about learning and formation?” | Sections **1**, **9** | [ERIC](https://eric.ed.gov/), [WWC](https://ies.ed.gov/ncee/wwc/), [Semantic Scholar](https://www.semanticscholar.org/) | “Science says” from **preprints** or **EdTech marketing** |
| “We serve EU partners / donors—what applies?” | Section **3** | [EDPB](https://edpb.europa.eu/edpb_en), [EU AI Act overview](https://digital-strategy.ec.europa.eu/en/policies/regulatory-framework-ai) | US-only blog posts presented as **global compliance** |
| “Map the debate without pretending neutrality” | Section **10** | Pick **two** opposing frames + one **rights/governance** anchor (e.g., [EFF](https://www.eff.org/), [AI Now](https://ainowinstitute.org/), [ITIF](https://itif.org/)) | “Both sides” symmetry when **power and harms** are asymmetric—name what is at stake |

## 1. US youth, families, schools, and mental health (where AI meets formation)

These sources help answer questions about **what young people are actually doing with AI**, **how adults perceive risks**, and **how digital environments relate to wellbeing** (often adjacent to “AI” specifically).

| Source | Why it is authoritative | Entry points |
| --- | --- | --- |
| **Pew Research Center** | Gold-standard public opinion and behavioral surveys; frequent releases on teens, schools, and technology. | Topic hub: [Teens & technology](https://www.pewresearch.org/topics/teens-and-technology/). AI topic hub: [Artificial intelligence](https://www.pewresearch.org/topic/science/science-issues/artificial-intelligence/). Internet & technology: [Internet & technology](https://www.pewresearch.org/topic/internet-technology/). |
| **Common Sense Media** | Large-scale US surveys on media/tech use with youth and parent angles; useful for **household norms** and concerns. | Research landing: [Research](https://www.commonsensemedia.org/research). |
| **US Surgeon General / HHS** | Official federal health guidance; especially strong for **adolescent mental health and social media** (often the upstream frame for “screens, identity, isolation”). | Advisory on social media and youth mental health: [HHS press release index](https://www.hhs.gov/about/news/index.html) (search “Surgeon General advisory social media”). Surgeon General site: [SurgeonGeneral.gov](https://www.hhs.gov/surgeongeneral/index.html). |
| **National Center for Education Statistics (NCES)** | Official US K–12 and postsecondary statistics; helpful for **institutional context** (device access, connectivity) that shapes AI use in schools. | [NCES](https://nces.ed.gov/). |
| **CRITICAL CONSUMPTION NOTE** | Longitudinal causal claims (“AI causes X”) are often **not** established in pop coverage. Prefer sources that separate **association**, **mechanism hypotheses**, and **intervention evidence**. | When writing for churches/nonprofits, pair **survey facts** (what % do what) with **pastoral ethics** (how should we respond), and label each layer clearly. |

## 9. Human formation, learning science, and “what does AI change about growth?”

## Maintenance

- **Review cadence:** quarterly for AI governance + quarterly for Pew/AI Index releases; annually for standards/regulator pages.
- **Versioning:** when you cite a source in content, copy the **exact URL** and **retrieval date** into your draft’s reference list.
