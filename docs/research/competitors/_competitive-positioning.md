# Competitive Positioning — Strategic Framing

This document is for use in pitch material, investor decks, and customer conversations. It draws on the 17 competitor profiles in this directory. Read [_summary.md](_summary.md) first for the landscape overview; this document is the *language* to use when the competitive question comes up.

---

## Part 1 — "Who are your competitors?" (for investor conversations)

Three to four paragraphs, written as if spoken.

> Our category has exactly one venture-backed direct competitor: Gloo, which IPO'd in November 2025 at roughly $873 million, with former Intel CEO Pat Gelsinger as Executive Chairman. Gloo is a platform-and-marketplace play — they sell AI infrastructure and developer tools to the faith ecosystem, sponsor the AI & the Church Hackathon, and published the Flourishing AI Christian Benchmark, which is a real category-defining artifact. We respect what they've built. They are not what we're building. Movemental is a partner-and-services play with single-vendor delivery on all four stages of an AI adoption path — Safety Documentation at $2,500, Sandbox Discovery at $15,000, Skills Development at $15,000 plus $5,000 a year for the LMS, and Solutions Deployment from $30,000, with a Path Bundle at $50,000. Gloo's Solutions analog is delivered through a partner called Faith Assistant. We deliver Solutions in-house.

> The larger competitive surface is incumbents shipping AI features inside existing platforms. On the church-tech side, Pushpay (private-equity-owned by Sixth Street and BGH Capital after a 2023 take-private), Subsplash (acquired by Roper Technologies for $800 million in July 2025), Tithe.ly, and Planning Center have each shipped AI features in 2025. On the nonprofit-CRM side, Virtuous raised $100 million from Susquehanna Growth Equity in September 2024, hired Nathan Chappell as Chief AI Officer in 2025, and acquired three AI-and-fundraising companies — Momentum AI, VOMO, and RaiseDonors. Salesforce has rebranded Nonprofit Cloud as Agentforce Nonprofit. Blackbaud has Intelligence for Good. Bloomerang has AI Content Assistant and Journey Automation. None of these companies sell what we sell. They sell platforms with AI features. We sell a four-stage formation path with bespoke agent work at the top of the stack.

> At the institutional tier, Big-4 consulting firms are entering. Deloitte launched Dot Good in early 2026 — a nonprofit AI services suite at "discounted from Big-4" rates, which still means six-figure engagements minimum. Accenture has not yet matched but is likely to follow inside eighteen months. Big-4 firms compete with us only at the largest faith-aligned NGOs and denominational service arms, where 7-figure engagement scale is acceptable. They do not call on individual churches. We are happy to lose those deals.

> The structural moat is two-fold. First, theological specificity grounded in Alan Hirsch's missiology — *The Forgotten Ways*, APEST, the dual-intelligence framework. No competitor including Gloo has authored content depth at this level. "Values-aligned" is broad. Missiologically-grounded is specific. Second, distribution through Brad Brisco's NAMB Send Network position and Hirsch's global movement-leader network. These are warm-trust channels that capital cannot buy. Every other competitor in the landscape sells through inside sales, partner ecosystems, or Big-4 field sales. We sell through movement leaders.

## Part 2 — "Why us instead of them?" (for customer conversations)

Segmented by buyer type.

### For a **church** buyer

A senior pastor, executive pastor, or board chair at a 500-to-5,000-attendance church.

> Most of what's marketed as "AI for church" today is one of two things: a feature inside the software you already pay for, or a free book and content series from a consulting firm. Pushpay has three AI features now. Subsplash has Pulpit AI, which turns one sermon into twenty pieces of content. Tithe.ly has an AI assistant in beta. Planning Center has a cautious list-building AI. These features are real. They will save your communications team and your preaching team time. We are not asking you to replace any of them.

> What none of those tools deliver is the work *upstream* of using AI well. Before your team starts running sermons through Pulpit AI, somebody on staff needs to know what your church's stance on AI actually is — not as a privacy policy buried in a vendor's terms, but as a documented, board-approved frame for how AI fits inside your discipleship and formation. That is Safety Documentation, and it is $2,500. Before your senior leadership decides which agents to build, your staff needs to play with AI in a curated environment that doesn't include open-internet experimentation. That is Sandbox Discovery, and it is $15,000. Before your church writes any custom AI tooling, your staff capability needs to be built across departments — preaching, kids, ops, finance — not just the pastor doing ChatGPT prompts. That is Skills Development, $15,000 for a cohort and $5,000 a year for the LMS. And when your church is ready to build the agents specific to your missiology — disciple-making, neighborhood engagement, equipping the saints — that's Solutions Deployment, starting at $30,000. The Path Bundle is $50,000 for all four stages.

> The reason this matters is that we are theologically specific in a way the platform vendors structurally cannot be. Alan Hirsch and Brad Brisco's work in missiology and apostolic movement is the substrate. We are not a "values-aligned" wrapper around generic AI. We are formation work that happens to be delivered through AI infrastructure.

### For a **nonprofit** buyer

A development director, executive director, or board member at a faith-aligned nonprofit (sub-$10M annual revenue).

> The nonprofit AI conversation in 2025 has been dominated by Virtuous, Bloomerang, and Salesforce Nonprofit Cloud, all of which now ship AI features. Virtuous + Momentum AI is a real intelligent agent for major-gift officers. Bloomerang's AI Content Assistant writes donor emails. Salesforce's Fundraising Gift Proposals generate personalized donor proposals. These tools work. Use them.

> What they cannot do is the work outside the CRM. They cannot write your nonprofit's AI policy as a board-ready, donor-defensible artifact. They cannot run a paid staff cohort that builds AI capability across program, development, and operations. They cannot build a bespoke agent tuned to your organization's mission framework — they can only configure a productized agent inside their CRM.

> Movemental does the work above the CRM. The reason we are not a CRM is intentional: we don't want to compete with the platform you've already chosen. We want to be the partner that takes your platform's AI capability and forms your team into an organization that can use it well. The four stages — Safety, Sandbox, Skills, Solutions — are the path. The Path Bundle is $50,000. For a faith-aligned nonprofit that's serious about formation, that price is a fraction of what a Deloitte Dot Good engagement would cost, with theological grounding Deloitte structurally cannot deliver.

### For an **institution** buyer

A denomination executive, seminary president, or large faith-based-NGO leader.

> At the institutional tier, the conversation is different. Salesforce Nonprofit Cloud and Blackbaud are the platforms of record. Deloitte's Dot Good and Accenture's nonprofit practice are starting to sell AI strategy engagements. Movemental is not a platform and not a Big-4 firm. We are a faith-aligned, missionally-framed services partner that integrates with whatever platform you've already chosen.

> The differentiators against Deloitte and Accenture are price, speed, and theological framing. Our $50,000 Path Bundle delivers more than a Big-4 discovery phase costs. We start in weeks, not quarters. We are missiologically grounded, not generic-mission. The differentiator against Salesforce and Blackbaud is that we don't compete with their platforms — we live above them. Solutions Deployment integrates with Raiser's Edge NXT or Agentforce Nonprofit via API where useful; we don't ask you to replace either.

> The structural reason we win institutional engagements is that we deliver all four stages in-house, on a single contract, with single-vendor accountability. Every other institutional option requires you to assemble a vendor stack — platform from one company, services from another, training from a third. We are end-to-end on a path designed for missional formation, not for generic operational efficiency.

## Part 3 — Specific positioning against the top 3 most-threatening competitors

### Against **Gloo** ([profile](gloo.md))

Gloo is the only public, venture-backed, faith-aligned AI competitor. Pat Gelsinger is their tech head. They have ~$873M valuation, a real benchmark (FAI-C), and a marketplace ecosystem.

**Position:** "Gloo is the platform layer; Movemental is the formation partnership. They're complementary, not competitive. The way to think about Gloo is the same way to think about AWS — it's infrastructure, and we build on top of it. Where Gloo sells API metering and developer tools, we sell a four-stage path that includes the missiologically-grounded formation work no platform can deliver. If your team is using Gloo for an AI chatbot, that's fine. If your team needs Safety Documentation, Sandbox Discovery, Skills Development, and bespoke Solutions Deployment with theological tuning, we are the partner — not Gloo, not Faith Assistant (their Solutions partner), and not anyone else."

**What to concede:** Gloo's FAI-C Benchmark is a legitimate category-defining artifact. Pat Gelsinger's involvement is credibility we can't match. The marketplace play, if it works, will be the dominant infrastructure. Don't pretend otherwise.

**What to own:** Theological *specificity* (Hirsch's missiology, not just "Christian worldview"). Single-vendor four-stage delivery. Movement-leader distribution. Named refusals.

### Against **Virtuous** ([profile](virtuous.md))

Virtuous is the most credible AI-for-nonprofits CRM player. $141M raised, Momentum AI acquisition, Nathan Chappell as CAIO, 10K+ customers.

**Position:** "Virtuous is a productized fundraising CRM with AI for content and major-gift workflow. Movemental is the formation partnership above the CRM, with theological grounding and a Solutions tier that productized SaaS economics structurally cannot include. If your nonprofit has chosen Virtuous as its CRM, that's a good choice — Momentum AI is a real productivity tool for your MGO team. Movemental does the work outside the CRM: AI policy as a board artifact, staff cohort across program and operations, and bespoke agents tuned to your mission framework that integrate with Virtuous via API."

**What to concede:** Virtuous + Momentum AI is the best in-CRM AI for nonprofit fundraising today. Don't pretend we have a better CRM. We don't have a CRM at all.

**What to own:** Theological grounding, Solutions tier with bespoke build, single-vendor delivery on the formation path, Path Bundle pricing reaching mid-market and small-institutions Virtuous's enterprise-CRM economics underprice.

### Against **Subsplash** under Roper Technologies ([profile](subsplash.md))

Subsplash is the second-largest US church-tech platform with 20K+ churches, Pulpit AI in market, and Roper Technologies as parent (acquired July 2025 for $800M). Roper's M&A playbook is the year-5 risk.

**Position:** "Subsplash sells engagement infrastructure with Pulpit AI as a sermon-to-content feature. Movemental sells the missional-formation layer above engagement infrastructure. If your church is on Subsplash, keep using it — Pulpit AI is real, and the rest of the Subsplash bundle does what Subsplash does. Movemental does the four-stage path that Subsplash's product roadmap will not include, no matter what Roper acquires next: theological policy work, staff-formation cohorts, and bespoke agents tuned to your missiology."

**What to concede:** Subsplash's bundled engagement platform is sticky. Pulpit AI works. Roper's capital base means Subsplash will keep shipping AI features.

**What to own:** Theological depth (Pulpit AI is generic; Movemental is missiologically grounded). Solutions tier (Subsplash sells productized SaaS; we deliver bespoke agent work). Movement-leader distribution. Named refusals — Subsplash positions AI ubiquitously; Movemental can articulate what we explicitly will not do.
