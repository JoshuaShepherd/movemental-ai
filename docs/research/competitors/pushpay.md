# Pushpay

## 1. Current state

- **Headquarters**: Redmond, Washington, USA (origin: Auckland, New Zealand) [^1][^2]
- **Founded**: 2011 [^1]
- **Leadership**: Kenny Wyatt (CEO, appointed March 2025; previously CRO at Conga and Salesforce); Molly Matthews transitioned to Senior Advisor [^3]
- **Employees**: Estimated 500–1,000 across ~29 locations (basis: ZoomInfo company profile, not officially published) [^2]
- **Funding history**: IPO'd on ASX (PPH) October 2016; taken private May 2023 by Sixth Street Partners + BGH Capital consortium; previously listed on NZX [^4][^5]
- **Current valuation / market cap**: Estimated NZ$1.53B (~US$960M) enterprise value at 2023 take-private; not currently disclosed (private-equity owned) [^4][^6]
- **Public/private status**: Private; PE-owned (Sixth Street + BGH Capital)

Pushpay is the dominant US church-giving + ChMS platform by transaction volume. It serves more than 14,000 church customers — predominantly evangelical Protestant churches plus its Catholic-specific ParishStaq line (5.6M+ sacramental records imported). After being taken private by US/Australian PE in 2023, Pushpay has accelerated its consolidation play (Resi livestream, Church Community Builder ChMS) and in 2025 launched its first wave of generative-AI features under a new CEO. [^1][^7][^8]

## 2. Category

**chms** — Pushpay is the prototypical incumbent in the ChMS / giving-platform bucket. It is **not in Movemental's category today** (it sells transactional infrastructure, not AI-formation services), but its 2025 AI roadmap and PE-backed consolidation thesis put it on a convergence path within 24–36 months.

GTM motion: **Inside sales + denominational/network deals + multi-year contracts (1/2/3-year terms)**. Pricing is opaque; published comparison points place Pushpay as the most expensive option in the category vs. Tithe.ly ($119/mo) and Breeze ($72/mo). [^9]

## 4. Overlap with Movemental's four stages

| Movemental stage | Competitor offering | Overlap level |
|------------------|---------------------|---------------|
| Safety Documentation ($2,500) | Has published "intentional approach to AI" stance for marketing; no policy/governance product for customers [^10] | adjacent |

| Skills Development ($15k cohort + $5k/yr LMS) | No cohort or staff-formation offering | none |
| Solutions Deployment (from $30,000) | Productized AI features inside existing platform; no bespoke build | adjacent |

Today, Pushpay's AI work is **feature-level retrofit inside an existing ChMS+giving platform**. Movemental's four stages are **a category Pushpay does not currently address**: posture/governance (Safety), guided experimentation (Sandbox), staff capability (Skills), and bespoke agent build (Solutions). The realistic convergence path is Pushpay extending its 2025 AI features into a "Pushpay AI for Ministry" service tier — possible but not announced as of late 2025. [^7][^10][^11]

## 5. Structural advantages and disadvantages versus Movemental

**They have that Movemental does not:**
- 14,000+ existing church customer relationships and the giving rails inside them [^8]
- ~$1B+ in PE backing for product investment and acquisitions
- Hardware + software bundling (Resi decoders) creating switching cost
- Catholic market presence via ParishStaq that no AI startup has

**Movemental has that they do not:**
- AI-native architecture (multi-tenant agent layer) — Pushpay's AI is bolt-on inside a payments/CRM stack
- Theological depth and named missiological frame (Hirsch's work) — Pushpay's AI marketing is generic ministry-productivity language
- Warm-network distribution through Hirsch + Brisco (NAMB) — Pushpay sells through inside-sales lead gen, not movement networks
- Named refusals — Pushpay positions AI as ubiquitous productivity, with no public refusals shaping the work
- Formation lens — Pushpay's stated goal is operational efficiency, not staff capability building

| Year 2 (2027) | high | If Pushpay packages its 2025 AI features into a paid services tier (likely under Wyatt's commercial-leadership background), they could occupy the Safety + Solutions slots inside their installed base before Movemental reaches them. |
| Year 5 (2030) | high | PE-backed roll-up logic favors Pushpay buying or building into agent-services and formation-cohort categories. They will not build theological depth, but they will compete on convenience and bundling. |

## 7. Recommended Movemental positioning response

**One-sentence differentiator**: *Pushpay sells the rails for transactions and people-data; Movemental builds the missional-formation layer that sits above any rails — including theirs — with theological depth and named refusals Pushpay's roadmap will never produce.* The honest concession is that Pushpay owns the giving + ChMS infrastructure for most evangelical churches above 1,000 attendance, and their AI features will deliver real productivity inside that infrastructure. Concede that. The differentiation is not "we replace Pushpay" — it's "we work above the rails Pushpay is good at."

In a sales conversation where the prospect already runs Pushpay: do not pitch displacement. Pitch **stages 1 and 2 (Safety + Sandbox) as a six-month engagement that complements Pushpay** — Movemental writes the AI policy Pushpay's platform requires the customer to have, and runs the staff-formation work Pushpay's bundled AI features cannot do. Position Solutions Deployment as the moat: bespoke agent work tuned to the church's missional theology, drawing on Pushpay data via API but not constrained by Pushpay's product roadmap.
