Biographical Overview

A comprehensive look at the life, work, and enduring legacy of Dr. Maya Angelou.

Early Life and Influences

Born Marguerite Annie Johnson on April 4, 1928, in St. Louis, Missouri, Maya Angelou's early years were marked by significant challenges and pivotal experiences that would deeply inform her later work. After her parents' separation, she and her older brother Bailey were sent to live with their paternal grandmother in Stamps, Arkansas. It was in Stamps that she experienced the brutal reality of racial discrimination, but also the enduring strength of the African American community.

"I know why the caged bird sings, ah me, / When his wing is bruised and his bosom sore..." — Paul Laurence Dunbar, a significant early influence.

Following a traumatic event at the age of seven, Angelou became mute for nearly five years. During this period of silence, she developed an extraordinary memory, a deep love for literature, and an acute ability to listen and observe.

Career and Activism

Angelou's career was incredibly diverse, spanning dance, theater, journalism, and literature. Her activism was deeply intertwined with her artistic pursuits.

  • Civil Rights Movement: She served as the northern coordinator for Dr. Martin Luther King Jr.'s Southern Christian Leadership Conference (SCLC).
  • International Work: Spent time living in Egypt and Ghana, working as a journalist and educator, and connecting with other expatriate civil rights leaders like Malcolm X.
  • Literary Breakthrough: The publication of I Know Why the Caged Bird Sings in 1969 brought her international acclaim and revolutionized the autobiographical form for Black women.

Legacy

Dr. Angelou's impact extends far beyond her published works. She was a respected academic, receiving over 50 honorary degrees. In 1993, she recited her poem "On the Pulse of Morning" at the inauguration of President Bill Clinton, becoming the first poet to make an inaugural recitation since Robert Frost.
