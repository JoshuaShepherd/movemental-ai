Chapter 1

The Crisis of Imagination

The Western church finds itself at an inflection point. After centuries of cultural dominance, the institutional structures and theological assumptions that once seemed permanent are rapidly eroding. Yet the crisis we face is not primarily organizational or strategic. It is, at its deepest level, a crisis of imagination.

We have lost the ability to see. Not because the world has become less revelatory, but because we have adopted frameworks of perception that flatten reality into something manageable, predictable, and ultimately lifeless. The Enlightenment gave us extraordinary tools for analyzing the material world, but it also bequeathed us a disenchanted cosmos -- a universe stripped of mystery, presence, and meaning beyond the measurable.

This disenchantment has seeped into the church more deeply than most of us realize. We speak of God's presence but organize our communities as if efficiency were the highest virtue. We confess the incarnation but practice a faith that is largely cerebral, disembodied, and disconnected from the rhythms of everyday life. We affirm the Holy Spirit's power but rely almost exclusively on human strategies and techniques.

The Mechanistic Trap

Consider how we typically approach church growth and renewal. We analyze demographics, implement programs, hire consultants, and develop strategic plans. None of these activities are inherently wrong, but they reveal an underlying assumption: that the church is fundamentally a machine to be optimized rather than a living organism to be cultivated.

This mechanistic thinking pervades our approach to discipleship as well. We create curricula, design pathways, and measure outcomes as if spiritual formation were a manufacturing process with predictable inputs and outputs. We have, in Charles Taylor's memorable phrase, constructed an "immanent frame" -- a way of seeing that acknowledges only what can be measured, managed, and controlled.

The church was never meant to be an institution that dispenses religious goods and services. It was meant to be a movement -- a living, breathing, expanding expression of the kingdom of God that transforms everything it touches.

The first step toward reframation is recognizing the frames we are already wearing. Like prescription lenses we have worn so long we have forgotten they are there, the Enlightenment's interpretive frameworks have become invisible to us. They shape how we read Scripture, understand community, practice leadership, and engage the world around us.

Recovering the Prophetic Imagination

Walter Brueggemann wrote powerfully about the "prophetic imagination" -- the capacity to envision an alternative reality that challenges the dominant consciousness. This is precisely what the church needs today. Not better programs or more sophisticated strategies, but a fundamentally different way of seeing.

The Hebrew prophets were not primarily fortune-tellers or social critics. They were people who saw reality differently because they were attuned to a deeper dimension of existence. They could perceive God's movement in history, name the idolatries of their culture, and articulate a vision of human flourishing that transcended the status quo.

Jesus himself was the ultimate reframer. His parables consistently subverted conventional wisdom and opened new vistas of understanding. The kingdom of God, he taught, is not a distant reality to be awaited but a present reality to be entered. It grows like yeast in dough, like a seed in soil -- organically, subversively, and often invisibly.

This is the kind of imagination we must recover. An imagination that sees the world as charged with the grandeur of God. An imagination that refuses to accept the church's current malaise as normal or inevitable. An imagination that dares to believe that the same Spirit who animated the early church is available to us today, if only we will learn to see and respond.

The Invitation

Reframation is an invitation to put on new lenses. It is a call to examine the assumptions and frameworks that have shaped our understanding of church, mission, discipleship, and even reality itself. It is an invitation to recover a sacramental worldview that sees God's presence and activity woven into the fabric of everyday existence.

This journey will require courage, because new ways of seeing inevitably lead to new ways of acting. Once we begin to perceive the church as a movement rather than an institution, our priorities change. Once we recognize discipleship as an organic, relational process rather than a programmatic one, our methods shift. Once we understand mission as participation in God's ongoing creative and redemptive work rather than a task we perform for God, everything changes.

The crisis of imagination is real, but it is not the final word. Throughout its history, the church has repeatedly demonstrated a remarkable capacity for renewal. In every era of decline, there have been communities and leaders who recovered the prophetic imagination and catalyzed movements of extraordinary vitality and impact. The early Celtic missionaries, the Franciscan movement, the Anabaptists, the Wesleyan revival, the underground church in China -- each represents a recovery of imaginative, embodied, missional faith.

We stand in that same tradition. And the same God who empowered those movements is inviting us to participate in the next chapter of the story.
