One of the reasons that knowledge is in a state of useless overproduction is due to its fragmentation—spoken in a thousand competitive voices, where insignificant fragments are magnified, and major insights are neglected.

The idea of "soft eyes"—a way of seeing often attributed to Native American tracking practices—calls us to defer judgment and remain open.

Whether in criminal investigation, medicine, mechanics, economics, or the arts, soft eyes are essential to discovery and innovation.

Hard eyes, conversely, represent unconscious prejudice or bias, arriving at conclusions without truly seeing.

While most of us are not detectives, we all require soft eyes at critical junctures: in research, leadership, creativity, and problem-solving.

Hard eyes—the unwillingness to see deeper or accurately diagnose—can be disastrous.

The contrast between soft and hard eyes is analogous to the difference between synthetic (systems) and analytic thinking.

Seeing the Trees: Analytic Thinking

Analytic thinking seeks to explain complex systems by reducing them to constituent parts.

The key is "reducing": to scrutinize something, we break it apart and simplify ideas into formulas that can be commonly shared.

Analytic thinking excels in stable contexts and when conventional formulas suffice; the stability of the whole allows for deep examination of parts.

However, during times of disruptive change, reductionism—over-reliance on details and routinized habits—can blind us to holistic understanding.

We risk misinterpreting the partial when we lose sight of the whole, akin to the proverbial blind men describing an elephant by its parts.

Mark the word "reductionism," as it is a central theme; contemporary theology and the church have inherited many such reductions that block our capacity to understand the whole and respond to new challenges.

Alvin Toffler puts it succinctly:

"Lacking a systematic framework for understanding the clash of forces in today's world, we are like a ship's crew, trapped in a storm and trying to navigate between dangerous reefs without compass or chart.

In a culture of warring specialisms, drowned in fragmented data and fine-toothed analysis, synthesis is not merely useful—it is crucial."

Seeing the Forest: Synthetic (Symphonic) Thinking

Unlike analytic thinking, synthesis explains reality ecologically, understanding the whole is greater than the sum of its parts.

Social psychologist Daniel Pink speaks of synthetic thinking as "symphony"—the blending of disparate elements into a new, imaginative whole.

Symphony requires imagination, holism, play, and divergent thinking.

True renewal—spiritual, social, or organizational—involves returning to the originating whole and rediscovering new meaning.

The Symphonic Idea of a Bicycle

An analysis of bicycle parts alone does not equate to understanding a bicycle.

The art of symphony is vital when grappling with big themes in theology or scripture.

Hans Urs von Balthasar notes that concepts gain meaning only through their relation to the whole—isolating any aspect causes the total form to disappear.

True meaning arises in the "art of total vision".

For example, eschatology is incomprehensible without reference to Christ's life and suffering; the Trinity must be understood as one whole, not isolated aspects.

Heresy often results from reductionism—disjoining parts to analyze them out of context, which can never recreate the whole.

An architecture of wholeness—symphony—is necessary for context and meaning.

The church needs symphony, not reductionist heresies, to renew its paradigm and invigorate its mission. 5Q, as introduced here, is a recombination of the apostolic, prophetic, evangelistic, shepherding, and teaching (APEST) capacities from Ephesians 4—a way to re-experience symphony in ministry.

Christian leaders will need soft eyes—an openness to new perspective—to navigate increasing complexities.

The craving for easy answers, formulas, and stability has made our eyes hard, blinding us to deeper patterns and potentialities that God has built into life, the church, and the cosmos.

Soft eyes are required to see beyond overfamiliar routines.

Approach this book with soft eyes:

  • Lay aside conventional formulations of church and ministry.
  • Be a "detective of divinity"—pursue the truth with openness.
  • Avoid over-focusing on constituent parts; seek the overarching framework.
  • Look for the big idea, the symphonic threads.
  • Do not prejudge outcomes before assessing all evidence.

If you do this, you may discover ministry and church as never before—just as a stereogram reveals 3D shapes when viewed properly, new vision emerges.

Recalibrating the Church

Historically, the church has functioned with severely reduced categories: pastor (shepherd) and teacher, omitting apostolic, prophetic, and evangelistic ministries from its operational life.

The result has been a reduction of the church's fullness into static hierarchical models: bishop-priest-deacon (BPD), eldership, modern church growth paradigms modeled after business corporations, and so on.

These often fail to reactivate the dynamic ministry present in Jesus and the New Testament.

As Robert Pirsig notes:

"To speak of certain government and establishment institutions as 'the system' is to speak correctly...

But to tear down a factory or to revolt against a [particular expression of] government is to attack the effects rather than the causes...

The true system, the real system, is the prevailing construction of systematic thought itself, rationality itself..."

Applying new structures to patch a broken system does not resolve the underlying rationality that created the problem.

True renewal arises from engaging with the deeper logic at work.

The 5Q System: Definitions and Purpose

APEST Defined: Broadly, APEST is as follows:

  • Apostle / Apostolic: From Greek "sent one." The pioneering, missional function, focused on extending and maintaining the core DNA and organizational principles of Christianity as a movement.

  • Prophet / Prophetic: Guardians of covenant relationship with God, emphasizing loyalty, justice, holiness, and faithfulness.

  • Evangelist / Evangelistic: Proclaimers of the good news, storytellers and recruiters, catalysts for receptivity and growth.

  • Shepherd / Shepherding: Developers of healthy, nurturing community; builders of spiritual maturity and communal health.

  • Teacher / Teaching: Mediators and stewards of wisdom and understanding; responsible for integrating and passing on intellectual and spiritual treasure.

The fivefold typology offers a lexicon for understanding the Bible, the church, and disciples' purpose in the world.

The distinctly Jesus-shaped version, or "J-APEST," will also be referenced throughout.

**Implicit vs.

Explicit APEST:**

  • Functions: What I call "implicit APEST," the corporate purposes given to the whole Body.
  • Callings: "Explicit APEST," the individual vocations or ministry expressions derived from those corporate purposes.
