# To a Lead Pastor

I want to begin with something already happening inside your church, whether or not anyone decided it should.

Right now someone on your staff is using AI. They are drafting an email with it, summarizing a meeting, or writing the first version of a sermon outline that will go out under your name. A few are pasting things into these tools they should not be pasting: pastoral notes, member details, counseling summaries. Almost none are checking the result against a policy, because almost no church has written one. Nobody invited any of this. It simply arrived. It is here now.

And the people watching most closely are young adults. Their feelings about all of this split nearly down the middle, half hopeful and half wary. They are asking the machine the questions they used to ask a mentor. How do I make sense of my life. How do I actually disciple people. What is the church even for. They are waiting to see whether the church has anything true to say in this moment, or whether it will answer with fear, or with a shrug.

And the thing most at risk is the very thing you exist to protect. For a business, an AI mistake loses a client; there is another client. For a church, credibility is not an advantage. It is the product. When a member cannot tell whether you wrote it, voice-cloned it, or a volunteer drafted it, trust erodes quietly. Voice cloning and impersonation are no longer theoretical. Scammers harvest sermon footage and livestreams to clone pastors' voices and solicit emergency gifts. Your name no longer proves it is really you.

Underneath that exposure sits the same deeper wound every church carries. Your teaching lives in sermons no one can search. Your hard-won, practiced wisdom lives in the founders' heads, one bus accident away from being lost. You know your people in fragments and cannot see your own body whole. AI built on top of that does not pull the pieces together. By default it reflects the scatter back, faster, louder, wearing your name without carrying your person.

So let me tell you what we do, and why the order of it matters.

The work has a sequence. Safety comes first, because AI is already in the room. Before anything else, a church needs a short AI Charter your elders can sign: what you believe about this, what you will do, what you refuse to do, pastoral care boundaries, disclosure when AI was used, and what you do when something goes wrong. This is not consulting overhead. It is a small step against costs you are already paying without naming them. After Safety comes Sandbox, where a team tests AI against real ministry work and decides honestly what helps, what is risky, and what is off limits. Then Training, where a group inside the church builds the capacities this work demands: discernment, authorship, care. Then Tech, where AI is integrated into how the church runs, last, because handing powerful tools to people who cannot yet use them wisely is not discipleship but replacement. We order the rebuild so it does not destroy the thing it was meant to serve.

Here is the gift hidden in your particular weight. Do this work, and you do not only protect your congregation. You answer the generation asking the machine the questions they used to ask the church, not with a shrug, but with people who can lead through this carefully.

I will be plain about why this is possible now. We use the tools we sell on ourselves, first. We do not use AI to author anything under a human's name. We are not the platform; the people and their work are the platform, and they own it. Start with Safety. We can facilitate it with you, or you can complete it on your own with the free handbook.

I will end where this really comes from. A generation of young adults is deciding, from what they watch you do, whether the church has anything true to say in this moment. Begin where it begins. Decide, in writing, what you will and will not do, while you still have room to choose together.
