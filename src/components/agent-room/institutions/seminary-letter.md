# To a Seminary President

I want to begin with something already happening inside your seminary, whether or not anyone decided it should.

Right now a student is drafting a sermon with AI. Another is using it for exegesis, or writing the first version of a paper, or, the use your faculty most fear, treating it as a kind of pastoral conversation partner, asking the machine the questions they once brought to a mentor. One of your professors welcomes this and folds it into the assignment. The professor down the hall calls the same act plagiarism. There is no shared policy between them, because most seminaries have not written one, and so the student navigates by trial, learning that the rules of formation depend on which door they knocked on. Nobody invited any of this. It simply arrived, and it is here now.

You carry a weight in this moment that a church or a nonprofit does not. You operate on two timelines at once: your own institution's adoption of AI, and what you form in the next generation of leaders about it. Whatever position you take internally becomes, by example or by curriculum, the position that shapes the pastors, missionaries, and educators who will fill churches and boards for decades. A seminary's stance on AI influences every church its graduates lead. That is why your silence is not neutral. An absence of guidance is itself a template; it spreads through your whole network as the message that no one needs to think carefully about this. Your graduates will inherit your unspoken assumptions without ever getting to examine them.

And the thing most exposed in this moment is the very thing you exist to protect. For a marketing firm, an AI mistake loses a client; there is another client. For a seminary, credibility is not an advantage. It is the product. There is no second thing to fall back on. The authority of the diploma has always rested on a public that could tell a real institution from an imitation of one. We have crossed into an environment where AI simulates institutional voice trivially, where a young leader's search results mix your actual scholarship with synthetic approximations of it, and where a seminary that has not gathered its own material into a foundation with verifiable provenance becomes, to that searcher, indistinguishable from a well-made fake. The authority is still real. The means of verifying it are quietly disappearing.

Underneath that exposure sits a deeper problem, and once you see it you see it everywhere in an institution your age: fragmentation. Your 2011 curriculum committee specified one thing. The 2014 textbook specified something subtly different. The 2023 denominational statement, something different again. No one reconciled the three, because reconciliation would mean naming disagreements the faculty preferred to leave implicit, so the credential you grant means slightly different things depending on the year and the campus, and neither the holders nor the churches receiving them know it. Meanwhile your archives are technically preserved and practically illegible. No one can find the 1998 accreditation self-study; the former president's papers are in a box somewhere. The institution chartered to provide intellectual continuity can no longer reliably learn from itself. The hard truth about AI is that built on top of a fragmented foundation, it amplifies that dysfunction rather than reducing it. It does not heal scatter. It scales it.

So let me tell you what we do, and why the order of it matters.

The work has a sequence, and Safety comes first, because AI is already in the room. Before anything else, an institution needs a short, ratified AI Charter: what you believe about this, what you will do, what you refuse to do, and what you do when something goes wrong. Five plain parts your board can actually sign. This is not consulting overhead. It is a small, urgent, concrete intervention against costs you are already paying without naming. After Safety comes Sandbox, where a team tests AI against real work and decides honestly what helps, what is risky, and what is off limits, because a Charter with nothing to test against is theater. Then Training, where a group inside the seminary forms the actual capacities this demands: discernment, authorship, stewardship. Then Technology, where AI is finally integrated into how the institution runs, last, because handing powerful tools to people who cannot yet steward them is not progress but replacement, and that is precisely how most AI efforts fail. We sequence the rebuild so it does not destroy the thing it was meant to serve. The order is not arbitrary. Skip a stage and the consequences compound, and for an institution like yours the compound interest is paid in the one currency you cannot afford to lose.

Here is the gift hidden in your particular weight. Do this work, and you do not only protect yourself. You become the template your graduates carry into every church and classroom they touch. The seminary that meets this moment with wisdom forms a generation of leaders who meet it with wisdom.

I will be plain about why this is possible now. The systems that used to cost an institution hundreds of thousands of dollars, and more to connect, can now be built for a fraction of that, in the exact window we are building inside. We use the tools we sell on ourselves, first. We do not use AI to author anything under a human's name. We are not the platform; the people and their work are the platform, and they own it. Start with Safety. We can facilitate it with you, or you can complete it on your own with the free handbook.

I will end where this really comes from. A generation of young adults is asking the machine the questions they used to ask the church, what is my life for, what does it mean to disciple anyone, what is the church even for. They are deciding, from what they watch us do, whether the church has anything true to say in this moment or whether it will answer with fear or a shrug. You form the people who will answer them. Begin where it begins. Decide, in writing, what you will and will not do, before the moment decides it for you.
