## 1. Why Safety is first

Most organizations are mirroring whatever happens to be in the room. A staff member tries a tool. The tool gets used in a constituent communication. Someone notices and asks a question that would have belonged in a policy if a policy existed. The policy gets written under pressure, after the fact, by whoever is in the meeting. The board hears about it later.

This is not a story about negligence. It is a story about a sequence that has been inverted across most of the nonprofit, faith, and institutional sectors at once. Adoption preceded governance. Use preceded discernment. Tools preceded conviction. Safety is the work of putting the sequence back in order, not by slowing AI use but by deciding, in advance, what the organization stands for in relation to it.

The data on this is unambiguous, and it has converged across at least six independent studies in the last twelve months.

In the nonprofit sector, the Virtuous and Fundraising.AI *2026 Nonprofit AI Adoption Report*, a benchmark survey of 346 organizations released February 16, 2026, found that 92 percent of nonprofits use AI in some capacity, but only 7 percent report major improvements for the whole organization. Eighty-one percent describe their use as individual and ad hoc, without shared workflows. Forty-seven percent have no AI governance policy at all.[^1]

In the faith sector, the Barna Group and Pushpay *State of Church Tech 2026* report, a survey of 1,306 U.S. church leaders fielded November and December 2025, released March 9, 2026, found that 60 percent of church leaders personally use AI at least once a month, but only 33 percent report their church is using AI in any part of ministry or operations. Sixty-four percent of leaders believe it is important for a church to have an AI use policy. Five percent of churches actually have one.[^2]

In the broader enterprise, the McKinsey *State of AI 2025* report (n=1,993, released November 2025) found that 88 percent of organizations now use AI in at least one business function, but only 6 percent qualify as AI high performers. Boston Consulting Group's *Widening AI Value Gap* (n=1,250 firms, September 2025) found 60 percent of companies generate no material value from AI investment; 5 percent are what BCG calls "future-built." MIT's NANDA Initiative *GenAI Divide* report (July 2025) documented that 95 percent of enterprise generative AI pilots fail to generate measurable financial returns.[^3]

The pattern is now visible across nonprofit, faith, and enterprise research at the same time. Adoption is broad. Capability gain is rare. The cohort that crosses from the first to the second sits between five and seven percent of organizations across every sector studied. McKinsey identifies workflow redesign, not tool selection, as the single strongest correlate. BCG's "future-built" firms are differentiated by an AI-first operating model and explicit governance. Virtuous identifies the same pattern in nonprofits: organizations with shared workflows and outcome measurement are the ones reporting major capability gains, while two-thirds remain stuck in reactive individual use.

The translation for mission-driven organizations is direct. The gap between adoption and capability is primarily an organizational, formation, and workflow problem rather than a tools problem. The question is not whether to use AI. The question is whether your organization can stand behind what it is already doing.

There is also an emerging fiduciary dimension that deserves attention. Forvis Mazars, in a February 13, 2026 brief titled *AI Governance for Nonprofit Boards*, frames AI governance as an expansion of the board's traditional fiduciary duties of Care, Loyalty, and Obedience.[^4] The argument is straightforward: if AI is touching constituent communication, fundraising appeals, financial decisions, or programmatic delivery, then the board's existing duty of care now extends to AI oversight whether the board has written it down or not. Boards that have not engaged this question are exposed to it regardless. Boards that name it can govern it.

The harm landscape deserves the same realism. The FBI's Internet Crime Complaint Center *2025 IC3 Annual Report*, published in 2026, documented 22,364 complaints with an AI-related descriptor and $893 million in adjusted losses tied specifically to AI-enabled scams.[^5] Voice cloning, layered into business email compromise schemes, has grown more than 400 percent year over year according to industry tracking sources cited in the FBI's reporting. The Vatican Dicastery for Communication has reported receiving "dozens" of deepfake reports per day involving Pope Leo XIV.[^6] Pastors in Alabama, Florida, Missouri, Nebraska, New York, and a Philippines megachurch issued public warnings during 2025 about AI impersonation scams targeting their congregations; the AI Incident Database catalogs the multi-state pattern as Incident 1355.[^7]

Safety is not the work of slowing AI down. It is the work of being able to say, when the question arrives, from a donor, from a journalist, from a member of your own staff, what your organization stands for, what data it protects, and what it refuses to do. Five percent of organizations can answer those questions today. The path into the cohort that can starts here.

## 2. The seven decisions

The Safety stage produces seven decisions. Six are written documents. The seventh is a single act of organizational communication. Together they take roughly two weeks to draft and ratify when an experienced facilitator is involved, somewhat longer when the organization works through them independently. They are not a complete risk-management program. They are the minimum that responsibly addresses the documented harms now arriving and the governance gaps the data has named.

Each decision is small enough to be remembered, concrete enough to be applied, and honest enough to be revised when reality teaches you something. None of them are templates to copy. They are documents your organization writes, in your own language, that pass through your own governance.

### 01 *AI Use Charter*

The principles and stance document. The Charter is the highest-level statement your organization makes about its relationship to AI. It names the values that will hold under pressure: what AI is for in your context, what it is not for, what convictions guide your choices, and how those convictions connect to the mission your organization already exists to serve.

The Charter is short by design. Two pages is enough. Longer than that and it stops being readable in a single sitting; shorter than that and it usually has not done the theological or ethical work the moment requires. Its purpose is to give your staff, your board, and the people you serve a single document they can point to when the question "what do we believe about this" comes up. It is the document that makes the rest of the decisions coherent rather than arbitrary.

A Charter that is doing its work names at least three things explicitly: the relational and mission commitments that AI use must serve rather than displace; the categories of work where the organization will not let efficiency be the deciding criterion; and the stance the organization holds toward both fear and uncritical enthusiasm. It is signed by the senior leadership and ratified by the board.

What is at stake without it: every other decision reads as arbitrary administrative compliance. Staff cannot tell what the rules mean because the rules are not connected to anything they recognize as the organization's identity. The first hard case produces an internal argument with no grounding to settle it.

### 02 *Acceptable Use Policy*

The operational rules for staff. The Acceptable Use Policy translates the Charter into specific guidance for the people who actually use AI in their work. It names the tools that are sanctioned, the tools that are prohibited, the categories of task where AI is welcomed, the categories where it is constrained, and the categories where it is forbidden. It is the document a new staff member reads in their first week and refers back to when they hit an edge case.

A useful AUP is concrete enough to be enforceable and short enough to be read. It distinguishes between drafting work, which can be appropriate territory for AI assistance with disclosure, and finalization work, where human judgment is the irreducible input. It addresses confidentiality directly: which categories of information may be shared with which tools, by whom, and under what conditions. It names the consequences for violation in the same plain language used elsewhere in the staff handbook.

What is at stake without it: shadow use. Staff use whatever is convenient because the organization has not named what is acceptable. By the time the question arrives, the practice is already entrenched, and the Policy has to be written against a fait accompli rather than as guidance.

### 03 *Data Handling Standards*

What data may touch which tools. Of the seven decisions, this is the one with the most operational weight, because it is the one that protects the people you serve. Donor records, member directories, client files, pastoral notes, financial details, employee data, none of these should leave your governed perimeter into a public AI tool without an explicit decision that says they may. Most organizations have not made that decision because no one has framed it as a decision.

The Data Handling Standard tiers your data, typically into something like public, internal, sensitive, and confidential, and pairs each tier with the categories of AI tool that may handle it. Enterprise tools with appropriate data processing agreements may handle different data than public consumer tools. Some categories of data may not be processed by any external AI tool at all. The Standard is operational, not theoretical: it gives staff a clear answer to the question "can I put this in this tool" before they have to guess.

A useful Standard is paired with a small set of pre-approved workflows that already comply with it, so staff have a way to do the work that does not require them to make a judgment call every time. It also names the procurement process: any new AI tool entering the organization is reviewed against the Standard before it is used with anything beyond public data.

What is at stake without it: a Blackbaud-style breach. The 2020 ransomware attack on the donor management platform exposed data at more than 13,000 organizations and produced a multistate settlement of more than $49.5 million plus a separate $6.75 million California settlement in 2025. The pattern of nonprofit cybersecurity exposure has not improved since then; the Identity Theft Resource Center documents a 50 percent increase in cyberattacks on nonprofits between 2020 and 2024.[^8] AI-tool data leakage is a new vector inside an old problem.

### 04 *Disclosure and Attribution Standard*

When AI involvement is named publicly. The Disclosure Standard is the smallest of the seven decisions and one of the most reputationally consequential. It defines, in advance, the conditions under which your organization tells the people you serve that AI was involved in producing what they are reading, hearing, or receiving. It is not a blanket disclaimer; it is a discrimination rule.

A useful Standard distinguishes between routine operational tasks where AI assistance is unremarkable, formatting, scheduling, summarizing internal documents, and personal communication where the absence of disclosure would be experienced as deception. A pastoral letter to a grieving family is the test case. A fundraising appeal in the personal voice of an executive director is another. The Lifeway *Pastors' Views on Artificial Intelligence* survey released in April 2026 found that 62 percent of pastors are concerned about non-disclosure of AI involvement; among Protestant churchgoers, 43 percent disagree with their pastor using AI for sermon preparation, with 24 percent disagreeing strongly.[^9] The trust gap is already present; the Standard prevents you from violating it without realizing.

The Standard also addresses attribution: when AI generates substantive content that your organization publishes, how is that named? Some organizations choose simple language ("AI-assisted under editorial review by [name]"). Others develop tiered disclosure tied to the level of AI involvement. The decision is less important than the discipline of having made the decision in advance.

What is at stake without it: a slow erosion of trust your organization will not see until it has already happened. Disclosure violations are usually discovered, not confessed. Once they are discovered, they are difficult to reverse.

### 05 *Pastoral, Programmatic, and Educational Care Boundaries*

Where AI must not go. This is the decision that protects the human covenant at the center of mission-driven work. It names the categories of work where the presence of a human being is the irreducible input, where substituting AI is not a question of efficiency but of category error. It is also the decision that requires the most theological and clinical judgment to draft, because the boundary is not always obvious in advance.

A useful Care Boundaries document names specific work, not categories alone. "Pastoral care" as a category will not survive a Tuesday afternoon when a chaplain is short-staffed and a chatbot is available. "Initial pastoral conversation following the death of a family member," named explicitly, will. The same logic applies to clinical counseling intake, crisis-line first contact, programmatic eligibility determinations, end-of-life conversation support, and disciplinary or corrective conversations with staff. Every organization has its own list. The work of drafting it is the work of asking what kinds of human presence your mission cannot abdicate.

The Care Boundaries document also names the secondary cases, the work where AI can prepare or support a human, but cannot substitute for one. A draft of a sympathy letter that a pastor edits and signs is different from a generated letter sent without review. The Standard sometimes specifies these workflows directly, particularly where the temptation toward substitution is strongest.

What is at stake without it: a slow displacement of human presence in exactly the places where the organization's reason to exist resides. The displacement happens without anyone deciding to do it, because the rules never said they could not.

### 06 *Voice Cloning and Impersonation Response Plan*

The protocol for the active criminal threat. Of the seven decisions, this one addresses harm that is already arriving. The FBI's 2025 IC3 Annual Report named voice cloning as a layered component of business email compromise schemes; senior officials, including current and former federal officials, are being impersonated in messaging and voice campaigns at industrial scale.[^5] The same pattern is appearing in mission-driven organizations: pastors in at least six U.S. states have been impersonated by AI voice cloning over the last twelve months.[^7]

A useful Response Plan answers four questions explicitly: how impersonation is reported by the constituent who encountered it; how the organization verifies whether the impersonation is real (including pre-arranged code-words for senior staff and standing protocols for fund-transfer requests); how the organization communicates publicly when an incident is confirmed; and how the organization works with law enforcement, including the IC3 reporting workflow at ic3.gov.

The Plan also names, in advance, the categories of AI-generated content the organization would treat as an emergency: a deepfake video impersonating a senior leader, a voice cloning attempt directed at staff or donors, a text or email purporting to come from an executive that requests immediate financial action. Naming these in advance means the organization is not improvising during an incident.

What is at stake without it: improvised response during an active fraud, slower public communication, larger financial loss, and a longer trust-recovery period after the incident. The IC3 numbers are not abstractions. They are the average case, multiplied across the sectors that have not yet built this discipline.

### 07 *Communication to the Constituent Base*

The public act. The seventh item is not a document. It is a single deliberate piece of public communication, a letter from the executive director, a paragraph in the next newsletter, a board statement on the website, that names what the organization has decided about AI and where the people you serve can read more. It is the act that makes the previous six decisions visible.

The Communication is short and substantive. It does not promise that the organization will never use AI; that promise is unkeepable, and constituents who have read the data know it. It does not claim a sophisticated AI strategy; that claim invites questions the organization is not yet equipped to answer. It says, in plain language, that the organization has done the work of governing AI in advance of deploying it, names the principles that guided the work, and points to the public-facing summary documents (typically the Charter and the Disclosure Standard) for those who want detail.

A useful Communication is signed by the senior leader and ratified by the board. It anchors the organization's position in a moment when most peer organizations have not anchored theirs, and it does so with a tone that is neither alarmed nor triumphalist.

What is at stake without it: the work of the previous six decisions remains internal, and the trust they exist to build never reaches the people they were drafted to protect.

## 3. The self-assessment

Ten questions a leadership team can answer together in thirty minutes. Each answer is yes, no, or partial. The pattern of answers tells you where to begin.

1. *Can your senior leadership name every AI tool currently in use across your staff?*

A strong answer is a list, written down, with the names of the staff using each tool and the categories of work each tool is touching. A weak answer is a description of what tools have been "approved" without verification of what is actually in use. The Virtuous data places 81 percent of nonprofits in the second category by default.

2. *Has your board or governing body ratified a written position on AI?*

A strong answer is a signed Charter or equivalent document of record. A weak answer is "we have talked about it." Forvis Mazars frames AI governance as a fiduciary expansion of the board's duties of Care, Loyalty, and Obedience; "we have talked about it" does not satisfy that expansion. Five percent of churches and an unmeasured but small minority of nonprofits have ratified positions.

3. *Have you identified the specific categories of work where AI must not be used in your organization?*

A strong answer is a Care Boundaries document or equivalent that names specific work, not abstract categories. A weak answer is a general statement of principle. The substitution risk is at the level of Tuesday afternoon decisions; the document has to operate at the same level.

4. *Do your communications with the people you serve disclose AI involvement when material?*

A strong answer is a written Disclosure Standard and a tracked compliance practice. A weak answer is "we generally try to." Forty-three percent of Protestant churchgoers disagree with their pastor using AI for sermon preparation; the absence of disclosure is the trust risk, not the use.

5. *Do you have a documented procedure for what happens when AI produces harmful, inaccurate, or inappropriate output?*

A strong answer is an Incident Response document with named owners, escalation paths, and a constituent-communication template. A weak answer is "we would handle it as it came up." Improvisation during an incident produces worse outcomes than preparation for the average incident.

6. *Have you named any specific applications of AI that your organization commits to refuse on principle?*

A strong answer is a written Named Refusals statement (see section 6) signed by senior leadership. A weak answer is an internal sense of unease that has not been put on paper. The refusals you can name are the convictions you can defend under pressure.

7. *Are your staff currently using AI tools individually, without shared workflows or shared evaluation?*

A strong answer is no, there are documented workflows, shared evaluation rituals, and a learning practice across the team. A weak answer is yes, with the acknowledgment that this is the default. Eighty-one percent of nonprofit AI use sits in the second category.

8. *Could a reasonable observer determine, from your published positions, what your organization stands for in its AI use?*

A strong answer is yes, the Charter, the Disclosure Standard, and the Communication to the Constituent Base are publicly accessible. A weak answer is no. The first time a journalist or a major donor asks the question is not the moment to start drafting.

9. *Is your data, donor records, member directories, client information, pastoral notes, adequately protected from third-party AI processing?*

A strong answer is a Data Handling Standard with tiered classifications and pre-approved workflows. A weak answer is reliance on individual staff discretion. The 2020 Blackbaud breach exposed data at more than 13,000 organizations; AI-tool data leakage is a new vector inside the same exposure pattern.

10. *If a senior leader were impersonated tomorrow by an AI deepfake or voice clone targeting your staff, donors, or constituents, would your team know what to do in the first hour?*

A strong answer is a Voice Cloning and Impersonation Response Plan with pre-arranged verification protocols and a constituent-communication template. A weak answer is no. The IC3 documented $893 million in AI-attributed losses in 2025 alone. The pattern is not theoretical.

*How to interpret the pattern.* Organizations who answer no to four or more of these questions should begin with Safety. Organizations who answer yes to most should consider whether their next move is Sandbox, bounded experimentation with shared evaluation. Organizations who answer yes to all are likely already in the five-to-seven percent high-performer cohort, and the question facing them is sustainability rather than entry.

## 4. Common mistakes

Five patterns of failure recur across the organizations we work with. Each one is preventable. Each one is endemic. Naming them is the first step in not repeating them.

*The unilateral staff member.* One person, often a senior one, makes AI decisions for the whole organization without authority or process. The decisions are usually reasonable in isolation but produce a precedent the organization has not consented to. The fix is governance, not personnel: until there is a Charter and a process, every staff member is making unilateral decisions by default.

*The technology-first trap.* The organization picks a tool, sometimes a vendor pitch, sometimes a peer recommendation, before deciding what they want it to do or what they want it not to do. The tool then dictates the policy by being the thing the policy has to accommodate. The fix is sequence: principles before tools, tools before deployment, deployment with review.

*The unauthorized chatbot.* A volunteer, intern, or junior staff member deploys public-facing AI, a website widget, a social media bot, a member-services automation, without senior sanction or governance review. The chatbot represents the organization to constituents who do not know it is unauthorized. The fix is procurement discipline: any AI tool that touches a constituent passes through review before it is deployed.

*The disclosure gap.* AI is used in constituent communication but not disclosed. The use is rarely discovered through formal channels; it is usually noticed by a careful reader who recognizes the voice. Once it is noticed, trust erodes silently and the organization rarely hears about it. The fix is the Disclosure Standard, applied early and consistently, before the first communication that should have been disclosed but was not.

*The fiduciary blind spot.* The board has not engaged AI governance because no one has framed it as a fiduciary question. Forvis Mazars now does, and so does the *Nonprofit Quarterly*'s October 2025 analysis on resisting the "AI efficiency trap."[^10] The fix is a single agenda item at the next board meeting, framed by the Charter and the Data Handling Standard, with a vote of record. The board does not need to become AI experts. It needs to do its existing job in a context that has changed.

If you have those internally, you do not need us. If you do not, this is the lowest-cost way we know to assemble them.

## 6. Named Refusals

The strategic and editorial heart of Safety. Of all the documents that come out of the seven decisions, the Named Refusals statement is the one that changes the conversation. It is the public commitment, signed by senior leadership and ratified by the board, that names specific applications of AI your organization will not pursue regardless of pressure, regardless of demonstration, regardless of efficiency. It is what the act of public refusal does that makes the rest of the decisions hold.

A Named Refusal is not a prediction. It is a covenant. The point is not whether you happen to think a particular use is unwise; the point is that you have decided in advance, in public, with the people who would be affected, that you will not pursue it. The decision is durable in the way private convictions are not. It can be cited. It can be defended. It can be referenced in the moment a vendor pitches the use anyway.

The list below is what we believe mission-driven organizations, churches, nonprofits, formative institutions, should refuse on principle. It is not exhaustive. Your organization's list will be specific to your context and may include refusals we have not named here. The shape of the list matters more than the items: each entry is a sentence, each sentence can be explained, each explanation names what is at stake.

### 01 *AI used to make eligibility determinations for services, programs, or membership.*

We refuse to use AI to determine who receives care, who is admitted, who is approved, who qualifies. The decisions that shape who is included and who is not in a community of trust are decisions that require human judgment, human accountability, and human face. Algorithmic eligibility determinations carry documented bias; they also carry a categorical mismatch between the mathematics of optimization and the moral seriousness of inclusion. We refuse the substitution.

What is at stake: the people we serve are not a distribution to be sorted. The decision to admit, to fund, to ordain, to enroll, to commission belongs to the human community making it.

### 02 *AI used to surveil the behavior, attendance, or giving patterns of the people we serve in ways that change service delivery.*

We refuse to use AI to construct behavioral surveillance of constituents that then differentiates how we treat them. Predictive donor modeling that quietly deprioritizes congregants of lower projected lifetime value; attendance pattern detection that flags families for "intervention"; engagement scoring that ranks the people in our care by their statistical responsiveness, we refuse the category. The mission is not behavior modification. The relationship is not a funnel.

What is at stake: a community of trust cannot be governed by the same logic that governs a marketing pipeline. The introduction of behavioral surveillance corrupts the relationship before it produces any measurable benefit.

### 03 *AI used to generate fundraising appeals or mission communication in the personal voice of staff or beneficiaries without disclosure.*

We refuse to write in someone's voice, with AI assistance, and present the writing as theirs. We refuse to compose appeals signed by a leader who did not write them and does not know they were composed. We refuse to use the testimony of a beneficiary, generated or recomposed by AI, in a way that would be experienced as deception by the beneficiary themselves. This refusal does not preclude AI assistance in drafting; it precludes presenting AI-assisted writing as personal authorship without disclosure.

What is at stake: the trust that makes giving meaningful is the trust that the leader's voice is the leader's voice. Once that trust is breached, even quietly, even effectively, the organization has compromised the asset that is most difficult to rebuild.

### 04 *AI used in pastoral, clinical, counseling, or formational relationships that require irreducible human presence.*

We refuse to substitute AI for the presence of a chaplain, pastor, counselor, social worker, mentor, spiritual director, or formator in the work that depends on the presence itself. AI may support preparation; it does not stand in for the human in the room. This refusal includes initial pastoral conversation following grief or trauma, crisis-line first contact, end-of-life care, premarital and marital counseling, and the formation of disciples or the equipping of leaders. Each organization will name its own list. The principle is consistent.

What is at stake: the work of pastoral and formative care is not a service to be delivered; it is a relationship to be inhabited. The absence of a person in that relationship is not an efficiency. It is the failure mode.

### 05 *AI deepfakes or impersonations of any kind.*

We refuse to generate or publish AI-imitated likenesses, voices, or speech of any person, historical figures, denominational leaders, organizational founders, current staff, deceased members of our community, or constituents we serve, for any purpose. The refusal includes uses that may seem clearly bounded or educational. The refusal also includes recreations of people no longer living, including those who cannot consent. The trust environment is fragile enough that the question of whether a particular impersonation is acceptable should not need to be litigated case by case.

What is at stake: every category-error in this domain trains the public to disbelieve what they see and hear. The faster the technical capacity to deceive grows, the more important the public refusal becomes.

### 06 *AI chatbots that present themselves as human, that impersonate specific staff members, or that operate without disclosure.*

We refuse to deploy a chatbot that does not identify itself as one. We refuse to give a chatbot the name, photograph, or persona of a specific staff member. We refuse to allow a chatbot to handle pastoral, clinical, or crisis-related interactions even with disclosure. Where chatbots are deployed for routine operational interactions, their nature is named in their first message and they hand off to a human when the interaction exits the routine.

What is at stake: the precondition for trust is the assumption that you are addressing who you think you are addressing. Once that assumption is violated by an organization a constituent had trusted, the constituent generalizes the violation.

### 07 *AI used to replace prayer, lament, scripture, or other irreducibly human spiritual practices.*

We refuse to substitute AI-generated content for the practices of prayer, communal lament, scripture reading, sacramental life, or spiritual direction. This refusal extends to AI-generated prayer lists used as prayer, AI-composed scripture readings used in liturgy, AI-generated lament used in pastoral response to suffering, and AI-generated spiritual counsel offered in place of formed and present spiritual leaders. AI may be a research aid in preparation. It is not a participant in the practices themselves.

What is at stake: the practices that form Christian life are practices of the human person before God in community. They are not content to be optimized. The refusal protects the practices and protects the people the practices form.

The act of publicly committing to specific refusals is itself a formative practice. The organization that names what it will not do is the organization that has decided what it stands for. The refusals you can write down are the convictions you can hold under pressure, and the moment of pressure is the only moment where the convictions matter at all.
