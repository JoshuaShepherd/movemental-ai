Core Story / Dallas Willard VIM (Vision–Intention–Means) · January 2026

Core Story / VIM

The anchor story Movemental embodies, and our story of change through Vision, Intention, and Means.

The Anchor Story (500 words)

For a long time, the people who were best at catalyzing real change—pastors and church planters, scholars and practitioners, leaders who thought in terms of movements rather than institutions—found themselves in a trap. They had the ideas, the frameworks, the hard-won credibility. They had audiences in rooms and relationships that trusted them. But the digital world that was supposed to amplify their voice didn’t belong to them. It belonged to platforms that could delete their accounts, tweak algorithms overnight, and keep most of the revenue their content generated. If they wanted to own their digital presence the way they owned their calling, they were told it would cost fifty or a hundred and fifty thousand dollars and take the better part of a year. So the very people advancing incarnational theology, multiplication, and kingdom-shaped leadership were left as digital sharecroppers: doing the work, while someone else owned the land and took the harvest.

That was the wrong story. The right story is one where movement leaders own the ground they stand on. Not as a luxury for the well-funded, but as the norm—platforms built for the cost of a used car, live in weeks, connected to each other so that credibility travels through relationship instead of through algorithms. In that story, the leader’s best work—sermons, courses, books, conversations—lives in one place they control. Their audience is theirs. The revenue that flows from their teaching flows to them and to their mission, not to a landlord. And because those platforms are linked in a shared network, the ideas don’t stay siloed. One leader’s readers discover another’s. Collaboration isn’t a sidebar; it’s how the system is built. Technology serves formation and multiplication instead of extraction. AI amplifies the human voice instead of replacing it. Success is shared: the platform builder only wins when the leader wins.

That’s the story Movemental exists to make real. We’re not building another app that optimizes for engagement. We’re building the infrastructure so that the people who are already forming leaders, multiplying movements, and teaching the practices that change communities can own their digital presence and connect it to a scenius—a collective genius—that makes each voice more findable and more credible. The future we’re proposing isn’t one where everyone gets a website. It’s one where the leaders the world most needs have platforms that reflect their values, keep their revenue, and plug into a network that multiplies impact. When that happens, movements multiply. When movements multiply, the world transforms. This is the story we’re telling with our hands: structure before copy, meaning before UI, ownership before optimization, and people before platforms.

Vision — If we do this, here’s what will change / happen

What we see when we look ahead: A world in which movement leaders are no longer defined by the platforms they rent. They own their digital presence. Their audiences are theirs—email, content, relationships—and the economic value their teaching creates flows to them and to their mission instead of to intermediaries that keep 70–90% of revenue. That shift alone changes the equation: leaders can fund full-time movement work from their own platforms instead of depending on gatekeepers or side gigs.

What will have changed in the landscape: The default for serious movement leadership will be owned infrastructure, not borrowed. A hundred, then a thousand, leaders will have platforms that are professionally built, AI-supported, and connected to each other. Credibility will travel through that network—through who links to whom, who collaborates, who endorses—so that in an age of synthetic content, real expertise is discoverable and verifiable. The “middle” that once carried publication and wisdom (publishers, trusted channels) will be rebuilt in a new form: not one gatekeeper, but a linked ecology of leaders who own their ground and point to each other.

What will be true for leaders: They will spend their time on movement building, not algorithm gaming. Their best work will be findable. The people they’re called to form will have access to the content that forms them. Collaboration will be normal—shared projects, cross-promotion, scenius—because the system rewards connection rather than hoarding. And the digital legacy of their teaching will outlast any single social platform.

What will be true for movements: Ideas will spread through owned channels and trusted networks. Economic sustainability will underwrite multiplication. The infrastructure for global movement leadership will exist in the digital age—not as a monopoly, but as a shared, values-aligned layer that serves mission over profit.

Intention — Here’s what it will take

The decision we’re making: We intend to build and sustain the infrastructure so that movement leaders can own their platforms and connect in a shared network. That’s not a side project. It’s the primary thing. We’re saying yes to that and no to building just another SaaS that optimizes for our own growth at the expense of our users’ ownership or to scaling in a way that turns leaders into inventory.

What we’re willing to pay: We’re willing to price so that ownership is accessible—$1,000 to build, 10% of what the leader earns—instead of charging market rate upfront and walking away. Our success is tied to their success. We’re willing to cap the collective at 1,000 so we build infrastructure, not infinite scale. We’re willing to hold non-negotiables—formation over growth, humans over hacks, scenius over genius, technology properly ordered—even when they cost us short-term metrics or easy growth.

What we’re refusing: We refuse to let platform economics dictate movement priorities. We refuse to automate away human voice or theological discernment. We refuse to compete with the leaders we serve for their audience or their revenue. We refuse to build in a way that makes the network a side feature instead of the point.

Who we’re for and who we’re not for: We’re for movement leaders—those who catalyze lasting change through systematic, network-based, multiplication-minded work. We’re not for everyone. We’re for leaders who want to own their ground and connect; we’re not for those who only want a rental with our logo. The intention includes saying no so we can say yes clearly.

Means — Here’s how we’ll do it

Product and delivery: We build complete digital publishing platforms—content, commerce, community, analytics—designed for movement leaders, on a timeline that matters (2–4 weeks). We use a shared-success revenue model so incentives align. We integrate AI that amplifies and preserves voice rather than replaces it, and we connect each platform to a network so that discovery and credibility flow through relationship.

Structure and practice: We work from a type-safe, multi-tenant technical foundation so we can move fast without breaking. We treat the dashboard and docs as a single source of truth so that strategy, copy, and product stay aligned. We use fit checks and site order so that the right people find us and understand us in the right sequence. We create content and tools that assume our principles—transparency about AI, voice preservation, scenius as credibility—so the means themselves embody the vision.

Network and collaboration: We build in links, referrals, and collaboration from the start. Leaders don’t just get a site; they get access to a collective. We design for cross-promotion, shared intelligence, and mutual verification. The means include the ongoing practice of connecting leaders to each other and to the infrastructure (Movement Leaders Collective, 100Movements, and the growing Movemental network) so that scenius becomes real, not rhetorical.

Governance and renewal: We lock principles into decision-making—formation over growth, humans over hacks, scenius over genius, technology properly ordered—and we revisit them. We measure by transformation and sustainability, not just traffic or MRR. We keep the anchor story and VIM in front of us so that “how we do it” stays accountable to “why we exist” and “what we intend.”

VIM in brief

Vision = If we do this, movement leaders will own their platforms, credibility will flow through a shared network, and movements will multiply. Intention = We’re committing to that outcome and to the cost: accessible pricing, capped scale, non-negotiables, and clear “no” so we can say “yes” to the right people. Means = We’ll do it through platforms built for movement leaders, a shared-success model, AI that amplifies voice, a connected network, and governance that keeps principles first. Our story of change is the move from sharecropping to ownership, from isolation to scenius, and from extraction to shared success—with Vision as the picture, Intention as the decision, and Means as the practice.
